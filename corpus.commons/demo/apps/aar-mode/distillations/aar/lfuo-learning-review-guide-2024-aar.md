# US Forest Service LFUO 2024, After-Action Review Distillation

**Source:** US Forest Service, Risk Management Council & Office of Safety & Occupational Health (2024). *Learning From Unintended Outcomes and Learning Review Implementation Guide*. Revised April 2024. **Licence:** Public domain (17 USC §105 — US federal-employee work). **Scope:** open.

## After-Action Review Relevance

The LFUO 2024 guide is the most complete HOP-tradition learning-review framework in the demo corpus. Its load-bearing contributions to AAR work are: a four-tool ladder that routes events to the right learning tool by learning opportunity rather than outcome severity; a coherent stance against causal statements and counterfactual reasoning; the Five Hows / Lessons Learned Analysis as the structured local-rationality reconstruction tool; the Ten Principles and Agreements as the pre-conditions for honest accounts; and the administrative-firewall commitment as the structural safety mechanism without which honest accounts will not surface. The guide carries borrowed-through citations to Dekker, Hollnagel, Weick, Klein, and Reason — the full HOP canon — but the demo does not hold those authors directly; citations to those lineages from this distillation are borrowed-through, not first-hand. The guide replaces "root cause" with *networked causality*: "the combined total of those conditions that collectively influenced the event or outcome" (Source: Glossary, "Networked causality"). Frame to bring: the FLA/Learning Review are not the same thing as an AAR — the four-tool ladder exists to prevent the wrong tool from being applied to the right event.

## Key Concepts for After-Action Review

1.  **Four-tool ladder: AAR / RLS / FLA / Learning Review.** Each tool serves a distinct scope. The AAR produces immediate local learning, confidential to the work unit. The RLS produces a simple shareable lesson. The FLA produces a scalable no-punitive-action learning product without recommendations. The Learning Review adds vetted recommendations via focus groups, academic SMEs, and a Learning Review Board. Scale by learning opportunity, not outcome severity. (Source: US Forest Service, *LFUO Guide*, Part 2, "The tools")

2.  **Learning opportunity over outcome severity.** "The natural tendency is to base the size and complexity of the review team upon the severity of the outcome. Often, the more 'serious the accident,' the more resources are put toward the review team. This tendency may pretermit stellar learning opportunities" (Source: Part 3, "Starting the process") The tree-cutting near-miss is the guide's worked example: a two-inch difference in tree fall would have been a fatality; the learning opportunity does not depend on that chance.

3.  **Administrative firewall as precondition.** "It is critical to maintain a solid firewall between the FLA and any potential administrative actions that may be taken against the employee. Information is the lifeblood of safety" (Source: Part 3, "Administrative assurance of no punitive actions") Without an explicit written no-punitive-action commitment from the Delegating Official, honest accounts will not surface.

4.  **Three foundational principles: Just Culture, Systems Thinking, Learning.** Just Culture is defined as the intersection of trust, fairness, and dignity — forward-looking accountability, not criminal-justice culpability. Systems Thinking examines policies, procedures, processes, and culture shaping the event. Learning treats unintended outcomes as "valued learning opportunity and as a means to reducing uncertainty" (Source: Part 1, all three sections) All three are intertwined; weakening one weakens the others.

5.  **Networked causality replaces root cause.** "Cause isn't something investigators 'find' or 'discover'; cause is always something we create by recreating the event showing how certain anomalies led to the accident" (Source: Appendix B, "A Deeper Dive into Causal Factors") The replacement vocabulary is *networked causality*. "FLAs must avoid causal statements" (Source: Part 5, "Recommendations vs Lessons Learned").

6.  **Counterfactual reasoning is a forbidden move.** "FLA teams must guard against making counterfactual arguments such as: 'If this person had done X, then the outcome would have been Y and the accident would not have occurred.' The FLA is only useful when it learns why people did what they actually did (why it made sense to them at the time)" (Source: Part 9, "Avoid Counterfactual Arguments").

7.  **Five Hows as local-rationality reconstruction tool.** The Lessons Learned Analysis applies the Five Hows to each key decision in the event: explore how and why decisions that seemed wrong in hindsight "were actually valid or at least legitimate in context at the time" (Source: Part 7, "The Five Hows – A Sensemaking Tool") Consensus is not the goal; tension between team-member perspectives is valuable.

8.  **Beliefs, perceptions, expectations, paradigms decomposition.** The Lessons Learned Analysis procedure surfaces "the beliefs, perceptions, expectations, and paradigms held before the experience that in hindsight turned out wrong, inaccurate, misleading, or deceptive" (Source: Part 7, step 4) This four-category decomposition makes invisible pre-event mental models visible and testable.

9.  **Ten Principles and Agreements.** The group-dialogue pre-conditions, including: no administrative actions from anything learned through the FLA; respectful discussion; learning-over-blame-assessment; willingness to be vulnerable; team is not there to "fix" a problem; "almost all human actions and decisions are intuitive responses to circumstances largely based on past experiences" (Source: Part 6, "Principles and Agreements for All Involved in the Facilitation") Read all ten aloud before opening the group dialogue.

10.  **Deflection-question protocol for emotional recall.** When a participant's account reaches a vivid traumatic memory, "be prepared with a few deflection questions to mentally 'walk the person away' from these vivid memories they are sharing with you" (Source: Part 6, "Interviews") Redirect to something they know well and are comfortable talking about before returning to the difficult passage.

11.  **Multiple improbable events as the pattern.** "Most often, the Lessons Learned Analysis will reveal that multiple improbable events were necessary for the accident to occur" (Source: Part 7, "The Lessons Learned Analysis") Single-cause anchoring is not just bad epistemics — it is empirically wrong for complex-system events.

12.  **Work-as-imagined vs work-as-done as the diagnostic frame.** "How were we actually completing the work vs how managers were expecting the work to be completed?" (Source: Part 1, "Systems Thinking") Illuminating the gap between the two "will illuminate substantial and critical organizational vulnerabilities" (Source: Part 9, "Display Misalignments").

13.  **Reckless-and-willful threshold as the FLA termination criterion.** "A reckless and willful disregard for human safety is conduct that is intentional, unjustifiable, and occurred with the foreknowledge that the conduct was likely to result in serious harm, death, or injury to a human" (Source: Appendix C) If this threshold is met, the FLA terminates; interview notes are sealed. Below the threshold, FLA continues.

14.  **Recommendations only through Learning Review.** "In many circumstances, recommendations actually interfere with learning. Often recommendations, when implemented, add complexity to the workplace, which has the paradoxical effect of increasing risk" (Source: Part 5, "Recommendations vs Lessons Learned") Recommendations require Learning Review with the Learning Review Board and Safety Action Plan.

## Questions to Ask During After-Action Review

### Phase 0: Scoping

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Which tool fits this event? | Apply the four-tool ladder: is this an immediate-local AAR, an RLS, an FLA, or a Learning Review? |
| Is the learning opportunity here even if the outcome was minor? | Would a small difference have changed the outcome classification? If yes, the learning opportunity is real regardless of actual outcome severity. |
| Is the administrative firewall in place? | Has the Delegating Official issued a signed delegation of authority committing to no punitive administrative actions? |
| Is there enough trust in this unit for honest accounts? | Is there enough trust here to support such an analysis? Are there conflicting stories surrounding the event? |

### Phase 1: Timeline and Local-Rationality Reconstruction

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| What did participants believe, perceive, expect, and assume before the event? | Work through the four-category decomposition for each key decision: what was the belief, perception, expectation, and paradigm the participant held at the time? |
| Are we interviewing before hindsight contaminates accounts? | Interview as soon as possible; "memories change, perspectives shift as time progresses" (Part 5, "Should the affected people take Notes?"). |
| Are accounts diverging? | "Don't see this as somebody being right and somebody being wrong" — diversity of narrative is a source of resilience in complex systems. |
| Is emotional recall blocking the interview? | Apply deflection-question protocol: redirect to something the person knows well; return to the difficult memory once they are steadier. |

### Phase 2: Contributory-Factor Analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Has anyone used the phrase "root cause"? | Redirect: cause is constructed, not discovered. What are the conditions — networked, not linear — that collectively influenced the outcome? |
| Is counterfactual reasoning creeping in? | Redirect to what people actually did and why it made sense to them at the time. |
| Are multiple improbable events visible? | The Lessons Learned Analysis almost always reveals that multiple improbable events were necessary. Is the team anchoring on one? |
| Are we applying the Five Hows to each key decision? | For each key action identified: how did the participant develop their understanding of the situation, and why did the action make sense to them? |
| Is the work-as-imagined / work-as-done gap visible? | How were employees actually completing the work vs how administrators expected it to be completed? |

### Phase 3: Just-Culture Sorting

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Has the reckless-and-willful threshold been assessed? | Was the conduct intentional, unjustifiable, and did the person have foreknowledge that it was likely to result in serious harm? This threshold is high and bright. |
| Is the team using accountabilism language? | "Accountabilism" is the guide's term for claiming to learn from accounts but then punishing via judgmental language or forced retraining. Is that pattern appearing? |
| Is the just-culture framing forward-looking? | "Preventing the next accident, not focused on correcting history" (Part 1, "Just Culture") — is the conversation pointed forward? |

### Phase 4: Action Design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Are recommendations being produced from an FLA? | Recommendations require a Learning Review with focus groups, academic SMEs, Learning Review Board, and Safety Action Plan. |
| Is the proposed action adding complexity to a complex system? | "Recommendations, when implemented, add complexity to the workplace, which has the paradoxical effect of increasing risk." Is the action a genuine barrier against a simple failure, or will it add variance? |
| Has the Safety Action Plan named responsibility and timing? | SAP entries must designate who is responsible for completing the recommendation(s) and be consulted before committing resources. |

### Phase 5: Learning-Loop Closure

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Has the story been validated through the Readback process? | Read the finalised learning product aloud to participants; they correct important details. "Remember this is their story, not yours" (Part 8, "Validating Stories through the Readback process"). |
| Is the learning product in the right format for its audience? | Organisational learning product (for policy-makers: gap between work-as-imagined and work-as-done) vs field learning product (for frontline: emotive, experiential, personally reflective). |
| Has the Recommended Reading gap been named? | The guide's Recommended Reading names Dekker, Hollnagel, Weick, Klein, Reason, Conklin — authors the demo does not hold directly. |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| A participant says "the root cause was X" | Root-cause framing has entered the room — the networked-causality position has not been established | Redirect: "Cause is constructed, not discovered. What are the conditions — networked, not linear — that collectively influenced the outcome?" |
| The group moves quickly from timeline to action design | Multiple improbable events have not been surfaced; the analysis has anchored on the most obvious factor | Return to the LLA: what other conditions had to align for this outcome to occur? |
| An FLA is producing a list of recommendations | Recommendations belong to the Learning Review pathway, not the FLA | Name this explicitly; if the sponsoring leader wants recommendations, initiate the Learning Review process |
| An interviewee's account slows or stops at a traumatic moment | Emotional recall is blocking the account | Apply the deflection-question protocol: redirect to a comfortable domain; return to the difficult passage after a settling period |
| The conversation uses "should have" or "could have" language | Counterfactual reasoning has entered — the forbidden move | Redirect to: "What did they actually do, and why did that make sense at the time?" |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this source when:
- The scoping decision needs a principled framework for choosing between AAR, RLS, FLA, and Learning Review — the four-tool ladder is the corpus's only explicit routing decision tree.
- The group is using root-cause language and needs a named epistemological replacement (networked causality).
- The administrative firewall question has not been answered and the review is about to proceed without it.
- A participant's account is approaching a traumatic memory and the deflection-question protocol is needed.
- Phase 4 is producing recommendations when the sponsoring leader has not initiated a Learning Review.

This is the primary methodology reference for the AAR axis. Most other sources in this axis complement LFUO; LFUO is the backbone.

## Worked Example

A maintenance team reviews a near-miss: a gas line was struck during a dig because the locate marks were placed two metres from the actual line. The incident caused no injury but a two-foot difference in dig position would have been a fatality. The facilitator applies the four-tool ladder (Source: US Forest Service, *LFUO Guide*, Part 2, "The tools"): the learning opportunity is high (near-fatality pattern) despite the minor outcome; the FLA is the right tool. The Delegating Official signs the administrative-firewall commitment before any interviews occur (Part 3, "Administrative assurance of no punitive actions"). The facilitator reads the Ten Principles and Agreements aloud before the group dialogue opens (Part 6). During Phase 1, the facilitator applies the four-category decomposition (Part 7, step 4) to the locate crew's key decision: what did they believe about the line's depth and position? what did they perceive from the as-built drawings? what did they expect the locate flags to represent? The Five Hows reveal that the as-built drawings were last updated twelve years ago and the locate crew had no reason to question them — the decision to trust the drawings was locally rational. In Phase 5, the facilitator runs the Readback: the team corrects two details in the learning narrative before it is finalised. The learning product distinguishes the organisational product (gap between work-as-imagined and work-as-done in the drawing-maintenance process) from the field product (emotive narrative for frontline crews about what the crew saw and decided in the moment).

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Scaling the review team to outcome severity rather than learning opportunity — "pretermitting stellar learning opportunities."
- Producing causal statements in an FLA; cause is constructed, not found.
- Applying counterfactual reasoning: learning why people did not do what hindsight suggests is worthless; learn why they did what they did.
- Anchoring on a single root cause when multiple improbable events were necessary.
- Adding recommendations to an FLA; without a Learning Review and Safety Action Plan, recommendations are unvetted and unowned.
- Delaying interviews until hindsight has already contaminated accounts.
- Allowing judgment language into accounts — "accountabilism" as the slang for the pattern that claims to learn but punishes.
- Proceeding without a signed administrative-firewall commitment; honest accounts will not surface without it.
- Treating the work-as-imagined gap as an individual failing rather than an organisational vulnerability.
- Citing Dekker, Hollnagel, Weick, Klein, or Reason as if the demo holds them directly; the citations are borrowed-through this guide's Recommended Reading.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** Sidney Dekker is cited repeatedly throughout the guide (Dekker's *Just Culture*, *The Field Guide to Understanding Human Error*, and the Dekker & Breakey paper on restorative justice are all cited in Recommended Reading and at key points in the body); the demo corpus does not hold Dekker directly. Erik Hollnagel (ETTO principle, Resilience Engineering) is cited borrowed-through. Karl Weick and Kathleen Sutcliffe (*Managing the Unexpected*, HRO principles) are cited borrowed-through. Gary Klein (Recognition Primed Decision Making) is cited borrowed-through. James Reason (*Managing the Risks of Organizational Accidents*) is cited borrowed-through. Todd Conklin (*Pre-Accident Investigations*) is cited in Recommended Reading. The Roese & Vohs 2012 paper on hindsight bias is cited borrowed-through. None of these authors are held as primary references in the demo corpus; their works are not openly licensed and cannot be cited directly here.

**Named limits of the source.** The LFUO guide is designed for the US Forest Service context and uses wildland-fire and forest-management worked examples throughout. Applicability to non-field, non-safety-critical, or non-US-federal contexts is by adaptation. The guide explicitly limits FLAs: they cannot produce recommendations (recommendations require a full Learning Review). The reckless-and-willful threshold definition (Appendix C) is the guide's own working definition, not a legal standard; facilitators operating in environments with specific legal accountability frameworks should check local applicability.

**Evidence-marker continuity.** The networked-causality definition is paraphrased from the source's Glossary; the deep ref carries it as `[V]` in context. The counterfactual-reasoning prohibition is `[V]` in the deep ref (Part 9); the distillation quotes it directly in Concept 6. The learning-opportunity framing is `[V]` in the deep ref (Part 3); the distillation quotes it in Concept 2. The Dekker and Hollnagel citations in the deep ref are all `[BT]`; the distillation preserves the borrowed-through status in this section and in the Anti-patterns section.
