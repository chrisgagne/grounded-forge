## AAR Relevance

The Liberating Structures Handbook projects onto the AAR axis as a facilitation-craft layer: it provides the conversation structures that turn a technically correct AAR protocol into a learning conversation people will actually own. The handbook's load-bearing framing distinction — "buy-in is the exact opposite [of ownership]… Aiming for buy-in creates lukewarm, pallid implementation and mediocre results" — is the structural argument for why an AAR must be discovery-based rather than verdict-based, which is the same argument TC 25-20 makes through its AAR-versus-critique distinction. The LS catalogue gives the facilitator named, combinable patterns for every AAR phase: a scoping conversation, a timeline-reconstruction, a contributory-factor dialogue, a locus-of-control reframe when participants feel helpless, and a learning-loop closure that ends in named owners and dates. Without these structures, an AAR can follow the right steps and still produce the wrong outcomes — a few dominant voices, invented root causes, vague action items, and a room that leaves feeling graded rather than heard.

## Key Concepts for AAR

1.  **After Action Debrief (What / So What / Now What).** The handbook's primary AAR structure, attributed to Center for Creative Leadership (borrowed-through). Three sequential questions in strict order: "WHAT? What is emerging here? What data stands out? What actually happened? SO WHAT? What do the data imply? Do you see a pattern? Does it make a difference? NOW WHAT? What action may help us move forward? Who else should be here?" (Source: Heft et al., *Liberating Structures Handbook*, "After Action Debrief") The discipline is sequence — the handbook holds the order because Now-What decisions land badly when the data and pattern questions have not been answered.

2.  **Ownership versus buy-in as the AAR's epistemic frame.** "Ownership is when you own or share the ownership of an idea, a decision, an action plan, a choice. It means that you have participated in its development; that it is your choice freely made." Buy-in is being convinced after the fact. The word *buy-in* is a diagnostic warning: "Anytime you or someone around you thinks or talks about buy-in, beware! It is a danger signal." (Source: "Ownership versus Buy-in") An AAR whose actions are handed down by the senior person in the room has produced buy-in, not ownership.

3.  **Discovery & Action Dialogues (DADs).** Six-question protocol that ends in the structural commitment move: "Who will do what when next?" The full sequence: "What do you know/think about ___? What do YOU do about ___? What keeps you from doing that all the time? Who/Where have you seen overcome those barriers? What other ideas do you have about removing barriers? What has to happen next to make that happen? Who will do what when next?" (Source: "Discovery & Action Dialogues") Used at Phase 4 (action design) to close the AAR on named owners rather than a vague list.

4.  **Wicked Questions.** "A question is 'wicked' if there is an embedded paradox or tension in the question… Wicked questions do not have obvious answers. Their value lies in their capacity to open up options, inquiry and bring to the surface the fundamental issues that need to be addressed." (Source: "Wicked Questions") In an AAR, the wicked question move surfaces system-level tensions that a single root-cause framing suppresses. Distinguishes paradox from complaint.

5.  **TRIZ (inversion before bounding positively).** From TRIZ origins (Soviet inventor Altshuller, borrowed-through), the single move the handbook uses: "Design a comprehensive system that makes it absolutely impossible to get that result. What policies, practices, and ways of operating would make it 100% certain there is no way any of the things you want can happen. Does the system you've designed have anything in common with the current state of affairs?" (Source: "TRIZ") In an AAR the inversion move surfaces currently-invisible self-sabotaging structures before the team names positive next steps.

6.  **15% Solutions (locus-of-control reframe).** "Most people have about 15-percent control over their work situations. The other 85 percent rests in the broader context, shaped by the general structures, systems, events and culture in which they operate." (Source: "15% Solutions", citing Gareth Morgan, borrowed-through) The diagnostic question: "Where do you have freedom to act? What's in your 15%?" In an AAR where participants feel the contributory factors are outside their control, the 15% Solutions move converts a paralysing whole-system framing into a tractable personal-agency question without denying the 85%.

7.  **Wise Crowds (5-6 person consulting team, client's back turned).** Groups of 5–6, sitting knee-to-knee. Client describes a challenge for ~10 minutes; then turns chair around and listens silently while the consulting team talks — about what might help: "questions, paraphrases, metaphors, examples, musings." The structural rule: "The Consulting Team's job is not to decide 'this is what The Client ought to do.'" (Source: "Wise Crowds") In an AAR this structure gives a team member who is stuck on a systemic issue a peer consulting round without the group drifting into prescription.

8.  **Just In Time Topics (Open Space lineage, emergent agenda).** Self-organising agenda: participants write a topic they want to spend 45–90 minutes on. Two operating laws: "Whoever comes are the right people" and "The law of two feet. If you're not contributing or getting value, use your feet to go somewhere else." (Source: "Just In Time Topics") In a large-group AAR where Phase 2 contributory-factor work needs to branch into topic-specific working groups, Just In Time Topics surfaces what the group most needs to talk about rather than imposing a fixed agenda.

9.  **Levels of Accountability ladder (above-the-line vs below-the-line).** A stair-step ladder from "Denial / Woe is me" at the bottom through "Blame others," "Blame self," "Wait 'n Hope," "Acknowledge situation," "Accept ownership," "Find Solutions," to "Take Action and Learn" at the top. (Source: "Levels of Accountability") In an AAR where the conversation has slid into blame language or helplessness, the ladder gives the facilitator a named frame for redirecting without shaming.

10.  **5 Whys.** Pair-based question-asking: "Keep going until you feel you've pared away enough layers to get at a fundamental truth." (Source: "5 Whys") In an AAR the 5 Whys move surfaces contributory factor chains that a flat brainstorm misses. Paired with LFUO's networked-causality framing — the 5 Whys provides the conversational tool; LFUO provides the anti-causal-statement discipline that keeps the Whys from converging on a single root cause.

11.  **1-2-4-Whole Group (against HiPPOs).** Four-stage scaling: individual reflection, pairs (5–10 minutes), groups of 4–6, then whole group. "More than six is too big to give everyone a chance to talk." (Source: "1-2-4-Whole Group") In an AAR where rank or seniority is suppressing candour, the 1-2-4 structure opens the timeline-reconstruction and contributory-factor phases to all perspectives before the dominant voice can anchor the room.

12.  **Mini Constellations (positional diversity).** Spatial-positioning: "Place an object on the floor to represent an idea, choice, move, etc. Ask participants to place themselves somewhere in the room that represents to what degree they feel positively about the idea." (Source: "Mini Constellations") In an AAR where the group appears to have reached consensus but the consensus is artificial, Mini Constellations surfaces the full range of views without forcing a vote.

13.  **Troika Consulting (peer iteration on a stuck problem).** Groups of three, 10 minutes per focal person. Focal person describes a challenge; partners ask honing questions, engage with each other, then with the focal person. After all three rounds the group surfaces patterns. (Source: "Troika Consulting") In an AAR where individual team leads are each carrying a different piece of the contributory-factor analysis, Troika Consulting gives each person a structured peer consultation round.

14.  **Nothing About Me Without Me / Engage the Unusual Suspects.** "Who's not here that could contribute to our conversation?" is named as "one of the most important questions you can ask." (Source: "Nothing About Me Without Me"; "Engage the Unusual Suspects") In an AAR scoping conversation (Phase 0), the question who must be present to reconstruct the full timeline is a structural decision, not a logistics detail.

## Questions to Ask During AAR

### Phase 0 — Scoping

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Who must be in the room? | "Who's not here that could contribute to our conversation?" (Engage the Unusual Suspects) — whose absence will produce a partial timeline or a biased contributory-factor analysis? |
| Is the group safe enough for discovery? | Do we have a working agreement that this AAR is discovery, not grading? If buy-in language surfaces in the scoping conversation, that is the danger signal — who was left out of the development? |

### Phase 1 — Timeline and local-rationality reconstruction

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Opening the timeline with all voices | Run 1-2-4-Whole Group: individuals write what they observed; pairs compare accounts; groups of 4–6 surface the divergences; whole group hears what surprised. The divergences are the data, not a problem. |
| Keeping the sequence on What before So What | What? What is emerging here? What data stands out? What actually happened? — hold this question before letting the group move to pattern-making. |

### Phase 2 — Contributory-factor analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Surfacing factors below the obvious | 5 Whys: "Why?" at least five times, relaxable past five; "Why is that important? And why do you care about that?" |
| Surfacing the system that made the outcome inevitable | TRIZ inversion: What policies and practices would make it 100% certain this outcome happens every time? Does the current system resemble that? |
| Naming the paradox in the system | Wicked Questions: Where is the embedded paradox or tension that no single action will resolve? |
| Testing for artificial consensus | Mini Constellations: place an object on the floor; where does each person stand relative to the proposed contributory factor? |

### Phase 3 — Just-culture sorting

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Redirecting blame language | Levels of Accountability: Where on the ladder is this conversation sitting? What do we need to do to work above the line? (Pairs with NHS just-culture decision tree for the formal locus question.) |
| Keeping the group in discovery mode | So What? What do the data imply? Do you see a pattern? Does it make a difference? — hold the So-What question before the group moves to actions. |

### Phase 4 — Action design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Closing on owned next steps | Discovery & Action Dialogues closing sequence: "What has to happen next to make that happen? Who will do what when next?" — refuse adjournment without named owner and date. |
| Opening the action space when participants feel stuck | 15% Solutions: "Where do you have freedom to act? What's in your 15%?" — acknowledge the 85% without letting it block the 15%. |
| Giving a stuck contributor a peer consulting round | Wise Crowds: consultant team of 5–6, client's back turned, "job is not to decide what the client ought to do." Client rejoins with what intrigued, surprised, or resonated. |
| Surfacing system-level actions that need leadership | Just In Time Topics: participants name the system-level topics; self-organising groups work each one; the law of two feet keeps energy in the right room. |

### Phase 5 — Learning-loop closure

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Closing the debrief on Now What | Now What? What action may help us move forward? Who else should be here? — name next steps and owners before the room clears. |
| Compressing the take-away to something memorable | What is the one pattern this AAR surfaced? Can it be stated in fewer than eight words? (Six Words compression move — useful for the facilitator's closing summary.) |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| The senior voice speaks first in Phase 1 and the room aligns immediately | HiPPO anchoring before the small-group round — the 1-2-4 sequence was bypassed | Pause and run 1-2-4: individual writing before any verbal sharing |
| The group closes on an action with "we'll look into it" | Now-What phase without named owner and date — buy-in rather than ownership | Run the DAD closing sequence: "Who will do what when next?" before adjourning |
| The contributory-factor analysis returns the same obvious factor every time more Whys are asked | The 5-Whys is circling rather than deepening | Introduce the TRIZ inversion: what would make this outcome inevitable every time? |
| Participants feel the system is the cause and they are helpless | Below-the-line positioning on the Levels of Accountability ladder | Run 15% Solutions: "Where do you have freedom to act right now?" |
| The group is reaching consensus on a factor but no one is challenging it | Artificial consensus — the positional diversity has not been tested | Run Mini Constellations: show me where you stand relative to this factor |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this source when:
- The AAR is at risk of being captured by a dominant voice — 1-2-4-Whole Group and Mini Constellations are the structural protections.
- The action-design phase is producing vague commitments — Discovery & Action Dialogues' closing sequence is the mechanism.
- Participants feel helpless in the face of system-level factors they cannot change — 15% Solutions is the reframe.
- The contributory-factor analysis needs to name a structural paradox, not solve it — Wicked Questions is the move.
- The facilitator needs a named structure for every phase, not just a protocol.

Do not reach for this source as the primary protocol or the just-culture tool — TC 25-20 and LFUO carry the protocol; NHS carries the just-culture decision tree. This source provides the conversation architecture that makes those protocols work in the room.

## Worked Example

A cross-functional team AAR is reviewing a product launch delay. The facilitator opens Phase 1 with 1-2-4-Whole Group (Source: Heft et al., *Liberating Structures Handbook*, "1-2-4-Whole Group"): each person writes individually for two minutes, then pairs share for five minutes, then groups of four surface divergences. The divergences reveal three competing accounts of where the decision to extend the timeline originated. Rather than adjudicating, the facilitator holds the What question: "What do the data — all three accounts — say about what happened?" (Source: "After Action Debrief"). In Phase 2, the TRIZ inversion surfaces that the current approval process would make the delay outcome inevitable in similar future situations. The Wicked Question that emerges: "How can we maintain the quality gate that delayed the launch and also meet the market window that the delay missed?" — a genuine paradox, not a solvable problem. In Phase 4, the facilitator runs a DAD closing sequence: "What has to happen next to make that happen? Who will do what when next?" (Source: "Discovery & Action Dialogues"). Three specific commitments emerge, each with a named individual and a date. The facilitator checks for buy-in vs ownership: each person can state in their own words why they chose this commitment — the test of ownership rather than consent.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Running an AAR where the senior person's view anchors the timeline before others have spoken — solved by 1-2-4-Whole Group's structural sequencing.
- Treating artificial consensus as genuine consensus — solved by Mini Constellations surfacing positional diversity before the group commits.
- Closing the AAR on vague collective actions with no named owners — solved by Discovery & Action Dialogues' "Who will do what when next?" discipline.
- Jumping to Now-What before What and So-What have been answered — solved by the strict three-question sequence of After Action Debrief.
- Participants feeling helpless in the face of system-level contributory factors they cannot change — solved by 15% Solutions' locus-of-control reframe.
- Surfacing a wicked tension and treating it as a solvable problem — solved by naming it as a Wicked Question rather than reaching for a single answer.
- Designing the AAR as a verdict on people rather than a discovery of the system — solved by the ownership-versus-buy-in frame and the Levels of Accountability ladder redirect.
- Leaving the AAR's structural system-contributors unexplored because the group ran out of time — solved by TRIZ inversion surfacing the invisible self-sabotaging structures before positive actions are named.
- Inviting people to an AAR as window dressing without the opportunity to contribute — solved by the Nothing About Me Without Me discipline applied at Phase 0.
