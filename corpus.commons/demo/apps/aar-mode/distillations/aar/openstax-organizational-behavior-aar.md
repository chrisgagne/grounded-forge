# OpenStax Organizational Behavior, AAR Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2019). *Organizational Behavior*. Lead author J. Stewart Black (INSEAD). ISBN 978-1-947172-72-2 (digital). Licence: CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. Scope: open-nc.

## AAR Relevance

Organizational Behavior is the facilitator's diagnostic vocabulary for what is happening in the room during an AAR. Its load-bearing contributions are not prescriptions — the textbook does not say "run an AAR this way." Instead it names the cognitive and social dynamics that pull an AAR off course: attribution biases that push observers toward individual blame, perceptual gaps between functional groups, bounded rationality that collapses a complex incident to one obvious cause, and conformity pressures that suppress dissent before all accounts are on the table. The facilitator who knows attribution theory can spot the fundamental attribution error arriving in real time and redirect to situational framing without shaming the person who voiced it. The OB lens also names what productive conflict looks like (process conflict can improve outcomes; relationship conflict almost never does), how to read cohesiveness as a risk factor when it suppresses dissent, and when the encoding-decoding-noise communication model explains why two participants experienced the same event differently.

## Key Concepts for AAR

1.  **Fundamental attribution error.** "A tendency to underestimate the effects of external or situational causes of behavior and to overestimate the effects of internal or personal causes" [V]. In AARs: observers attribute the incident to the individual who acted rather than to the conditions the individual was in. This is the primary driver of blame language. The facilitator's move is to redirect from *who* to *what conditions made this the rational choice at the time*. (Source: Black et al., *Organizational Behavior*, Ch 3.3, "Attributional Bias")

2.  **Actor-observer bias.** Observers attribute others' behaviour dispositionally; actors attribute their own behaviour situationally. In AARs: the person closest to the incident attributes their choices to the situation they faced; the observer room attributes those same choices to the person's character. Both accounts are partial. The facilitator's move is to surface the actor's situational account before the observer's dispositional verdict lands. (Source: Ch 3.3, "Attributional Bias")

3.  **Self-serving bias.** The tendency to attribute success to one's own actions and failure to others. In AARs: departments or functions will surface their own contributions to the response while attributing the incident itself to adjacent teams. Recognising this as a systematic bias, not bad faith, helps the facilitator probe without accusation. (Source: Ch 3.3, "Attributional Bias")

4.  **Perceptual selectivity and functional background.** The Dearborn and Simon study: executives from different departments reading the same case identified different primary problems based on their functional background [BT]. For AARs: a cross-functional room will produce divergent accounts of what the central problem was — this is not inconsistency, it is partial perception. The facilitator's move is to treat divergence as data, not as a dispute to resolve. (Source: Ch 3.1, "Organizational Role")

5.  **Supervisor-subordinate perceptual gaps.** Likert's data: supervisors reported giving "sincere and thorough praise" 80 per cent of the time; subordinates reported receiving it 14 per cent of the time [V]. In AARs: the gap between leadership's account of how clear direction was and workers' account of the same direction is often this large. The facilitator surfaces the gap without adjudicating it. (Source: Ch 3.1, Table 3.1)

6.  **Bounded rationality.** Cognitive limits prevent fully rational treatment of complex issues. In AARs: a bounded account of the incident collapses a multi-factor event to one obvious cause. The facilitator's prompt after "the root cause was X" is: what else was in play? (Source: Ch 6.4)

7.  **Escalation of commitment.** Decision-makers stay committed to poor decisions even as costs mount. In AARs: understanding that escalation of commitment is a documented human pattern — not a character flaw — helps the facilitator reframe "why didn't they stop?" into "what kept the logic alive at each decision point?" (Source: Ch 6.4)

8.  **Confirmation bias.** Paying attention to information that confirms existing beliefs [V]. In AARs: participants who already have a theory of the incident will hear confirming data more clearly than disconfirming data. The facilitator's move is to name the disconfirming data explicitly. (Source: Ch 6.4, "Conflict")

9.  **Process vs relationship conflict.** Process conflict (about the best way to do something) "can actually lead to improved performance" [V]; relationship conflict is generally harmful. For AARs: disagreement about what went wrong is process conflict — it is the facilitator's job to keep it there and prevent it from becoming personal. (Source: Ch 6.4)

10.  **Six barriers to effective decision-making.** Bounded rationality, escalation of commitment, time constraints, uncertainty, personal biases, conflict — as a diagnostic set. For AARs: when the group is struggling to produce a clear contributory-factor list, this taxonomy helps the facilitator name which barrier is in play without attributing it to any individual. (Source: Ch 6.4)

11.  **Group cohesiveness and conformity.** The Asch conformity finding: "in over a third of trials, subjects denied the evidence of their own senses to agree with a unanimous group" [BT]. High cohesiveness can suppress minority accounts. In AARs: a tight team or unit may produce a single shared story that is in fact the dominant member's account. (Source: Ch 9.2, "Conformity and Deviance")

12.  **Communication encoding-decoding-noise model.** The basic model — communicator encodes, receiver decodes, noise distorts — names why participants who were physically present at the same event can produce accounts that seem to describe different incidents. Noise is not malice or memory failure; it is the structural condition of organisational communication. (Source: Ch 11.1)

13.  **Mintzberg's disturbance-handler role.** Managers in disturbance/crisis-handler mode are under reactive-system pressure. Decisions made under crisis conditions are less reflective, more heuristic-driven, more confirmation-biased. The AAR should reconstruct the conditions of the crisis, not evaluate the decision by post-event standards. (Source: Ch 11.3, "Decisional"; Ch 6.2)

## Questions to Ask During AAR

### Phase 1 — Timeline and Local-Rationality Reconstruction

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Actor-observer bias arriving ("they should have known") | "What was [person]'s account of what they were seeing at that point? Let's put their local view on the table before we evaluate it." |
| Perceptual gap between functions | "Your two groups seem to have seen the central problem differently. Can you each describe what stood out to you and why?" |
| Confirmation bias narrowing the timeline | "What evidence existed at the time that didn't fit the diagnosis that was forming? What got set aside?" |

### Phase 2 — Contributory-Factor Analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Bounded rationality (single-cause landing) | "That's one factor. What else was making this situation hard to navigate? We're looking for the conditions, not the cause." |
| Escalation of commitment as a factor | "At what point did the logic of continuing feel compelling rather than optional? What was keeping it alive?" |
| Attribution bias landing on a person | "What were the situational pressures on [person] at that point — time, resources, information, authority? Let's start there." |

### Phase 3 — Just-Culture Sorting

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Relationship conflict emerging | "I want to keep this as a question about what happened, not about who. Can we park the personal framing and come back to conditions?" |
| Conformity suppressing a dissenting account | "I'm sensing there may be a different read of this in the room. If there's a minority view, now is the time to name it — that's data too." |

### Phase 4 — Action Design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Action addressing symptom, not condition | "This action addresses what happened. Which of the contributing conditions does it change — and which does it leave in place?" |

### Phase 5 — Learning-Loop Closure

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Confirming actions will not be blocked by escalation of commitment | "Is there anything about these actions that might be hard to sustain when things get busy again? What's the risk of reverting?" |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| "Why did they do that?" used as a genuine diagnostic question | Fundamental attribution error arriving — the dispositional frame has replaced the situational one | Redirect: "What were the conditions they were in? What was the situation making rational?" |
| One functional group's account dominates the timeline | Perceptual selectivity — the dominant account reflects one role's vantage point, not the whole picture | Actively solicit accounts from each function represented before converging |
| The room reaches consensus quickly on a tight team's account | Conformity (Asch) suppressing minority views — cohesion is doing social work, not epistemic work | Ask the person who hasn't spoken yet; create a private-write round before the verbal discussion |
| Contributory-factor list has one item | Bounded rationality — the group is pattern-matching to the most obvious cause | Use the six-barriers taxonomy to name what else may be in play |
| Two participants describe the same event as if they were in different rooms | Encoding-decoding-noise — not inconsistency or dishonesty, but structural perceptual divergence | Treat the divergence as data: what does each account tell us about the system the person was operating in? |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this source when:
- The AAR room is drifting toward individual blame and the facilitator needs a named cognitive mechanism — not a moral argument — to redirect.
- Perceptual divergence between functions is producing contested accounts; OB's functional-background framing treats the divergence as signal, not noise.
- The incident involved crisis-mode decision-making and the facilitator needs to prevent post-hoc evaluation of decisions made under reactive-system conditions.
- Conformity pressure from a senior voice or a tight team is suppressing minority accounts.

This source is most useful as a room-management tool: it gives the facilitator precise vocabulary for the dynamics unfolding in real time. It does not carry timeline reconstruction protocol, just-culture decision frameworks, or action-design discipline. TC 25-20, LFUO, and NHS carry those.

## Worked Example

A cross-functional team reviews a missed safety alert. The operations team says the signal was clear; the night-shift technician says the dashboard was ambiguous. The facilitator applies the Dearborn-Simon perceptual-selectivity frame (Ch 3.1): the operations team's "clear signal" is the signal as seen from a functional vantage point with full system context; the technician's "ambiguous dashboard" is the signal as seen from the point of action with incomplete context. Rather than adjudicating which account is correct, the facilitator treats the divergence as data: the two groups are describing different epistemic positions in the system, not different events. In Phase 2, the facilitator introduces the bounded-rationality frame (Ch 6.4) when the group lands on "the technician should have escalated" as the root cause — probing for the five conditions that made non-escalation the locally rational choice: time pressure, authority ambiguity, information state, prior norm, and status asymmetry. The conformity check (Ch 9.2) is applied when a senior operations manager endorses the individual-blame account and the room falls quiet: the facilitator invites a written round before verbal discussion resumes, which surfaces two dissenting accounts from junior members who had stayed silent.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Treating "why did they do that?" as a genuine diagnostic question rather than recognising it as the fundamental attribution error arriving.
- Accepting the dominant functional group's account of the incident without actively surfacing the minority account from the group closest to the action.
- Allowing a tight, cohesive team to produce a single agreed narrative without probing for dissenting accounts suppressed by conformity.
- Letting confirmation bias narrow the timeline to the data that fits the pre-formed theory while leaving disconfirming evidence unexamined.
- Treating the disturbance-handler's crisis-mode decisions as if they were made in reflective-mode conditions.
- Confusing perceptual divergence between participants (different functional backgrounds seeing different problems) with inconsistency or dishonesty.
- Allowing relationship conflict — personal attribution — to replace the process conflict that is the productive form of disagreement in an AAR.
- Accepting "bounded" accounts that name one cause without probing for the five or six conditions that made the single cause sufficient.
- Designing actions that address the visible symptom while leaving the noise, bias, or structural condition that produced the incident unchanged.
