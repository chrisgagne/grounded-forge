# OpenStax Principles of Management, AAR Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2019). *Principles of Management*. Lead author David S. Bright et al. Houston, TX: Rice University. Licence: CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. Scope: open-nc.

## AAR Relevance

Principles of Management enters the AAR axis at two distinct moments: when an AAR surfaces organisational-design questions that need naming and routing, and when the learning loop moves from finding to fixing. The PDCA cycle (Plan-Do-Check-Act) frames the control cycle that makes AAR output actionable: the Check step is the AAR; Act closes the loop into the next Plan. Mintzberg's six structural types and contingency factors give the facilitator vocabulary to name *which* part of the organisation a contributory factor lives in, so the right interlocutor can be identified. French-Raven power bases explain why certain actions stall after the AAR ends. Motivation frameworks (Maslow, Herzberg, ERG, McClelland, SDT) diagnose why a team stops engaging with follow-through. The change-management canon (Lewin, Kotter, Cooperrider) frames how to move AAR outputs into lasting structural change without triggering defensive resistance.

## Key Concepts for AAR

1.  **PDCA / Deming-Shewhart cycle.** Plan → Do → Check → Act → return to Plan; the AAR occupies the Check step and feeds the next Plan. Without the Act step, AAR output is stranded. (Source: Bright et al., *Principles of Management*, Ch 17.2, "Planning, Implementation, and Controlling")

2.  **Six organisational structure types.** Functional, divisional, geographic, matrix, networked-team, virtual; each has distinct accountability lines and communication pathways. When an AAR surfaces a structural contributory factor, this taxonomy names the structure and its failure modes (silos in functional; dual-reporting conflict in matrix). (Source: Ch 4.3, "Organizational Designs and Structures")

3.  **Mechanistic vs organic continuum.** Mechanistic structures are rule-based, centralised, narrow span; organic are flat, participatory, fluid. AAR findings that attribute an incident to rigidity in a dynamic environment may surface a mechanistic-organic mismatch as the contributing condition. (Source: Ch 4.3)

4.  **Contingency factors shaping structure.** Strategy, environment (simple-stable to complex-unstable), size, technology; the "it depends" principle from Joan Woodward. When the AAR asks why the structure did not flex to the situation, contingency analysis provides the hypothesis-generator. (Source: Ch 3.7, Ch 4.2)

5.  **French-Raven five power bases.** Reward, coercive, referent, expert, legitimate. Research shows rationality and expertise produce commitment; coercive power often alienates. When an AAR surfaces a leadership-behaviour contributory factor, this taxonomy names the mechanism. (Source: Ch 13.3, "Leadership as an Exercise of Influence")

6.  **LMX (Leader-Member Exchange) theory.** Leadership as dyadic relationships of varying quality; high-quality exchange produces in-group engagement, low-quality produces out-group dynamics. When the AAR reveals that certain individuals' accounts were dismissed or unsurfaced during the incident, LMX suggests why. (Source: Ch 13.2)

7.  **Motivation content theories.** Maslow's hierarchy, Herzberg's motivator-hygiene (hygiene factors prevent dissatisfaction; motivators drive engagement), Alderfer's ERG with frustration regression, McClelland's nAch / nAff / nPow. When a team shows no motivation to act on AAR outputs, these frameworks diagnose the gap. (Source: Ch 14.2)

8.  **Self-determination theory (SDT).** Intrinsic motivation requires competence, autonomy, and relatedness; extrinsic rewards reduce intrinsic motivation for interesting tasks. AAR follow-through fails when actions are assigned without autonomy. (Source: Ch 14.2)

9.  **Tuckman's team-development stages.** Forming, storming, norming, performing; regression under scope or membership change [BT]. When an AAR diagnoses a team-dynamics contributory factor, Tuckman locates the team's current stage and its implications. (Source: Ch 15.2)

10.  **Follett's three conflict paths.** Dominance, compromise, integration [V terms]. Integration — both parties state preferences and reach a creative resolution — is the design target for AAR action design when stakeholder interests conflict. The library-window example: "the window in the next room where no one was sitting" [V]. (Source: Ch 3.6, "Follett and Conflict Resolution")

11.  **Four change-management models.** Lewin's three-stage (unfreeze / change / refreeze), Kotter's eight-step (urgency → guiding coalition → vision → communicate → remove obstacles → small wins → consolidate → anchor), Cooperrider's Appreciative Inquiry 5-D (define / discover / dream / design / destiny), Olson-Eoyang Complex Adaptive Systems (empower agents, strengthen connections, ensure flow). Each suits a different complexity and power-distribution context; the choice depends on whether the org is in deficit or abundance, top-down or bottom-up. (Source: Ch 10.3)

12.  **Balanced-scorecard-style performance measurement.** Performance evaluation feeds back into strategic analysis; benchmarking against clear standards closes the strategy cycle. AAR outputs need a monitoring mechanism; the BSC framing names what metrics track whether actions landed. (Source: Ch 9.6)

13.  **Goal theory (Locke).** Difficult, specific, accepted, committed-to goals produce better performance; participation in goal-setting increases commitment [V, paraphrased]. AAR action design benefits from goal specificity: vague actions do not produce Locke-compliant commitment. (Source: Ch 14.3, "Goal Theory")

14.  **Groupthink and devil's advocate.** Groupthink suppresses dissent; assigning a devil's advocate role surfaces latent disagreement [V]. When an AAR's debrief produces suspiciously fast consensus on actions, groupthink may be operating. (Source: Ch 2.6)

## Questions to Ask During AAR

### Phase 0: Scoping

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify structural ownership of the incident | Which of the six structural types does the affected unit sit in? Where are the accountability lines, and who in this structure has authority to act on findings? |
| Identify the power dynamics before convening | What power bases are in the room? Will coercive power suppress candid accounts, or is there enough referent and expert power to sustain openness? |

### Phase 1: Timeline and local-rationality reconstruction

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Name the team-stage context | Where is this team on Tuckman's curve? Storming teams produce contested accounts; performing teams surface local-rationality more readily. |
| Surface LMX dynamics in accounts | Whose account was heard during the incident? Whose was not? In-group / out-group LMX dynamics may explain which signals were surfaced and which were suppressed. |

### Phase 2: Contributory-factor analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Diagnose structural mismatch | Is the structure mechanistic in an environment demanding organic response, or vice versa? Does the contingency analysis (strategy / environment / size / technology) surface a mismatch that contributed? |
| Name the conflict type | Is the conflict process-type (best-way disagreement, potentially productive) or relationship-type (personal attack, harmful)? The distinction shapes whether the contributory factor is the disagreement itself or the way it was handled. |

### Phase 4: Action design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Route escalation to the right interlocutor | Which structural type holds the lever for the change being requested? In a functional structure, functional VPs; in a matrix, the project-office owner and the functional owner jointly. Name both. |
| Design for goal-theoretic commitment | Is the action specific, measurable, accepted, and committed to? Vague actions do not survive to next review. Apply Locke's criteria before adjourning. |
| Choose the change model for this action | Is the organisation in deficit or abundance? Is the change top-down or participatory? Lewin suits stable contexts; Cooperrider's AI suits abundance-based cultural change; CAS suits complex self-organising systems. |

### Phase 5: Learning-loop closure

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Confirm the PDCA loop is closed | The AAR is the Check step. What is the Act step — who owns it, by when, and what does the next Plan incorporate from this Check? |
| Diagnose motivation to follow through | If follow-through has failed before: are hygiene factors (Herzberg) causing dissatisfaction that crowds out action? Is SDT's autonomy need unmet? Is McClelland's nPow driving a political response to the action? |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| AAR finding lands on the wrong desk and stalls | Structural interlocutor not named — the finding was routed to a function without authority to act | Use the six-structure taxonomy to identify who holds the lever; re-route |
| "Leadership problem" in the finding that no one owns | Power-base unspecified — coercive, referent, expert, or legitimate framing changes the corrective | Name the power base in play; tailor the corrective to the mechanism |
| Fast consensus on AAR actions, room falls quiet | Groupthink operating — dissent is suppressed by team cohesion or a dominant voice | Assign a devil's advocate before closing; invite private written input |
| Same action was tried after the last similar incident and didn't hold | Escalation of commitment or structural constraint not addressed | Ask: does Kotter's urgency-coalition logic need to be invoked, or is the structure itself the barrier? |
| Team shows no energy for AAR follow-through | Motivation-hygiene or SDT gap — hygiene factors depleted, or actions were assigned without autonomy | Diagnose: is it dissatisfaction crowding out capacity, or lack of felt ownership? |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this source when:
- The AAR surfaces a structural contributory factor that needs routing — which part of the organisation owns it and has authority to act?
- Actions stall in implementation and the block is a leadership-behaviour or power dynamic that needs naming.
- The team's follow-through motivation has collapsed and the facilitator needs a diagnosis beyond "people aren't trying."
- AAR output must move into lasting structural change and the right change model for the context needs choosing.

This source is most valuable as a structural-routing and action-design complement to TC 25-20 and LFUO. Its distinctive contribution is naming the organisational interlocutor, the power mechanism, and the motivation gap — the three most common reasons AAR outputs die before they produce change.

## Worked Example

A team completes an AAR on a product-launch delay. The facilitator applies Principles of Management framing in Phase 2 and Phase 4. In Phase 2, the structural-mismatch question (Ch 4.3) surfaces that the affected unit is a matrix structure — dual reporting to a functional VP and a project-office owner — and that both owners believed the other had approved a critical resource allocation. The mechanistic-organic analysis (Ch 4.3) surfaces that the project used rigid milestone-gate governance (mechanistic) in an environment that changed rapidly after launch (complex-unstable), a mismatch the Woodward contingency frame (Ch 3.7) would have flagged in advance. In Phase 4, the facilitator uses the French-Raven power-base taxonomy (Ch 13.3) to name why a prior action recommendation from the project manager failed to move: the PM held legitimate and expert power but not referent power with the functional VP, so the recommendation was filed and not acted on. The action design applies Locke's goal-theory criteria (Ch 14.3) — specific, accepted, committed-to, with a named review date — and uses Kotter's eight-step urgency frame (Ch 10.3) to build a guiding coalition before the next planning cycle. The PDCA loop (Ch 17.2) closes explicitly: the AAR is the Check; the Act is a named structural change to the resource-approval process, owned by the project-office owner, with a BSC-style tracking metric.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Escalating an AAR finding without naming the structural interlocutor; the finding lands on the wrong desk and stalls.
- Diagnosing a leadership-behaviour contributory factor without specifying the power base in play, producing a vague "leadership problem" that no one owns.
- Designing actions without Locke-compliant specificity; vague actions are non-commitments.
- Choosing Kotter's urgency-framed change model in an abundance context, triggering resistance rather than engagement.
- Treating post-AAR follow-through failure as laziness when motivation-hygiene factors (pay, conditions, security concerns) are depleting the team's capacity to act.
- Missing groupthink in the action-design phase: fast consensus on AAR actions often reflects suppression of dissent, not genuine alignment.
- Ignoring LMX dynamics that explain whose voice shaped the incident account and whose was sidelined.
- Mistaking a mechanistic-organic mismatch for an individual-judgement failure; the structure, not the person, may be the load-bearing contributory factor.
