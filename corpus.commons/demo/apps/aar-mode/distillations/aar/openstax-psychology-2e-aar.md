# OpenStax Psychology 2e, AAR Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2020). *Psychology 2e*. Senior contributing author Rose M. Spielman. ISBN 978-1-951693-23-7 (digital). Licence: **CC BY-NC-SA 4.0**. Scope: open-nc.

## AAR Relevance

Psychology 2e is the facilitator's cognitive-science substrate for understanding why post-event accounts are systematically unreliable and why attribution bias is the default, not the exception. Its load-bearing contributions to AAR facilitation are: hindsight bias (the conviction that the outcome was predictable), the availability heuristic (recent or vivid events loom larger than base rates), anchoring (the first account sets the frame for all subsequent ones), confirmation bias (evidence that fits the forming theory is processed more strongly), and the social-influence canon — Asch, Milgram, Zimbardo — which together establish that situation shapes behaviour more powerfully than most AAR participants assume. Memory's reconstructive character (Schacter's seven sins; Loftus's misinformation effect) grounds the discipline of interviewing participants as soon as possible and keeping them on their own account rather than a composite. The facilitator draws on Psychology 2e not to diagnose participants but to name the cognitive conditions that make an honest account hard to produce, and to build a process that works against those conditions.

## Key Concepts for AAR

1. **Hindsight bias.** "Belief that the event just experienced was predictable" [V] (Source: OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 7.3, "Pitfalls to Problem Solving", Table 7.3). In AARs: once the outcome is known, participants experience the contributing factors as obvious. This bias contaminates both participant accounts and observer evaluations. Interviewing as soon as possible and prohibiting counterfactual framing ("but we should have known...") are the direct procedural responses.

2. **Availability heuristic.** "Decision is based upon either an available precedent or an example that may be faulty" [V] (Source: OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 7.3, Table 7.3). In AARs: participants weight vivid or recent incidents more than base rates. The most salient recent failure shapes the theory of the current incident regardless of how representative that failure is.

3. **Anchoring bias.** "Tendency to focus on one particular piece of information when making decisions or problem-solving" [V] (Source: OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 7.3, Table 7.3). In AARs: whichever account is offered first anchors the group's understanding of the incident. A facilitator who gathers accounts before offering any synthesis prevents anchoring from narrowing the contributory-factor space.

4. **Confirmation bias.** "Focuses on information that confirms existing beliefs" [V] (Source: OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 7.3, Table 7.3). In AARs: a group that has a working theory of the incident will process confirming data more strongly than disconfirming data. The facilitator's move is to name disconfirming data explicitly and ask the group to account for it.

5. **Fundamental attribution error.** "People tend to overemphasize internal factors as explanations… for the behavior of other people" and "underestimate the power of the situation on the behavior of others" [V] (Source: OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 12.1, "Fundamental Attribution Error"). The Ross, Amabile, and Steinmetz quizmaster study: observers rated questioners as more knowledgeable than contestants despite the obvious situational asymmetry [BT]. In AARs: this is the mechanism behind individual blame. The facilitator's vocabulary is local rationality and situational pressure, not character assessment.

6. **Actor-observer bias and self-serving bias.** The actor attributes their own behaviour situationally and others' dispositionally; the self-serving bias attributes success to disposition and failure to situation (Source: OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 12.1). In AARs: these two biases together produce the pattern in which each function attributes the incident to adjacent functions while explaining its own choices as situationally forced.

7. **Memory as reconstructive.** Memory encoding, storage, and retrieval are all subject to distortion (Source: OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 8.1). Schacter's seven sins include suggestibility and bias: "current beliefs reshape memories" (Ch 8.3, Table 8.1). Loftus's misinformation effect: leading questions can alter eyewitness memory [BT] (Ch 8.3). In AARs: facilitator question wording shapes what participants remember. Questions that embed a causal theory ("when you failed to escalate...") produce accounts that confirm that theory. Open-ended narrative questions ("walk me through what you were seeing") produce more accurate accounts.

8. **Conformity — Asch line studies.** Seventy-six per cent of participants conformed on at least one trial despite the obvious correct answer; the presence of a single dissenter dropped conformity to near zero [BT] (Source: OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 12.4). In AARs: a strong early account, delivered by a senior voice, can suppress minority accounts through conformity pressure alone. Written-first input before verbal discussion breaks this dynamic.

9. **Obedience to authority — Milgram.** Sixty-five per cent of participants administered what they believed to be lethal shocks under instruction from a researcher [BT] (Source: OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 12.4). In AARs: behaviours that look reckless from outside were often obedient to the authority structure and norms of the moment. The Milgram finding is the psychological substrate for local-rationality framing: people did what authority, norms, and context made rational.

10. **Situational power — Zimbardo.** The Stanford Prison Experiment demonstrated the power of role assignment on behaviour (Source: OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 12.2). Both the finding and subsequent critique of the design carry AAR weight: the design's flaws (explicit guidance to guards) remind the facilitator that situations are shaped, not just found. Roles and authority structures shape what participants felt they could do.

11. **Groupthink.** Groups prioritise cohesion and unanimity over critical thinking; symptoms include self-censorship and illusion of unanimity (Source: OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 12.4). In AARs: a tight team or a team with a dominant leader may produce a single agreed narrative faster than the evidence warrants. Preventatives: private written accounts before discussion, leaders withholding initial positions, explicit invitation of minority views.

12. **Perception as top-down and bottom-up.** Perception involves "both bottom-up (data-driven) and top-down (knowledge- and expectation-driven) processing" (Source: OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 5.1, "Perception"). In AARs: participants' expectations of how incidents unfold (top-down) shape what they notice and remember. Two participants can be physically present at the same event and produce genuinely different accounts — neither is lying.

13. **Stress and the general adaptation syndrome.** Selye's three-stage sequence: alarm → resistance → exhaustion (Source: OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 14.1). The Yerkes-Dodson law: optimal arousal varies with task complexity; complex tasks favour lower arousal (Ch 10.1). In AARs: decisions made during the alarm or exhaustion stages are not comparable to decisions made at optimal arousal. Reconstructing the physiological and attentional state of the incident moment produces a more honest account of why choices made sense.

## Questions to Ask During AAR

### Phase 1 — Timeline and Local-Rationality Reconstruction

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Hindsight bias arriving ("it was obvious") | "At the time — before the outcome was known — what was the evidence that this was the likely path? What did people have in front of them?" |
| Anchoring: first account dominating | "That's one account of the sequence. Before we build on it, let's hear from someone who was in a different position. What did you see?" |
| Memory reconstructed by leading questions | "Don't tell me what you think happened — tell me what you personally observed or decided. Stay in your own account." |
| Conformity suppressing a minority view | "I want to get everyone's individual read before we converge. Let's take two minutes for each person to write down their account independently." |
| Reconstructing the stress state of the incident | "What was the workload and arousal level at the moment this decision was made? How does that compare to conditions where this kind of decision goes well?" |

### Phase 2 — Contributory-Factor Analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Availability heuristic (recent vivid incident dominating) | "Is this incident actually similar to [previous incident], or does it just feel that way? What's different about the conditions?" |
| Fundamental attribution error ("they just didn't care") | "What was the situational pressure on [person] at that moment — what were they responsible for, what information did they have, what were they authorised to do?" |
| Confirmation bias narrowing factors | "What evidence existed at the time that didn't fit the theory we're building? What are we not accounting for?" |
| Actor-observer bias producing functional blame | "Each function is explaining its own choices as situationally forced. What is the honest situational account for the choices made by the adjacent function?" |

### Phase 3 — Just-Culture Sorting

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Obedience framing — "they were just following orders" | "Were they following an explicit instruction, an implicit norm, or a role expectation? Which of those is the system factor to address?" |
| Groupthink preventing a dissenting account | "Before we close on this, I want to hear from the person who hasn't agreed yet. What's your account?" |

### Phase 4 — Action Design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Action addressing person, not situation | "This action focuses on the individual. Which situational factor — the role structure, the information available, the authority norm — does it address? If none, what system action would address that factor?" |

### Phase 5 — Learning-Loop Closure

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Self-serving attribution in the closing summary | "Let's check: are we attributing the things that went well to our team's skill and the things that went wrong to the situation? That's a systematic bias. What's the honest read on both?" |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| The group converges on a narrative before all accounts are on the table | Anchoring — the first account (often the most senior voice) has narrowed the contributory-factor space before divergent accounts could surface | Use written-first input; go around the room before open discussion |
| A participant's account changes significantly after hearing a senior colleague's version | Conformity pressure — the Asch dynamic is operating; the public response is conforming to the majority | Create private channels (written submissions, anonymous input) before the group integrates |
| The timeline describes behaviours that look reckless in retrospect but were routine at the time | Hindsight bias is in play — the outcome is inflating the apparent predictability of the contributing factors | Ask: "What did the decision-maker have in front of them at the moment they chose? What would a competent person have done with that information?" |
| Individual blame lands on one person while others describe their own choices as forced by circumstances | Fundamental attribution error combined with actor-observer bias — the situated account applies to oneself but not to others | Apply the same situational analysis to the blamed person: role, information, authorisation, norms at the moment |
| The incident looks like a recent salient failure the team has discussed before | Availability heuristic — the vivid comparison is shaping the framing before the current incident's specific conditions have been examined | Ask: "What is actually different about this incident? What conditions do not match the prior one?" |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this source when:
- Participants are producing accounts that shift in the direction of the facilitator's emerging theory, suggesting question wording is shaping memory.
- One function is blaming another while explaining its own choices as situationally forced — the actor-observer bias is the mechanism to name.
- Senior voices are dominating early, and minority accounts are not surfacing.
- The group is converging on an individual-blame conclusion without examining the situational conditions that made the blamed behaviour rational at the time.
- Decisions made during the incident were made under high arousal or exhaustion, and the AAR is judging them against calm-state standards.

This source is most useful as the cognitive-science substrate beneath TC 25-20's facilitation procedure and LFUO's local-rationality discipline. Its distinctive contribution is naming the *mechanisms* — not just the outcomes — of unreliable accounts and attribution error.

## Worked Example

A cross-functional team reviews a customer data breach. The initial verbal discussion, led by the infrastructure lead, converges quickly on the conclusion that the security engineer who approved the deployment was negligent. Three other participants remain quiet. The facilitator pauses and asks for written accounts before any further verbal discussion.

The written accounts reveal a different picture. Two participants note that the approval was made during a high-urgency release window with three simultaneous critical incidents under active management — the alarm stage of Selye's sequence (Source: OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 14.1), a context the verbal discussion had not surfaced. A third participant notes that the approval protocol had been informally relaxed over the prior six months under pressure from releases — an authority-norm shift the engineer was following, not inventing.

The facilitator names what has happened. The verbal opening created an anchor (Source: Ch 7.3, Table 7.3). The quick convergence showed conformity pressure suppressing divergent accounts (Source: Ch 12.4). The individual-blame frame is the fundamental attribution error applied without reconstructing the situational conditions (Source: Ch 12.1).

The group rebuilds the timeline from the written accounts. The incident's contributory factors shift from "negligent engineer" to "degraded approval protocol combined with concurrent high-arousal incident load." The action design addresses the protocol and the load-management practice, not the individual.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Letting hindsight bias turn a reconstructed account into a verdict on pre-event decisions that were made under genuine uncertainty.
- Opening the discussion verbally rather than written-first, allowing the first (often most senior) account to anchor the group's understanding before all accounts are on the table.
- Using leading questions that embed a causal theory, then accepting the resulting accounts as independent confirmation of that theory.
- Treating conformity-produced consensus as genuine agreement without checking for suppressed minority accounts.
- Attributing incident-phase decisions to character or competence without reconstructing the stress state, information state, and authority context of the moment.
- Letting the availability of a vivid recent incident drive the framing of an incident that may have different underlying conditions.
- Accepting individual blame without applying the Milgram-Zimbardo situational lens: what did role, authority, and norm make rational at the time?
- Closing the AAR without asking whether the actions address situational factors rather than personal ones.
- Treating functional groups' divergent accounts as inconsistency rather than as partial perception shaped by different roles and vantage points.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The deep reference carries several borrowed-through citations that land directly in AAR-relevant distillation concepts. Roese and Vohs's research on hindsight bias is presented via the Psychology 2e text, not held directly in this corpus. Ross, Amabile, and Steinmetz's quizmaster study (fundamental attribution error) is borrowed-through. Loftus and Palmer's (1974) misinformation effect study is borrowed-through. Asch's (1955) line-judgment conformity study is borrowed-through. Milgram's (1963) obedience study is borrowed-through. Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment is borrowed-through with the text's own caveat that subsequent attempts have not fully replicated the original findings and that design flaws (explicit guidance to guards) are acknowledged. Schacter's seven sins are presented via the textbook; Schacter is not held directly.

**Named limits of the source.** Psychology 2e is a broad-scope undergraduate textbook, not a facilitation manual or organisational-learning resource. Its AAR application depends on facilitator translation: the text does not address AARs, post-incident reviews, or organisational learning directly. Clinical content (Ch 15–16) is excluded from distillation projection per the corpus planning log. The text's social-psychology findings are primarily from Western, educated, industrialised, rich, democratic (WEIRD) populations; the text flags this explicitly for the fundamental attribution error (more pronounced in individualistic cultures). The replication crisis in social psychology affects some of the cited studies; the text mentions this openly (Ch 2.3) but does not retract the studies cited in this distillation.

**Evidence-marker continuity.** The five bias definitions in Concept 1–4 are `[V]` in the deep ref (Ch 7.3, Table 7.3) and carry through to this distillation as direct quotation in the concept section. The fundamental attribution error definition (Concept 5) is `[V]` in the deep ref (Ch 12.1) and is quoted directly. The Asch conformity rate (76%) and Milgram obedience rate (65%) are `[V]` in the deep ref and appear as paraphrase in this distillation with their `[BT]` markers preserved — the findings are those of the original researchers cited in the textbook, not the textbook's own original research. The Loftus misinformation effect is `[BT]` in the deep ref and is presented here as a borrowed-through finding consistently.
