## AAR Relevance

SSDL's five briefs project onto the AAR axis at two critical junctures. In Phase 1 (timeline and local-rationality reconstruction), the mental-models brief (1.03) provides the conceptual substrate for the facilitator's core epistemic stance: every participant holds a partial-but-valid model of what happened, shaped by their "lived experiences, values, assumptions, power, and biases" — diverging accounts are a feature, not noise. In Phase 2 (contributory-factor analysis), the system-archetypes brief (1.07) supplies five hypothesis-generators — *fixes that fail*, *success to the successful*, *shifting the burden*, *drifting goals*, *limits to growth* — for naming recurring structural patterns without collapsing to a single root cause. The feedback-thinking brief (1.05) grounds the shared-model artefact (the CLD) as a hypothesis open to revision, not a verdict to defend. Crucially, the briefs carry the "broken-ankle test" (Brief 1.02): system dynamics apparatus has overhead; the AAR facilitator should reach for these tools when a recurring or complex pattern warrants it, not by default.

## Key Concepts for AAR

1. **Complex-problem characteristics.** Five markers distinguish complex events from simple ones: dynamic (changing over time), multi-stakeholder with conflicting goals, time delays between action and result, unintended consequences, and accumulations with history-dependence (Source: SSDL, *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education"). All five apply to organisational incidents. Recognising them upfront helps the facilitator scope the AAR appropriately.

2. **Mental models as partial-but-valid maps.** "Mental models are the cognitive 'maps' that help us each, individually, understand the world around us. They are how we imagine in our minds that complex systems work." (Source: Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT") Every participant's account of the incident reflects their mental model, not the whole event. Divergence between accounts is evidence of different vantage points, not of one party lying.

3. **Mental models are incomplete.** "None of us have complete knowledge of systems. Our understanding is informed by our lived experiences, values, assumptions, power, and biases." (Source: Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT") The facilitator's task in Phase 1 is to reconstruct the local rationality of each position, not to arbitrate which account is correct.

4. **Mental-model stickiness and power asymmetry.** "Mental models are resistant to change, especially among individuals who aren't personally experiencing any negative system effects." (Source: Brief 1.03, "+CONSIDERATIONS") In an AAR, those who were not harmed by the incident are the hardest to move toward a systems reading. The facilitator should anticipate this and plan for it.

5. **Group Model Building as the participatory tool.** "Group model building (GMB) is a process for bringing people together to develop a model of the system, to create shared insights and common language, and to build consensus for implementing change." (Source: Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT") In an AAR context, GMB is the shared-model construction move that converts divergent accounts into a negotiated picture of the system.

6. **Feedback thinking versus linear thinking.** "Feedback thinking recognizes relationships and interdependencies between the parts. Using feedback thinking, we look for causal relationships in both directions." (Source: Brief 1.05, "+Linear vs. Feedback Perspective") Single-cause analysis ("the root cause was X") is linear thinking applied to a feedback system. The AAR move is to replace the line with a loop and ask what the loop was doing before the event.

7. **CLDs as hypotheses, not verdicts.** "A CLD is a hypothesis — there is not one single 'right' answer to the variables that are included or the relationships that exist between them. The model we produce is informed by each of our individual mental models." (Source: Brief 1.05, "+ CONSIDERATIONS") A CLD built during an AAR is a working theory of the contributory structure, not a settled causal account. It is revisable.

8. **Accumulations and history-dependence.** A system's behaviour at the time of an incident reflects not only what was happening in that moment but the accumulated stock of prior states: trust, fatigue, technical debt, degraded procedures, eroded margins (Source: Brief 1.06, opening; Brief 1.06, "+ WHY DOES THIS ACCUMULATION CONCEPT MATTER?"). The incident did not emerge from nowhere; it surfaced from a history.

9. **Intuition is unreliable about accumulations.** "Even students with strong mathematical training make persistent mistakes understanding even simple dynamic systems that include accumulations." (Source: Brief 1.06, "+ CONSIDERATIONS WHEN APPLYING THE CONCEPT OF ACCUMULATIONS") [BT, citing Cronin, Gonzalez & Sterman 2009] Participants' intuitions about how fast or slow a safety margin was eroding before the incident are not to be taken at face value; stock-and-flow thinking is the discipline.

10. **System archetypes as hypothesis-generators.** "System archetypes are visual descriptions of generic, recurring system structures in the form of Causal Loop Diagrams." (Source: Brief 1.07, "+ CONCEPT") Five are available: *fixes that fail* (short-term fix worsens the original problem over time), *success to the successful* (initial advantage compounds into structural inequality), *shifting the burden* (symptomatic fix erodes investment in the fundamental solution), *drifting goals* (standard lowers instead of performance rising), *limits to growth* (reinforcing growth hits a balancing constraint). Each is a hypothesis to test against the incident, not a label to apply.

11. **Archetypes are not prescriptive.** "System archetypes are not meant to erase the uniqueness of a situation. Oversimplifying a problem or jumping to the conclusion that it is best represented by an archetype can impede the creative and iterative process." (Source: Brief 1.07, "+ CONSIDERATIONS") The facilitator surfaces an archetype as a question: does this pattern fit what happened here? The team tests it against their experience, not the other way around.

12. **The broken-ankle test.** "If you are out on a hike and you break your ankle, call an ambulance. You don't need to make a system dynamics model about it!" (Source: Brief 1.02, "+CONSIDERATIONS") [BT, attributed to Adam Raney, former Changing Systems Fellow] System-dynamics apparatus is suited to complex, recurring problems. For a one-off, clearly bounded incident with an obvious fix, reach for simpler tools. The AAR facilitator earns the right to introduce systems-thinking tools by being honest about when they are not needed.

## Questions to Ask During AAR

### Phase 1: Timeline and Local-Rationality Reconstruction

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Accounts diverge and the facilitator risks arbitrating | Each account reflects a different mental model of the same event. What does each account reveal about that person's position in the system — what they could see, what they could not see, and what they assumed? (Source: Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT") |
| The facilitator wants to surface the local rationality of a decision that looks bad in hindsight | Given what this person knew at the time, what would their mental map of the situation have looked like? What would have made their action rational from their vantage point? (Source: Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT") |
| The group is treating one account as the ground truth | All participants hold partial models. Who in the room had a view of the system no one else had? Whose account, added to the others, makes the picture more complete? (Source: Brief 1.03, figure caption: "each of us has our own, valid mental picture of the parts, but our understanding of the whole is limited by our own unique vantage point") |
| The timeline reveals a gap between action and outcome | Was there a time delay between the causal factor and the visible effect? What was accumulating in that gap — trust, pressure, fatigue, degraded margin — that made the final outcome possible? (Source: Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education") |

### Phase 2: Contributory-Factor Analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| The group gravitates toward a single root cause | What was the feedback structure that kept this problem in place? If we fixed the named cause, which loop would bring the problem back? (Source: Brief 1.05, "+Linear vs. Feedback Perspective") |
| The same incident pattern has appeared before | Which of the five system archetypes fits as a hypothesis? Is this *fixes that fail* (short-term fixes masking a worsening structural problem)? *Shifting the burden* (symptomatic response crowding out the fundamental fix)? *Drifting goals* (the standard moved instead of the performance)? Test the hypothesis against the specifics — do not apply the label. (Source: Brief 1.07, "+ CONCEPT"; Brief 1.07, "+ CONSIDERATIONS") |
| The group wants to draw a shared causal map | A CLD is a hypothesis built from the group's collective mental models. Build it together; treat it as a question to test, not an answer to defend. Expect to revise it. (Source: Brief 1.05, "+ CONSIDERATIONS") |
| The group is surprised by how quickly the situation deteriorated | What was the stock that had been depleting before the incident? Safety margins, trust, attention, slack capacity — which of these had been draining, and what flows were feeding or depleting them? (Source: Brief 1.06, opening) |
| Participants are defending their decisions by citing what they knew at the time | That is exactly the right frame. Surface each person's mental model as partial-but-valid. Then ask what, if any participant had access to another's vantage point, would have changed. (Source: Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT") |

### Phase 4: Action Design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| The proposed action targets the symptom, not the structure | Does this action address the feedback loop or the event? If it targets the event, which archetype predicts what happens next? (Source: Brief 1.07, "+ CONCEPT") |
| The action assumes a direct line from intervention to outcome | What are the unintended consequences this action might introduce? Which loops in the system will it strengthen or weaken — and which will push back? (Source: Brief 1.05, "+Linear vs. Feedback Perspective") |

### Phase 5: Learning-Loop Closure

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| The same action has been recommended before with no lasting effect | If the fix has been tried and the problem returned, that is a *fixes that fail* or *shifting the burden* signal. What is the fundamental solution the symptomatic fix has been crowding out? (Source: Brief 1.07, "Archetype #1: Fixes that fail"; "Archetype #3: Shifting the burden") |
| The team wants to build recurring pattern awareness | Which archetype, if any, names the recurring structure? Can the team use that pattern name as shared vocabulary for recognising the dynamic earlier in future events? (Source: Brief 1.07, "+ HOW TO USE SYSTEM ARCHETYPES IN PRACTICE") |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| The same preventive action has been taken after each of three similar incidents and the incidents keep recurring | *Fixes that fail* or *shifting the burden* pattern — the action is symptomatic; the structural source continues; the fix may even be crowding out the fundamental solution | Apply archetype-as-hypothesis: which structural pattern fits? What is the fundamental solution the symptomatic fix is making it harder to reach? (Source: Brief 1.07, "Archetype #1"; "Archetype #3") |
| Participants agree on what happened but are surprised it happened so fast | Accumulation — the incident did not emerge from the visible trigger; it surfaced from a depleting stock (trust, margin, capacity) that was already near threshold | Ask: what was accumulating or depleting in the period before the event? What flows were contributing to that stock? (Source: Brief 1.06, opening; "+ WHY DOES THIS ACCUMULATION CONCEPT MATTER?") |
| One function's account of the incident is treated as the definitive narrative by everyone else | Mental-model power asymmetry — those not harmed by the incident are more resistant to mental-model revision; the dominant account may reflect power, not accuracy | Surface each vantage point separately before integrating. Ask what each account reveals about what that person could and could not see from their position. (Source: Brief 1.03, "+CONSIDERATIONS") |
| The contributory-factor list is a single chain: A caused B caused C | Linear thinking applied to a feedback system — the chain will suggest a single root-cause fix that does not address the loop that will restore the problem | Replace the chain with a loop question: what was the feedback structure that kept A in place? (Source: Brief 1.05, "+Linear vs. Feedback Perspective") |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this source when:
- The same incident pattern has appeared two or more times and the prior fix did not hold — archetypes give the facilitator a hypothesis-vocabulary for the structural pattern.
- Divergent accounts are being treated as a conflict to resolve rather than as evidence to synthesise — the mental-models brief grounds the facilitator's alternative frame.
- The incident analysis is converging on a single root cause and the facilitator wants to redirect to a feedback-structure analysis.
- Participants are surprised by accumulation effects — how quickly a margin eroded, how much prior experience shaped the moment — and need a conceptual frame for history-dependence.
- The proposed actions are all event-level interventions and the facilitator wants to prompt structural thinking.

Use the broken-ankle test before reaching for any of this apparatus. If the incident is clearly bounded, non-recurring, and has an obvious fix, simpler tools are right. Systems-thinking apparatus earns its overhead only on complex, recurring, or structurally ambiguous problems (Source: Brief 1.02, "+CONSIDERATIONS").

## Worked Example

A facilities team reviews a third successive equipment failure in the same production line. Each prior review concluded with a preventive maintenance schedule update. The schedules were updated, but the failures continued. The facilitator introduces the *fixes that fail* archetype as a hypothesis rather than a conclusion (Source: Brief 1.07, "+ CONCEPT"; "+ CONSIDERATIONS").

The team builds a CLD together, treating it as a hypothesis open to revision (Source: Brief 1.05, "+ CONSIDERATIONS"). The CLD reveals two loops: a short-term fix loop in which maintenance schedule updates reduce reported failures temporarily, and an unintended consequence loop in which the schedule updates increase technician workload, reduce inspection thoroughness, and allow actual equipment degradation to continue undetected. The symptomatic fix is crowding out the fundamental solution: capital equipment replacement that would address the underlying wear accumulation (Source: Brief 1.06, "+ WHY DOES THIS ACCUMULATION CONCEPT MATTER?").

The facilitator asks: what was accumulating in the equipment before each failure? The team identifies a stock of deferred maintenance debt that each schedule update adds to rather than draws down — a flows-versus-stocks analysis the participants had not considered intuitively (Source: Brief 1.06, opening). The action design shifts from schedule updates (symptomatic) to a capital-replacement proposal and a process for making the maintenance-debt stock visible to decision-makers (structural).

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Treating divergent participant accounts as a conflict to resolve rather than a feature of a multi-stakeholder system to synthesise.
- Accepting the first account offered as the ground truth and building the timeline around one vantage point.
- Collapsing contributory-factor analysis to a single root cause by applying linear cause-effect thinking to a feedback system.
- Applying a system archetype as a diagnostic label before testing whether it fits the specific incident's structure.
- Ignoring the accumulated history — the stock of degraded margin, eroded trust, or unaddressed prior incidents — that made the specific event possible.
- Using mental-model surfacing as the endpoint (bringing assumptions out is necessary but not sufficient; power asymmetry means those not harmed by the incident will resist revising their mental models).
- Skipping systems-mapping apparatus on a recurring pattern because it "seems obvious" — accumulations and feedback dynamics are counter-intuitive even to skilled practitioners.
- Reaching for systems-dynamics tools on a straightforward, non-recurring incident when simpler analysis would serve (the broken-ankle test).
- Presenting a CLD built during the AAR as a settled causal account rather than as a shared hypothesis requiring continued testing.
- Recommending the same intervention that was tried after the last similar incident without examining why it failed to hold.
