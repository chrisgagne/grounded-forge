# US Army TC 25-20, After-Action Review Distillation

**Source:** Headquarters, Department of the Army (1993). *A Leader's Guide to After-Action Reviews*. Training Circular 25-20, 30 September 1993. **Licence:** Public domain (17 USC §105 — US Government work product). **Scope:** open.

## AAR Relevance

TC 25-20 is the canonical AAR doctrine. Its load-bearing contribution to the AAR task is the full four-step cycle — Planning, Preparing, Conducting, Following up — framed around a single epistemic commitment: soldiers *discover for themselves* what happened, rather than being told by an observer. Every facilitation move in the TC follows from this commitment. The AAR is not a critique, does not grade, and produces its benefit only when follow-up action happens. The source gives the facilitator three concrete tools: a discussion-organisation technique choice (chronological / BOS / key events), an attendance and site discipline, and a question-design rule (open-ended over yes/no). The TC also isolates two mandatory standing-agenda items — fratricide-equivalent events and force protection — that override normal time-budget discipline. Frame to bring with you: TC 25-20 is the upstream input to the next decision, not a verdict on the past one.

## Key Concepts for AAR

1.  **Discovery frame, not critique frame.** "An after-action review (AAR) is a professional discussion of an event, focused on performance standards, that enables soldiers to discover for themselves what happened, why it happened, and how to sustain strengths and improve on weaknesses" [V] (Source: Department of the Army, *TC 25-20*, Ch 1, "Definition and Purpose"). A critique transmits one viewpoint and stifles learning; the AAR aggregates and tests views across all participants.

2.  **Four-step cycle: Planning, Preparing, Conducting, Following up.** The AAR is a process, not an event. Chapter 5 is explicit: "The real benefits of AARs come from taking the results and applying them to future training" [V] (Source: *TC 25-20*, Ch 5, "Benefits"). Follow-up is not optional post-processing — it is where the benefit is.

3.  **Formal vs informal AAR.** Formal AARs are resource-intensive, planned 6–8 weeks out, typically company-level and above. Informal AARs use the same format with minimal resources — "using nothing more than pinecones to represent squad members" (Source: *TC 25-20*, Ch 1, "Informal"). Choose by fit, not by formality preference.

4.  **Two-echelons-above evaluator rule.** External evaluations are conducted "two levels above the unit being evaluated" (Source: *TC 25-20*, Ch 2, "The After-Action Review Plan"). One echelon up is too close for honest independent assessment.

5.  **Experience over rank for OC selection.** "If trainers must choose between experience and understanding of current TTPs or rank, they should go with experience" (Source: *TC 25-20*, Ch 2, "Selecting and Training Observers and Controllers"). A junior person with deep role experience observes better than a senior person without it.

6.  **Three discussion-organisation techniques.** Chronological for narrative recall; Battlefield Operating Systems (BOS) for systemic cross-phase patterns; key events/themes for tight focus when time is limited. The leader can switch between them within a single AAR when systemic issues emerge (Source: *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Flexibility").

7.  **Open-ended question discipline.** "SGT Johnson, what happened when your Bradley crested the hill?" over "SGT Johnson, why didn't you engage the enemy tanks to your front?" (Source: *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Summary of Recent Events"). The first invites narrative; the second invites self-defence.

8.  **Spirit and climate as operational concerns.** "The environment and climate surrounding an AAR must be one in which the soldiers and leaders openly and honestly discuss what actually transpired" (Source: *TC 25-20*, Preface). Soldier participation is directly related to the atmosphere created during the introduction.

9.  **Train to weakness, not to strength.** "Train to weakness, not to strength" (Source: *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Tasks to Sustain/Improve"). Allocate finite development resource to known weakness. Deviation from this default requires a reason.

10.  **Critical gate tasks as stop-rules.** "The unit must always retrain and perform critical gate tasks derived from the Combined Arms Training Strategy (CATS) to standard before progressing" (Source: *TC 25-20*, Ch 5, "Immediate Retraining"). Gate task failure is a stop, not a partial score.

11.  **Fratricide as mandatory deep-discussion topic.** "All incidents or near incidents of fratricide…will be discussed in detail" (Source: *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Fratricide"). Name your fratricide-equivalents — severity-overrides-time-budget topics — in advance, not after the fact.

12.  **Force protection touched every AAR.** "Safety should be specifically addressed in every AAR and discussed in detail when it impacts unit effectiveness" (Source: *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Discussion of Force Protection"). Standing-agenda discipline: certain topics fire regardless of whether they came up during the event.

13.  **Statistics serve teaching, not grading.** "Chart after chart of ratios, bar graphs, and tables quickly obscures any meaning and lends itself to a 'grading' of unit performance. This stifles discussion and degrades the AAR's value" (Source: *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Statistics"). Metrics support observations; they do not assign verdicts.

14.  **T-P-U assessment for follow-up.** Trained / Practiced / Untrained: three buckets, not a continuous scale. The categorisation drives the future-training concept immediately after the AAR (Source: *TC 25-20*, Ch 5, "Benefits").

15.  **Closing-comments rule.** The AAR leader summarises key points, ends positively, links to next training, then leaves so the unit can discuss in private (Source: *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Closing Comments"). The leader communicates the commitment, then creates space.

## Questions to Ask During AAR

### Phase 0: Scoping

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Which tool fits this event? | Is this a 15-minute on-the-spot informal AAR (platoon-equivalent, immediate feedback while fresh) or a formal AAR requiring 6–8 weeks of planning, site reconnaissance, and OC selection? |
| Who is the right evaluator? | Is the evaluator two echelons above the unit being evaluated? If using an OC, does experience beat rank in this case? |
| How much time is honest? | 30–45 min platoon; 1 hr company; 2 hr battalion-and-above. Are we budgeting realistically or pretending a 30-minute slot will support a 2-hour question? |
| What are our fratricide-equivalents? | What events in this domain, if they had occurred, would override normal time-budget rules and require deep discussion regardless? Name them now. |
| Is the climate ready? | Has the leader set the spirit — professional discussion, not critique, not a grading exercise — before the first question is asked? |

### Phase 1: Timeline and Local-Rationality Reconstruction

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| How to organise the discussion? | Which technique fits the question: chronological (narrative recall), BOS-equivalent (systemic cross-phase patterns), or key events (tight focus, limited time)? Can we switch when systemic issues emerge? |
| Are accounts diverging? | Are divergent perspectives a problem to solve or evidence to surface? The AAR is multi-perspective discovery: "no commander, no matter how skilled, will see as much as the individual soldiers and leaders who actually conduct the training." |
| Are we asking open-ended questions? | Is each question inviting narrative ("what happened when…") or inviting self-defence ("why didn't you…")? |
| Is the OPFOR perspective represented? | "The enemy's perspective is often useful in identifying why a unit was or was not successful." Who holds the OPFOR view in this incident? Have we heard it? |

### Phase 2: Contributory-Factor Analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Are we naming contributory factors or a single cause? | TC 25-20 consistently uses plural framing — "weaknesses to improve and strengths to sustain." What are the multiple factors, not the cause? |
| Are we switching discussion technique to surface systemic patterns? | When a BOS-equivalent pattern emerges across phases, does the discussion technique need to switch from chronological to BOS? |
| Are statistics serving teaching or grading? | Is each chart identifying a trend or reinforcing a teaching point, or has it slipped into scoring the unit's performance? |

### Phase 3: Just-Culture Sorting

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Are we evaluating the action or the actor? | The AAR rule is to evaluate performance against the standard, not the person against a verdict. Has the conversation drifted into critique mode? |
| What is the standard here? | Without an explicit published criterion, the review will drift toward opinions about what good looks like. What is the equivalent of the Army standard for this incident? |

### Phase 4: Action Design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Is this a gate task? | Is performance below standard a stop signal (critical gate task equivalent) or a gradient to work around? Have we identified which capabilities are gates? |
| Are retraining resources going to weakness? | Are we about to invest further in a known-strong capability? What is the reason for deviating from the train-to-weakness default? |
| Is ownership named before we close? | Who owns each action, and when does it occur? The benefit lives in the follow-up — an AAR without a clear retraining commitment has not produced its benefit. |
| Does any action require an SOP revision? | If the AAR has surfaced SOP problems, is the revision committed before the next iteration? |

### Phase 5: Learning-Loop Closure

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Is the next-action commitment locked? | "The real benefits of AARs come from taking the results and applying them to future training." Who owns the next action, and when? |
| If retraining is delayed, is the delay visible? | Soldiers must understand the task was not performed to standard and retraining will occur. Is the deferral timed and communicated, not silent? |
| Has the SOP been revised? | Did the review surface SOP problems? Has the revision been implemented, not just noted? |
| Did the leader close correctly? | Summary of key points, positive ending, link to next training, then leave so the unit can discuss in private? |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| The post-event discussion opens with a senior participant explaining what happened, and others affirm rather than contribute | Critique mode — one viewpoint is being transmitted rather than multiple perspectives being discovered; the TC is explicit that a critique "stifles learning and team building" | Reframe: "What we've heard is one account. Let's hear from someone who was in a different position." Open-ended questions from here forward. |
| The discussion ends without a named owner and date for each action | Follow-up phase omitted — the TC is explicit: "the real benefits of AARs come from taking the results and applying them to future training." The AAR cycle has not closed | Before the room clears: assign owners, set dates, flag which items require SOP revision |
| Post-event statistics are being used to rank or score team members | Statistics-as-verdict — the TC warns directly that statistical presentations "lend themselves to a 'grading' of unit performance" and "stifles discussion" | Redirect: "What does this trend tell us about what happened? What teaching point does it reinforce?" |
| The AAR skips the review of what the standard was before discussing what happened | No-standard problem — without knowing the pre-committed criterion, the discussion will debate what good looks like rather than what happened | Name the standard first, even retroactively; if no standard existed, make setting one an output |
| Resources are being directed toward a known-strength capability for the next training event | Training-to-strength failure mode — the TC's default is explicit: "train to weakness, not to strength" | Ask: "What is the reason for deviating from the train-to-weakness default?" If no reason is offered, redirect resources |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this source when:
- You need the foundational AAR protocol — four steps, five discussion phases, question design, and closing discipline — grounded in the canonical source.
- The question is which format (formal vs informal) fits the event and the resources available.
- There is a choice between evaluators and experience versus rank is the deciding factor.
- An incident has occurred where a safety or severity-equivalent topic may have been squeezed by time budget — the fratricide-mandatory-discussion rule is the principle to invoke.
- Post-event statistics are being used to grade rather than to teach, and the facilitator needs a principled argument for the redirect.

This source is the AAR's procedural spine. Every other source in this corpus is a complement to TC 25-20, not a replacement for it. Use it alongside Psychology 2e for the cognitive-science substrate, SSDL for structural analysis on recurring patterns, and OPL for software-team and AI-incident vocabulary.

## Worked Example

A product team has run three quarterly business reviews that ended with no committed actions. The retrospective discipline from their Scrum practice nominally covers it, but the reviews keep producing lists of discussion topics rather than decisions. The team lead proposes a formal AAR on the quarterly review process itself.

The facilitator plans 90 minutes (company-level equivalent), confirms the standard in advance — the review was meant to produce three to five committed actions per quarter — and selects key-events organisation because time is limited and the most critical gaps (no action commitment, no owner named) are visible patterns, not a full-sequence narrative (Source: *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Key Events/Themes/Issues").

The introduction establishes the discovery frame explicitly: this is not a verdict on any individual's performance, does not grade success or failure, and anyone with an observation should contribute (Source: *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Introduction and Rules"). The OPFOR perspective is asked for: who in this process has the external view, like a sponsor or a customer whose expectations were not met?

Phase 2 reveals two factors: the agenda was structured as presentation followed by Q&A rather than as a working session (a critique structure, not an AAR structure), and no pre-committed standard existed for what "done" looked like for the review. The facilitator names the standard-gap first, because without it the team cannot honestly assess whether the review succeeded.

Phase 4 names two gate tasks with owners: (1) a written standard for what the quarterly review produces (owner: team lead, due before next quarter) and (2) a revised meeting format that replaces the presentation block with structured contributory-factor discussion (owner: facilitator, due next cycle). The SOP revision is committed before the room clears. The leader summarises, closes on a positive note, and leaves so the team can debrief privately (Source: *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Closing Comments").

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Using a post-event review as a graded verdict on people rather than as evidence input for the next decision.
- Convening a review without identifying the next decision it must inform.
- Choosing the wrong scale: formal review when informal would produce better feedback faster; informal when the depth required demands formal planning.
- Choosing reviewers by rank when the rank-experience trade-off favours experience.
- Convening at one echelon up when independence requires two echelons.
- Asking yes/no questions when open-ended questions would surface the underlying narrative.
- Training to known strengths because they are visible and rewarding, while known weaknesses go untrained.
- Treating critical gate-task failure as a partial score rather than a stop signal.
- Using post-event statistics to assign blame or rate performance rather than to identify trends and reinforce teaching points.
- Failing to name fratricide-equivalent events in advance, then discovering the most important thing was squeezed by time budget.
- Closing without a next-action commitment and timeline.
- Failing to revise SOPs that the review surfaced as broken, then repeating the same failure next iteration.
- Over-investing in elaborate training aids when pencil, paper, and the leader's attention would do.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** TC 25-20's bibliography is minimal: two field manuals (FM 25-100 *Training the Force*, November 1988; FM 25-101 *Battle Focused Training*, September 1990) are cited in the References page. The Combined Arms Training Strategy (CATS) is cited as the source of critical gate tasks (Ch 5) but is not held in this corpus [BT]. Combat training center (CTC) observer/controller practice is cited as the source for Appendix A's AAR techniques but the CTCs are not held as a primary reference [BT]. No research studies, psychological literature, or external theoretical frameworks are cited; the TC presents itself as doctrine, not as empirical research.

**Named limits of the source.** TC 25-20 is a 1993 Army training manual scoped to military unit training events. Its facilitation mechanics transfer to organisational AARs directly, but its domain vocabulary (METL, BOS, OPFOR, OC, CATS, T-P-U) requires translation for non-military contexts. The TC does not address just-culture methodology, attribution error, psychological safety mechanisms, or structural/systemic contributory-factor analysis; those are carried by Psychology 2e, OPL, and SSDL respectively. The TC's "two-echelons-above" evaluator rule applies to formal military evaluations; the organisational equivalent (sufficient independence to observe honestly) requires context-specific judgment. The TC uses masculine pronouns throughout per its 1993 era; the Preface states they refer to both men and women.

**Evidence-marker continuity.** All concept-section quotations are `[V]` in the deep ref (full verbatim extractions from the TC). The distillation applies them as paraphrased concepts with direct quotation where the precise wording is the load-bearing claim (discovery frame definition in Concept 1; train-to-weakness directive in Concept 9; fratricide mandate in Concept 11; statistics warning in Concept 13; real-benefits-in-follow-up in Concept 2). The TC has no borrowed-through claims affecting AAR-relevant concepts; all claims in the deep ref trace to the TC's own prose, figures, or glossary, as confirmed by the deep-ref citation integrity section. No evidence-marker ambiguity exists: the text presents itself as doctrine without empirical studies, comparative evidence, or external citations that would require `[AP]`, `[AE]`, or `[BT]` qualification.
