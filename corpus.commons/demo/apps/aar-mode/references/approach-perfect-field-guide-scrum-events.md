
# Gagné, The Approach Perfect Field Guide to Scrum Events (light reference)

**Source:** Gagné, C. (2020). *The Approach Perfect Field Guide to Scrum Events*. April 3, 2020 edition. Approach Perfect, Limited (Wellington, New Zealand). Public licence: CC BY-ND 4.0; relicensed CC BY 4.0 by the author for this corpus. https://approachperfect.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/04/The-Approach-Perfect-Field-Guide-to-Scrum-Events-2020-04-03.pdf.
**Structure:** A 17-page practitioner field guide with an opening letter (Introduction), a one-page summary table ("Scrum Events at a Glance"), then one section per event in order: The Sprint, Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective, Backlog Refinement. Each event section follows the same template — Goal(s), Timing, Participants (Required / Strongly Suggested / Invited), Inputs, Output(s), Sample Agenda for a specified Sprint length, Tips. Written as a "tear-out" reference (intentionally repetitive across events so single sections print self-sufficiently).

## Author's Thesis (condensed)

The Field Guide positions itself as "the 'bare minimum' instruction required to successfully bootstrap the five standard Scrum Events and Backlog Refinement," illustrating both textbook Scrum (scrumguides.org) and how it is conventionally taught and practiced. The author's recommended posture is the martial-arts student's: practice the basic forms diligently before deviating, and when deviation comes, do it as a well-formed experiment under the guidance of a knowledgeable Scrum Master or Agile Coach, with humility and honest curiosity. The guide does not claim to be a best practice in all contexts; high-performing teams will eventually find a better framework, but only after the basics are mastered.

Each event is treated operationally — purpose, participant tiers, timebox, sample agenda — and the guide's job is to make all four concrete enough to facilitate. Where the Scrum Guide defines Scrum (what each event *is*), the Field Guide instructs on running it (how long Sprint Planning typically takes per Sprint-week, what a sample 1½-hour Sprint Retrospective agenda looks like, named techniques to use, and field-tested tips for each ceremony). The repetition across events is a deliberate design choice — the guide is meant to be torn apart and used a section at a time.

## The Sprint

Goal: deliver a usable, potentially-releasable product Increment that meets the Definition of "Done" and accomplishes the Sprint Goal. Length: 1–4 weeks, most commonly 2 weeks, maximum one month. Sprints start immediately after the last ends; the duration does not extend. Required participants are the self-organising Scrum Team (Product Owner, Scrum Master, cross-functional Development Team); the team should work daily with business people. Scrum does not recognise individual titles (engineer / QA / designer); there are no sub-teams.

Inputs: enough prioritised "Ready" Backlog Items. Outputs: a usable Increment, and *increased useful knowledge* about product, customers, competition, and the team itself — knowledge is a first-class output. Sample two-week Sprint cadence starts on a Wednesday (to avoid holiday rescheduling), with a 3-hour Sprint Planning, 15-minute Daily Scrums, up to 10% of the Sprint on Backlog Refinement, a 1-hour Sprint Review, a 1½-hour Sprint Retrospective, and an unstructured "connect and celebrate" segment.

Tips: new teams should consider 1-week sprints (citing Sutherland et al., "Shock Therapy"); one-week sprints don't double overhead because Planning and Review events take about 40% less time per event when fewer items are involved; no unsustainable pace; no quality compromise; Sprint Goal preserved when renegotiating scope; Sprint cancellation only by the Product Owner, usually in consultation with Scrum Master and Development Team, when the Sprint Goal is obsolete.

## Sprint Planning

Three named goals: identify a specific, measurable, achievable Sprint Goal; realistically forecast what Increment can be delivered; jointly plan how. Timing: typically 1–2 hours per week of Sprint duration, up to 8 hours for a one-month Sprint. Conclusion of Sprint Planning officially starts the Sprint. Required: entire Scrum Team. Invited: stakeholders and SMEs (with a "this is not their event" behavioural constraint).

Five named inputs: a healthy Product Backlog with 1½–2 sprints' worth of mostly-Ready items; Backlog Items include user stories, defects, spikes, chores; the latest Increment; the Development Team's likely Capacity (accounting for leave); the team's historical performance (Velocity in Story Points — explicitly flagged as not officially Scrum). Three named outputs: a prioritised Sprint Backlog the team expects to complete; an initial sense of how the team will collaborate; a Sprint Goal meetable through "at least most" of the Sprint Backlog — *the Sprint Goal is not coextensive with the Sprint Backlog*.

The sample 3-hour Sprint Planning agenda for a two-week Sprint runs eleven named segments: 5 min social check-in, 5 min reviewing incomplete carry-over (no reestimation, no partial credit), 2 min capacity, 3 min velocity (with an explicit Capacity-adjusted average-of-last-3-or-4-sprints formula), 5 min Sprint Goal alignment, 70 min "what can be done" item-by-item selection (stop when the Development Team — not PO or SM — has the right amount of work; capture outcomes not implementations; at least six items per Sprint; Definition of Ready optional but recommended; story points last-responsible-moment), 15 min break (10–15 min break every 60–90 min throughout planning), 90 min "how" with tasking-out into day-or-less tasks. Velocity worked example: 25/24/31 SP over 45/43/50 days with forecast capacity 47 yields about 30 SP forecast. Pull-back rule: if carry-over WIP meets or exceeds forecast velocity and is all still valuable, take in zero new work; may even need to remove items.

Tips: Fists of Five when no stable Velocity; **no firm commitment** to either the Sprint Backlog or the Sprint Goal — attempting either leads to burnout, poor quality, loss of trust, gaming; mature teams deliver 85–115% of planned work consistently; new backlogs need ~10% of time on backlog prep before each Planning.

## Daily Scrum

Also known as "Standup." Two named goals: inspect progress towards the Sprint Goal and Sprint Backlog completion; plan the next 24 hours and renegotiate the Sprint Backlog if needed. Maximum 15 minutes, every working day, same place and time. Participants tiered: Development Team required; Product Owner and Scrum Master strongly suggested; stakeholders and SMEs invited (with "not their event" constraint).

Sample 15-minute agenda: 13 minutes for each Development Team member to answer the three questions from the Scrum Guide (what did I do yesterday to help the team meet the Sprint Goal; what will I do today; do I see any impediment); 2 minutes to update the Sprint Backlog and Burn-Down Chart; optional "Parking Lot" lingering after the event for deeper discussion, blocker resolution, replanning, or stakeholder concerns.

Tips: the Parking Lot mechanism lets anyone defer too-long discussions; distributed teams should keep the bridge open longer for Parking Lot conversations; format is flexible as long as the 15-minute timebox and the goals hold; **the Daily Scrum is *not* a status meeting** for the Product Owner, Scrum Master, or (especially) stakeholders; start on time even if members are missing (lateness becomes self-correcting); watch for attention and on-Sprint-Backlog focus; the Scrum Master doesn't need to facilitate, only ensure the event happens within timebox; "standing" is the ergonomic enforcement of brevity.

## Sprint Review

Two named goals: elicit feedback from stakeholders — ideally end users — and collaboratively improve the Product Backlog; foster a culture of transparency and trust between Scrum Team and stakeholders. Up to 4 hours for a one-month Sprint, most commonly 15–60 minutes per week of Sprint duration, ideally 1 hour for a 2-week Sprint. Required: Scrum Team. Strongly suggested: key stakeholders and end users invited by the Product Owner.

Sample 1-hour agenda: 5 min Product Owner welcome and Sprint Goal status; 5 min Development Team highs/lows/blockers; 30 min demo of Done Backlog Items with feedback and Q&A; 5 min Product Owner on Product Backlog state and forecasts; 5 min stakeholders on market or context changes; 5 min next-release timing/resources/features/channels; 5 min collaboratively deciding what to do next.

Tips: **not a status meeting** — collaboration to improve the Product Backlog; **don't demonstrate work that isn't Done** — it erodes trust, persists the illusion that incomplete work is valuable, and forgets that work in process is a liability not an asset (borrowed from Theory of Constraints).

## Sprint Retrospective

Goal: the Scrum Team sincerely self-reflects on tools, process, structure, and culture and proposes improvements or experiments for the next Sprint. Up to 3 hours for a one-month Sprint, typically 1½ hours for a 2-week Sprint. Occurs after the Sprint Review (so observations from it can be learned from) and just before Sprint Planning (to minimise gap between Sprints). Participants: entire Scrum Team only, with Product Owner and Scrum Master participating as peers to the Development Team; **no others may attend unless specifically invited by the entire team**.

Inputs: objective data on Sprint Goal/Backlog progress, the well-being of people and relationships, and the effectiveness of tools/process/structure/culture; plus a culture of courage, commitment, respect, and openness. Outputs: Backlog Items capturing improvement experiments for the next Sprint; changes to the Definition of Done; informally, changes to the Definition of Ready and Working Agreement.

Sample 1½-hour agenda (derived from Derby & Larsen's *Agile Retrospectives*): 5 min "set the stage" (feeling check-in, read Norm Kerth's Retrospective Prime Directive, appreciations); 5 min "gather data" using a positives/deltas/insights collection device on sticky notes, grouped on the wall; 3 min read and deduplicate topics; 2 min vote (√n votes per person works well — 9 topics gets 3 votes, 16 topics gets 4); 70 min on top 3 voted topics at 15–20 min each, alternating "generating insights" (5-Whys; if you're rarely surprised by root causes, you're not digging deep enough) with "actions" (decisions = changes to Definition of Done, Definition of Ready, Working Agreement; actions = new Backlog Items); stay focused (one or two improvements per Sprint beats too many); 5 min "wrap up" with appreciations and meta-retrospective on the retrospective.

The Retrospective Prime Directive (verbatim from Norm Kerth): *"Regardless of what we discover, we understand and truly believe that everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time, their skills and abilities, the resources available, and the situation at hand."*

Tips: Kanban teams can run retros every two weeks; 1-week Sprint teams complaining about event load can run retros bi-weekly; 3M's Super Sticky Post-It® notes are worth the upgrade; digital sticky-note tools (Ideaboardz, FunRetro, Retrium); read *Agile Retrospectives* (Derby & Larsen) and *Project Retrospectives* (Kerth); bring snacks (even remote); explicit confidentiality — the Vegas Rule (unanimous explicit consent before sharing) or Chatham House Rule (information shared, identities not) — to create the safe container.

## Backlog Refinement (not an official Scrum event)

The author flags Backlog Refinement as outside canonical Scrum but treats it with the same template. Two named goals: detail, improve, estimate, and prioritise Product Backlog items collaboratively (to increase shared understanding, reduce risk, and shorten Sprint Planning); maintain 1½–2 sprints' worth of "Ready" items. Up to 10% of total Sprint duration; the author's preferred cadence is at least 1½ hours per week. Required: Product Owner plus at least one Development Team member. Strongly suggested: entire Scrum Team plus useful SMEs/stakeholders (even a developer without immediate context benefits by understanding the domain and spotting novel approaches, edge cases, risks).

Sample 1½-hour agenda: 90 minutes refining items in priority order — brief conversation about description/order/value; split big items using Vertical Slicing; capture Acceptance Criteria as bulleted testable conditions; estimate in Story Points using Planning Poker or Affinity Estimating; team proposes ordering changes (Product Owner makes the final decision on ordering).

Tips: most Backlog Items should be user stories; Product Owner pre-circulates the list (so the team can read ahead and the event can end early); Scrum Master keeps time per item (the "we've been on this 10 minutes" intervention); read Patton's *User Story Mapping* and Cohn's *User Stories Applied*; new backlogs need 3–4 hours per week; **don't plan in detail too far ahead** — empirical, iterative product development requires continuous re-prioritisation as the Sprint Reviews and Increments produce new learning; Planning Poker for small batches, Affinity Estimating for many items; *take your time estimating* — the conversation around a 1-vs-5 split is more valuable than the number itself; **estimate all items including defects** — even though root cause is unpredictable, the team better maintains smooth flow when defect cost is accounted for.

## Signature Contrarian Positions

1. **Practice the form before deviating.** Deviation from textbook Scrum is allowed, but only as a well-formed experiment after diligent practice under a knowledgeable Scrum Master or Agile Coach — "with humility and honest curiosity."
2. **Knowledge is a Sprint output.** Not just the Increment — "increased useful knowledge about product, customers, competition, and the *Scrum Team*" is named as an output of every Sprint.
3. **One-week Sprints don't double overhead.** Planning and Review events take about 40% less time per event for one-week Sprints because there are fewer items to process.
4. **The Sprint Goal is not the Sprint Backlog.** "It should be possible to meet the Sprint Goal without completing the entire Sprint Backlog." The Goal is the outcome; the Backlog is the forecast.
5. **No firm commitments.** The Development Team cannot and must not commit firmly to delivering the entire Sprint Backlog or even the Sprint Goal. Firm commitments lead to burnout, poor quality, loss of trust, and gaming. Sprint Backlog and Sprint Goal are *focusing tools and good-faith forecasts*, not contracts.
6. **Capture outcomes, not implementations.** Backlog Items should be expressed from the customer's perspective (the coffee-drinker wants just-right temperature), not the system's behaviour (Type-K thermocouple millivolt range). Implementation belongs to the Development Team.
7. **Carrying over more than forecast velocity means zero new work.** Even if it's painful. Honest empirical process control says the data tells you what the team is capable of.
8. **The Daily Scrum is not a status meeting.** Not for the Product Owner, the Scrum Master, or (especially) stakeholders. If it becomes one, push back.
9. **The Sprint Review is not a status meeting either.** Stakeholders are there to collaborate on the Product Backlog with shared context, not to receive a report.
10. **Don't demonstrate work that isn't Done.** It erodes trust, persists the illusion that incomplete work is valuable, and treats work in process as an asset rather than a liability.
11. **Mature retros take at least 1½ hours.** Shorter retros don't dig deep enough into tools, processes, structure, and culture to find meaningful improvements, even for a week-long Sprint.
12. **Retrospective attendance is closed-by-default.** No one outside the Scrum Team attends without explicit invitation from the entire team.
13. **One or two improvements per Sprint beats many.** Stay focused; too much change at once dilutes the experiment.
14. **Don't detail-plan too far ahead.** Empirical product development requires continuous re-prioritisation; plans made before the Reviews that should inform them are wasted effort and become anchors.
15. **The estimation conversation is more valuable than the number.** A 1-vs-5 split is an opportunity for shared knowledge — possibly a faster technique, possibly a hidden edge case. A few minutes of conversation saves hours of execution.
16. **Estimate everything, including defects.** Even when root cause is hard to predict, the attempt smooths flow because defect cost is accounted for.
17. **The decisions/actions distinction.** Retrospective outputs split cleanly: *decisions* change the Definition of Done, Definition of Ready, or Working Agreement; *actions* are new Backlog Items. Different mechanisms register different kinds of commitment.
18. **The Vegas Rule or Chatham House Rule, explicitly.** Retrospective confidentiality is a precondition for raising difficult issues; pick one rule and name it.
