## Author's thesis (paraphrased from source)

*Principles of Economics 3e* is presented as an introduction to economics framed around three foundational claims: (1) economics is "the study of how humans make decisions in the face of scarcity" — individual, family, business, and societal decisions made because "human wants for goods, services and resources exceed what is available" [V] (Ch 1.1, "What Is Economics, and Why Is It Important?"); (2) the "division and specialization of labor" together with trade are the central mechanisms through which humans escape the binding of scarcity [AR] (Ch 1.1, "The Division of and Specialization of Labor"); (3) economics is "both a subject area and a way of viewing the world," distinguished by the use of theories and models to organise reasoning about choice, tradeoffs, and unintended consequences (Ch 1, "Introduction").

The book frames every choice as carrying an opportunity cost — "the cost of one item is the lost opportunity to do or consume something else" [V] (Ch 2.1, "The Concept of Opportunity Cost") — and treats the budget constraint and production possibilities frontier as twin diagrams expressing this scarcity logic at the individual and social level (Ch 2). On this foundation, the text builds a microeconomic edifice (demand and supply, elasticity, consumer choice, firm production and costs, market structures from perfect competition through monopoly), addresses market failures (externalities, public goods, information asymmetries, imperfect competition), examines distributional questions (labour markets, poverty, inequality, immigration, public economy), and then constructs a parallel macroeconomic frame (GDP, growth, unemployment, inflation, AS-AD, Keynesian and Neoclassical perspectives, money and banking, fiscal and monetary policy, international trade and finance).

A second load-bearing claim is that markets are extraordinarily powerful coordinating institutions but are not self-correcting in every domain. The text repeatedly distinguishes between markets that achieve allocative and productive efficiency (perfect competition, Ch 8) and markets that do not (monopoly Ch 9; monopolistic competition and oligopoly Ch 10; markets with externalities Ch 12; markets failing to provide public goods Ch 13; markets with information asymmetries Ch 16). Throughout, government intervention is treated as a tool that can correct market failures but is itself prone to failure through regulatory capture, special-interest politics, voting cycles, and rational ignorance (Chs 11.4, 18). This framing is symmetric: neither markets nor governments are idealised; both are evaluated on outcomes.

A third load-bearing claim is that decisions made at the individual level can produce macroeconomic outcomes that no individual intended. The text uses Keynes's law ("demand creates its own supply") and Say's law ("supply creates its own demand") as the two organising frames for short-run and long-run macroeconomic analysis (Ch 24.1). Combined with the AD-AS model and the three macroeconomic goals — economic growth, low unemployment, low inflation (Ch 19) — the framework shows why recessions, inflation, and growth slowdowns can occur even when individual households and firms are behaving rationally, and why monetary and fiscal policy may be needed to coordinate aggregate behaviour.

A fourth load-bearing claim, woven throughout, is that international trade and globalization are economic-welfare-enhancing in aggregate even when distributional consequences are uneven. The text invokes Ricardo's comparative advantage (Ch 33.1) and demonstrates that "by specializing in a good that it gives up the least to produce, a country can produce more and offer that additional output for sale," with gains accruing even when one country has absolute advantage in all goods (Ch 33.2). The text confronts the political-economy critique of trade — that protectionism is "an indirect subsidy from consumers to producers" (Ch 34.1) — and argues that infant-industry, anti-dumping, environmental, consumer-safety, and national-interest arguments are weaker than they first appear (Ch 34.3).

## Part I: Foundations — scarcity, choice, and the economic way of thinking (Ch 1)

### Economics as the study of choice under scarcity

The text defines **economics** as "the study of how humans make decisions in the face of scarcity" [V] (Ch 1.1) and **scarcity** as the condition that "human wants for goods, services and resources exceed what is available" [V] (Ch 1.1). Scarcity is treated as a universal condition, present even in wealthy economies because "the ultimate scarce resource is time — everyone, rich or poor, has just 24 expendable hours in the day" [V] (Ch 1.1).

The book opens with Adam Smith's Wealth of Nations (1776) and the **division of labour**: dividing production into distinct tasks that different workers perform [AP] (Ch 1.1, "The Division of and Specialization of Labor"). Smith counted "18 distinct tasks" in pin-making and observed that one worker alone might make 20 pins a day while ten workers specialising could make 48,000 [V] (Ch 1.1). Three reasons explain why specialisation increases output: workers focus on areas of advantage; specialised workers learn and innovate; and specialisation allows businesses to "take advantage of economies of scale" [AP] (Ch 1.1).

### Microeconomics versus macroeconomics; theories and models

**Microeconomics** "focuses on the actions of individual agents within the economy, like households, workers, and businesses" while **macroeconomics** "looks at the economy as a whole" [V] (Ch 1.2). Both perspectives study the same economy from different vantage points (Ch 1.2).

A **theory** is "a simplified representation of how two or more variables interact with each other" [V] (Ch 1.3). The text introduces the **circular flow diagram** (Ch 1.3) as the foundational macro model, with households and firms interacting in goods-and-services markets (households demand, firms supply) and labour/factor markets (households supply, firms demand).

### Three economic systems and globalization

Three ways societies organise economies: **traditional economies** (organised by tradition); **command economies** (decisions passed down by ruling authority, with government ownership of resources and businesses); **market economies** (decentralised, with private enterprise and market forces determining outcomes) [AP] (Ch 1.4). Most real economies are mixed; the U.S. is positioned toward the market end. **Underground economies** arise when heavy regulation drives transactions outside government approval (Ch 1.4).

**Globalization** — "expanding cultural, political, and economic connections between people around the world" [V] (Ch 1.4) — is measured by exports/GDP ratios. The U.S. share is below the global average "because large economies like the United States can contain more of the division of labor inside their national borders" [AP] (Ch 1.4).

## Part II: Choice, tradeoffs, and the economic approach (Ch 2)

### Budget constraints and opportunity cost

The **budget constraint** shows all combinations of two goods that an individual can afford given prices and income (Ch 2.1). The slope is the relative price ratio. **Opportunity cost** "is the value of the next best alternative" — what you give up to get what you choose [V] (Ch 2.1).

The text emphasises that opportunity cost extends beyond monetary cost: time, foregone earnings, and non-monetary alternatives all count (Ch 2.1, "Identifying Opportunity Cost"). College education has out-of-pocket costs (tuition, books, room and board) but also "an opportunity cost of lost earnings" [AP] (Ch 2.1). The Sky Marshal example — $3 billion per year for armed marshals on every flight versus an estimated $8 billion per year in opportunity-cost-of-passenger-time from longer security delays — illustrates that "the opportunity costs of waiting time can be just as important as costs that involve direct spending" [V] (Ch 2.1, "What is the opportunity cost...").

### Marginal analysis, diminishing marginal utility, and sunk costs

**Marginal analysis** examines "the benefits and costs of choosing a little more or a little less of a good" [V] (Ch 2.1). The text takes a strong position: "Marginal analysis is used throughout economics" — most real-world choices are not all-or-nothing but incremental [AR] (Ch 2.1).

**Utility** is the satisfaction or value goods and services provide. The **law of diminishing marginal utility** holds that "as a person receives more of a good, the additional (or marginal) utility from each additional unit of the good declines" [V] (Ch 2.1).

**Sunk costs** "are costs that were incurred in the past and cannot be recovered" [V] (Ch 2.1). The text explicitly frames the policy implication: "the lesson of sunk costs is to forget about the money and time that is irretrievably gone and instead to focus on the marginal costs and benefits of current and future options" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Sunk Costs"). This applies to firms as well as individuals — many firms find it hard to abandon failing products because they over-weight prior investment.

### Production possibilities frontier and law of increasing opportunity cost

The **production possibilities frontier (PPF)** plays for society the role the budget constraint plays for the individual: it shows the productively efficient combinations of two goods given resources and technology (Ch 2.2). Unlike the budget constraint (a straight line), the PPF is curved outward because of the **law of increasing opportunity cost**: "as production of a good or service increases, the marginal opportunity cost of producing it increases as well" because "some resources are better suited for producing certain goods and services instead of others" [V] (Ch 2.2).

Two efficiency concepts: **productive efficiency** ("it is impossible to produce more of one good without decreasing the quantity that is produced of another good" [V]) and **allocative efficiency** ("the particular combination of goods and services on the production possibility curve that a society produces represents the combination that society most desires" [V]) [V] (Ch 2.2).

### Comparative advantage at the level of nations

A country has **comparative advantage** in a good when it "can produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than another country" [V] (Ch 2.2, "The PPF and Comparative Advantage"). The text uses Brazil (sugar cane) and the U.S. (wheat) as the canonical example. Comparative advantage, not absolute advantage, drives the gains from trade — a theme picked up rigorously in Ch 33.

### Confronting objections to the economic approach

Two common objections: that people don't actually behave like budget-constraint-maximisers, and that they shouldn't (Ch 2.3). The text answers the first with a basketball analogy: "the fact that a good player can throw the ball accurately because of practice and skill, without making a physics calculation, does not mean that the physics calculation is wrong" [V] (Ch 2.3). The second is answered partly by distinguishing **positive statements** ("describe the world as it is" [V]) from **normative statements** ("describe how the world should be" [V]) (Ch 2.3) and partly by Adam Smith's invisible hand (Ch 2.3): self-interested behaviour can produce socially beneficial outcomes through market coordination.

## Part III: Demand and supply (Ch 3)

### Demand, supply, and equilibrium

**Demand** is "the amount of some good or service consumers are willing and able to purchase at each price" [V] (Ch 3.1). The **law of demand** holds that price and quantity demanded move in opposite directions, holding all else constant (Ch 3.1). **Supply** is the corresponding willingness-to-sell relationship; the **law of supply** is the positive relationship between price and quantity supplied (Ch 3.1).

**Equilibrium** is "the only price where the plans of consumers and the plans of producers agree" — quantity demanded equals quantity supplied [V] (Ch 3.1). Above equilibrium: **excess supply** (surplus). Below equilibrium: **excess demand** (shortage). Market forces push toward equilibrium.

### Shifts in demand and supply; the four-step process

The text identifies six factors that shift demand: income, tastes/preferences, population/composition, prices of substitutes and complements, and expectations about future prices (Ch 3.2). Five factors shift supply: input prices, natural conditions, technology, taxes/regulations, and subsidies (Ch 3.2).

The **four-step process** for analysing how an economic event affects equilibrium: (1) sketch the demand-supply diagram before the event; (2) decide whether the event affects demand or supply; (3) decide whether the shift is rightward or leftward; (4) compare new equilibrium to old (Ch 3.3). The text takes a clear position against confusion: "a change in the price of a good never causes the demand or supply curve for that good to shift" — only movements along curves [V] (Ch 3.3, "What is the difference...").

### Price ceilings, price floors, and inefficiency

**Price ceilings** (legal maximums, e.g. rent control) generate shortages, lower quality, and benefit some renters at the expense of others (Ch 3.4). **Price floors** (legal minimums, e.g. minimum wage, agricultural price supports) generate surpluses (Ch 3.4). Both create **deadweight loss** — "the loss in social surplus that occurs when the economy produces at an inefficient quantity" [V] (Ch 3.5).

**Consumer surplus** and **producer surplus** sum to **social surplus** at equilibrium; equilibrium maximises social surplus (Ch 3.5). The text takes a strong position: "Removing such barriers, so that prices and quantities can adjust to their equilibrium level, will increase the economy's social surplus" [V] (Ch 3.5). Quoting Alfred Marshall (1890): asking whether supply or demand determines price is "like arguing 'whether it is the upper or the under blade of a pair of scissors that cuts a piece of paper'" [V] (Ch 3.5).

## Part IV: Labour and financial markets (Ch 4)

The same demand-supply framework applies to labour and financial markets. In labour markets, households supply labour and firms demand it (Ch 4.1). The wage acts as the price; equilibrium occurs where quantity supplied equals quantity demanded. Demand for labour is **derived demand**: it comes from the demand for the firms' products (Ch 4.1, "Shifts in Labor Demand"). Factors shifting labour demand: demand for output, education/training, technology (substitute or complement), number of companies, government regulations, prices of other inputs (Ch 4.1, Table 4.2).

**Minimum wage laws** are price floors. The U.S. federal minimum wage is generally set close to the equilibrium wage for low-skilled labour, so its impact has historically been modest; a 10% increase in minimum wage is estimated to reduce hiring of unskilled workers by 1-2% [BT] (Ch 4.1). A **living wage** ordinance pegs wages above the minimum to provide a "reasonable standard of living" [AP] (Ch 4.1).

In financial markets, savers supply financial capital and borrowers demand it; the interest rate is the price (Ch 4.2). **Usury laws** are price ceilings on interest rates — generally non-binding because limits are above market rates (Ch 4.2). The U.S. has run a current-account deficit financed by foreign investors. If foreign confidence diminishes, supply of financial capital shifts left, interest rates rise, and U.S. borrowers face higher costs (Ch 4.2, "The United States as a Global Borrower").

The market system serves as "an efficient mechanism for information": prices "convey information about conditions of demand and supply" and serve as "messengers" [V] (Ch 4.3). The text applies this to the Mao-era price controls during the Great Leap Forward, where the government "kept food prices artificially low, with the result that 30 to 40 million people died of starvation because the low prices depressed farm production" [V] (Ch 4.3) — a stark illustration of price-control failure.

## Part V: Elasticity (Ch 5)

**Elasticity** measures responsiveness of one variable to changes in another (Ch 5.1). **Price elasticity of demand** = % change in quantity demanded / % change in price. **Price elasticity of supply** is the analogue on the supply side. The **midpoint method** uses average values to ensure symmetry between price increases and decreases.

Categories: **elastic** (|ε| > 1), **unitary** (|ε| = 1), **inelastic** (|ε| < 1). Polar cases: **infinite/perfect elasticity** (horizontal curve) and **zero/perfect inelasticity** (vertical curve) (Ch 5.2).

Demand for necessities (housing 0.12, electricity 0.20, gasoline 0.35) tends to be inelastic; demand for substitutable items (restaurant meals 2.27, premium cable 1.77) tends to be elastic [V] (Ch 5.3, Table 5.2).

**Tax incidence** depends on relative elasticities: "if demand is more inelastic than supply, consumers bear most of the tax burden, and if supply is more inelastic than demand, sellers bear most of the tax burden" [V] (Ch 5.3). Cigarettes are inelastic (~0.3); a 10% price increase reduces adult smoking by ~3%, so cigarette taxes are absorbed mostly by consumers [BT] (Ch 5.3).

Elasticities are typically lower in the short run and higher in the long run (Ch 5.3). **Income elasticity of demand** distinguishes normal goods (positive) from inferior goods (negative) (Ch 5.4). **Cross-price elasticity of demand** distinguishes substitutes (positive) from complements (negative) (Ch 5.4). **Wage elasticity of labour supply** describes the response of hours worked to wage changes; **elasticity of savings** describes the response of savings to interest rates (Ch 5.4).

## Part VI: Consumer choices (Ch 6)

**Total utility** and **marginal utility** drive consumption choices subject to a budget constraint (Ch 6.1). The text explains the consumer-equilibrium rule: at the utility-maximising point, the ratio of marginal utility to price is equal across goods. This is equivalent to saying the price ratio equals the marginal-utility ratio: "the marginal utility per dollar spent is the same for both goods" [AP] (Ch 6.1, "A Rule for Maximizing Utility").

The text addresses a measurement objection: "the key assumption is not that an outside party can measure utility but only that individuals can decide which of two alternatives they prefer" [AP] (Ch 6.1, "Measuring Utility with Numbers"). Utility theory is preference-based; cardinal utility is a convenience.

A change in income shifts the budget constraint outward; the consumer typically buys more of all normal goods (Ch 6.2, "How Changes in Income Affect Consumer Choices"). A change in price rotates the budget constraint and produces a **substitution effect** ("consumers have an incentive to consume less of the good with a relatively higher price and more of the good with a relatively lower price" [V]) and an **income effect** ("a higher price means... the buying power of income has been reduced" [V]) (Ch 6.2).

**Behavioural economics** challenges the strict rationality assumption. Kahneman and Tversky (1979) documented **loss aversion**: "a $1 loss pains us 2.25 times more than a $1 gain helps us" [V] (Ch 6.3). Other anomalies include mental accounting, lack of self-control (people pay to constrain their own future choices), and the failure of fungibility — people treat money differently depending on its source (Ch 6.3).

## Part VII: Production, costs, and industry structure (Ch 7)

**Accounting profit** = revenue − explicit costs. **Economic profit** = revenue − (explicit + implicit) costs. Implicit costs include the opportunity cost of resources the firm already owns (e.g. owner's labour, owner's building) [AP] (Ch 7.1). A firm earning zero economic profit is earning a normal rate of return on all resources, which is sustainable.

The **production function** maps inputs to outputs (Ch 7.2). In the **short run**, at least one factor (typically capital) is fixed; in the **long run**, all factors are variable. **Marginal product** is the additional output from one more unit of variable input, holding other inputs constant. The **law of diminishing marginal product** holds that, with at least one fixed factor, "as we add workers, the marginal product increases at first, but sooner or later additional workers will have decreasing marginal product" [V] (Ch 7.2).

**Fixed costs** are independent of output; **variable costs** rise with output. **Total cost** = FC + VC. **Average total cost** (ATC) = TC/Q, **average variable cost** (AVC) = VC/Q, **marginal cost** (MC) = ΔTC/ΔQ (Ch 7.3). The marginal cost curve crosses both the ATC and AVC curves at their minima — this is the textbook geometry of cost curves (Ch 7.3, "Where do marginal and average costs meet?"). The intuition: if MC is below AC, MC pulls AC down; if above, MC pulls AC up.

In the **long run**, all factors are variable, allowing capital deepening and substitution (Ch 7.4). **Economies of scale** mean ATC declines with output; **constant returns to scale** flat ATC; **diseconomies of scale** rising ATC (Ch 7.5). The long-run average cost (LRAC) curve is the lower envelope of short-run average cost (SRAC) curves. Economies of scale arise from physical (e.g. chemical-pipe geometry: doubling pipe circumference quadruples cross-sectional area, the famous "six-tenths rule" [BT]) and managerial sources (Ch 7.5).

The shape of the LRAC affects industry structure: a single bottom point implies firms of similar size; a flat range allows multiple sizes; if minimum efficient scale exceeds market demand, monopoly results (Ch 7.5, "The Size and Number of Firms in an Industry").

## Part VIII: Perfect competition (Ch 8)

A **perfectly competitive market** has four conditions: (1) many firms producing identical products; (2) many buyers and sellers; (3) full information; (4) free entry and exit (Ch 8.1). A **price taker** firm "must accept the prevailing equilibrium price in the market" [V] (Ch 8.1).

A perfectly competitive firm faces a perfectly elastic (horizontal) demand curve at the market price. Marginal revenue equals price; there is no incentive to charge other than market price. The profit-maximising rule is **P = MC** in the short run (Ch 8.2). At the **break-even point**, P = MC = ATC and economic profit is zero (Ch 8.2). At the **shutdown point**, P = MC = AVC; below this price, "the firm is not even covering its variable costs" and should shut down [V] (Ch 8.2). Between these points, the firm operates at a loss but covers some fixed costs.

The supply curve of a perfectly competitive firm is the marginal cost curve above AVC.

In the long run, **entry** and **exit** drive economic profits to zero: "the price level in a perfectly competitive market will move toward the zero-profit point" [V] (Ch 8.3). Long-run equilibrium occurs at the minimum of the LRAC curve. Industries can be constant-cost, increasing-cost, or decreasing-cost, producing different long-run supply curves (Ch 8.3).

Long-run equilibrium under perfect competition achieves both **productive efficiency** (P = minimum LRAC) and **allocative efficiency** (P = MC) (Ch 8.4). This is the textbook benchmark against which other market structures are compared.

## Part IX: Monopoly (Ch 9)

A **monopoly** is a firm that "produces all of the output in a market" [V] (Ch 9). Five barriers to entry sustain monopoly: (1) **natural monopoly** from economies of scale relative to market demand; (2) control of a physical resource (e.g. ALCOA's bauxite, DeBeers's diamonds); (3) **legal monopoly** (regulation, exclusive licences); (4) intellectual property (patents, trademarks, copyrights, trade secrets) granting time-limited exclusive rights; (5) intimidating potential competitors through **predatory pricing** or large advertising budgets (Ch 9.1).

A monopoly faces the market demand curve, which is downward-sloping. Marginal revenue lies below the demand curve because lowering price to sell one more unit also lowers price on all previous units. The profit-maximising rule is **MR = MC** (Ch 9.2). The monopoly then sets price by reading the demand curve at that quantity. The price exceeds marginal cost: P > MC at the profit-maximising output.

The text takes a clear position on monopoly: monopolies are not allocatively efficient because P > MC means the social benefit of additional units (measured by P) exceeds the social cost (measured by MC) — society would gain from more output (Ch 9.2, "The Inefficiency of Monopoly"). Monopolies are also not productively efficient because they don't produce at minimum LRAC. John Hicks's quote: "The best of all monopoly profits is a quiet life" [V] — meaning monopolies, lacking competitive pressure, may slack on innovation and customer service (Ch 9.2).

## Part X: Monopolistic competition and oligopoly (Ch 10)

**Monopolistic competition** has many firms, free entry, but **differentiated products**. Each firm has a downward-sloping demand curve (some pricing power) but faces close substitutes (Ch 10.1). Differentiation can be physical, locational, intangible (reputation, service), or perception-based (brand). Profit-maximising rule: MR = MC. Free entry drives long-run economic profit to zero, but firms operate above minimum LRAC and at a price above MC — neither productively nor allocatively efficient. Variety and innovation are the offsetting benefits (Ch 10.1).

**Oligopoly** is a market dominated by a few large firms. Barriers to entry, plus the strategic interdependence of firms, produce outcomes that depend on whether firms compete or collude (Ch 10.2). A **cartel** is a formal agreement to act as a monopoly — typically illegal under U.S. and EU antitrust law. A **prisoner's dilemma** structure makes cartels unstable: each member has private incentives to cheat by producing more than agreed, even though all members are worse off if all cheat (Ch 10.2).

The **kinked demand curve** explains tacit collusion: if rivals match price cuts but not price increases, individual firms have weak incentives to deviate (Ch 10.2). Game theory provides the analytical apparatus for oligopoly (Ch 10.2).

## Part XI: Antitrust and natural monopoly regulation (Ch 11)

A **merger** combines two firms; an **acquisition** is a purchase. **Antitrust laws** — Sherman Act (1890), Clayton Act (1914), Celler-Kefauver Act (1950) — give government power to block anticompetitive mergers and break up large firms (Ch 11.1). The **four-firm concentration ratio** and **Herfindahl-Hirschman Index** (sum of squared market shares) are tools for measuring market concentration. The FTC's HHI guidelines: under 1,000 generally approved; over 1,800 generally challenged; in between, case-by-case (Ch 11.1). Modern antitrust analysis increasingly relies on detailed competition studies rather than concentration ratios alone (Ch 11.1).

**Restrictive practices** beyond mergers: exclusive dealing, tying sales, bundling, predatory pricing (Ch 11.2). The Microsoft case (1998-2002) involved alleged tying of Internet Explorer to Windows operating system.

For **natural monopolies** (where economies of scale are large relative to market demand), splitting up wastes scale economies and competitive entry is impractical. Regulation options: cost-plus regulation (covers cost plus normal profit but reduces incentive to control costs); price cap regulation (sets price ceiling for several years, allowing firms to retain savings if costs fall — a stronger incentive for efficiency) (Ch 11.3).

The U.S. underwent significant deregulation from the late 1970s onward (airlines, trucking, banking, natural gas, electricity), generally improving consumer outcomes (Ch 11.4). **Regulatory capture** — where the regulated industry captures the regulator — provides a theoretical reason why regulation can serve incumbent interests rather than consumers (Ch 11.4). Sarbanes-Oxley (2002) and Dodd-Frank (2010) responded to corporate accounting scandals and the financial crisis, respectively.

## Part XII: Environmental protection and negative externalities (Ch 12)

An **externality** (or spillover) is "a market exchange that affects a third party who is outside or 'external' to the exchange" [V] (Ch 12.1). Pollution is a **negative externality**: the social cost of production exceeds the private cost. **Market failure** occurs because firms don't account for the full social cost — the result is too much pollution and too much production (Ch 12.1).

**Command-and-control regulation** imposes specific pollution limits or required technologies. It has been highly successful in the U.S. (Clean Air Act 1970, Clean Water Act 1972) but has three weaknesses: no incentive to reduce pollution beyond the limit; inflexibility (same standard for all polluters); and political vulnerability to loopholes (Ch 12.2).

**Market-oriented environmental tools** address these weaknesses. **Pollution charges** (taxes per unit of emissions) give firms incentive to find the cheapest abatement methods. **Marketable permits** (cap-and-trade) allocate a total allowable amount of pollution and let firms trade permits, so reduction occurs where it is cheapest. **Better-defined property rights** internalise externalities — Coase (1960) argued that with clear property rights and low transaction costs, parties can negotiate efficient outcomes regardless of which party has the rights (Ch 12.3).

The text estimates that benefits of U.S. environmental regulation have substantially exceeded costs — one study found Clean Air Act benefits 44 times costs from 1970-1990 [BT] (Ch 12.4). The EPA values a statistical life at about $7.4 million (2006 dollars) for cost-benefit analyses (Ch 12.4).

International externalities (global warming, biodiversity loss) require international cooperation because no single nation can solve them alone (Ch 12.5). High-income countries historically caused most cumulative emissions; pressuring low-income countries to reduce their emissions raises distributional questions.

## Part XIII: Positive externalities and public goods (Ch 13)

Investment in new technology produces **positive externalities** because the inventor captures only one-third to one-half of total social value [BT] (Ch 13.1). The private rate of return is lower than the social rate of return, so private firms underinvest in R&D (Ch 13.1, Figure 13.2). Education has both private rates of return (about 10-15% per year of schooling [BT]) and social returns (better health, lower crime, more stable democracy) (Ch 13.1).

Government policies to encourage innovation: intellectual property protection (patents, trademarks, copyrights, trade secrets); direct R&D spending (about 22.7% of total U.S. R&D in 2018 [V] — Ch 13.2, Table 13.3); R&D tax credits; cooperative research between universities and industry (Ch 13.2). The text emphasises the tradeoff: patents must last long enough to incentivise invention but not so long that monopoly pricing harms consumers (Ch 13.2).

A **public good** has two defining features: **nonexcludable** ("it is costly or impossible to exclude someone from using the good" [V]) and **non-rival** ("when one person uses the public good, another can also use it" [V]) [V] (Ch 13.3). National defence is the canonical example. Markets fail to provide public goods because of the **free rider problem**: individuals have an incentive to use the good without paying (Ch 13.3).

The text relates this to the prisoner's-dilemma structure: rational individual behaviour produces collective failure (Ch 13.3). Government provision financed by taxation is the standard solution; some public goods can be provided through advertising-supported private provision (e.g. broadcast radio); in some cases social pressure or collective action can overcome free-riding (Ch 13.3).

**Common resources** (rivalrous but nonexcludable) are subject to the "tragedy of the commons" (Hardin 1968 [BT]) — overharvesting because no individual bears the cost of depletion (Ch 13.3). Marketable permits, harvest limits, and licences are policy tools. Elinor Ostrom (Nobel laureate) demonstrated that local communities can sometimes self-govern common resources without external regulation [BT] (Ch 13.3, "non-tragedy of the commons").

## Part XIV: Labour markets and income (Ch 14)

A **perfectly competitive labour market** has firms acting as wage-takers (Ch 14.1). The **first rule of labour markets**: "an employer will never pay a worker more than the value of their marginal productivity to the firm" [V] (Ch 14.1). For competitive output markets, demand for labour is the **value of the marginal product** (VMPL = MPL × P). For imperfectly competitive output markets, demand is the **marginal revenue product** (MRPL = MPL × MR), which lies below VMPL because MR < P. This produces lower employment in non-competitive output markets even at the same wage (Ch 14.1, "Demand for Labor in Imperfectly Competitive Output Markets").

Demand for labour is **derived demand** — derived from demand for the firm's products (Ch 14.1).

A **monopsony** is a labour market with one employer. The monopsonist faces an upward-sloping labour supply curve, so the marginal cost of labour exceeds the wage (raising wages for one more worker requires raising it for all previous workers). The monopsonist hires where MCL = MRPL, paying a lower wage and hiring fewer workers than a competitive market would (Ch 14.2).

**Labour unions** act like supply-side monopolies in labour markets. They negotiate higher wages and benefits through **collective bargaining**, but at the cost of reduced employment (Ch 14.3). U.S. union membership has declined from ~25% in 1955 to ~10.3% today, due to: shift from manufacturing to services; import competition; passage of worker-protection laws reducing demand for unions; and a less union-friendly U.S. legal environment (Ch 14.3).

A **bilateral monopoly** combines union (supply-side) and monopsony (demand-side) power. Employment is unambiguously lower than competitive; the wage is indeterminate, depending on relative bargaining power (Ch 14.4).

**Discrimination** "involves acting on the belief that members of a certain group are inferior or deserve less solely because of a factor such as race, gender, or religion" [V] (Ch 14.5). The text documents persistent earnings gaps by gender (women earn ~82% of men's earnings on average; the gap narrows substantially after controlling for occupation, experience, and family responsibilities) and by race (Black men earn ~75% of White men's earnings [BT]). Earnings gaps persist after controlling for measurable factors, suggesting either discrimination or unmeasured factors (Ch 14.5). Becker's economics-of-discrimination argument: competitive markets penalise discriminating firms, but discrimination persists when bigoted customers, fellow workers, or imperfect information sustain it. William Darity Jr. argues that competitive markets have not been sufficient to eliminate employment and wage discrimination [BT] (Ch 14.5).

Lisa D. Cook estimates that GDP could be 4.4% higher if women and minority populations were fully able to participate in science and technology innovation [BT] (Ch 14.5) — an indication of the macroeconomic cost of discrimination.

**Affirmative action** programmes exist to help close earnings gaps. **Redlining** (intentional discriminatory withholding of services based on race) has had lasting effects on educational and financial opportunity. Anti-discrimination laws — Civil Rights Act 1964, Pregnancy Discrimination Act 1978, Americans with Disabilities Act 1990, Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act 2009 — have helped narrow gaps but have not eliminated them (Ch 14.5).

**Immigration** raises overall U.S. economic output but has distributional effects: low-skill domestic workers may face slight wage reductions (~1%) due to competition from low-skill immigrants, while consumers benefit from lower prices for services [BT] (Ch 14.6). Immigration's net fiscal effect is positive at the federal level but can be negative at the state and local level where many low-skill immigrants concentrate (Ch 14.6).

## Part XV: Poverty and economic inequality (Ch 15)

**Poverty** is a level-of-income concept; **income inequality** is a distributional concept (Ch 15.1). The U.S. **poverty line** traces to Mollie Orshansky (1963), who computed it as three times the cost of a nutritionally adequate diet [BT]. The 2021 line for a single person was $12,880; for a household of four, $26,500. The poverty rate has been around 10.5% to 15.1% since the 1960s; in 2020 it was 11.4% (Ch 15.1).

The **poverty trap** occurs when government benefits decline as recipients earn income, so working produces little or no net income gain (Ch 15.2). Mitigation: phasing out benefits more slowly (50¢ reduction per $1 earned rather than $1 per $1), work requirements, time limits.

The **safety net** includes Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF), Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC), Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP), Medicaid, and Supplemental Security Income (Ch 15.3). The 1996 welfare reform replaced AFDC with TANF, imposing work requirements and a 5-year lifetime limit. EITC is "one of the largest assistance programs for low-income groups" — about 25 million workers received about $60 billion in 2021 [V] (Ch 15.3). EITC phases out gradually, reducing the poverty trap. Medicaid similarly faces poverty-trap issues; expanded eligibility helps.

**Quintile analysis** divides the population into fifths of equal size by income. In 2020, the bottom quintile received 3.0% of total U.S. income, the top quintile 52.2%, and the top 5% alone 23.0% (Ch 15.4, Table 15.5). The U.S. is more unequal than most other high-income countries (Germany: top quintile 39.6%, bottom 7.6%) but less unequal than Brazil, Mexico, or Russia (Ch 15.4).

The **Lorenz curve** plots cumulative income share against cumulative population share; the further from the 45-degree line, the more unequal (Ch 15.4). U.S. income inequality has increased markedly since the late 1970s, driven by two factors: changes in household composition (more two-earner couples and single-parent families) and "winner-take-all" labour markets where high-skill workers' wages have grown faster than low-skill workers' due to technology and globalisation (Ch 15.4).

Government policies to reduce inequality: redistribution through progressive taxes and transfer programmes; ladder-of-opportunity programmes (early childhood education, public schools, college access); estate taxes (Ch 15.5). The text frames a tradeoff: aggressive redistribution can reduce incentives to work and produce, but well-designed programmes (e.g. education) can increase both equality and output (Ch 15.5).

## Part XVI: Information, risk, and insurance (Ch 16)

Many transactions involve **imperfect information** and **asymmetric information** — situations where one party has better information than the other (Ch 16.1). Akerlof's "lemons" problem: in used-car markets, buyers cannot tell good cars from bad, so sellers of good cars are driven out by adverse selection [BT] (Ch 16.1).

Mechanisms to reduce information asymmetry: money-back guarantees, warranties, service contracts, reputation, brand names, occupational licences, cosigners, collateral (Ch 16.1).

**Insurance** spreads risk: members pay premiums, and those who suffer covered losses receive payments. Premiums must equal expected payouts plus administrative costs plus profit. **Risk groups** of similar probability share the same premium (actuarial fairness) (Ch 16.2).

Two information problems plague insurance markets:

**Moral hazard** — "people engage in riskier behaviour with insurance than they would if they did not have insurance" [V] (Ch 16.2). Mitigation: deductibles, copayments, coinsurance — all of which require the insured to bear part of the loss. Health Maintenance Organisations (HMOs) shift moral hazard from patients to providers by paying providers a fixed amount per enrolled person (Ch 16.2).

**Adverse selection** — high-risk individuals are more likely to seek insurance, while low-risk individuals avoid it. Insurers respond by raising prices, which drives out more low-risk customers, in a death spiral (Ch 16.2). Mitigation: group insurance through employers (mixes risks), mandates that everyone buy insurance, and government provision.

The U.S. healthcare system is unusually expensive ($10,948 per person in 2020 versus ~$5,000-7,000 in other high-income countries) but produces worse outcomes (life expectancy lower than peer countries) (Ch 16.2, Table 16.2). The Affordable Care Act (2010) used an individual mandate (penalty since eliminated), insurance exchanges, and an employer mandate to expand coverage. Coverage rose from ~80% to ~91% of the under-65 population.

## Part XVII: Financial markets (Ch 17)

Firms raise financial capital from four sources: early-stage investors (founders, angel investors, venture capital); reinvested profits; borrowing (bank loans and bonds); and equity (selling stock through IPOs and secondary offerings) (Ch 17.1). The choice depends on the firm's age, size, profitability, and the capital amount needed.

Households supply financial capital through bank accounts (checking, savings, CDs), bonds (corporate, municipal, Treasury), stocks, mutual funds, and tangible assets (housing, gold, collectibles) (Ch 17.2). Each is evaluated on three dimensions: **expected rate of return**, **risk**, and **liquidity** — and there is generally a tradeoff: higher returns come with higher risk and/or lower liquidity.

The **bond yield** depends on the bond's face value, coupon rate, market interest rates, and time to maturity. When market rates rise, existing bonds with lower coupons trade below face value; when market rates fall, existing higher-coupon bonds trade above face. The text explicitly works through the calculation: "yield, or total return, means interest payments, plus capital gains" [V] (Ch 17.2).

Stocks have averaged about 7% real return historically, but with substantial volatility (the S&P 500 fell 38% in 2008 and rebounded; the average decade-by-decade returns have varied from -1% in 2000-09 to over 18% in the 1990s [V] — Ch 17.2, Table 17.2). **Mutual funds**, especially **index funds**, allow diversification at low cost. Diversification reduces risk by averaging out idiosyncratic shocks.

Housing has been a major store of household wealth ($23.6 trillion in U.S. home equity by mid-2021), but home prices declined sharply in the 2008-09 housing crisis (-7% in each of 2008 and 2009) (Ch 17.2).

The **random walk theory** of stock prices: future price changes are unpredictable because all known information is already reflected in current prices. Most actively managed mutual funds underperform the market average over time (Ch 17.3). The reliable path to wealth: education plus early saving plus the power of compound interest.

## Part XVIII: Public economy (Ch 18)

Democratic governments may not always pursue the public interest. The text identifies several reasons:

**Rational ignorance** (Anthony Downs 1957): individual voters have weak incentives to become well-informed because their single vote is unlikely to determine the outcome [BT] (Ch 18.1). U.S. voter turnout in presidential elections is typically 55-65%; in midterms, often less than 50%.

**Special interest groups** are small in number but well-organised and focused. They can lobby for policies that benefit them at the expense of a much larger but unorganised public (Ch 18.2). The 2009 tariff on Chinese tires saved ~1,200 U.S. jobs at a cost to consumers of ~$926,500 per job saved — yet most consumers didn't notice or organise [BT] (Ch 18 closing case).

**Pork-barrel spending** and **logrolling** are mechanisms by which legislators trade votes to fund pet projects in their districts. Concentrated benefits and dispersed costs are a recipe for legislative success even when total social cost exceeds total social benefit (Ch 18.2).

**Voting cycles**: when there are three or more choices, no choice may be a Condorcet winner. The order of votes and procedural rules can determine outcomes — "majority view may not win" in any meaningful sense (Ch 18.3). This complicates simplistic claims about democratic legitimacy.

Government agencies, unlike firms, do not face direct competitive pressure or bankruptcy risk. They may lack incentives for efficiency that markets impose (Ch 18.3). The text's overall stance: both markets and governments are imperfect; sensible policy requires comparing real-world markets to real-world governments rather than idealised versions of either (Ch 18.3).

## Part XIX: The macroeconomic perspective and GDP (Ch 19)

Three macroeconomic goals: **economic growth** (rising standards of living), **low unemployment**, **low inflation** (Ch 19, "Goals"). These are tracked using two main analytical frameworks: aggregate demand-aggregate supply (AD-AS) and the Keynesian / Neoclassical perspectives. Two main policy tools: monetary policy (central bank) and fiscal policy (legislature).

**Gross domestic product (GDP)** is "the value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given year" [V] (Ch 19.1). U.S. GDP in 2020 was $20.9 trillion, the world's largest. GDP can be measured by demand (consumption + investment + government spending + net exports = C + I + G + NX) or by supply (sum of value added across sectors); both approaches yield the same total.

Consumption is ~67% of U.S. GDP; investment ~17%; government ~19%; net exports about -3% (deficit). Government spending and net exports show different patterns over time (Ch 19.1, Table 19.1).

**Real GDP** adjusts for inflation; **nominal GDP** does not. The **GDP deflator** is the price index used (Ch 19.2). U.S. real GDP per capita has grown roughly 2-3% per year in modern times.

GDP is an imperfect measure of well-being: it omits non-market production (household work, volunteering); does not count the value of leisure; does not adjust for environmental degradation or for distribution; and counts spending on disasters as positive contributions to GDP (Ch 19.5). Nonetheless, GDP correlates strongly with measures of well-being like life expectancy, education, and health.

## Part XX: Economic growth (Ch 20)

Modern economic growth — sustained increases in GDP per capita of ~2% per year — began with the Industrial Revolution in late 18th century Britain (Ch 20.1). Before this, growth was sporadic and small. The shift was driven by power-driven machinery (steam engine, power loom, locomotive) and by institutional changes including secure property rights, contractual rights, and rule of law (Ch 20.1).

**Labour productivity** — output per worker or per hour worked — is the main driver of long-run growth (Ch 20.2). Productivity depends on three factors: human capital (education, skills, experience); physical capital (plant, equipment, infrastructure); and technology (Ch 20.2). The **aggregate production function** combines these inputs into output.

**Compound growth rates** matter enormously over time: a 1% annual growth rate produces 64% growth over 50 years; a 5% rate produces 1,047% growth over the same period (Ch 20.2, Table 20.3).

**Capital deepening** (more capital per worker) and technological progress drive growth. Growth-accounting studies for the U.S. economy generally find that technology accounts for about half of growth, with capital deepening (physical and human) accounting for the rest (Ch 20.3). Healthy growth requires market-oriented institutions, supportive government policy (education, infrastructure, scientific research, special economic zones), and rule of law.

**Convergence** is the process by which low-income countries grow faster than high-income countries, narrowing income gaps over time. Some low-income countries (China, India, South Korea, Vietnam) have shown rapid catch-up growth; others (in sub-Saharan Africa) have not. Arguments for convergence: diminishing marginal returns to capital (gains larger where capital is scarce); easier to copy existing technology than invent new. Arguments against: low-income countries may lack institutions to absorb technology; technology can offset diminishing returns in high-income countries (Ch 20.4).

## Part XXI: Unemployment (Ch 21)

The labour force = employed + unemployed (actively seeking work). Those not in the labour force are not counted as unemployed. The **unemployment rate** = unemployed / labour force (Ch 21.1). The Bureau of Labor Statistics conducts the Current Population Survey monthly to compute this.

**Discouraged workers** have given up looking and are out of the labour force. **Underemployed** workers want full-time work but have only part-time or are working below their skill level. These constitute "hidden unemployment" — the unemployment rate may understate true labour-market slack (Ch 21.1).

**Labour force participation rate** = labour force / adult population. The U.S. rate peaked at ~67% around 2000 and declined to ~62% by 2022, with the COVID-19 pandemic accelerating the decline.

Unemployment varies by demographic group: higher for younger workers, racial/ethnic minorities, and the less-educated; lower for prime-age (25-54) and college-educated workers (Ch 21.2). U.S. unemployment is generally lower than other high-income countries (France, Germany historically have had higher rates).

**Cyclical unemployment** rises during recessions (Ch 21.3). The text explains why wages don't simply fall to clear the labour market: implicit contracts, efficiency wages (paying more elicits more productivity), adverse selection (cutting wages would drive away the best workers), insider-outsider dynamics, and relative wage coordination problems all produce **sticky downward wages**. With sticky wages, falling labour demand produces unemployment rather than wage cuts.

The **natural rate of unemployment** is the level that persists at full employment (Ch 21.4). It comprises **frictional unemployment** (workers between jobs in a dynamic economy) and **structural unemployment** (workers whose skills don't match available jobs). Natural rate estimates for the U.S. have been around 4.5-5.5% in the 2000s, lower than mid-century estimates of 6%.

## Part XXII: Inflation (Ch 22)

**Inflation** is "a general and ongoing rise in the level of prices in an entire economy" [V] (Ch 22). Measured using a basket-of-goods approach with index numbers (Ch 22.1).

The **Consumer Price Index (CPI)** is the most-cited U.S. inflation measure (Ch 22.2). It tracks 80,000 prices across about 23,000 stores in 87 urban areas. Eight major categories: food and beverages; housing; apparel; transportation; medical care; recreation; education and communication; other (Ch 22.2). The CPI has known biases: **substitution bias** (consumers substitute toward goods with relatively falling prices, but the basket is fixed); **quality bias** (improvements in quality are mismeasured); **new goods bias** (new products enter the basket with delay). These biases have likely caused the CPI to overstate true inflation by ~0.5% per year (Ch 22.2).

The **Producer Price Index** measures input prices; the **GDP deflator** measures all prices in GDP; the **International Price Index** measures import/export prices.

U.S. inflation has been generally low (1-4% per year) since the mid-1980s, after reaching double-digit levels in the 1970s. Russia experienced **hyperinflation** in the early 1990s (~2,500% per year). Zimbabwe in 2008 reached 79.6 billion percent annually. The closest the U.S. has come to hyperinflation was the Confederate states in 1860-65 (Ch 22.3).

Inflation has effects through three channels: **unintended redistribution of purchasing power** (creditors lose when inflation is unexpected, debtors gain); **blurred price signals** (when prices change because of inflation, it's harder to tell whether they signal real shifts in supply or demand); and **problems for long-term planning** (Ch 22.4). Modest inflation may have benefits: it may help nominal-wage adjustments in a downward direction (Ch 22.4).

**Indexing** ties payments to a price index. The U.S. tax brackets are now indexed (since 1981); Social Security benefits are indexed; some labour contracts have **cost-of-living adjustments**; **adjustable-rate mortgages** are indexed to interest rates (Ch 22.5).

## Part XXIII: International trade and capital flows (Ch 23)

A country's **balance of payments** has two main components. The **current account** records flows of goods, services, income, and current transfers. The **financial account** records investment flows. Sum to zero (with statistical discrepancy) (Ch 23).

A **trade deficit** (imports > exports) must be balanced by net financial inflows. The U.S. has run persistent current-account deficits since the 1980s, financed by foreign investors purchasing U.S. assets. This is a "savings shortage" view: the U.S. economy doesn't save enough domestically to finance investment, so it imports savings from abroad (Ch 23).

National savings = private savings + public savings. Public savings = government surplus (negative when government runs a deficit). The savings-investment identity: S = I + (X - M). If domestic savings fall short of domestic investment, the country runs a current-account deficit (Ch 23).

Trade deficits are not inherently bad: they can finance productive investment that produces future returns to repay foreign creditors. But persistent deficits financed by consumption rather than investment can lead to crisis if foreign confidence wavers (Ch 23).

## Part XXIV: AD-AS and the macro frameworks (Ch 24)

Two organising perspectives:

**Say's law** ("supply creates its own demand"): production generates income, which is spent on output. **Neoclassical economists** following Say emphasise aggregate supply and the long run. Real economic capacity (labour, capital, technology) determines output (Ch 24.1).

**Keynes's law** ("demand creates its own supply"): production responds to demand. **Keynesian economists** emphasise aggregate demand and the short run. Recessions reflect insufficient demand (Ch 24.1).

The **AD-AS model** combines both (Ch 24.2). The **aggregate supply (AS) curve** is upward-sloping in the short run because higher output prices (with input prices fixed) increase profitability and induce more production. As output approaches potential GDP, the SRAS curve becomes steeper. The **long-run aggregate supply (LRAS) curve** is vertical at potential GDP — the maximum sustainable output given resources and technology.

The **aggregate demand (AD) curve** is downward-sloping for three reasons: **wealth effect** (higher prices reduce real wealth, lowering consumption); **interest rate effect** (higher prices increase money demand, raising interest rates, lowering investment); **foreign price effect** (higher domestic prices make exports less competitive and imports more attractive) (Ch 24.2).

Equilibrium occurs where AD intersects AS. Three zones of the SRAS curve:
- **Keynesian zone** (flat, far below potential): AD shifts move output, not prices. Typical of recession.
- **Neoclassical zone** (vertical, at or above potential): AD shifts move prices, not output. Inflationary pressure.
- **Intermediate zone**: AD shifts move both (Ch 24.6).

AS shifts: rightward from productivity growth; leftward from input-price increases (oil, wages). **Stagflation** — the 1970s pattern of stagnant output plus high inflation — results from a leftward AS shift (Ch 24.3).

AD shifts: changes in consumer/business confidence; changes in government spending and taxes; changes in trade conditions; changes in money supply / interest rates (Ch 24.4).

## Part XXV: The Keynesian perspective (Ch 25)

Keynesian analysis focuses on aggregate demand (Ch 25.1). C + I + G + NX comprise AD. Determinants of consumption: disposable income (the most important); expected future income; wealth/credit. Determinants of investment: expected future profits; interest rates. Government spending is determined politically. Net exports depend on relative growth and prices.

Recessions occur when AD falls short of potential GDP (Ch 25.2). Key Keynesian assumptions: (1) AD can be insufficient to support full employment; (2) wages and prices are sticky (especially downward), so adjustment is slow. The **coordination argument** explains downward stickiness: workers don't accept wage cuts unless they're sure others are also accepting them. **Menu costs** explain price stickiness — changing prices is costly for firms.

The **expenditure multiplier** says that an autonomous increase in spending produces a more-than-proportionate increase in GDP, because one person's spending is another's income, which is then partially re-spent (Ch 25.2). This makes fiscal policy potentially powerful.

The **Phillips curve** plots the inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation (Ch 25.3). A.W. Phillips found this in 60 years of British data. In the 1960s, economists treated it as a stable policy menu. The 1970s **stagflation** broke the relationship — unemployment and inflation rose together, contradicting the Phillips curve. Two factors cause shifts: supply shocks (1970s oil); changes in expected inflation. The Phillips curve appears valid in short-run periods but shifts over longer horizons.

Keynesian policy prescription: **expansionary fiscal policy** (tax cuts, government spending increases) during recessions; **contractionary fiscal policy** during inflation (Ch 25.3). The 2008-09 stimulus and 2020 pandemic-induced response were both Keynesian responses to AD shocks.

## Part XXVI: The Neoclassical perspective (Ch 26)

Neoclassical analysis focuses on long-run aggregate supply (Ch 26.1). It assumes wages and prices are flexible (eventually); the economy returns to potential GDP in the long run; and government attempts to stimulate AD beyond potential GDP cause inflation rather than sustained output gains.

Key implications: monetary and fiscal policy that aims to push output above potential is futile; better policy focuses on the supply side (productivity-enhancing investments, removing distortions) (Ch 26.2). **Rational expectations** (Lucas, Sargent) further argue that systematic policy effects are anticipated and thus undermined by private behaviour.

The **balance** between Keynesian and Neoclassical views: most modern macroeconomic policy combines both. Short-run policy responses (fiscal stimulus during recession; monetary expansion during contraction) follow Keynesian logic. Long-run frameworks (potential GDP, sustainable growth, low inflation as a long-run goal) follow neoclassical logic (Ch 26.3).

## Part XXVII: Money and banking (Ch 27)

**Money** serves four functions: medium of exchange, store of value, unit of account, standard of deferred payment (Ch 27.1). It solves the **double coincidence of wants** problem of barter. **Commodity money** has intrinsic use value (gold, silver, cowrie shells); **commodity-backed money** is paper redeemable for commodity (1957-era U.S. Silver Certificates); **fiat money** has value only by government decree and social acceptance.

The U.S. money supply has two main definitions:
- **M1**: currency + checkable deposits + savings deposits (since May 2020 redefinition)
- **M2**: M1 + small time deposits + retail money market funds

In November 2021, M1 was about $20.3 trillion; M2 was about $21.4 trillion (Ch 27.2).

**Banks** are **financial intermediaries** between savers and borrowers (Ch 27.3). A bank's balance sheet (T-account) lists assets (loans, bonds, reserves) and liabilities (deposits) plus net worth (bank capital). Banks can become insolvent if loan defaults or interest-rate movements reduce asset values below liabilities. **Diversification** across loan types reduces risk.

In a system with **limited reserves** (banks holding only the legally required minimum), banks create money through lending: the **money multiplier** = 1 / reserve ratio (Ch 27.4). A $1,000 deposit, with a 10% reserve requirement, can support up to $10,000 in money supply through repeated lending and re-depositing. Since 2008, U.S. banks have held **ample reserves** (significantly above required), so the money multiplier mechanism operates differently.

**Cryptocurrency** (Bitcoin from 2009; Ethereum, Binance Coin, others) is a new form of digital, decentralised currency. It is not fiat (not government-issued) but functions as money to varying degrees. Its broader adoption as a medium of exchange remains uncertain (Ch 27 closing case).

## Part XXVIII: Monetary policy and bank regulation (Ch 28)

The U.S. **Federal Reserve** (founded 1913) has three functions: conduct monetary policy; promote financial stability; provide banking services to commercial banks and the federal government (Ch 28.1). The Fed is structured with a Board of Governors (7 members, 14-year staggered terms) and 12 regional Reserve Banks. The Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) makes monetary policy decisions.

**Bank regulation** maintains solvency and financial stability through reserve requirements, capital requirements, restrictions on investments, and supervision (Ch 28.2). **Deposit insurance** (FDIC) and **lender of last resort** services protect against bank runs.

Three traditional monetary policy tools (in a limited-reserves regime):
1. **Open market operations**: Fed buys/sells Treasury bonds. Buying bonds increases reserves and lowers the federal funds rate, easing credit. Selling bonds reverses (Ch 28.3).
2. **Discount rate**: rate at which Fed lends to banks. Lowering it encourages bank borrowing and lending.
3. **Reserve requirements**: percentage of deposits banks must hold as reserves. Lowering frees up funds for lending. Effectively eliminated in March 2020.

Since 2008, the Fed has primarily operated in an **ample reserves** regime, using the **interest rate on reserve balances (IORB)** to signal monetary policy stance. Lowering IORB encourages banks to lend rather than hold reserves; raising IORB does the reverse (Ch 28.3, "Monetary Policy and Ample Reserves").

**Quantitative easing (QE)**: large-scale purchases of long-term Treasury bonds and mortgage-backed securities, used during the 2008-09 Great Recession (QE1, QE2, QE3) and during the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic ("QE4") (Ch 28.3). QE works around the **zero lower bound** when short-term rates can't go lower.

**Expansionary monetary policy** (loose) lowers interest rates, increases lending, increases AD; **contractionary** (tight) is the reverse (Ch 28.4). The Fed faces **time lags** (recognition lag, decision lag, implementation lag) and uncertainty in calibrating policy.

## Part XXIX: Exchange rates and international capital flows (Ch 29)

**Exchange rates** are the prices of one currency in terms of another (Ch 29.1). Markets for currencies have demand (from importers, foreign investors, tourists) and supply (from exporters, foreign investors selling assets, tourists). Equilibrium rates clear the market.

Three main exchange-rate regimes (Ch 29.4):
- **Floating** (e.g. U.S., U.K., EU, Japan): rates determined by markets
- **Fixed/pegged** (e.g. China managed against dollar): government commits to a rate and intervenes to maintain it
- **Currency boards or dollarization**: country uses another country's currency or commits rigidly to a peg

A country with a fixed exchange rate cannot independently set monetary policy if capital is mobile (the **trilemma**: choose any two of fixed exchange rate, free capital flow, independent monetary policy).

**Purchasing power parity (PPP)** is a long-run equilibrium concept: the same basket of goods should cost the same across countries when expressed in a common currency. Deviations occur over time because of trade barriers, transport costs, and non-traded goods (Ch 29.3).

## Part XXX: Government budgets and fiscal policy (Ch 30)

The federal government runs a **budget deficit** when spending exceeds revenue, **surplus** when revenue exceeds spending. The U.S. has run deficits in most years since 1961, reaching record peaks in WWII, the 1980s, the 2008-09 Great Recession, and 2020 (15% of GDP — largest since WWII) (Ch 30.1, 30.3).

Federal spending categories: about 60% goes to four areas (national defence, Social Security, healthcare, interest payments); 40% covers everything else (Ch 30.1). State and local government spend an additional ~14% of GDP, mostly on education.

Federal taxes: individual income tax (largest source); payroll taxes (Social Security and Medicare; close to ~half of federal revenue); corporate income tax (declining as a share of GDP); excise taxes; estate taxes. The income tax is **progressive** (higher income, higher marginal rate). The Medicare payroll tax is **proportional**. The Social Security payroll tax is proportional up to a wage limit, then **regressive** above it (Ch 30.2).

The **national debt** is the cumulative sum of past deficits and surpluses. The debt-to-GDP ratio rose from ~30% in the 1970s to ~120% by 2020.

**Discretionary fiscal policy** is explicit changes in tax or spending rules. **Automatic stabilizers** are existing programs (unemployment insurance, food stamps, progressive taxation) that automatically expand during downturns and contract during expansions (Ch 30.5). Automatic stabilizers offset about 10% of any initial output movement.

The **standardized employment budget** estimates what the deficit/surplus would be at potential GDP. Comparing actual to standardized reveals the impact of automatic stabilizers (Ch 30.5).

Practical problems with discretionary fiscal policy (Ch 30.6):
- Time lags (recognition, legislative, implementation)
- Fiscal-monetary policy coordination needs
- **Crowding out**: government borrowing raises interest rates, which can offset fiscal stimulus
- Temporary vs. permanent: temporary changes have weaker effects
- Political: politicians prefer expansionary policy in good times and bad

The text argues against rigid balanced-budget requirements: they would prevent automatic stabilizers from working and worsen recessions (Ch 30.7). However, persistent large deficits can pose long-term problems through reduced national savings, slower growth, and potential crisis (Ch 30.7, building on Ch 31).

## Part XXXI: Impacts of government borrowing (Ch 31)

Persistent fiscal deficits affect the macroeconomy through several channels (Ch 31). They can crowd out private investment by raising interest rates. They can be financed by foreign borrowing, which is recorded as a current-account deficit. They reduce national savings, which reduces capital formation and slows long-run growth. Fiscal deficits combined with high debt can undermine investor confidence and trigger fiscal crises (as in Greece 2010s).

The text frames a long-run vs. short-run tension: fiscal stimulus can be helpful in a recession (Keynesian short-run argument) but persistent deficits can harm growth (neoclassical long-run argument). The right policy depends on cyclical conditions and the productive use of borrowed funds.

## Part XXXII: Macroeconomic policy around the world (Ch 32)

The text frames countries by their economic challenges and constraints (Ch 32). High-income countries focus primarily on smoothing business cycles and managing demographic aging. Middle-income countries face challenges of catching up while avoiding the "middle-income trap." Low-income countries face deeper problems of building institutions, infrastructure, and productive capacity.

Key institutional foundations matter most for growth: rule of law, property rights, openness to trade, education, sound macroeconomic policy (low inflation, sustainable fiscal position), and limited corruption (Ch 32). Different macroeconomic regimes face different policy levers; small open economies have less independence than large ones.

## Part XXXIII: International trade — comparative advantage (Ch 33)

The cornerstone result: **comparative advantage** (Ricardo 1817) means countries gain from trade by specialising in goods where they have lower opportunity cost (Ch 33.1). This holds even when one country has absolute advantage in everything (Ch 33.2).

The text uses Saudi Arabia (oil) and the U.S. (corn) — and U.S. (refrigerators) and Mexico (shoes) — as canonical examples. Trade allows each country to consume beyond its production possibilities frontier (Ch 33.1, 33.2).

**Intra-industry trade** — trade in similar goods among similar countries — accounts for ~60% of world trade. The standard comparative-advantage story (different opportunity costs from geography or factor endowments) doesn't explain this. The drivers are: specialised learning along the **value chain** (production split across countries); **economies of scale** that allow large producers to compete globally; product differentiation enabling variety with competitive intensity (Ch 33.3).

The text's bottom line: gains from international trade are real, especially for smaller countries, and are typically large enough to outweigh distributional concerns. The WTO Doha Round was estimated to add $121-202 billion annually to the world economy (Ch 33.4).

## Part XXXIV: Globalization and protectionism (Ch 34)

**Protectionism** (tariffs, import quotas, non-tariff barriers) is "an indirect subsidy from consumers to producers" [V] (Ch 34.1). The text uses sugar markets (Brazil-U.S.) to demonstrate that free trade creates social surplus and that quotas reduce it.

The text takes a clear position on trade and jobs: protectionism saves jobs in the protected industry but costs jobs in unprotected industries through (1) lower consumer purchasing power and (2) higher input costs for downstream firms (Ch 34.2). Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas estimates: saving a job in apparel and textiles costs consumers ~$199,000 per year; in sugar ~$826,000; in dairy ~$685,000 (Ch 34.2, Table 34.2).

Trade increases average wages by raising productivity (workers play to comparative advantage). But specific groups (low-skill workers competing with imports) may see wage declines while others (in export-oriented sectors) gain. Half of U.S. trade is intra-industry trade with similar high-wage countries — these effects are limited (Ch 34.2).

Five arguments for restricting imports (Ch 34.3):
1. **Infant industry**: protect new industries until competitive. Theoretically defensible; empirically often fails (Brazilian computer industry).
2. **Anti-dumping**: protect against foreign sales below cost. Theoretically weak; politically prone to capture.
3. **Environmental protection**: prevent "race to the bottom." Empirically, environmental costs are 1-2% of plant costs; not the driver of location decisions.
4. **Unsafe consumer products**: legitimate but better addressed through safety standards than import bans.
5. **National interest/security**: legitimate but often used as a cover for politically-motivated protection.

Trade policy is enacted through the **WTO** (founded 1995, building on GATT 1947), regional agreements (EU, USMCA, ASEAN, Mercosur), and bilateral agreements (Ch 34.4). The WTO administers dispute resolution and rules. The trend over decades has been toward lower trade barriers, with a recent reversal of that trend in 2017-19 (Trump tariffs) and continued in 2020s (Ch 34.5).

The text's overall position: gains from trade are real and significant; protectionism's distributional effects are real but better addressed through retraining, safety nets, and adjustment assistance than through import barriers.

## Positions the authors explicitly frame against

- **Mercantilism / protectionism**: the text takes a clear pro-trade stance, arguing that comparative advantage gains accrue even when one country has absolute advantage (Ch 33). Protectionism is "an indirect subsidy from consumers to producers" (Ch 34.1) [V].
- **Sticky wage adjustments are bad**: the text argues that some downward stickiness is rational and supports macroeconomic stability — workers shouldn't be expected to take rapid wage cuts during recessions (Ch 21.3, Ch 25.2).
- **Strict balanced-budget requirements**: the text argues against rigid annual balanced budgets, which would prevent automatic stabilisers from working and worsen recessions (Ch 30.7).
- **Government as the only solution to market failures**: the text takes a balanced view — markets and governments are both imperfect; sensible policy compares real-world institutions, not idealised versions (Chs 11, 18).
- **Markets as always self-correcting**: the text identifies multiple cases where markets fail (monopoly, externalities, public goods, information asymmetries, unemployment, financial crises). It does not subscribe to a strong-form-efficient-markets view.
- **Cost-of-living measures should be unindexed**: the text argues for indexing tax brackets, Social Security, and other government programs to reduce arbitrary inflation effects (Ch 22.5).
- **Discretionary fiscal policy as easy fine-tuning**: the text identifies multiple practical problems (lags, calibration, political incentives) that limit the precision of discretionary fiscal policy (Ch 30.6).
- **Strict deficit-equals-bad framing**: the text argues that deficits during recessions are appropriate (counter-cyclical), but persistent peacetime deficits during good times can harm long-run growth (Chs 30, 31).
