Public licence: CC BY-ND 4.0; relicensed CC BY 4.0 by the author for this corpus. https://approachperfect.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/04/The-Approach-Perfect-Field-Guide-to-Scrum-Events-2020-04-03.pdf.

## Decision-Making Relevance

The Field Guide is, at the practitioner level, a facilitation manual for structured decision cadences. Where the Scrum Guide *defines* the decision rights and timeboxes, the Field Guide *operationalises* them — turning each Scrum event into a working agenda with concrete time-allocations, named techniques, decision-points within the agenda, and tips for keeping the decision discipline intact.

Three decision-discipline threads run through the guide.

**First, each Scrum event is a structured decision moment with a named purpose.** The Sprint is a containing decision (what to deliver against the Sprint Goal). Sprint Planning is three sequenced decisions (Sprint Goal, scope selection, delivery plan) running 1–2 hours per Sprint-week, capped at 8 hours for a one-month Sprint. The Daily Scrum is a daily course-correction decision (15 minutes; what's the plan for the next 24 hours?). The Sprint Review is a what-to-do-next decision made *with* stakeholders (1 hour ideal for a 2-week Sprint, max 4 hours). The Sprint Retrospective is a process-improvement decision (1½ hours for a 2-week Sprint, max 3 hours; output is improvement Backlog Items plus changes to the Definition of Done). Backlog Refinement is a continuous prioritisation-and-readiness decision (~10% of Sprint Capacity).

**Second, the Field Guide makes decision rights and decision constraints explicit and concrete.** The Development Team — not the Product Owner or Scrum Master — decides when the Sprint Backlog has enough work. The Product Owner — usually in consultation with the Scrum Master and Development Team — has Sprint cancellation authority, "usually when the *Sprint Goal* is obsolete." The Developers decide the Daily Scrum format and the tasking-out approach. The Product Owner pre-circulates Backlog Refinement items and makes the final ordering decision after the team proposes changes. The Scrum Master keeps time but doesn't have to facilitate. The strongest contrarian decision-rule in the guide: *no firm commitment* to delivering either the Sprint Backlog or the Sprint Goal — both are focusing tools and good-faith forecasts, not contracts; treating them as firm commitments "will only lead to burnout, poor quality, loss of trust, and/or attempts at 'gaming the system.'"

**Third, the Field Guide encodes decision-quality rules that operate within and across events.** The Definition of Done is the pre-committed release-gate decision rule. The carry-over rule for Sprint Planning: if the carried-over WIP meets or exceeds forecast velocity, take in zero new work and consider removing items — "honest empirical process control." The Sprint Goal preservation rule during the Sprint: scope can be clarified and renegotiated, but the Sprint Goal should be preserved whenever possible. The "capture outcomes, not implementations" rule for Backlog Items (the coffee-temperature worked example): the *what* belongs to the Product Owner and end users; the *how* belongs to the Development Team. The "estimation is conversation" rule: a 1-vs-5 vote spread surfaces hidden information; accepting averages or senior-developer opinion forfeits the learning. The Retrospective stay-focused rule: one or two improvement items per Sprint beats too much to change at once.

This distillation gathers these threads into a working pattern: which decision belongs to which Scrum role, which decision is registered in which Scrum artefact, which Scrum event is the right forum for which decision moment, and which Field-Guide tips keep the decision discipline intact across the cadence. It is a practitioner's reach when running the events; not the canonical framework definition (that's the Scrum Guide).

## Key Concepts for Decision-Making

1. **Each Scrum event is a structured decision cadence with concrete time-allocations.** Sprint Planning 1–2h per Sprint-week (max 8h); Daily Scrum ≤15 min; Sprint Review 15–60 min per Sprint-week, ideal 1h for 2-week (max 4h); Sprint Retrospective 1½h for 2-week (max 3h); Backlog Refinement ~10% of Sprint Capacity. The Field Guide turns these timeboxes into agendas, so the decision moments inside each event are bounded by named segments. (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide to Scrum Events* 2020, Scrum Events at a Glance; each event section)

2. **Three sequential decisions in Sprint Planning, with a stop-rule.** Topic One: why is this Sprint valuable? (Sprint Goal). Topic Two: what can be done? (Backlog Item selection — the Development Team, not PO or SM, decides when there's enough work). Topic Three: how will the work get done? (delivery plan — at the Development Team's discretion). The stop-rule on Topic Two is important: the team chooses to stop adding items based on their own judgement of capacity. ("Sprint Planning", "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda")

3. **No firm commitment to the Sprint Backlog or Sprint Goal.** "The *Development Team* cannot and must not make a firm commitment to delivering the entire *Sprint Backlog* or even the *Sprint Goal*. Attempting to do so will only lead to burnout, poor quality, loss of trust, and/or attempts at 'gaming the system.' Instead, the *Sprint Backlog* and *Sprint Goal* is a focusing tool that limits context switching and a good-faith forecast that the *Development Team* attempts to live up to." The Sprint Backlog and Sprint Goal anchor the team's attention; they don't bind it to a contract. Treat them as focus, not commitment. ("Sprint Planning", "Tips")

4. **The Sprint Goal is not coextensive with the Sprint Backlog.** "It should be possible to meet the *Sprint Goal* without completing the entire *Sprint Backlog*." Decisions should anchor on the Goal, not the Backlog; if the work turns out different than expected, the Backlog can be renegotiated *without* changing the Sprint Goal. ("Sprint Planning", "Outputs")

5. **The carry-over rule: if WIP meets or exceeds forecast velocity, take in zero new work.** "If the work in process in your carried-over *Sprint Backlog* meets or exceeds your forecast velocity and it is all still valuable to work on, we strongly recommend against taking in *any* new work this *Sprint*. You may even need to *remove* some items." A capacity-discipline decision rule that protects against optimistic over-commitment. ("Sprint Planning", "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda")

6. **Velocity is a Capacity-adjusted decision input, not the decision.** The author offers an explicit formula: average the Capacity-adjusted Velocity of the last 3–4 sprints, multiply by the forecast Capacity for the next sprint. Worked example: 25, 24, 31 SP over 45, 43, 50 days with forecast capacity 47 → ≈30 SP forecast. The formula is a starting point; the team still decides whether the forecast captures the real situation. ("Sprint Planning", "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda")

7. **Capture outcomes, not implementations.** Backlog Items should be expressed as customer-perspective outcomes ("As a coffee drinker, I can have coffee that's just the right temperature so that I can delight in its ideal taste") not implementation specifications ("Integrate the Acme steam-heating element into the brew assembly"). The decision-rule separation is clean: outcomes are Product Owner / stakeholder decisions; implementation is the Development Team's decision. (Agile principles #1 and #11.) ("Sprint Planning", "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda")

8. **The Daily Scrum is a 15-minute daily course-correction decision.** Output: "A plan for the next 24 hours and a newly updated *Sprint Backlog* as necessary." Format is at the Development Team's discretion. The Parking Lot mechanism is the named device for deferring decisions that would exceed the 15-minute timebox without losing them — the team meets after the Daily Scrum to make those decisions. ("Daily Scrum", "Goals", "Output", "Sample 15-Minute Daily Scrum Agenda", "Tips")

9. **The Daily Scrum is not a status meeting.** "The *Daily Scrum* is not a status review or report for anyone, including *Product Owners*, *Scrum Masters*, or (especially) stakeholders. Don't let it become one." The decision-rule consequence: information flow at the Daily Scrum is for the Development Team's planning, not for upstream reporting. Status pressure corrupts the decision-quality of the event. ("Daily Scrum", "Tips")

10. **The Sprint Review is a working what-to-do-next decision *with* stakeholders, not a status meeting.** "This is *not* a status meeting. Rather, it is an opportunity for stakeholders to collaborate with the team and improve the *Product Backlog* with shared context and learnings." The decision-output is an updated Product Backlog reflecting stakeholder feedback and market/context changes. ("Sprint Review", "Tips")

11. **Don't demonstrate work that isn't Done.** "Don't demonstrate work that isn't '*Done*.' It can significantly erode trust between the *Scrum Team*, stakeholders, and end users if the demonstrated work doesn't get shipped as expected. It also allows the *Scrum Team* to persist the illusion that incomplete work is still valuable. Remember: work in process is a liability, not an asset." A decision-quality rule: Definition of Done is the gate; demonstrating below-the-gate work makes the next decision worse. ("Sprint Review", "Tips")

12. **The Sprint Retrospective is a process-improvement decision with named output mechanisms.** Outputs split into *decisions* (changes to the Definition of Done, Definition of Ready, or Working Agreement) and *actions* (new Backlog Items capturing improvement experiments for the next Sprint). The two mechanisms register different kinds of commitment: decisions change the rules; actions change the work. ("Sprint Retrospective", "Output", "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda")

13. **One or two retrospective improvements per Sprint beats many.** "It's better to have one or two improvement items for the next *Sprint* than to have too much to change at once." A decision-quality rule on the size of the improvement experiment — pick one constraint to act on, leaving non-constraints alone. ("Sprint Retrospective", "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda")

14. **The Retrospective Prime Directive frames decision posture.** "Regardless of what we discover, we understand and truly believe that everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time, their skills and abilities, the resources available, and the situation at hand." A pre-commitment about how the retrospective will interpret its evidence — a decision-posture rule that protects against blame-and-shame interpretations. ("Sprint Retrospective", "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda")

15. **The Vegas Rule or Chatham House Rule, explicitly chosen.** Retrospective confidentiality is a precondition for raising difficult issues; the Scrum Team should explicitly pick one rule (Vegas = unanimous explicit consent before sharing anything; Chatham House = information may be shared, identities may not). A decision about decision-making conditions. ("Sprint Retrospective", "Tips")

16. **Sprint cancellation is a Product Owner decision, usually in consultation.** "The *Product Owner* may cancel a *Sprint* before the time-box has elapsed, usually in consultation with the *Scrum Master* and *Development Team*. This usually happens when the *Sprint Goal* is obsolete. A *Sprint* cancellation is traumatic to the team and consumes resources, so should be avoided if possible." A high-cost decision reserved to the role accountable for product value; the trigger condition is Sprint-Goal obsolescence. ("The Sprint", "Tips")

17. **Definition of Done is the release-gate decision rule.** Work failing the Definition of Done cannot be presented as Done at the Sprint Review; demonstrating it erodes trust. The DoD replaces case-by-case Done judgements at the moment of release. ("Sprint Review", "Sample 1-Hour Sprint Review Agenda"; "Sprint Review", "Tips"; "Sprint Retrospective", "Output")

18. **Don't detail-plan too far ahead.** "Don't do detailed planning too far in the future. Scrum is an empirical, iterative approach to product development. This means that you must continuously update and improve your Product Backlog based on what you learn from stakeholders and end users with each *Sprint Review* and product *Increment* release." A decision-quality rule for Backlog Refinement: the further out you plan, the less Real-world feedback you have; over-planned futures become anchors rather than guides. ("Backlog Refinement", "Tips")

19. **The estimation conversation is more valuable than the estimate.** "Take your time when estimating *Backlog Items*. Estimation serves a dual purpose… we can also learn a great deal from the ensuing conversation. Suppose one developer rates a *Backlog Item* as a '1,' while another rates it as a '5'… If the *Scrum Team* accepts an average or the most senior developer's opinion, they may miss out on an opportunity for shared knowledge." A decision-process rule: the spread is the signal; investigate it rather than averaging it away. ("Backlog Refinement", "Tips")

20. **Estimate everything, including defects.** "Estimate all *Backlog Items*, even defects. Some *Development Teams* don't like to estimate defects because the root cause can sometimes be difficult to predict. The advantage of making the attempt is that your team will be better able to maintain a smooth, steady flow of work when the expected cost of resolving defects is also accounted for." A capacity-decision rule: defect cost belongs in the team's capacity model even when it's hard to estimate. ("Backlog Refinement", "Tips")

21. **Backlog Refinement Product-Owner ordering decision is final.** "Allow the *Scrum Team* to propose changes to the order based on a thoughtful consideration of its value, estimate, and dependencies (the *Product Owner* makes the final decision." Team proposes, PO decides. ("Backlog Refinement", "Sample 1½-hour Backlog Refinement Agenda")

22. **Practise the form before deviating.** "A high-performing team will eventually find a better framework than 'textbook' Scrum for their environment. However, I suggest that you deviate from it only with well-formed experiments after practicing diligently for several sprints under the guidance of a knowledgeable Scrum Master or Agile Coach." A decision-rule about when to deviate from the framework: only after diligent practice, only as a well-formed experiment, with humility and honest curiosity. ("Introduction")

## Questions to Ask During Decision-Making

### Phase 1: Framing (Recognising the decision and gathering context)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify the appropriate Scrum event for this decision | Is this a Sprint Planning decision (commitment), a Daily Scrum decision (today's plan), a Sprint Review decision (what-next with stakeholders), a Sprint Retrospective decision (process change), or a Backlog Refinement decision (what's Ready, what's prioritised)? Don't make Sprint-level decisions in the Daily Scrum, or retrospective decisions in the Sprint Review. |
| Surface who has the decision right | Is this a Product Owner decision (Product Backlog ordering, Sprint cancellation), a Development Team decision (sizing, how-to-build, Daily Scrum format, when the Sprint Backlog has enough work), or a whole-team decision (Sprint Goal, Definition of Done within team scope)? |
| Detect status-meeting drift | Is the Daily Scrum being treated as a report to PO/SM/stakeholders? Is the Sprint Review being treated as a presentation rather than a working session? Both corrupt the decision quality of the event. |
| Test for "is this Done?" decision rule | Is the team about to judge an item Done case-by-case, or against the team's pre-committed Definition of Done? Use the DoD; don't re-litigate at the boundary. |
| Distinguish outcome decisions from implementation decisions | Is this a *what to build* question (PO + stakeholder decision, expressed as outcomes from the customer's perspective) or a *how to build it* question (Development Team's decision, at their sole discretion)? |
| Check the Retrospective Prime Directive posture | If this is a retrospective decision, does the team's posture honour the Prime Directive ("everyone did the best job they could given what they knew")? If not, the analysis will tilt toward blame and the decision quality will suffer. |

### Phase 2: Bounding (Defining the decision and its constraints)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Bound by the right event timebox | Sprint Planning 1–2h per Sprint-week (max 8h for 1-month Sprint); Daily Scrum 15 min; Sprint Review 15–60 min per Sprint-week (max 4h for 1-month Sprint, ideal 1h for 2-week); Sprint Retrospective 1½h for 2-week (max 3h); Backlog Refinement ~10% of Capacity. Is the decision being attempted in the right event-shaped container? |
| Anchor on the Sprint Goal | Does this decision serve the Sprint Goal, threaten it, or change it? Scope can be clarified and renegotiated; the Sprint Goal should be preserved whenever possible. |
| Apply the Definition of Done | What's the team's Definition of Done for this Increment? If it isn't explicit, name it before the decision lands. |
| Test against the carry-over rule | If carry-over WIP meets or exceeds forecast velocity and is all still valuable, the decision rule is: zero new work this Sprint, and possibly remove items. Painful but honest. |
| Plan for capacity, not optimism | Has the Development Team's Capacity been honestly stated (accounting for leave) and Velocity been honestly forecast (Capacity-adjusted average of last 3–4 sprints)? Or is the plan running on wishful thinking? |
| Bound by Scrum's deliberate under-specification | Is the team trying to commit to a "best practice" the Field Guide doesn't claim to provide? "A high-performing team will eventually find a better framework than 'textbook' Scrum for their environment" — but only after diligent practice. |

### Phase 3: Exploring (Generating alternatives)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Run the Sprint Planning three Topics | Topic One: Sprint Goal alternatives (why is this Sprint valuable?). Topic Two: Backlog Item selection alternatives (what can be done?). Topic Three: delivery-plan alternatives (how will the work get done? — Development Team's discretion). |
| Surface forecast inputs without letting them decide | Is the Velocity formula being used as a starting point for the conversation, or as the answer? "All other things equal" the team adjusts; the formula doesn't substitute for empirical decision-making. |
| Use Fists of Five when Velocity is unavailable | "If your team does not have a known, stable Velocity (ideally 3-4 sprints worth of data) or chooses not to use a quantitative approach, try using 'Fists of Five.'" |
| Pre-circulate Backlog items before Refinement | Is the Product Owner pre-sending the list so the Scrum Team can read ahead? This is what allows the event to end early when items are already Ready. |
| Estimate with conversation, not averaging | If estimators are split (one says 1, another says 5), are they exploring the spread (faster technique? hidden edge case?) rather than averaging it away or deferring to seniority? |
| Generate retrospective candidates with positives/deltas/insights | If the decision is a process change, has the team gathered positives, deltas, and insights as candidates and grouped them, before voting on the top 3 to deep-dive? |
| Use 5-Whys until you're surprised | "If you're rarely surprised by your root causes, you may not be digging deep enough." Decision-quality signal: surprise indicates progress toward root cause. |

### Phase 4: Deciding (Analysing and selecting)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply the role-specific decision rule | Who decides? PO: Product Backlog ordering, Sprint cancellation, Backlog Refinement final ordering. Development Team: sizing, how-to-build, Daily Scrum format, when Sprint Backlog is full. Whole team: Sprint Goal, Definition of Done. Don't let the wrong role's voice tip the decision. |
| Stop adding items when the Development Team says so | At Sprint Planning Topic Two: "Stop adding items to your *Sprint Backlog* when the *Development Team*—not the *Product Owner* or *Scrum Master*—has concluded that they have the right amount of work for the *Sprint* timebox." |
| Finalise the Sprint Goal before Planning ends | "The Sprint Goal must be finalized prior to the end of Sprint Planning." A loose Sprint Goal entering the Sprint corrupts every in-Sprint decision that should anchor on it. |
| No firm commitments | The Development Team must not firmly commit to the entire Sprint Backlog or even the Sprint Goal. Decision form is *good-faith forecast*, not contract. Burnout and gaming follow from confusing the two. |
| Choose decisions vs actions in the retrospective | Is this improvement a *decision* (change the Definition of Done, Definition of Ready, or Working Agreement) or an *action* (a new Backlog Item)? Different mechanisms register different kinds of commitment. |
| Stay focused — one or two retro improvements | Pick the highest-impact improvement(s) for the next Sprint rather than committing to many. Too much change at once dilutes every experiment. |
| Apply the carry-over rule when over-committed | If WIP from last Sprint meets or exceeds forecast velocity, take in no new work and remove items as needed. Empirical, not wishful. |
| Pick the confidentiality rule explicitly | Vegas Rule or Chatham House Rule — the Scrum Team should explicitly choose for the retrospective, not leave it implicit. |

### Phase 5: Ratifying (Implementing the decision)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Update the right artefact | A Scrum decision is ratified by updating the relevant artefact: Sprint Backlog (scope, plan, Sprint Goal), Product Backlog (order, content, Product Goal), Definition of Done (team standard), Working Agreement (informal). Without an artefact update, the decision dissolves. |
| Capture retro improvements as Backlog Items | Improvement experiments are encoded as *Backlog Items* "to be attempted in the next *Sprint*" — not left as informal commitments. The artefact carries the commitment. |
| Communicate Sprint Goal to stakeholders | The Sprint Goal "communicates why the Sprint is valuable to stakeholders." Has the Product Owner relayed it in a way they can carry into the Sprint Review? |
| Set the Definition of Ready / Working Agreement updates | If the retrospective produced rule-changes (penalties for late arrival, changes to Definition of Done), are they captured in the Working Agreement / Definition of Done / Definition of Ready respectively? |
| Schedule the inspection point | Daily Scrum (today's plan vs Sprint Goal), Sprint Review (Sprint outcome vs Sprint Goal and Product Goal), Sprint Retrospective (process vs effectiveness). Decisions without an inspection point have no feedback loop. |
| Use the Parking Lot for deferred decisions | Decisions that can't fit in the Daily Scrum's 15-minute timebox go to a named Parking Lot, with specific participants invited to stay after — not dropped, not held in indefinite limbo. |

### Phase 6: Monitoring (Was the decision a good one?)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Daily inspection at the Daily Scrum | Are we tracking toward the Sprint Goal? Is the plan for the next 24 hours actionable? Are impediments being surfaced? Is the team working on items in the Sprint Backlog, or drifting to work outside it? |
| Renegotiate scope when conditions change | When work turns out different than expected, is the team renegotiating Sprint Backlog scope *with* the Product Owner *without* affecting the Sprint Goal? |
| Watch for mature-team delivery range | "A mature team should be able to deliver 85-115% of the planned work or Velocity every sprint. If a team consistently underdelivers, this usually means that they are not being truly empirical and they are taking on more work than their historical performance supports." Out-of-range is the trigger. |
| Inspect outcomes at the Sprint Review with stakeholders | Is the Sprint Review a working session, not a presentation? Has the Product Backlog been adjusted based on what stakeholders contributed? Are end users in the room? |
| Don't demonstrate undone work | If the Increment isn't Done, it doesn't get presented. Period. The cost of demonstrating undone work is trust erosion and a future of less-good decisions. |
| Run the meta-retrospective | At the end of the Retrospective: "How did it go for the team? What would they like to try next time?" The retro on the retro is the Scrum Team's process-of-the-process improvement. |
| Watch for "we skipped the retro" drift | Skipping events, watering down accountabilities, weakening the Definition of Done are signals of partial-implementation drift. "Implementing only parts of Scrum is possible, the result is not Scrum." |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| Team or stakeholders using "commitment" or "guarantee" language about the Sprint Backlog | The no-firm-commitment principle is being violated; burnout and gaming follow (Source: Sprint Planning, "Tips") | Name the firm-commitment failure mode explicitly; reframe the Sprint Backlog and Sprint Goal as a good-faith forecast and focusing tool |
| Daily Scrum has become a round-robin status report to the Product Owner or Scrum Master | The Daily Scrum has drifted into status-meeting mode, degrading its decision-quality (Source: Daily Scrum, "Tips") | Redirect to three questions focused on Sprint Goal progress; move PO to observer role; use Parking Lot for cross-role conversations |
| Sprint Review feels like a one-sided demo with no stakeholder input to the Product Backlog | The Sprint Review is functioning as a status meeting, not a working what-next decision (Source: Sprint Review, "Tips") | Re-structure the agenda to include stakeholder discussion segments; end with explicit Product Backlog adjustments |
| Estimation discussions always defer to the most senior developer without exploring divergence | The estimation-as-conversation principle is being bypassed (Source: Backlog Refinement, "Tips") | When a spread appears (1 vs 5), stop and explore it; the spread is the signal, not the numbers |
| Carry-over WIP equals or exceeds forecast velocity and new items are still being taken in | The carry-over rule is being ignored; the team is running on optimism rather than empirical process control (Source: Sprint Planning, "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda") | Apply the rule: zero new work; possibly remove items; communicate the honest position to the Product Owner |
| The retrospective produces five or more improvement items every Sprint | The one-or-two-improvements rule is being ignored; experiment scope exceeds the team's change capacity (Source: Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda") | Force a vote to the top one or two items; encode them as Backlog Items; defer the rest to future Sprints |
| 5-Whys analysis rarely surprises anyone | Root-cause investigation is stopping too early (Source: Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda") | Keep asking until the answer is unexpected; surprise is the signal that you have reached structural root cause |

## When to Use This Reference

- A Scrum event needs a working agenda — Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective, or Backlog Refinement — and the question is "what are the time-segments, decision-points, and named techniques?"
- Commitment-language drift needs naming: the team or organisation is treating the Sprint Backlog or Sprint Goal as a firm contract; the Field Guide explicitly frames against that.
- A Definition of Done boundary decision arises, including the question of whether to demonstrate work that isn't Done.
- A carry-over over-commitment situation: last Sprint's WIP is dominating forecast velocity; the rule is zero new work and possible removal.
- A retrospective design decision: how to structure 1½ hours into set-the-stage / gather-data / vote / deep-dive / wrap-up, including the Prime Directive framing and the confidentiality rule choice.
- A retrospective improvement size decision: how many improvements to commit to (answer: one or two), and how to split decisions (DoD/DoR/WA changes) from actions (Backlog Items).
- A Backlog Refinement design question: how often, who's required, what techniques (Vertical Slicing, Acceptance Criteria, Planning Poker, Affinity Estimating), and how the Product Owner handles final ordering.
- A capacity / velocity model decision: Capacity-adjusted average of last 3–4 sprints, with the worked formula and example as a sanity check.
- An outcome-vs-implementation Backlog Item drafting question: the coffee-temperature contrast as the template.
- A Parking Lot deferral: a Daily Scrum decision that would exceed the timebox without being lost.
- A deviation from textbook Scrum question: the practise-the-form-before-deviating posture, with humility and honest curiosity, as a well-formed experiment.
- Reach for the Scrum Guide distillation instead when the question is "what does Scrum formally require?" — the Field Guide illustrates textbook Scrum without defining it.

## Worked Example

A software team is running its fourth Sprint. At Sprint Planning, the Product Owner arrives with a backlog. The team calculates their Capacity-adjusted Velocity using the Field Guide's formula: 25, 24, and 31 Story Points over the last three Sprints with 45, 43, and 50 days of Capacity respectively, and a forecast Capacity of 47 days. This resolves to approximately 30 Story Points forecast. But the team also carries 28 Story Points of unfinished work from the previous Sprint.

The carry-over rule applies: 28 Story Points of WIP meets or nearly meets the 30-Story-Point forecast. Following the Field Guide's directive — "we strongly recommend against taking in any new work this Sprint" — the team and Product Owner agree to take in zero new items and consider removing two lower-priority items from the carry-over (Source: Sprint Planning, "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda"). This is "painful, but it's honest empirical process control."

At Sprint Planning Topic Two, the team identifies the Sprint Goal: deliver the authentication flow to a usable state. This goal can be met even if two of the eight carry-over items don't complete — the Goal and the Backlog are deliberately not coextensive (Source: Sprint Planning, "Outputs"). The Daily Scrum throughout the Sprint is 15 minutes; when a cross-team dependency surfaces on Day 4 that threatens the Sprint Goal, the team uses the Parking Lot mechanism to schedule a 30-minute post-standup session with the relevant members (Source: Daily Scrum, "Tips").

At the Sprint Review, the Product Owner announces which items are Done and which are not — and the team demonstrates only Done work, resisting the pressure to show a "nearly done" item that would have eroded stakeholder trust (Source: Sprint Review, "Tips"). The Sprint Retrospective runs 1½ hours; the team identifies nine topics, votes using the √9 ≈ 3 votes per person heuristic, and deep-dives on the top three. The output is two improvement items: one Backlog Item for a staging-environment reliability fix, and one change to the Working Agreement about the Definition of Done's code-review criterion (Source: Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda").

The source's case study is operator-authored here; all framework citations trace back through the deep reference.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Treating the Sprint Backlog or Sprint Goal as a firm commitment.** The Field Guide is explicit: "The Development Team cannot and must not make a firm commitment to delivering the entire Sprint Backlog or even the Sprint Goal." Burnout, gaming, and loss of trust follow from confusing a focusing tool with a contract. (Source: Sprint Planning, "Tips")
- **Running the Daily Scrum as a status report.** The Daily Scrum is for the Development Team's planning, not for upstream reporting. Status-meeting drift corrupts the decision quality of the event. (Source: Daily Scrum, "Tips")
- **Demonstrating undone work at the Sprint Review.** "Work in process is a liability, not an asset." Demonstrating below-the-DoD work makes the next decision worse and erodes trust. (Source: Sprint Review, "Tips")
- **Carrying over overloaded WIP and adding new items.** The carry-over rule is clear: if WIP meets or exceeds forecast velocity, take zero new work and consider removing items. Over-committing a Sprint that is already full is optimism dressed as planning. (Source: Sprint Planning, "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda")
- **Taking the average or senior-developer estimate instead of exploring the spread.** A 1-vs-5 vote spread is the signal; investigating it may surface a faster technique or a hidden edge case. Averaging it away forfeits the learning. (Source: Backlog Refinement, "Tips")
- **Committing to too many retrospective improvements.** One or two improvement items per Sprint is the directive; too much change at once dilutes every experiment. (Source: Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda")
- **Deviating from textbook Scrum without diligent practice first.** "Practice the form before deviating" is the martial-arts posture the guide encodes. Deviating early is not agility — it is skipping the learning cycle. (Source: Introduction)
- **Skipping the retrospective or shortening it below 1½ hours.** The guide is direct: less than 1½ hours for a 2-week Sprint "doesn't give the team enough time to truly dig into their tools, processes, structure, and culture." (Source: The Sprint, "Sample Sprint Agenda")

The two pair tightly: Scrum Guide for the rules, Field Guide for the facilitation depth (sample agendas, time-allocation heuristics, named techniques, Field-tested tips for each ceremony).
- **Letaw, *Handbook of Software Engineering Methods*** (Ch 1, "Agile"): Letaw populates the Scrum events with methods — Planning Poker, Story Points, Ideal Days, INVEST, RACI, Tuckman, the Project Priority Matrix, the Fist of Five voting method. The Field Guide names some of the same methods (Planning Poker, Fists of Five, Velocity, Story Points) at the operational level — when and how to use them inside which event segment. Use Letaw for the methods themselves; use the Field Guide for the per-event facilitation fit. The two operate at slightly different abstraction levels: Letaw is the methods textbook; the Field Guide is the operator's manual.
- **Jones, *Evidence-based Software Engineering*** (Ch 1 on Agile's evidence base): Jones reads Agile critically — the post-1980 evidence collapse means most software-engineering claims are folklore-laden. The Field Guide is unapologetically Agile-positive and operational ("'bare minimum' instruction required to successfully bootstrap"). Use Jones when the question is "what evidence supports the choice between Scrum and alternatives?", and the Field Guide when the team has already chosen Scrum and needs to run the events well.
- **OpenStax, *Organizational Behavior*** (Ch 13 on group decision-making, Ch 14 on conflict): provides general team-decision theory the Field Guide assumes. The retrospective's positives/deltas/insights structure, 5-Whys, and the decisions/actions split are operational devices that map onto OB's six-step decision process; the Field Guide is silent on the theory but explicit on the practice.
- **OpenStax, *Principles of Management*** (Ch 18 on control, Ch 1 on PDCA): the Plan-Do-Check-Act control cycle maps onto Scrum (Plan = Sprint Planning, Do = Sprint execution, Check = Sprint Review + Retrospective, Act = Product Backlog and team-practice adaptation). The Field Guide is one populated implementation of PDCA at the Scrum-event timescale.
- **NHS, *A Just Culture Guide*** and **US Forest Service, *Learning Review Guide* (2024)**: both are post-incident-learning resources. The Field Guide's Sprint Retrospective is the routine-Sprint-cadence version of the same posture — Norm Kerth's Retrospective Prime Directive ("everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time…") is the same blameless-by-default stance NHS and FS operationalise for major incidents. Use the Field Guide for the per-Sprint cadence; use NHS / FS for incident-scale reviews.
- **Approach Perfect linked resources** (`ap.tips/...`): the Field Guide cross-references its own companion videos and articles for Definition of Done, Definition of Ready, role pages, Story Points, the Velocity calculator, Burn-Down charts, and several named techniques. Operators should treat those as the author's own further-reading list; they're outside this corpus's bounded set.
