# OpenStax Principles of Finance, Decision-Making Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2022). *Principles of Finance*. Rice University. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/principles-finance. Senior contributing authors Julie Dahlquist and Rainford Knight, with six contributing authors.

## Decision-Making Relevance

OpenStax's *Principles of Finance* is a decision-making text from its opening line: "business finance is about developing and understanding the tools that help people make consistently good and repeatable decisions." Across 20 chapters, the book treats financial work as a structured decision toolkit organised around three primary domains — working capital management, capital budgeting, and capital structure — plus the supporting decisions in valuation, forecasting, and risk management.

The book is uniquely strong on decisions where money has a time dimension (capital budgeting, valuation, debt amortisation, retirement planning), where uncertainty must be priced (CAPM, bond pricing, options), and where competing options must be ranked under resource constraints (NPV vs IRR vs payback for project selection, PI ranking for capital budgeting under budget limits). It is less strong on decisions involving qualitative judgement, multi-party negotiation, or organisational dynamics — those are better served by the *Organizational Behavior* distillation.

This distillation consolidates the book's threads into a working pattern: how to recognise the decision shape; how to choose the right financial method; how to translate cash flows into present-value terms; how to integrate risk; how to compare alternatives; and how to reconcile the answer with operational and ethical reality.

## Key Concepts for Decision-Making

1. **The risk-return tradeoff.** Higher expected returns require investors to bear greater uncertainty. This principle organises bond pricing, CAPM, capital structure, and risk-management decisions. Practitioners should always pair return targets with risk assessment. (Source: OpenStax, *Principles of Finance*, Ch 1.1, "Risk and Return in Finance"; Ch 15)

2. **The time value of money (TVM).** A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow because money invested earns interest, future cash carries default risk, and consumers prefer present consumption. Compounding (forward) and discounting (backward) are inverses of the same operation. Single-sum, annuity, and mixed-stream applications all reduce to applying the TVM toolkit. (Source: Ch 7, "Why It Matters"; Ch 7.2)

3. **Cash flow versus accounting earnings.** Earnings are calculated under accrual accounting and can be manipulated through estimates and timing; cash flow is the actual liquid resource that determines viability. The text takes a position that "cash flow is the axis upon which the world of business spins" (Simmons interview). Decisions should be evaluated against projected cash flows, not just earnings. (Source: Ch 4.5, "Profit versus Cash Flow"; Ch 5.5; Ch 9.1)

4. **The five capital-budgeting methods and when to use each.** Payback (simple but flawed), discounted payback (better but limited), NPV (theoretically correct, accept if positive), IRR (intuitive but mathematically problematic for some cash-flow patterns and for ranking projects of different scales), MIRR (resolves IRR's reinvestment-rate flaw but not the scale problem), and PI (ranks projects under capital constraints, favouring those with the most NPV per dollar invested). (Source: Ch 16)

5. **For mutually exclusive projects, NPV beats IRR.** The text takes the position that the highest-NPV project should be chosen, not the highest-IRR. IRR ignores scale; a 30 per cent return on $1,000 may be inferior to a 12 per cent return on $1,000,000. (Source: Ch 16.5, "Choosing between Mutually Exclusive Projects")

6. **The CAPM expected return formula.** Re = Rf + β(Rm - Rf). Investors are rewarded for systematic risk only because firm-specific risk can be diversified away. Roughly 12 stocks suffice for a reasonably diversified portfolio. The CAPM is used both for stock-return expectations and for estimating the cost of equity in WACC. (Source: Ch 15.3, "CAPM Equation"; Ch 17.2)

7. **Weighted average cost of capital (WACC).** WACC = D% × Rd × (1-T) + P% × Rp + E% × Re. Weights are calculated from market values, not book values. The interest tax shield (Rd × D × T) lowers WACC and encourages debt use, but financial distress costs grow with leverage. Trade-off theory weighs these. (Source: Ch 17.3, "Calculating the WACC"; Ch 17.4)

8. **Three forms of risk.** Default risk, inflation risk, diversifiable risk, non-diversifiable risk, and political risk for general investments; transaction, translation, and economic exposures for currency. Bond risks add credit, liquidity, duration, call, reinvestment, and term. Practitioners should classify the type of risk before selecting a hedge. (Source: Ch 1.1, "Risk and Return in Finance"; Ch 10.4; Ch 20.3)

9. **The hedging-versus-speculating distinction.** Hedging reduces exposure to risk; speculating bets on a direction for profit. Many tools (futures, options, swaps) can be used either way. The financial manager's role is to hedge — to use these tools as risk-management rather than profit-seeking instruments. (Source: Ch 20.1, "Risk and Firm Value")

10. **The DuPont method for ROE diagnosis.** ROE = profit margin × asset turnover × equity multiplier. Decomposing ROE into its three components attributes changes in returns to operational efficiency, asset usage, or leverage — preventing managers from confusing the levers and applying the wrong fix. (Source: Ch 6.6, "The DuPont Method")

11. **The cash conversion cycle.** Operating cycle (inventory acquisition to sale) plus receivables collection minus payables deferral. Shortening the cycle frees cash; lengthening strains liquidity. Working capital management is decision-rich on the AR (credit policy), inventory (reorder points), and AP (payment terms) sides. (Source: Ch 19.1, "The Cash Cycle")

12. **The trade-credit trap.** Forgoing a 2/10 net 30 quick-payment discount costs 36.73 per cent annualised — far higher than most short-term financing rates. Practitioners should compute the implicit cost of trade credit before treating it as "free" financing. (Source: Ch 19.2, "Cost of Trade Credit")

## Questions to Ask During Decision-Making

### Phase 1: Framing (recognising the decision shape)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify the decision domain | Is this a working-capital decision (short-term cash flows), a capital-budgeting decision (long-term project), or a capital-structure decision (financing mix)? Each has different methods and different time horizons. |
| Test for time-value relevance | Do the cash flows occur at different points in time? If yes, TVM tools must be applied; comparing nominal future amounts to nominal present amounts will mislead. |
| Test for risk relevance | Are the cash flows certain or uncertain? If uncertain, what type of risk (default, market, political, currency)? Match the hedge to the risk type. |
| Identify the appropriate cash flow | Are we evaluating earnings or cash flow? For valuation and capital budgeting, cash flow is the load-bearing measure; earnings can be manipulated through estimates and accruals. |
| Identify mutual exclusivity | Is this a single project (yes/no decision) or a choice among competing projects (rank-and-select)? Different methods are appropriate for each. |
| Test for sub-annual compounding | Are interest, discount, or growth rates being compared on the same basis (annual, semiannual, monthly, daily)? Effective rate must equalise frequency. |

### Phase 2: Bounding (defining the cash flow stream)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Project the cash flows | What is the timing and magnitude of each cash inflow and outflow? For uncertain cash flows, what are the most likely values, and what is the range of plausible alternatives? |
| Choose the discount rate | What is the firm's cost of capital? If the project has different risk than the firm's average, should we adjust the discount rate? Use WACC as the default; adjust upward for higher risk. |
| Identify mutual exclusivity | Are there competing projects with different lives? If yes, replacement chain or equal annuity adjustment is needed. |
| Identify resource constraints | Are there capital constraints? If yes, profitability index ranking is the appropriate selection criterion. |
| Surface ethical implications | Are there stakeholders without voice (employees, communities, future generations) whose interests should weigh in even though they don't appear in cash flow projections? |

### Phase 3: Exploring (generating alternatives)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Generate enough alternatives | What is the no-action baseline? What variants exist (different scale, different timing, different vendors, different financing mix)? |
| Test for capital-structure alternatives | Could this project be financed at the firm's existing capital structure, or does it warrant separate financing? Would project debt or new equity issuance change the project's NPV? |
| Test for hedging alternatives | If the project carries commodity, currency, or interest-rate risk, can hedging reduce that risk and how does it affect NPV? |
| Test for option-like flexibility | Does the project carry real options — staged investment, abandonment, expansion? These are not captured in straight NPV but can be valuable. |
| Consider creative recombinations | Are there alternatives that mix features (a smaller initial scale, a longer horizon with optionality, a vertical integration play)? |

### Phase 4: Deciding (computing and selecting)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Compute NPV | What is the present value of the cash flow stream at the chosen discount rate? Is NPV positive (accept) or negative (reject)? |
| Compute IRR | What is the internal rate of return? How does it compare to the cost of capital? |
| Reconcile NPV and IRR conflicts | If NPV and IRR rankings differ across mutually exclusive projects, NPV is theoretically correct. Why are they conflicting (typically scale or timing differences)? |
| Run sensitivity analysis | What happens to NPV if the cost of capital is +1%? +2%? -1%? If revenues are 10% lower? What's the breakeven discount rate (the IRR)? |
| Run scenario analysis | What does NPV look like under recession, base case, and expansion scenarios? Is the project robust across plausible futures? |
| Apply ethical and stakeholder filters | Beyond the NPV rule, does this decision pass the news-test (would it look acceptable on the front page tomorrow)? Are there stakeholder considerations (Ch 2) that the cash flow projections do not capture? |

### Phase 5: Ratifying (committing and communicating)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Document the decision criterion used | Did we use NPV? IRR? Payback? PI? Why this criterion in this context? |
| Document the inputs and assumptions | Discount rate, cash flow projections, growth rate, terminal value. What sensitivities were tested? |
| Communicate clearly | Have we explained the reasoning, not just the outcome? |
| Plan implementation | What capital expenditure schedule? What financing arrangement? What hedges to put in place? |

### Phase 6: Monitoring (post-decision)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Compare actual to projected cash flows | At each milestone, are actual cash flows tracking projections? Where are the deviations and what do they tell us? |
| Distinguish bad luck from bad decision | A negative outcome may reflect uncertainty, not poor judgement; a positive outcome may reflect luck rather than skill. Evaluate the process separately from the result. |
| Reassess against new information | If the cost of capital has changed, if the market has shifted, or if a competitor has acted, does the project remain accretive? Is course correction warranted? |
| Recognise abandonment optionality | If the project is no longer adding value, what is the cost of abandonment relative to continuation? Sunk costs are sunk. |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: A capital-budgeting decision is being made on payback period alone.** Signal: managers are choosing the project that breaks even soonest. Diagnosis (in source vocabulary): payback ignores time value of money, has no theoretically grounded threshold, ignores cash flows after breakeven, and makes no risk adjustment. Follow-up: compute NPV (and IRR) to validate the decision; if payback is acceptable but NPV is negative, the decision is wrong.

- **Pattern: A choice between mutually exclusive projects is being made on IRR.** Signal: managers are choosing the project with the highest IRR even though it is much smaller in scale. Diagnosis: IRR ignores scale; the higher-IRR small project may have lower NPV than the larger lower-IRR project. Follow-up: rank by NPV and override IRR-based intuition.

- **Pattern: The discount rate is being applied uniformly across projects of different risk.** Signal: a high-risk venture is being discounted at the firm's average WACC. Diagnosis: cost of capital should be project-specific; high-risk projects warrant higher discount rates. Follow-up: adjust the discount rate to reflect project-specific systematic risk.

- **Pattern: A long-term project is being evaluated using stated rather than effective interest rates.** Signal: 6 per cent annual stated, paid semiannually, is being compared to 6 per cent annual stated, compounded daily. Diagnosis: effective rates differ; comparison must be on the same compounding basis. Follow-up: convert all rates to effective annual rates before comparing.

- **Pattern: A profitable project produces a cash flow gap.** Signal: forecasted income statement looks healthy but the cash flow forecast shows months when cash falls below the firm's minimum threshold. Diagnosis: profit and cash flow are not the same; growth in receivables and inventory may consume cash even as profits rise. Follow-up: build a cash forecast, identify gaps, plan short-term financing or accelerate collections.

- **Pattern: Trade credit is being treated as "free" financing.** Signal: a firm is paying suppliers on net 30 even when 2/10 discounts are available. Diagnosis: forgoing the discount costs 36.73 per cent annualised — vastly more than most short-term financing. Follow-up: compute the implicit cost; take the discount unless the cost of immediate cash exceeds the discount.

- **Pattern: A decision is being defended after evidence has shifted.** Signal: a project's actual cash flows are well below projections but the firm continues to invest at the projected pace. Diagnosis: escalation of commitment; sunk costs are sunk. Follow-up: ask "what would I do if I were starting fresh today?" If the answer differs from the current path, course-correct.

- **Pattern: An IRR has multiple solutions.** Signal: the cash flow stream has more than one sign change (negative-positive-negative or worse). Diagnosis: multiple IRRs may exist mathematically; IRR is not well-defined as a single number. Follow-up: use NPV instead, or use MIRR if a single rate is needed.

- **Pattern: An option is being exercised because the strike is favourable, ignoring premium.** Signal: holder exercises a call option because the strike is below spot, but does not factor in the premium paid. Diagnosis: payoff = spot − strike − premium for calls, with similar logic for puts; favourable strike does not always mean profitable exercise. Follow-up: compute net payoff including premium before exercising.

## When to Use This Reference

- A capital-budgeting decision is non-trivial and warrants systematic NPV-based analysis.
- A decision involves cash flows over multiple time periods and TVM tools must be applied.
- A choice exists among competing projects with different scales, lives, or risk profiles.
- A firm is evaluating its capital structure or considering issuing new debt or equity.
- A firm faces commodity price, currency, or interest rate risk and is considering hedging.
- A firm is forecasting future financial performance and must reconcile income, balance, and cash flow projections.
- A firm is making a stock or bond valuation decision (DDM, DCF, P/E ratio, bond YTM).
- A firm is making a working-capital management decision (credit terms, inventory levels, payment timing).

## Worked Example

A regional retailer is considering replacing its existing point-of-sale (POS) system. The IT director believes the new system will reduce checkout times by 30 per cent, decrease shrinkage from inventory mismatches, and enable more sophisticated customer analytics. The investment is $850,000 up front. The IT director estimates annual cash benefits of $180,000 for years 1-3, $220,000 for years 4-7, and $260,000 for years 8-10. After year 10, the system will need replacement. The CFO requires a financial analysis before signing off.

**Framing**: This is a capital-budgeting decision. The cash flows are spread over 10 years, so TVM matters. The cash benefits are estimates with substantial uncertainty (productivity gains, shrinkage reduction, analytics value), and the upfront investment is certain.

**Bounding**: The firm's WACC is 9 per cent. The project carries some additional risk because the analytics-driven benefits are speculative; the CFO decides to apply a 12 per cent discount rate to reflect project-specific risk.

**Exploring**: Three alternatives are generated. (A) Replace the system as proposed. (B) Defer two years and replace then, when prices may be lower and the firm has more data. (C) Upgrade the existing system rather than full replacement, at lower upfront cost ($350,000) but with smaller benefits.

**Deciding**: For Alternative A, NPV at 12 per cent is approximately $383,000 (positive, accept on standalone basis); IRR is roughly 21 per cent. For Alternative B (deferred replacement), NPV is somewhat lower because two years of forgone benefits are not recovered; about $280,000. For Alternative C (upgrade), NPV is approximately $190,000 with IRR ~22 per cent.

By NPV, A wins (highest absolute value). By IRR alone, C is roughly tied with A despite being a smaller project. The CFO chooses A because it adds the most value, but pauses to reconsider:

- *Sensitivity*: if the productivity-gain estimate is 50 per cent of estimate, NPV falls to $40,000 — still positive but marginal. If 25 per cent of estimate, NPV becomes negative. The CFO asks the IT director to provide more rigorous benefit projections.
- *Scenario*: in a recession scenario, the productivity gains might be lower (less throughput because foot traffic is lower); the CFO models this and finds Alternative A still positive.
- *Real option*: the deferred-replacement option (B) has an embedded value because waiting reveals more information; this is not captured in straight NPV. The CFO weighs the option value qualitatively.

**Ratifying**: A is selected. Procurement begins; capital expenditure budget is approved.

**Monitoring**: At year 1, actual benefits are tracking 80 per cent of projection (lower than expected); at year 2, 95 per cent (better, as the team adapts). Cumulatively, the project remains net-positive but with less margin than projected. The CFO maintains the decision, recognising that a bad outcome doesn't necessarily mean a bad decision; the analysis at the time of decision was sound.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Choosing projects on payback period alone.** Quick break-even feels intuitive but ignores time value of money and post-breakeven cash flows.
- **Ranking mutually exclusive projects on IRR.** IRR ignores scale; a high-IRR small project may have lower NPV than a lower-IRR large project.
- **Comparing earnings to cash flow as if they were the same.** Profit can rise while cash flow falls (and vice versa); decisions should be made on cash flows.
- **Treating trade credit as free financing.** Forgoing quick-payment discounts costs more than most alternative short-term financing rates.
- **Comparing rates without normalising compounding frequency.** Stated annual rates with different compounding periods are not comparable.
- **Defending a sunk-cost decision.** Escalation of commitment leads to throwing good money after bad.
- **Making bond decisions without understanding the price-yield inverse.** Bond prices fall as rates rise; existing bonds are revalued daily.
- **Hedging when speculation is the goal (or vice versa).** Many derivatives can be used either way; misalignment between intent and tool produces unintended exposure.

| Bounded rationality (Simon) | Finance methods presume substantial computational capacity; in practice, managers may simplify in ways that produce systematic error (e.g., using payback because it's easy). Awareness of bounded rationality argues for tooling that lowers the cost of doing NPV correctly. |
| Cost-volume-profit analysis | Used in operations and managerial accounting to identify the breakeven volume; complements TVM-based analysis when the question is "at what volume is this project breakeven?" |

The Finance book mentions option-like features (mortgage prepayment, convertible bonds, callable bonds) but does not develop real options for capital budgeting; this is a known extension. |

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The deep reference contains the following `[BT]` citations — authors and frameworks cited by the source but not held as primary references in this corpus:

- *Modigliani and Miller, MM Proposition II* — in a perfect market with no taxes, capital structure does not affect firm value; with corporate taxes, the interest tax shield favours debt (Ch 17.4) [BT]. Key Concept 7 references WACC and trade-off theory, which are framed relative to MM.
- *Irving Fisher, Fisher effect* — nominal vs real interest rates: (1 + i) = (1 + R)(1 + h) (Ch 7.4) [BT].
- *DuPont method* — developed at DuPont in 1919; ROE decomposition into profit margin × asset turnover × equity multiplier (Ch 6.6) [BT]. Key Concept 10 traces here.
- *Meir Statman* — research establishing that ~12 stocks produce a reasonably diversified portfolio (Ch 15.2) [BT]. Key Concept 6 references this threshold.
- *Benjamin Graham and Warren Buffett* — value investing methodology; Buffett's dividend-policy view as "almost a last resort" (Ch 12.1, Ch 12.4) [BT].
- *Burton Malkiel* — cited for the empirical finding that IPOs underperform the market by ~4 per cent per year on average (Ch 12.1) [BT].
- *Gordon growth model* — constant-growth dividend discount model: P = D₁ / (r - g) (Ch 11.2) [BT].
- *Karl Pearson* — developer of the correlation coefficient (Ch 14.1) [BT].
- *Adam Smith and Karl Marx* — referenced as classical economists identifying capital as a factor of production (Ch 19.1) [BT].

**Named limits of the source.** The book is an introductory finance text; it develops the core quantitative toolkit (TVM, NPV/IRR, CAPM, WACC) with solid rigour but does not cover advanced topics: real options theory for capital budgeting, credit derivatives, securitisation mechanics, or private-equity valuation. Qualitative and behavioural dimensions of financial decision-making (cognitive biases in capital allocation, overconfidence in forecasts) are acknowledged but not developed; those gaps are better served by the Organizational Behavior distillation.

**Evidence-marker continuity.** This distillation paraphrases throughout; verbatim passages live in the deep reference (`openstax-principles-finance-deep.md`). Key Concepts are `[AP]` (the source's stated positions on method and principle) or `[AR]` (the source's reasoning, e.g., why NPV beats IRR for mutually exclusive projects). The DuPont method, MM Proposition II, Fisher effect, and Gordon growth model are `[BT]` — the source presents them as established frameworks without claiming to originate them.
