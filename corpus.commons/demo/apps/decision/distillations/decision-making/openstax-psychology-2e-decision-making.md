# OpenStax Psychology 2e, Decision-Making Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2020). *Psychology 2e*. Rice University. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/psychology-2e. Senior contributing authors Rose M. Spielman, William J. Jenkins, Marilyn D. Lovett; 10 contributing authors.

## Decision-Making Relevance

OpenStax's *Psychology 2e* is an introductory survey, not a decision-making manual; nonetheless several of its substantive chapters provide load-bearing material for everyday decision-making. The richest contribution is Chapter 7 (Thinking and Intelligence), which catalogues the heuristic shortcuts and systematic biases — anchoring, confirmation, hindsight, representativeness, availability — alongside mental set and functional fixedness. Chapter 8 (Memory) shows that the "evidence" decision-makers retrieve is itself reconstructive and bias-prone (Schacter's seven sins; the misinformation effect; eyewitness misidentification). Chapter 12 (Social Psychology) supplies the situational corrective: the fundamental attribution error, the actor-observer asymmetry, the self-serving bias, conformity (Asch), obedience (Milgram), groupthink (Janis), group polarisation, and the implicit-bias vocabulary. Chapter 10 (Motivation and Emotion) supplies the affect-and-emotion frame, including arousal effects on judgement (Yerkes-Dodson) and the cognitive labelling that determines what an arousal state will be experienced as. Chapter 6 (Learning) explains why we keep deciding the way we have decided before — reinforcement schedules and observational learning shape decision habits — and Chapter 13 (Industrial-Organisational) carries the work-context discussion of structured versus unstructured judgement (interviewing; performance evaluation; the team halo effect).

This distillation draws on this material — psychology 2e's *cognitive*, *social*, *motivational*, and *organisational* content — to support decision-making practice. Therapy and clinical content from Chapters 15 and 16 is excluded from this guide; the framework here is for organisational and personal decision-making, not for diagnosing or treating psychological disorders.

The book's central decision-relevant insight is dual: cognition is constructive (perception, judgement, memory all operate by reconstruction and inference, not by faithful recording), and the situation often shapes behaviour more than dispositional accounts predict. Together these warrant systematic humility about confidence in one's own judgement and explicit attention to situational variables others may be facing.

## Verbatim anchors from the source

The source's bias inventory — the load-bearing list for any decision-making humility check — is defined verbatim in the text:

- *Anchoring bias*: "tendency to focus on one particular piece of information when making decisions or problem-solving" [V] (OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 7.3, Table 7.3).
- *Confirmation bias*: "focuses on information that confirms existing beliefs" [V] (Ch 7.3, Table 7.3).
- *Hindsight bias*: "belief that the event just experienced was predictable" [V] (Ch 7.3, Table 7.3).
- *Availability heuristic*: "decision is based upon either an available precedent or an example that may be faulty" [V] (Ch 7.3, Table 7.3).

Heuristics — the shortcut mode that defaults under load — "are not always the best method for making a rational decision" [V] (Ch 7.3, "Problem-Solving Strategies").

Self-efficacy — the precondition for any decision to attempt difficult work — is defined as "an individual's belief in their own capability to complete a task" [V] (Ch 10.1, "Self-efficacy and Social Motives").

## Key Concepts for Decision-Making

1.  **Heuristics versus algorithms.** Algorithms produce the same result every time; heuristics are mental shortcuts that save time without guaranteeing optimality. Five conditions invoke heuristics: information overload, time pressure, low-stakes decisions, limited information, and a heuristic readily coming to mind. Recognising which mode you are in is itself a decision. (Source: OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 7.3, "Problem-Solving Strategies")

2.  **The five judgement biases.** Anchoring (over-weighting the first information encountered), confirmation (favouring information that supports existing beliefs), hindsight (treating past events as obvious in retrospect), representativeness (judging by perceived similarity to a stereotype), and availability (judging frequency or probability by how readily examples come to mind). (Source: Ch 7.3, Table 7.3)

3.  **Mental set and functional fixedness.** Mental set is persisting with a previously successful approach that no longer fits the current problem. Functional fixedness is the inability to perceive an object or strategy as usable for something other than its designed purpose. Both reduce the option space available to a decision-maker. (Source: Ch 7.3, "Pitfalls to Problem Solving")

4.  **Reconstructive memory.** Memory is not a faithful recording but a reconstruction, vulnerable to Schacter's seven sins (transience, absentmindedness, blocking, misattribution, suggestibility, bias, persistence). The misinformation effect demonstrates how leading questions alter eyewitness memory. Decisions made by recalling "what happened" are decisions made on partly reconstructed evidence. (Source: Ch 8.3, "The Misinformation Effect")

5.  **Fundamental attribution error.** Observers tend to overweight dispositional explanations and underweight situational ones when interpreting others' behaviour. The error is more pronounced in individualistic than collectivistic cultures. The implication for decision-making about people is that "they did it because of who they are" is often a more confident attribution than the evidence warrants. (Source: Ch 12.1, "Fundamental Attribution Error")

6.  **Actor-observer asymmetry and self-serving bias.** People attribute others' behaviour to disposition and their own to situation; they attribute their own successes to disposition and failures to situation. This pair distorts both performance reviews and self-assessments. (Source: Ch 12.1)

7.  **Asch conformity and Milgram obedience.** Group pressure shifts judgement even on unambiguous tasks (76% of Asch's participants conformed at least once); authority pressure shifts behaviour even against moral judgement (65% of Milgram's participants administered maximum-voltage shocks). The presence of even one dissenter dramatically reduces conformity. The implication: solo dissent is rare and costly; structuring for dissent is a design choice. (Source: Ch 12.4)

8.  **Groupthink and group polarisation.** Groups under cohesion pressure suppress critical thinking (groupthink); discussion in already-leaning groups strengthens that lean (polarisation). Groupthink symptoms include perceived invulnerability, moral certainty, self-censorship, illusion of unanimity, and out-group stereotyping. Counters: external opinions, private voting, leaders withholding initial positions, devil's advocate roles. (Source: Ch 12.4)

9.  **Social loafing and diffusion of responsibility.** Effort declines when individual contributions are pooled; smaller groups, individual evaluation, and challenging tasks counter this. The bystander effect (diffusion of responsibility) is the same mechanism applied to helping decisions. (Source: Ch 12.4 and Ch 12.6)

10.  **Yerkes-Dodson arousal-performance curve.** Optimal arousal varies by task complexity: simple tasks favour higher arousal, complex tasks favour lower. Strong emotion narrows attention and pushes processing toward heuristic, type-1 modes. High-stakes decisions made under high arousal often warrant deferral until arousal drops to a level matched to the task's complexity. (Source: Ch 10.1, "Arousal Theory")

11.  **Schachter-Singer two-factor emotion.** Physiological arousal is interpreted in context to produce emotion; the cognitive label is essential. Decision-makers who notice arousal can re-label it (e.g., from anxiety to anticipation) and partly shape its effect. (Source: Ch 10.4)

12.  **Implicit bias.** The dual attitudes model distinguishes explicit from implicit attitudes; implicit bias can operate even where explicit bias is absent. Decisions about people (hiring, promotion, evaluation) are vulnerable to implicit bias even when decision-makers are sincere about their conscious values. (Source: Ch 12.5)

13.  **Self-efficacy.** Bandura's "individual's belief in their own capability to complete a task" predicts goal-setting and persistence. Self-efficacy is shaped by past performance, vicarious experience, social persuasion, and physiological state. Decisions about whether to attempt difficult work are partly decisions about whether the actor believes they can succeed. (Source: Ch 10.1; Ch 11.4)

14.  **Locus of control.** Rotter's internal versus external orientation: internals attribute outcomes to their own actions; externals attribute outcomes to forces outside themselves. The implication for decision-making is that the same evidence may produce different responses depending on the actor's attributional habit. (Source: Ch 11.4)

15.  **Reinforcement schedules and decision habits.** Variable-ratio schedules produce the most extinction-resistant behaviour (the gambling pattern); operant conditioning shapes the decisions we keep making. Recognising one's own variable-ratio reinforcement is a step toward changing entrenched decision habits. (Source: Ch 6.3, "Reinforcement Schedules")

## Questions to Ask During Decision-Making

### Phase 1: Framing (Recognising the decision and gathering context)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Detect heuristic versus systematic mode | Am I in a situation where heuristics are appropriate (low stakes, time pressure, recurring) or where systematic processing is warranted (novel, ambiguous, high stakes)? |
| Detect arousal effects | What is my arousal level, and is it matched to the complexity of the decision? Should I defer until arousal drops? |
| Detect emotional labelling | What am I labelling the arousal as — fear, anger, urgency, anticipation? Could I label it differently with the same physiological state? |
| Detect emotional bypass | Is the decision being made through a thalamus-amygdala bypass (immediate, fear-driven) rather than through reflection? Can I create space for cortical interpretation? |
| Surface the situational frame | What features of the situation am I attending to, and what am I missing? Is my attention itself selective? |

### Phase 2: Bounding (Defining the decision and its constraints)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Define the actual decision | What am I deciding, and on what evidence? What is the question and what is the noise around it? |
| Recognise reconstructive evidence | The "facts" I am working from are partly reconstructed memory and selective perception. Where would my evidence differ if I had a fresh, faithful record? |
| Examine social context | Is a group, audience, or authority shaping what I think I should decide? Would I decide differently in privacy? |
| Examine cultural context | Am I applying culturally specific assumptions (about hierarchy, dispositional attribution, individualism) without naming them? |

### Phase 3: Exploring (Generating alternatives)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Counter mental set | Have I been pursuing a previously successful approach that may not fit this problem? What alternative approach would I consider if this were my first attempt? |
| Counter functional fixedness | Are there resources, tools, or strategies I am not considering because of how I usually use them? |
| Counter availability | Am I generating options based on what comes to mind first? Have I deliberately searched for low-availability options? |
| Counter representativeness | Am I judging an option as good or bad because it resembles a familiar pattern, rather than on its own merits? |
| Counter confirmation | What evidence would change my preferred view, and have I actively sought it? Am I reading the case against my preferred option as carefully as the case for it? |
| Counter anchoring | What was the first number, framing, or reference point I encountered? Would I generate the same options if I started from a different anchor? |

### Phase 4: Deciding (Analysing and selecting)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Assess group dynamics | If I am in a group, are dissenting views being heard? Is anyone playing devil's advocate? Has consensus formed too quickly? Are some members self-censoring? |
| Assess attribution about people | Am I explaining a person's behaviour dispositionally when situational variables would account for it? What is their situation actually? |
| Examine self-serving framing | If this decision goes well, will I attribute the success to my judgement? If it goes poorly, will I attribute the failure to circumstances? Is my framing symmetric? |
| Examine implicit bias | Am I judging a person or option more harshly than my conscious values would predict, in ways that pattern with group membership? |
| Test for hindsight | Am I reasoning about a past event as though its outcome was obvious in advance? Would the decision-maker at the time have had access to my retrospective frame? |

### Phase 5: Implementing (Acting on the decision)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Communicate clearly | Have I explained the reasoning, not just the outcome, to those affected? Am I building shared understanding? |
| Address conformity pressure | Will dissenting voices feel safe to speak as the implementation unfolds? Have I structured for psychological safety? |
| Plan for revision | What evidence would prompt me to revise, and have I committed in advance to act on it? |

### Phase 6: Reviewing (Evaluating effectiveness)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Distinguish process from outcome | A bad outcome may follow a sound process under high uncertainty; a good outcome may follow a poor process. Which case is this? |
| Counter hindsight in learning | Am I treating the outcome as more predictable than it was? What did I actually know at the time? |
| Counter confirmation in retrospection | Am I crafting a retrospective narrative that confirms my prior beliefs? What account would a sceptic offer? |
| Capture the bias signature | What biases were operating in this decision, and how can I recognise them earlier next time? Bias awareness compounds. |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: Strong confidence in a complex judgement made quickly.** Signal: rapid, certain conclusions on novel matters. Diagnosis (in source vocabulary): heuristic processing where systematic processing is warranted; possibly availability or representativeness bias. Follow-up: slow down; require explicit reasoning; identify what evidence is being ignored.
- **Pattern: Disagreement is dismissed before it is examined.** Signal: a dissent is reframed as the dissenter's flaw rather than engaged on the merits. Diagnosis: groupthink; possibly fundamental attribution error applied to the dissenter. Follow-up: separate the merit of the argument from the personality of the arguer; structure for explicit consideration.
- **Pattern: A decision is being defended by reciting the reasons the decision-maker had in mind, without engaging the new evidence.** Signal: arguments cycle back to original framing. Diagnosis: confirmation bias plus possibly mental set. Follow-up: ask what evidence would change the decision; if no evidence would, the decision is not being made on evidence.
- **Pattern: The decision-maker explains other people's behaviour dispositionally and their own situationally.** Signal: "they're like that; I had no choice." Diagnosis: actor-observer bias. Follow-up: invert the framing as a check; what situational factors might explain their behaviour, and what dispositional factors might explain mine?
- **Pattern: A retrospective narrative makes the current outcome look inevitable.** Signal: "of course this would happen." Diagnosis: hindsight bias. Follow-up: ask what would have looked plausible from inside the prior moment; was the outcome actually predictable on the evidence available?
- **Pattern: Group consensus emerges too smoothly.** Signal: no dissent voiced; shared certainty. Diagnosis: groupthink or pluralistic ignorance. Follow-up: ask each member privately for their view; assign devil's advocate roles; revisit with explicit invitation to challenge.
- **Pattern: Decision quality declines as arousal rises.** Signal: under pressure, the decision-maker reverts to prior habits or snap judgements. Diagnosis: Yerkes-Dodson — arousal exceeds optimal for the task complexity. Follow-up: structurally lower arousal (delay, change context, sleep); reduce task complexity at decision point; pre-commit to specific responses for predictable high-arousal scenarios.
- **Pattern: The same kind of decision keeps being made the same way despite mixed outcomes.** Signal: no change in approach across repeated cases. Diagnosis: variable-ratio reinforcement (occasional success sustains the habit); possibly mental set. Follow-up: track outcomes systematically rather than by impression; vary approach deliberately to gather comparison data.
- **Pattern: Hiring or evaluation decisions pattern with group membership independent of merit.** Signal: outcomes correlate with demographic variables in ways that defy the conscious values stated. Diagnosis: implicit bias; the dual attitudes model. Follow-up: structured interviews; standardised criteria; blind evaluation where possible; counter-stereotypic exemplar exposure.

## When to Use This Reference

- A decision-maker wants to surface the cognitive biases likely to be operating in a high-stakes choice.
- A team is making a decision about people (hiring, promotion, evaluation) and wants to structure against attribution errors and implicit bias.
- A group is forming consensus and the convener wants to test for groupthink.
- A retrospective is being held and the team wants to learn from outcomes without falling into hindsight bias.
- A decision is being made under high arousal and the actor wants to assess whether arousal is appropriate to task complexity.
- A recurring decision pattern is producing mixed results and the actor wants to step back from the variable-ratio reinforcement loop.

## Worked Example

A board chair must decide whether to renew a CEO contract after a year of mixed results. The financials are slightly down on the prior year; the CEO has driven a strategic pivot whose payoff is multi-year; the board members hold sharply different views.

Framing: arousal is high — the chair is anxious about being seen to defend an underperforming CEO. The decision is not low stakes. The chair notes that her arousal is pushing toward a heuristic ("the numbers say no"), and decides to slow down before convening the board.

Bounding: the decision is not "is this CEO good"; it is "is the strategic pivot more likely to deliver in the next twelve months than the alternative of replacing the CEO." Reconstructive evidence is a concern: the chair's memory of the pivot rationale a year ago has likely been reshaped by intervening evidence. She retrieves the original board paper to anchor against the actual prior reasoning rather than her current reconstruction.

Exploring: the chair notes that two alternatives (renew, replace) are both available because they came to mind first (availability). She forces herself to generate three more (renew with modified scope; renew with explicit milestones; transition to interim CEO while the board re-runs the strategy review). She solicits views from board members individually before any group meeting.

Deciding: in the individual conversations, the chair notices that two board members are explaining the underperformance dispositionally ("the CEO doesn't have what it takes") while attributing the prior year's success to circumstances. She flags this attribution asymmetry to the board explicitly when convening. She also notices a pattern in which the same two members keep referring to a single high-availability example (a comparable company that pivoted and failed) and weighting it heavily.

When the board meets, the chair structures the conversation to surface dissent: each member writes their preferred option privately before discussion; a designated devil's advocate argues against the apparent consensus. The chair declines to share her own preference until others have shared theirs, to avoid anchoring.

Implementing: the board lands on renewal with explicit twelve-month milestones and a quarterly check. The chair commits in advance to act on the milestone evidence at twelve months regardless of how she feels about it then — a precommitment against escalation of commitment and confirmation bias.

Reviewing: at twelve months, the chair runs a structured review, asking what was actually known at decision time versus what is known now. She notes that the eventual outcome was not as inevitable as the retrospective narrative makes it sound, and captures the lesson that the original anchoring on "the numbers say no" would have produced a different, plausibly worse, decision.

This worked example illustrates several key concepts in the source: heuristic-versus-systematic processing, availability and anchoring biases, fundamental attribution error and actor-observer asymmetry applied to performance evaluation, group structuring against premature consensus, and hindsight management in retrospective review.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Snap judgement on high-stakes novel problems.** Heuristic processing where systematic processing is warranted; the cost is poor outcome and inability to learn.
- **Dispositional explanation of others' behaviour as default.** Attribution error makes "they're like that" feel definitive; situational variables remain unexamined.
- **Self-serving narratives about past decisions.** Successes attributed to judgement, failures to circumstance; the asymmetry corrupts learning.
- **Hindsight in retrospectives.** Treating outcomes as obvious in retrospect erodes the lesson and unfairly judges the prior decision-maker (sometimes oneself a year ago).
- **Premature consensus in groups.** Groupthink produces unchallenged decisions that look unanimous in the room and fragile in the world.
- **Decisions under high arousal on complex problems.** Yerkes-Dodson predicts the failure mode; the cost is reversible decisions made expensively or irreversible decisions made wrongly.
- **Hiring or evaluation by impression alone.** Implicit bias plus the team halo effect plus actor-observer asymmetry produce decisions that pattern with demographics rather than merit.
- **Reading retrospective accounts as faithful evidence.** Reconstructive memory means the "what happened" account is partly an inference from now-known outcomes.
