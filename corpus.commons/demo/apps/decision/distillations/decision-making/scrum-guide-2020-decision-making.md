# Schwaber & Sutherland, The Scrum Guide — Decision-Making Distillation

**Source:** Schwaber, K. & Sutherland, J. (2020). *The Scrum Guide: The Definitive Guide to Scrum: The Rules of the Game*. November 2020 edition. scrumguides.org. Licence: CC BY-SA 4.0. https://scrumguides.org/docs/scrumguide/v2020/2020-Scrum-Guide-US.pdf.

## Decision-Making Relevance

The Scrum Guide is, at its core, a decision-process framework for complex problems. Its working definition — "a lightweight framework that helps people, teams and organizations generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems" — names decision-making under uncertainty as the problem space, and its foundation in *empiricism* ("knowledge comes from experience and making decisions based on what is observed") makes the framework a structured discipline for making decisions when prediction is unreliable.

The Guide encodes decision discipline in three layers. The **three pillars** — Transparency, Inspection, Adaptation — are a causal chain: low-transparency artefacts produce decisions that "diminish value and increase risk"; inspection without transparency is misleading; inspection without adaptation is pointless. The **five Scrum events** are structured decision points on a cadence: Sprint Planning makes three commitment decisions (Sprint Goal, scope selection, plan); the Daily Scrum is a daily course-correct decision; the Sprint Review is a what-to-do-next decision with stakeholders; the Sprint Retrospective is a process-improvement decision; the Sprint container fixes the timebox in which all this happens. The **three artefacts**, each with their commitment, encode where decisions register: what to build (Product Backlog with Product Goal), what to commit this Sprint (Sprint Backlog with Sprint Goal), and what counts as Done (Increment with Definition of Done).

A second decision-discipline thread runs through the explicit assignment of decision rights. The Product Owner — and only the Product Owner — orders the Product Backlog, defines the Product Goal, and may cancel the Sprint. The Developers — and only the Developers — size Product Backlog items, decide how to turn selected items into the Increment, and structure the Daily Scrum. The Scrum Master decides nothing about the product but is accountable for the team's ability to make decisions empirically. "The Product Owner is one person, not a committee" is the most direct expression of the principle that a complex decision needs a single accountable owner, even when many stakeholders want to influence it.

A third thread is the deliberate refusal to over-specify. The framework is "purposefully incomplete, only defining the parts required to implement Scrum theory"; "rather than provide people with detailed instructions, the rules of Scrum guide their relationships and interactions"; the Daily Scrum's structure is "at the sole discretion of the Developers"; and the Definition of Done is set at the organisational level if standardised, otherwise by the team. The Guide makes the meta-decision that local context decides, and structures only what must be common.

This distillation gathers these threads into a working pattern: how the Scrum framework structures decision-making under complexity; which decision rights sit with which Scrum role; how the events serve as decision cadences; and how the artefacts encode commitments that anchor future decisions.

## Key Concepts for Decision-Making

1.  **Empiricism as decision foundation.** "Empiricism asserts that knowledge comes from experience and making decisions based on what is observed" [V] (Source: Schwaber & Sutherland, *Scrum Guide* 2020, "Scrum Theory"). When prediction is unreliable, the cure is a tight observe-decide-act loop, not a longer plan. Scrum operationalises this through frequent inspection-and-adaptation cycles. (Source: Schwaber & Sutherland, *Scrum Guide* 2020, "Scrum Theory")

2.  **The three pillars as a decision-quality discipline.** Transparency enables Inspection; Inspection enables Adaptation; Adaptation must be prompt. A decision made on a low-transparency artefact will "diminish value and increase risk." A team that inspects without adapting is making the inspection itself a waste. The three pillars together form the minimum discipline for empirical decision-making. ("Scrum Theory", "Transparency", "Inspection", "Adaptation")

3.  **Single-owner decision rights for complex commitments.** "The Product Owner is one person, not a committee" [V] (Source: Schwaber & Sutherland, *Scrum Guide* 2020, "Scrum Team", "Product Owner"). Stakeholders wanting to change the Product Backlog must convince the Product Owner. The single-owner constraint exists because complex decisions need a single accountable owner, even when many stakeholders want input. ("Scrum Team", "Product Owner")

4.  **Sprint Planning as three sequential decisions.** Topic One: Why is this Sprint valuable? (defines the Sprint Goal). Topic Two: What can be Done this Sprint? (Developers select Product Backlog items in conversation with the Product Owner). Topic Three: How will the chosen work get done? (Developers plan the work; "no one else tells them how"). The order matters: value before scope before plan. ("Scrum Events", "Sprint Planning")

5.  **The Sprint Goal as a decision anchor.** "The Sprint Goal is the single objective for the Sprint." It is a commitment by the Developers that "provides flexibility in terms of the exact work needed to achieve it." When the work turns out different than expected, the Developers negotiate Sprint Backlog scope with the Product Owner *without affecting the Sprint Goal*. The Sprint Goal is the decision the team rallies around when local trade-offs need to be made. ("Scrum Artifacts", "Sprint Backlog", "Commitment: Sprint Goal")

6.  **The Daily Scrum as daily decision-cadence.** A 15-minute event where Developers "inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal and adapt the Sprint Backlog as necessary, adjusting the upcoming planned work." Format is at Developers' discretion as long as the meeting focuses on Sprint Goal progress and produces "an actionable plan for the next day of work." The decision output is the plan; the decision input is inspected progress. ("Scrum Events", "Daily Scrum")

7.  **Sprint Review as collaborative what-next decision.** Not a presentation. "The Scrum Team and stakeholders review what was accomplished in the Sprint and what has changed in their environment. Based on this information, attendees collaborate on what to do next. The Product Backlog may also be adjusted to meet new opportunities." Decision-making happens *in the room*, with stakeholders. ("Scrum Events", "Sprint Review")

8.  **Sprint Retrospective as process-improvement decision.** "The purpose of the Sprint Retrospective is to plan ways to increase quality and effectiveness." The team identifies "the most helpful changes" and addresses "the most impactful improvements... as soon as possible. They may even be added to the Sprint Backlog for the next Sprint." A decision that produces no commitment is a decision that won't take effect. ("Scrum Events", "Sprint Retrospective")

9.  **Definition of Done as the release gate.** "Work cannot be considered part of an Increment unless it meets the Definition of Done" [V] (Source: Schwaber & Sutherland, *Scrum Guide* 2020, "Scrum Artifacts", "Increment"). Items failing the DoD "cannot be released or even presented at the Sprint Review" [V] (Source: Schwaber & Sutherland, *Scrum Guide* 2020, "Scrum Artifacts", "Increment", "Commitment: Definition of Done"). The DoD is the team's pre-committed decision rule for what counts as Done — it replaces case-by-case judgement at the moment-of-release. ("Scrum Artifacts", "Increment", "Commitment: Definition of Done")

10.  **Sprint cancellation authority — Product Owner only.** "A Sprint could be cancelled if the Sprint Goal becomes obsolete. Only the Product Owner has the authority to cancel the Sprint" [V] (Source: Schwaber & Sutherland, *Scrum Guide* 2020, "Scrum Events", "The Sprint"). The most disruptive decision in Scrum is reserved to the role accountable for product value, not the team or the Scrum Master. ("Scrum Events", "The Sprint")

11.  **Sizing decision belongs to the Developers.** "The Developers who will be doing the work are responsible for the sizing. The Product Owner may influence the Developers by helping them understand and select trade-offs." The decision rule is *accountability follows the doer*; influence is permitted, but the decision rests with whoever will be on the hook for delivery. ("Scrum Artifacts", "Product Backlog")

12.  **Forecasting does not replace empiricism.** Burn-downs, burn-ups, and cumulative flows are useful for forecasting progress, but "these do not replace the importance of empiricism. In complex environments, what will happen is unknown. Only what has already happened may be used for forward-looking decision making." The forecasting artefact is an input to the decision, not the decision itself. ("Scrum Events", "The Sprint")

13.  **The Scrum Values as decision-quality enablers.** Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, Courage. The Guide treats these as conditions for empiricism: openness about challenges enables transparency; courage to do the right thing enables adaptation; respect for capable, independent colleagues enables the self-management that adaptation requires. ("Scrum Values")

14.  **The framework is immutable; the tactics are local.** "The Scrum framework, as outlined herein, is immutable. While implementing only parts of Scrum is possible, the result is not Scrum." But the structure and techniques of any given event, the team's specific Definition of Done, the format of the Daily Scrum, the decomposition of Product Backlog items, are all local-context decisions. The meta-decision is: change the local, not the structure. ("End Note"; throughout)

## Questions to Ask During Decision-Making

### Phase 1: Framing (Recognising the decision and gathering context)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Test for "complex problem" framing | Is this a complex problem (high uncertainty, what-will-happen is unknown) — and therefore one Scrum's empirical approach fits? Or is it a known, predictable problem better served by a plan-and-execute approach? |
| Identify the appropriate Scrum decision moment | Is this decision a Sprint Planning decision (commitment for the upcoming Sprint), a Daily Scrum decision (today's plan adjustment), a Sprint Review decision (what to do next based on inspected outcome), or a Sprint Retrospective decision (process change)? |
| Surface decision rights | Who has the formal decision right here — the Product Owner (product value, ordering, cancellation), the Developers (sizing, how-to-build, Daily Scrum format), or the team collectively (Sprint Goal definition, Definition of Done within team scope)? |
| Detect single-owner violation | Is this being treated as a committee decision when the framework assigns it to one person? Most acutely: is the Product Backlog being shaped by a committee rather than a single Product Owner? |
| Test for transparency before deciding | Are the artefacts informing this decision (Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, Increment) transparent enough? Low transparency yields decisions that "diminish value and increase risk." |
| Anchor on the Sprint Goal | If this is an in-Sprint decision, does the Sprint Goal frame it? Decisions that endanger the Sprint Goal are prohibited; decisions that require renegotiating scope to protect the Sprint Goal are encouraged. |

### Phase 2: Bounding (Defining the decision and its constraints)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Bound by Sprint timebox | Is the decision being made inside the Sprint container? If so, what's the relevant timebox — the Sprint itself (one month or less), Sprint Planning (8 hours), Sprint Review (4 hours), Sprint Retrospective (3 hours), Daily Scrum (15 minutes)? |
| Bound by the immutable framework | Is the proposed adaptation a local-tactics change (permitted) or a framework change (not Scrum if accepted)? The framework "is immutable. While implementing only parts of Scrum is possible, the result is not Scrum." |
| Bound by the Definition of Done | What's the DoD against which this Increment will be judged? If it's not explicit, the team must create one before the decision lands. |
| Set the Sprint Goal as a constraint | If the decision affects what the team will work on, does the work proposed actually advance the Sprint Goal? "No changes are made that would endanger the Sprint Goal" during the Sprint. |
| Identify which artefact registers the commitment | Will this decision change the Product Backlog (longer-term), the Sprint Backlog (this-Sprint), or the Increment (what's been Done)? The artefact identifies where the decision is anchored. |

### Phase 3: Exploring (Generating alternatives)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Use the Sprint Planning three-Topic exploration | If this is a Sprint commitment decision: Topic One — why is this Sprint valuable? (Sprint Goal alternatives). Topic Two — what can be Done this Sprint? (scope alternatives). Topic Three — how will the work get done? (delivery-approach alternatives). |
| Surface forecast inputs without letting them decide | What does the burn-down, burn-up, or cumulative flow say about likely progress? Treat these as decision inputs, not decision-makers — "what will happen is unknown." |
| Explore Sprint Retrospective candidates | If this is a process-improvement decision: what are the candidate changes the Retrospective identified? Which would have the most impact on quality and effectiveness? |
| Invite advisors when warranted | Sprint Planning allows: "The Scrum Team may also invite other people to attend Sprint Planning to provide advice." Are there advisors whose input would broaden the option set? |
| Refine Product Backlog items before commitment | Are the candidate Product Backlog items "Done by the Scrum Team within one Sprint" — i.e., refined to "ready" via "the act of breaking down and further defining Product Backlog items into smaller more precise items"? |

### Phase 4: Deciding (Analysing and selecting)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply the right decision rule per role | Who decides this specific question? Product Owner for what to build and Sprint cancellation; Developers for how to build, sizing, and Daily Scrum format; the team for Sprint Goal definition; the team (or organisation if standardised) for Definition of Done. |
| Stay inside the timebox | Is the decision being driven by the meeting's timebox? Sprint Planning is 8 hours for a one-month Sprint; the Sprint Goal "must be finalized prior to the end of Sprint Planning." |
| Make the Sprint Goal explicit | If the decision is the Sprint commitment: have the Developers, Product Owner, and Scrum Master collaborated to a single, finalised Sprint Goal that "communicates why the Sprint is valuable to stakeholders"? |
| Anchor the commitment to one of the three artefact-commitments | Does this decision register against the Product Goal (long-term), the Sprint Goal (this Sprint), or the Definition of Done (per Increment)? If it doesn't anchor anywhere, the decision will dissolve. |
| Test against transparency-inspection-adaptation | After this decision, will the resulting state be visible (transparency)? Will the team be able to inspect outcomes against it? If outcomes diverge, will adaptation be possible "as soon as possible to minimize further deviation"? |

### Phase 5: Ratifying (Implementing the decision)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Update the relevant artefact | Does this decision change the Product Backlog (order or content), the Sprint Backlog (scope, plan, Sprint Goal), or the Increment (status of work)? The artefact update is the ratification. |
| Lock the Sprint Goal | If this is a Sprint commitment, "the Sprint Goal must be finalized prior to the end of Sprint Planning." Has it been? |
| Confirm Definition of Done covers the work | If the decision is to commit to work, does the existing DoD cover it? If multiple Scrum Teams work on the product, they "must mutually define and comply with the same Definition of Done." |
| Communicate to stakeholders the Product Owner represents | The Product Owner represents stakeholders in the Product Backlog. Has the decision been transparent enough that those stakeholders understand the ordering rationale and the Product Goal it serves? |
| Schedule the next inspection point | When will this decision be inspected? Daily Scrum (daily progress vs Sprint Goal), Sprint Review (outcome vs Product Goal), Sprint Retrospective (process effectiveness)? Decisions without an inspection point have no adaptation loop. |

### Phase 6: Monitoring (Was the decision a good one?)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Inspect at the Daily Scrum | Daily: are we tracking toward the Sprint Goal? What's the actionable plan for the next day? Are there impediments to surface? |
| Adapt the Sprint Backlog when conditions change | When the work turns out to be different than expected, are Developers negotiating Sprint Backlog scope with the Product Owner *without affecting the Sprint Goal*? Are forecasting tools informing — but not replacing — empirical decision-making? |
| Inspect outcomes at the Sprint Review | Has the Sprint Review actually been a working session with key stakeholders, or has it degraded into a presentation? Has the Product Backlog been adjusted based on what was learned? |
| Improve the process at the Sprint Retrospective | What assumptions led the team astray, and what were their origins? What went well, what problems were encountered, and how were they (or weren't they) solved? What's the most impactful change to address next? |
| Consider Sprint cancellation only if Sprint Goal obsolete | "A Sprint could be cancelled if the Sprint Goal becomes obsolete. Only the Product Owner has the authority to cancel the Sprint." Is the Sprint Goal still valuable, or has the world changed enough to obsolete it? |
| Watch for partial-implementation drift | Is the team quietly dropping events ("we skipped the Retrospective this Sprint"), watering down accountabilities (committee Product Ownership), or weakening the Definition of Done? "Implementing only parts of Scrum is possible, the result is not Scrum." |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: The Product Backlog is being shaped by a committee.** Signal: multiple stakeholders are directly editing the backlog ordering; the Product Owner is treating all inputs equally. Diagnosis: single-owner violation — "The Product Owner is one person, not a committee." Follow-up: name the Product Owner as the single decision-maker; stakeholders may influence by persuading the Product Owner, not by direct edit.
- **Pattern: The Daily Scrum has drifted into a status report for management.** Signal: Developers report to a manager, not to each other; the Sprint Goal is not the focus. Diagnosis: the Daily Scrum's purpose has been lost — "The Developers can select whatever structure and techniques they want, as long as their Daily Scrum focuses on progress toward the Sprint Goal." Follow-up: remove management observers; re-anchor the 15 minutes on Sprint Goal progress and today's plan.
- **Pattern: The Sprint Review is a presentation, not a working session.** Signal: the team presents a slide deck; stakeholders receive but do not contribute. Diagnosis: the Sprint Review is supposed to be a collaborative session where "attendees collaborate on what to do next"; it is "not a status update." Follow-up: restructure as a working demo with live feedback and Product Backlog adjustment.
- **Pattern: Burn-downs are being treated as the decision, not the input.** Signal: the team uses the burn-down chart to predict Sprint completion and acts as if the forecast is certain. Diagnosis: forecasting-overreach — "these do not replace the importance of empiricism. In complex environments, what will happen is unknown. Only what has already happened may be used for forward-looking decision making." Follow-up: use burn-downs to inform, not to decide; base decisions on what has been inspected.
- **Pattern: Scrum events are being skipped selectively.** Signal: Retrospectives are skipped when the team is "too busy," Sprint Reviews are dropped when the increment is small. Diagnosis: partial-implementation drift — "implementing only parts of Scrum is possible, the result is not Scrum." Follow-up: restore the full event cadence; address the time pressure that motivated the skip rather than skipping the event.
- **Pattern: The Definition of Done is vague or inconsistently applied.** Signal: items are "done" by some Developers' reckoning but not by others'; the Sprint Review reveals undisclosed quality gaps. Diagnosis: the DoD is the team's pre-committed decision rule; "Work cannot be considered part of an Increment unless it meets the Definition of Done." Follow-up: write an explicit DoD; apply it mechanically at the Sprint boundary.
- **Pattern: The Sprint Goal becomes a list of features, not a coherent objective.** Signal: Sprint Planning produces a backlog-selection list with no single unifying goal; the Sprint Goal box is left blank or filled with a comma-separated list. Diagnosis: the Sprint Goal is "the single objective for the Sprint" that "provides flexibility in terms of the exact work needed to achieve it." Follow-up: draft the Sprint Goal as a sentence about the value to be delivered, before selecting which backlog items serve it.

## When to Use This Reference

- A *complex problem* needs a structured decision discipline because prediction is unreliable and outcomes need to be observed before acted upon — empiricism is the principle, the five Scrum events are the cadence.
- A *decision-rights ambiguity* on a software (or other complex-work) project — Product Owner / Developers / Scrum Master role responsibilities need to be named.
- A *Sprint Planning decision* needs structuring around the three Topics (why valuable / what can be Done / how it will get done).
- A *commitment* needs anchoring — the team needs a Sprint Goal to rally around, a Definition of Done to gate against, or a Product Goal to plan toward.
- A *daily course-correction* needs a forum — the 15-minute Daily Scrum on Sprint Goal progress.
- A *what-to-do-next decision with stakeholders* needs a forum — the Sprint Review as working session, not presentation.
- A *process-improvement decision* needs surfacing — the Sprint Retrospective as identifying and committing the most impactful change.
- A *single-owner discipline* needs naming for a Product Backlog being managed by committee.
- A *Sprint cancellation question* — only the Product Owner can; only if the Sprint Goal is obsolete.
- A *forecasting-overreach* pattern — burn-downs and cumulative flows being treated as decisions rather than as inputs.

## Worked Example

A software product team has been operating in a hybrid mode: they run Sprints but allow the engineering manager to reorder the Sprint Backlog mid-Sprint in response to stakeholder requests, and they skip the Sprint Retrospective when the Sprint is "clean." The Product Owner is technically a named role but defers most ordering decisions to the engineering manager. A new Scrum Master joins and needs to help the team regain empirical discipline.

The Scrum Master works through the framework decisions in order:

1. *Single-owner decision right (Key Concept 3).* The engineering manager's mid-Sprint reordering is a Product Owner responsibility violation. "The Product Owner is one person, not a committee." The Scrum Master names this: stakeholders and managers influence the Product Owner by persuasion; they do not directly edit the backlog or Sprint scope.

2. *Sprint Goal as anchor (Key Concept 5).* The team discovers they have not been writing Sprint Goals at all — they select a list of backlog items and call that the Sprint plan. The Scrum Master holds a Sprint Planning session (Topic One first) to draft the Sprint Goal before any items are selected: "By the end of this Sprint, we will have validated the new checkout flow with the five pilot retailers."

3. *Daily Scrum as decision cadence (Key Concept 6).* The Daily Scrum has been a 30-minute standup where Developers report to the engineering manager. The Scrum Master restructures to 15 minutes, Developers only, anchored on "are we on track for the Sprint Goal?" and "what is the plan for today?" The engineering manager is invited to listen but does not receive reports.

4. *Sprint Retrospective as process-improvement decision (Key Concept 8).* The team has skipped three of the last five Retrospectives. The Scrum Master restores the cadence and makes one concrete commitment per Retrospective — not a list of improvements, one. The commitment is added to the next Sprint Backlog.

5. *Transparency check (Key Concept 2).* The burn-down chart has been the team's primary decision tool. The Scrum Master reframes it as an input, not a decision: "This chart tells us where we were; the Daily Scrum tells us what we're doing about it today."

By Sprint 3 of the restored practice, the Sprint Goal structure reduces mid-Sprint scope changes by approximately half — the Product Owner can answer stakeholder requests by pointing to the Sprint Goal as the committed objective, rather than negotiating case-by-case. This is the framework's structural contribution: commitments at the right level make decision-making cheaper at every level below them.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Committee Product Ownership.** Multiple stakeholders editing the Product Backlog directly, diluting the Product Owner's single-accountable-owner authority.
- **Sprint Goal as backlog summary.** Writing a Sprint Goal that is just a list of items, not a coherent objective that "provides flexibility in terms of the exact work needed to achieve it."
- **Sprint Review as presentation.** Turning the Sprint Review into a slide-deck session rather than a stakeholder working session where "attendees collaborate on what to do next."
- **Forecasting replacing empiricism.** Treating burn-downs and cumulative flows as if they make decisions rather than inform them; acting as if "what will happen is known" when the Guide is explicit it is not.
- **Selective Scrum implementation.** Skipping Retrospectives, dropping Sprint Planning Topics, or weakening the Definition of Done. "Implementing only parts of Scrum is possible, the result is not Scrum."
- **Definition of Done as suggestion.** Applying the DoD inconsistently or softening it under Sprint pressure. "Work cannot be considered part of an Increment unless it meets the Definition of Done."
- **Manager-as-decider of how Developers work.** Overriding Developers' authority over their own sizing and Daily Scrum structure. "No one else tells them how."

## Integration with Other References

| Reference | Connection |
|---|---|
| Letaw, *Handbook of Software Engineering Methods* (Ch 1) | Letaw cites the *Scrum Guide* directly for Scrum's three roles, five events, and three artefacts, then operationalises the framework at the methods level (story points, ideal days, planning poker, INVEST, RACI, Tuckman). The Scrum Guide is the framework; Letaw is one fill-in of how to run inside it. Use the Scrum Guide when the question is "what does Scrum actually require?" and Letaw when the question is "what method should the team use inside that requirement?" |
| Jones, *Evidence-based Software Engineering* | Jones reads Agile and Scrum critically: the post-1980 evidence collapse means most software-engineering claims, including some about Agile, are folklore-laden. Use Jones when the question is "what evidence supports the choice between Scrum and an alternative?", noting that the Guide itself makes empiricism the basis of decision-making within Scrum — an internal, not comparative, claim. |
| OpenStax, *Organizational Behavior* (Ch 13, Ch 14) | Provides the general team-decision-making theory (programmed vs non-programmed decisions, six-step process, groupthink, escalation of commitment). Scrum's empirical cadence (frequent inspection, prompt adaptation) is a structural counter to the bounded-rationality and escalation barriers OB describes. The Sprint Retrospective is the Scrum-specific instantiation of OB's "evaluate the decision" stage. |
| OpenStax, *Principles of Management* (Ch 17, Ch 18, PDCA) | The Plan-Do-Check-Act control cycle maps onto Scrum's Sprint cycle (Plan = Sprint Planning; Do = Sprint execution; Check = Sprint Review and Retrospective; Act = adapt Product Backlog and team practices for next Sprint). Use Principles of Management for the broader management-control framing within which Scrum's team-level cycle sits. |
| OpenStax, *Business Ethics* (Ch 3) | The Product Owner's role of representing many stakeholders in the Product Backlog is a stakeholder-prioritisation decision. Mitchell-Agle-Wood salience (power, legitimacy, urgency) is the theory Scrum's single-owner constraint operationalises in practice. |
| OpenStax, *Entrepreneurship* (lean startup, build-measure-learn) | The build-measure-learn cycle is conceptually adjacent to Scrum's inspect-and-adapt cycle. Both respond to high-uncertainty problem spaces. Use entrepreneurship for venture-level pivots; use the Scrum Guide for product-development cadence inside an established venture. |

**What this reference does not cover.** The Guide intentionally under-specifies facilitation techniques (Developers choose their own Daily Scrum format), estimation methods (planning poker, story points, and ideal days are not in the Guide), quantitative decision tools (NPV, IRR, CAPM), strategic decision frameworks (SWOT, PESTEL, Five Forces, VRIO), cross-team scaling (no SAFe, LeSS, Nexus, or Scrum of Scrums detail — the Guide stops at the team level), and Retrospective-specific facilitation techniques (Start/Stop/Continue, 4Ls, Sailboat, etc.). For those, look to the Letaw distillation, strategy references, or the broader retrospective literature.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The *Scrum Guide* contains no `[BT]` markers: it is an original specification text, not a survey of others' work. All named concepts (empiricism, Transparency-Inspection-Adaptation, Sprint Goal, Product Goal, Definition of Done, the five events, the three artefacts, the five values) are the authors' own framework definitions.

**Named limits of the source.** The Guide is intentionally minimal — 13 pages in the 2020 edition. It defines structure and accountability without prescribing tactics, estimation methods, facilitation techniques, or evidence-base comparisons. Practitioners who need evidence for Scrum's effectiveness relative to other approaches must go beyond the Guide. The framework is designed for *complex problems*; the Guide is explicit that "non-empirical decisions" — routine, well-understood problems — do not need a Sprint container.

**Evidence-marker continuity.** All Key Concepts in this distillation are `[AP]` (Schwaber and Sutherland's stated positions as framework authors) or `[V]` (verbatim passages from the Guide — the deep reference carries these). No `[BT]` markers exist. The Guide makes no empirical claims about comparative software-engineering outcomes; those claims, where made, originate outside the Guide and appear in the Jones and Letaw distillations.
