## Decision-Making Relevance

The SSDL *Systems Thinking Foundations* briefs are not a decision-making text — they are a curriculum in systems thinking and system dynamics — but they reshape decision-making in five load-bearing ways. First, they sharpen the diagnostic for *when a decision is being made under conditions where linear cause-effect reasoning will mislead* — the five characteristics of complex problems (dynamic, multi-stakeholder, time delays, unintended consequences, accumulations) double as a checklist for spotting when a decision needs systems treatment rather than calculation or quick action. Second, they make the *mental-model surfacing* move explicit and operational — most decisions in complex domains are shaped by unspoken cognitive maps that, left unsurfaced, produce ineffective or counterproductive choices. Third, they offer *feedback thinking* as a structural replacement for the X-causes-Y frame that most decision rules implicitly assume, with Causal Loop Diagrams as the named tool for surfacing structural-rather-than-event explanations. Fourth, they offer *accumulation/stock-and-flow* thinking as a discipline against the failure mode of confusing flow rates with stock levels — a confusion that drives a wide class of misframed decisions (treating signups today as the signal when the waitlist is the signal). Fifth, they offer *system archetypes* as named diagnostic patterns for recognising recurring decision traps (fixes that fail, success to the successful, shifting the burden, drifting goals, limits to growth) — pattern-matching shortcuts that help a decision-maker anticipate where an intervention is likely to backfire.

Critically, the source is explicit about *when system dynamics is the wrong tool*: "Sometimes what's actually needed is a simple calculation... a survey... or even just a meeting between stakeholders. Sometimes the path forward is obvious, or a decision has to be made immediately" (Brief 1.02). The distillation respects that limit — systems thinking enters the decision process when complexity makes linear methods unreliable, and stays out when it doesn't.

The distillation pairs naturally with Barbrook-Johnson & Penn *Systems Mapping* (already in corpus) — Barbrook-Johnson provides the methods-level grounding (seven causal-mapping methods, the appropriateness triangle for method choice); SSDL provides the concepts-level grounding (the patterns the methods are built to surface). Together they cover the systems-thinking dimension that the prior corpus largely lacked.

## Verbatim anchors from the source

The mental-models thesis underpinning every diagnostic move in this distillation: "A central principle of the systems thinking perspective is that system structures are supported and held in place by our underlying beliefs, mindsets, and goals" [V] (Brief 1.03, opening header).

The feedback-thinking replacement for X-causes-Y framing: "we look for causal relationships in both directions" [V] (Brief 1.05, "+Linear vs. Feedback Perspective").

The CLD epistemic move that protects decisions from false analytical confidence: "a CLD is a hypothesis – there is not one single 'right' answer to the variables that are included or the relationships that exist between them" [V] (Brief 1.05, "+ CONSIDERATIONS").

The empirical warning against trusting intuition on stocks: "even students with strong mathematical training make persistent mistakes understanding even simple dynamic systems that include accumulations" [V] (Brief 1.06, "+ CONSIDERATIONS WHEN APPLYING THE CONCEPT OF ACCUMULATIONS").

## Key Concepts for Decision-Making

1.  **The five characteristics of complex problems as a "should we treat this as complex?" diagnostic.** A decision is operating in complex territory if the situation exhibits *dynamic* components (the variables themselves change over time), *multiple stakeholders with conflicting goals and interlinked decisions*, *time delays between action and result*, *unintended consequences from prior interventions*, or *accumulation/history dependence* in the state being decided upon. Any one of the five is a signal; multiple of them together is a strong signal that linear decision rules will misfire. (Source: Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems")

2.  **Logic models as a decision-making failure mode in complex domains.** Traditional program-design tools that compute Resources → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact in a clear linear flow "can fail to account for the characteristics of complex problems, causing our best plans to fail or lead to surprising results." When the decision is for a complex domain, a linear logic model is producing false confidence; the decision-maker needs feedback-thinking and stock-and-flow tools as substrate. (Source: Brief 1.02, "+System dynamics offers tools for working with complex problems")

3.  **System dynamics as wrong tool when the path is obvious.** "Because system dynamics is especially suited for gaining insight into complex problems, it is not the right method for every situation. Sometimes what's actually needed is a simple calculation... a survey... or even just a meeting between stakeholders. Sometimes the path forward is obvious, or a decision has to be made immediately." A first-pass decision-making move is to ask whether the situation actually needs systems treatment, or whether a calculation, survey, conversation, or immediate action will do. The hike-and-broken-ankle test from Brief 1.02: "you don't need to make a system dynamics model about it!" (Source: Brief 1.02, "+CONSIDERATIONS")

4.  **Mental-model surfacing as a precondition for decision-quality in complex domains.** "System structures are supported and held in place by our underlying beliefs, mindsets, and goals. Therefore, in order to transform and redesign systems we must also transform our mindsets." Most decision frames are shaped by unspoken cognitive maps that "influence how we define a problem, the language we use to describe it, and the actions we take to solve it." System-dynamics tools "get our mental models out of our minds and onto the paper" — making them explicit so they can be challenged, negotiated, and improved. For a decision-maker, this maps directly to a move: before deciding, surface the mental models in the room. (Source: Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT")

5.  **Mental models are incomplete and inherently partial.** None of us has complete knowledge of a system; our understanding is informed by "lived experiences, values, assumptions, power, and biases." The implication for decision-making: any one decision-maker's analysis is operating from a partial mental model, and decisions made from partial models risk being "ineffective, counterproductive, or even harmful." The decision-quality move is to amplify the perspectives of groups with differing vantage points before deciding. (Source: Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT")

6.  **Mental models are sticky, especially among the unaffected.** "We cannot assume that just by sharing assumptions, alone, broadens one's mental model or set of values and beliefs. In fact, mental models are resistant to change, especially among individuals who aren't personally experiencing any negative system effects." For a decision-maker, this is a precise warning: those least exposed to a decision's downside are the most resistant to revising the frame that produced the decision. Voice and exposure correlate. (Source: Brief 1.03, "+CONSIDERATIONS")

7.  **Linear (X-causes-Y) thinking as the conventional decision frame to be replaced.** "In many fields, we are conditioned to approach problems through linear thinking, which we could also call 'event' or 'cause-effect' thinking. When looking at problems, we look for causal relationships in one direction: 'X' causes 'Y'." Feedback thinking complicates this: causal relationships run in both directions; "feedback structure" explains why problems persist despite well-intentioned interventions. For decision-making, the move is from a one-step "what should I do about Y? change X" reflex to a structural "what loops are producing the pattern I see, and which would I add/remove/strengthen/weaken to change it?" (Source: Brief 1.05, "+Linear vs. Feedback Perspective")

8.  **CLDs as decision-hypotheses, not decision-answers.** "A causal loop diagram (CLD) is a systems thinking tool to help us approach problems from a feedback perspective. Remember, a CLD is a hypothesis – there is not one single 'right' answer to the variables that are included or the relationships that exist between them." This is a load-bearing epistemic point for decision-making: a CLD is a means of structuring inquiry, not a means of computing the answer. The diagram's value is in the conversation it shapes; the diagram is iterated, not solved. (Source: Brief 1.05, "+ CONSIDERATIONS")

9.  **Intervention design as loop-structure change.** "When designing interventions using feedback thinking, we seek to change the structure of the system by adding or removing feedback loops; strengthening or weakening loops, and adding or removing links." The decision-making payoff: an intervention that targets a variable without identifying the loop it sits within is operating on the event surface; an intervention that names the loop it intends to add, remove, strengthen, or weaken is operating on structure. (Source: Brief 1.05, "+Linear vs. Feedback Perspective")

10.  **Accumulations as the structural form of history-dependence.** "An accumulation is something that builds up or drains over time, like a bathtub." Many decision frames mistake current flow for current state — the count of signups this week instead of the size of the backlog; the resources allocated this quarter instead of the depletion-or-growth of the underlying stock. The discipline: ask "what is the stock?", "what are the inflows?", "what are the outflows?", and "what is the history that produced the current stock level?" Decisions framed in flow terms when the relevant variable is a stock will repeatedly under-fire. (Source: Brief 1.06, "+ WHAT DO WE MEAN BY ACCUMULATION?")

11.  **The "blank slate" failure mode.** "When meeting a new [stakeholder], it can be easy to assume that [they] are coming to you with a blank slate – an empty tub. But that [stakeholder] might have had years or generations of distrust that has built over time, and you are meeting that [stakeholder] as an accumulation of their history, not just the short interaction of that moment." A decision that treats a new interaction as if no prior accumulation exists will be unable to account for the system's actual state. (Source: Brief 1.06, "+ WHY DOES THIS ACCUMULATION CONCEPT MATTER?")

12.  **Intuition about accumulations is unreliable.** "Using intuition alone can lead to incorrect assumptions about accumulations... Studies show that even students with strong mathematical training make persistent mistakes understanding even simple dynamic systems that include accumulations. Understanding dynamic systems gets even harder once you add feedback loops and time delays." The decision-quality move when stocks and flows are central: do not trust intuition; draw the stock-and-flow diagram. (Source: Brief 1.06, "+ CONSIDERATIONS WHEN APPLYING THE CONCEPT OF ACCUMULATIONS", citing Cronin/Gonzalez/Sterman 2009)

13.  **The "illusion of accuracy" failure mode.** "Stock and flow diagrams can lead to the illusion of accuracy. These diagrams may look formal and mathematical, but it is important to remember that the quality of output of a model depends on the mental models, stories, and experiences of the people who informed it!" The decision-quality move: stock-and-flow visualisation does not exempt the underlying model from the mental-models limitations of its builders. Treat the diagram as a structured conversation, not as a proof. (Source: Brief 1.06, "+ CONSIDERATIONS WHEN APPLYING THE CONCEPT OF ACCUMULATIONS")

14.  **System archetypes as pattern-recognition shortcuts for recurring decision traps.** "System archetypes are visual descriptions of generic, recurring system structures in the form of Causal Loop Diagrams... they can be applied to multiple different scenarios." Five named patterns from Kim (1993), each describing a decision-trap whose structural shape is the same across contexts:

    - **Fixes that fail.** A short-term fix solves the surface problem but generates unintended consequences that make the original problem worse.
    - **Success to the successful.** Competition for resources between groups; the initially-advantaged group is rewarded with more resources and becomes more successful; the disadvantaged group is trapped in a downward spiral.
    - **Shifting the burden.** A symptomatic fix is easier and faster than an upstream solution but "may actually impede our ability to invest time and energy to the fundamental solution," making the fundamental solution harder to apply and the symptomatic fix more tempting over time.
    - **Drifting goals.** A gap between Goal and Actual can be closed by corrective action *or* by lowering the goal; lowering the goal shrinks the gap, reducing the incentive to take corrective action; the goal drifts down rather than performance up.
    - **Limits to growth.** A reinforcing growth loop (effort → improvement → more effort) hits a balancing loop driven by a resource constraint; growth flattens or reverses.

    (Source: Brief 1.07, "+ APPLYING SYSTEM ARCHETYPES IN K-12 EDUCATION", adapting Kim 1993)

15.  **Archetypes are hypothesis-generators, not labels.** "System archetypes should not be used prescriptively: System archetypes are not meant to erase the uniqueness of a situation. Oversimplifying a problem or jumping to the conclusion that it is best represented by an archetype can impede the creative and iterative process that makes system dynamics modeling so useful for thinking and dialogue." For decision-making, an archetype suggests *what to look for*; it does not deliver the diagnosis. (Source: Brief 1.07, "+ CONSIDERATIONS")

16.  **Archetypes can reinforce expert/novice dynamics in decision-rooms.** "Jumping in to use archetypes when facilitating a group model building workshop or community session has the potential to reinforce a dynamic of expert vs. novice. The modeler draws on their modeling 'expertise' to diagnose an archetype in a way that overshadows the lived experience of the participants, who actually understand their situation more deeply." For decision-making, this is a warning about who names the pattern: the expert who labels a situation as a *Shifting the Burden* in front of stakeholders who have lived inside it for years risks closing the inquiry rather than opening it. (Source: Brief 1.07, "+ CONSIDERATIONS")

17.  **The canonical archetype list is not fixed.** "There is no definitive list of system archetypes; there is a broad list of eight to twelve core archetypes, but some even argue that four archetypes can explain all the other archetypes." For decision-making practice, the implication is: the five archetypes presented in Brief 1.07 are useful patterns, not an exhaustive vocabulary. Look for additional patterns; combine archetypes; expect that some situations need a structure the canonical set does not name. (Source: Brief 1.07, "+ CONCEPT")

## Questions to Ask During Decision-Making

### Phase 1: Triage — is this a systems-thinking decision at all?

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Decide whether systems treatment is warranted | Does the situation exhibit any of the five characteristics of complex problems? Is the variable I care about *dynamic* (changes over time as a matter of the system, not just of the data)? Are there *multiple stakeholders with conflicting goals* whose decisions affect each other? Is there a *time delay between intervention and result*? Have *prior interventions produced unintended consequences*? Is there an *accumulation/history dependence* in the state I'm deciding on? |
| Test the alternative tools | Is a simple calculation enough? A survey of stakeholders? A meeting? An immediate decision? If yes, the systems-thinking apparatus is overkill. |
| Apply the broken-ankle test | If the path forward is obvious, or the decision has to be made immediately, the answer is to act, not to model. Do not produce diagrams when the situation calls for action. |

### Phase 2: Mental-model surfacing before decision

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Surface the room's mental models | Before deciding, ask each participant what they believe is producing the pattern under decision. Expect partial, overlapping, but distinct models. |
| Audit my own mental model for completeness | What do I take for granted in this frame? What is my lived experience producing as the obvious-seeming explanation? Whose lived experience would produce a different explanation? |
| Identify whose mental model is shaping the decision | Who is in the room? Whose voice is loudest? Who is absent? "Mental models are very sticky", "especially among individuals who aren't personally experiencing any negative system effects" — those most insulated from the consequences may be the most certain about the frame. |
| Make the mental-model surfacing visible | Are we drawing the mental model on a whiteboard, or are we asking people to defend their conclusions? The first practice surfaces models; the second hides them. |

### Phase 3: Feedback thinking — is the pattern event-shaped or structure-shaped?

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Replace X-causes-Y framing | What loops produce the pattern I see? Where does the effect feed back to the cause? If I draw the relationship as a single arrow, what relationships am I leaving out? |
| Identify reinforcing and balancing loops | Where does success breed more success (reinforcing)? Where does action provoke a counter-action that restores the prior state (balancing)? |
| Name the loop the intervention will operate on | Is my proposed intervention adding a loop, removing a loop, strengthening a loop, or weakening a loop? If I can't say which, I'm operating on the event surface. |
| Treat the diagram as hypothesis | Am I trying to get the diagram "right" the first time, or am I treating it as a thinking aid to be iterated? The latter is correct; the former produces false confidence. |

### Phase 4: Stock-and-flow diagnostic — what is the actual variable of interest?

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Distinguish stock from flow | Is the variable I care about a *stock* (accumulated state — trust, capability, technical debt, customer-base, talent-bench, on-call fatigue) or a *flow* (rate per unit time — signups today, incidents this Sprint, lines of code added)? If I'm acting on the flow but the relevant variable is the stock, my intervention will under-fire. |
| Identify history dependence | What is the history of inflows and outflows that produced the current state? What experiences have accumulated for the stakeholders in this decision? Am I treating someone or some part of the system as a "blank slate" when in reality I'm meeting them as an accumulation of their history? |
| Draw the diagram even if it feels obvious | Intuition about accumulations is unreliable even for the mathematically trained. The decision-quality move when stocks matter is to draw the stock and flow, not to reason from intuition. |
| Audit for the illusion of accuracy | Does the formal-looking diagram I produced exempt me from re-examining the mental models that informed it? If I'm now treating the diagram as proof, I've fallen for the failure mode. |

### Phase 5: Archetype pattern-matching — are we walking into a known trap?

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Test the situation against *Fixes that fail* | Is the proposed intervention a short-term fix? Does the fix produce a side effect that, after a delay, makes the original problem worse? Examples of decision-trap shape: emergency hiring that increases onboarding burden and slows existing work; bonus-based incentives that distort effort allocation; capacity-stretching that increases error rates. |
| Test against *Success to the successful* | Are resources being concentrated where success is already accruing? Is the disadvantaged side trapped in a downward spiral driven by structural resource allocation, not by individual choice? Examples: budget allocation favouring high-visibility teams; promotion pipelines that select for past success rather than current capability. |
| Test against *Shifting the burden* | Are we addressing a symptom because the fundamental solution would take time we feel we don't have? Does the symptomatic fix erode the conditions that would have let us apply the fundamental solution later? Examples: hot-fixing rather than refactoring; vendor escalation rather than building internal capability; reactive customer-service compensation rather than fixing the underlying defect. |
| Test against *Drifting goals* | Is the goal being lowered because the gap was too uncomfortable to face? Is the response to chronic under-performance a redefinition of "performance" rather than a corrective intervention? Examples: SLAs revised downward when missed; product-quality bars lowered when launch deadlines slip; reliability targets reframed as aspirational when sustained mismet. |
| Test against *Limits to growth* | Is the reinforcing growth loop hitting a resource constraint we are ignoring? Is the resource constraint internal (people, time, attention) or external (regulatory, market)? Examples: marketing scaling without engineering capacity; team growing without management bandwidth; feature velocity hitting platform-fragility ceilings. |
| Use the archetype as hypothesis, not label | If I name an archetype, am I closing the inquiry or opening it? Stakeholders with lived experience inside the situation often understand it more deeply than the modeller; the archetype is a question to test, not a verdict to deliver. |
| Look for archetypes the canonical list doesn't name | The five archetypes are not exhaustive — "there is no definitive list of system archetypes; there is a broad list of eight to twelve core archetypes, but some even argue that four archetypes can explain all the other archetypes." Combine archetypes; expect new shapes; treat the canonical five as starting hypotheses. |

### Phase 6: Intervention design as loop-structure change

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Name what loop the intervention will change | Is the intervention adding a new loop? Strengthening an existing one? Weakening or removing one? Decoupling a link? Adding a time delay? Removing one? |
| Test for second-order effects | What new feedback loops does my intervention create? If the intervention works as intended, what will the new pattern look like, and which loops in that new pattern might themselves need attention? |
| Distinguish structural from event interventions | Am I changing the structure that produced the pattern, or am I responding to a particular event? Structural interventions are slower, costlier, harder to roll out, and produce durable changes; event interventions are faster but tend to require repeated reapplication. Both are legitimate; naming which is which lets the decision-maker pick consciously. |
| Plan for the delay before signal | How long after the intervention will the effect become visible? "Often funding or buy-in is gone long before an initiative can demonstrate impact." Has the decision-maker secured commitment that exceeds the expected feedback time? |

### Phase 7: Decision-quality discipline against systems-thinking failure modes

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Avoid logic-model overconfidence | Am I treating a Resources → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact chain as if it's the actual mechanism? In complex domains, that chain hides feedback loops, accumulations, and unintended consequences. |
| Avoid treating CLDs as solved | Am I treating my diagram as the answer rather than as the hypothesis? CLDs are conversation structures; their value is in the iteration, not in their initial form. |
| Avoid the illusion of accuracy in stock-and-flow | Does the formal appearance of my stock-and-flow diagram lead me to trust outputs more than the underlying mental models warrant? |
| Avoid prescriptive archetype-application | Am I labelling the situation with an archetype rather than testing the archetype as a hypothesis? |
| Avoid expert/novice dynamics in modelling | Am I, as the modeller, overshadowing stakeholders' lived experience by diagnosing their situation in modelling-language? |
| Avoid systems-thinking when it's overkill | Is the path forward obvious, or is the decision time-critical, or is this a simple calculation? If yes, do that instead. |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: A decision-maker insists an intervention failed because of poor execution, not because the logic was wrong.** Signal: multiple rounds of the same intervention with the same disappointing result; attribution shifts to "people didn't follow through." Diagnosis: probable *Fixes that fail* structure — the intervention is addressing the symptom while generating an unintended consequence that replenishes the problem. Follow-up: surface the feedback loop the current intervention sits inside; ask what happens to the problem source *after* the fix is applied.
- **Pattern: Resources consistently flow to the already-successful unit or person.** Signal: one team's budget grows every cycle; another's shrinks despite the organisation claiming to want diversity of output. Diagnosis: *Success to the Successful* archetype — resource allocation is reinforcing the advantage, not responding to need. Follow-up: audit the allocation rule; ask whether resources track success signals or strategic need; name the downward spiral on the other side.
- **Pattern: The team keeps reaching for the fast fix even when everyone knows the real problem.** Signal: "we know we need to refactor / rebuild / restructure, but for now let's just patch." Diagnosis: *Shifting the Burden* — the symptomatic fix is crowding out the fundamental solution and over time making it harder to apply. Follow-up: name the dynamic explicitly; protect time for the fundamental solution before the symptomatic fix consumes all capacity.
- **Pattern: Performance targets are quietly revised downward when missed.** Signal: goals from three cycles ago are no longer referenced; the current target is measurably lower. Diagnosis: *Drifting Goals* — the corrective action taken was to lower the standard, not to close the gap. Follow-up: display the historical target alongside the current one; name the drift; choose whether to restore the original target or formally revise it with a rationale.
- **Pattern: A growth initiative has stalled despite continued investment.** Signal: the same effort that produced results in the first year is producing less in year three; the team is trying harder and growing slower. Diagnosis: *Limits to Growth* — a reinforcing loop has hit a balancing constraint. Follow-up: identify the constraining resource; intervene on the constraint rather than on the growth effort.
- **Pattern: Stakeholders present confident, conflicting accounts of why the problem exists.** Signal: in the same room, two groups with different vantage points give incompatible causal accounts. Diagnosis: each is operating from a partial mental model shaped by their lived experience and exposure — "none of us has complete knowledge of a system." Follow-up: run a Group Model Building session; draw the mental models; identify which partial account is closest to the shared structure.
- **Pattern: A stock-and-flow diagram is being treated as the answer.** Signal: the model is circulated as a deliverable rather than as a thinking tool; objections are answered by pointing to the diagram. Diagnosis: illusion of accuracy — "the quality of output of a model depends on the mental models, stories, and experiences of the people who informed it." Follow-up: re-open the underlying assumptions; treat the diagram as the best current hypothesis, not the answer.

## When to Use This Reference

- A decision involves a system that exhibits any of the five complex-problem characteristics: dynamic, multi-stakeholder with conflicting goals, time delays, unintended consequences from prior interventions, or accumulation/history dependence.
- An intervention has been tried before and produced disappointing or counterproductive results, and the team wants to understand why before trying again.
- A team disagrees about what is causing a pattern, and each party's account is internally coherent but incompatible with the others.
- A decision will affect stocks (trust, capability, technical debt, morale) rather than flows, and the team needs tools for reasoning about accumulation correctly.
- A recurring archetype pattern (Fixes that fail, Shifting the Burden, Drifting Goals, etc.) may be operating and the team needs a shared vocabulary to name it.
- A group model-building session is being planned and a conceptual frame for what the session is producing is needed.
- A program or initiative's logic model is being challenged and the team wants to replace linear cause-effect reasoning with feedback thinking.

The framework is not the right tool when the path forward is obvious, when a simple calculation or survey is sufficient, or when a decision must be made immediately without time for structural diagnosis.

## Worked Example

Consider a software engineering team facing rising production incidents. The conventional decision frame names the cause-effect link: *X (insufficient testing) causes Y (incidents)*; therefore *add more tests*. The intervention is launched. After Sprint 1, incident counts drop modestly. By Sprint 4, incident counts have climbed back up and the engineers report exhaustion.

A feedback-thinking diagnostic surfaces a different shape. The pattern matches **fixes that fail** as a hypothesis: adding tests is a short-term fix that solves the symptom (test coverage gap) while generating an unintended consequence — engineers redirected to test-writing have less time for refactoring, and the underlying brittleness that produced incidents continues to accumulate, producing more incidents under the test-writing load.

Drawing the CLD as a hypothesis (per Brief 1.05), the team identifies three loops:
- A *Short-term fix* loop: incidents → testing pressure → tests written → caught defects → incidents temporarily drop.
- An *Unintended consequence on capacity* loop: testing pressure → engineer-hours redirected from refactoring → brittleness accumulates → incidents rise.
- An accumulation: a *stock of technical debt* (per Brief 1.06's stock-and-flow framing) that grows when test-writing displaces refactoring, regardless of how many tests are added. The flow that matters is *refactoring rate*, not *tests written this Sprint*.

The decision-quality moves at each step:
- *Surface mental models* (Brief 1.03): before deciding, ask each team member what they believe is producing incidents. The expected outcome is a set of partially overlapping but distinct mental models — testers attributing it to coverage, senior engineers attributing it to brittleness, product attributing it to deadline pressure. Each is partial; none is wrong.
- *Identify the stock* (Brief 1.06): the stock is *brittleness* (or *unrefactored complexity*); the inflow is *new code under deadline pressure*; the outflow is *refactoring effort*. Tests address symptoms but not the stock.
- *Name the archetype as hypothesis* (Brief 1.07): *Fixes that fail* if the test-writing is the only intervention; *Shifting the burden* if the engineers know refactoring is the fundamental solution but the team keeps reaching for test-writing because it produces visible progress in the Sprint Review.
- *Design the intervention as a loop-structure change* (Brief 1.05): the test-writing intervention adds a small balancing loop on incidents; adding a *fixed refactoring allocation per Sprint* adds a balancing loop on the underlying stock. Both are needed; the structural intervention is the second.

The decision is not "add more tests vs. don't add more tests"; it is "what structure is producing the incident pattern, and which loops do I want to add, remove, strengthen, or weaken." That is the systems-thinking decision frame.

A second worked example — a hiring-and-attrition pattern in a fast-growing team. Hiring volume is up; the team is also losing senior engineers at an unprecedented rate. The conventional decision frame: hire more, faster. A feedback diagnostic surfaces *Success to the Successful* between the team's two paths — engineers who get on the high-visibility new-product team accrue scope, recognition, and growth opportunities, while engineers on the maintenance team don't; over time, the maintenance team's senior people leave, the maintenance load is borne by less-experienced engineers, who produce more incidents, which keeps the maintenance team in firefighting mode, which prevents them from accruing the visibility that would justify their promotion or transfer. The hiring-more decision treats the team as an undifferentiated stock; the structural intervention is to redesign the work-allocation pattern that produces the success-to-the-successful loop in the first place.

The point of these examples is not that systems thinking always reverses the conventional decision — sometimes it confirms it. The point is that the systems-thinking diagnostic *forces the decision-maker to name the structure they are operating on*, which makes the decision auditable in a way that a one-step cause-effect intervention is not.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Treating a complex problem with linear cause-effect framing.** Logic models, single-variable interventions, and X-causes-Y reasoning fail when any of the five complex-problem characteristics are present.
- **Skipping mental-model surfacing.** Acting on the first mental model in the room, especially when the decision-makers in the room are the least exposed to the decision's downside.
- **Treating a partial mental model as comprehensive.** Acting from one decision-maker's analysis without amplifying the perspectives of stakeholders with different vantage points.
- **Treating signup or activity flow as the relevant variable when the stock is the relevant variable.** Counting tests written when the variable that matters is technical-debt stock; counting onboarding hires when the variable that matters is institutional-knowledge stock; counting trust-building events when the variable that matters is the accumulated history of past interactions.
- **Treating a stakeholder or part-of-system as a blank slate.** Failing to account for the history of accumulated experience that the stakeholder brings to the interaction.
- **Trusting intuition about accumulations.** Even the mathematically trained make persistent errors; draw the stock-and-flow diagram before deciding.
- **Treating stock-and-flow diagrams as proof rather than as structured conversations.** The diagram's apparent precision does not exempt the underlying mental models from their limits.
- **Treating a CLD as having a "right answer".** CLDs are hypotheses; their value is in iteration, not in being correct first time.
- **Labelling a situation with a system archetype as if the label is the diagnosis.** Archetypes are starting hypotheses; the unique features of the situation matter and may shape the response differently.
- **Letting the modeller's archetype-language overshadow stakeholders' lived experience.** Using systems-thinking vocabulary in a room where it produces an expert/novice dynamic.
- **Intervening on the event surface when the relevant change is structural.** A test-coverage intervention when the relevant change is refactoring allocation; a customer-service-script change when the relevant change is product reliability; a hiring-volume change when the relevant change is work-allocation pattern.
- **Locking in *Fixes that fail*** by repeatedly applying the same short-term fix while the underlying problem worsens.
- **Concentrating resources where success is accruing without auditing the spiral on the other side** — the *Success to the Successful* trap.
- **Letting symptomatic fixes erode the conditions for fundamental solutions** — the *Shifting the Burden* trap; particularly common when the symptomatic fix produces visible Sprint-cycle progress and the fundamental solution operates on a longer timescale.
- **Lowering goals quietly rather than naming the gap** — *Drifting Goals*; the failure mode is hardest to spot because the metric improves (the gap shrinks) even as performance does not.
- **Running reinforcing growth loops into balancing constraints without prepared response** — *Limits to Growth*; the failure mode is the surprise of growth flattening despite continued effort.
- **Applying systems-thinking apparatus when a simple calculation, survey, conversation, or immediate action will do.** The systems-thinking method has a high overhead; using it for situations that do not require it both delays the decision and tells the team to wait for an answer that would have been obvious otherwise.
