# US Army TC 25-20, Decision-Making Distillation

**Source:** Headquarters, Department of the Army (1993). *A Leader's Guide to After-Action Reviews*. Training Circular 25-20, 30 September 1993. **Licence:** Public domain (17 USC §105 — US Government work product). Open scope; no copyleft propagation.

## Decision-Making Relevance

TC 25-20 is primarily a learning-from-events protocol, not a general decision-making framework. The decision-making content in the TC is narrow but distinctive: it concerns how to *evaluate* a completed action so that the next decision can be made on better evidence than the one that led to the action under review. The TC's load-bearing decision-making contributions are (a) the *AAR-vs-critique* frame as an epistemic decision rule about how to construct evaluation knowledge, (b) the *retraining-as-stop-rule* logic in Chapter 5 that converts AAR output into action, (c) the *train-to-weakness-not-to-strength* allocation rule, (d) the *statistics-as-double-edged-sword* caution about measurement use, (e) the *experience-over-rank* OC-selection rule, and (f) the *fratricide-as-mandatory-deep-discussion* exception that overrides normal time-budget discipline. Each of these is a small but durable decision rule.

The frame to bring with you when reading TC 25-20 for decision-making content: this is about how to make the *next* decision better by extracting truthful, multi-perspective learning from the *previous* one. The AAR is upstream of the next plan, not part of the current operation.

## Key Concepts for Decision-Making

1.  **The AAR is the keystone of evaluation, not the evaluation itself.** Evaluation is "the basis for the commander's unit-training assessment" and "no commander, no matter how skilled, will see as much as the individual soldiers and leaders who actually conduct the training" (Ch 1, "Definition and Purpose"). The AAR's job is to widen the evidence base for the next decision; it is not a verdict on the past one. Decision-makers using AAR output should treat it as evidence input, not as a graded outcome.

2.  **AAR vs critique is a decision rule about how to construct evaluation evidence.** The TC's working definition: an AAR is "a professional discussion of an event, focused on performance standards, that enables soldiers to discover for themselves what happened, why it happened, and how to sustain strengths and improve on weaknesses" [V] (Ch 1, "Definition and Purpose"). A critique transmits one observer's view; the AAR aggregates and tests views across participants. The TC argues the AAR produces stronger recall and behaviour change because participants *discover* what happened, not because they are told. When choosing between a critique and an AAR for a post-event review, prefer the AAR for any decision that will affect future similar actions.

3.  **AARs do not grade.** "An AAR is not a critique. No one, regardless of rank, position, or strength of personality, has all of the information or answers" [V] (Ch 4, "Introduction and Rules"); "An AAR does not grade success or failure. There are always weaknesses to improve and strengths to sustain" [V] (Ch 4, "Introduction and Rules"). A decision-maker tempted to use an AAR as a yes/no verdict on a person or a unit will get worse data than one who uses it as a structured surfacing of conditions, choices, and consequences.

4.  **Train to weakness, not to strength.** The TC states it directly: "Train to weakness, not to strength" [V] (Ch 4, "Tasks to Sustain/Improve"). This is the TC's resource-allocation rule for retraining. Decision-makers face this rule whenever a development resource (time, money, attention) is finite and the question is whether to invest in a known-strong capability or a known-weak one. The TC's directive form is unusually crisp.

5.  **Critical gate tasks must be retrained to standard before progression.** "The unit must always retrain and perform critical gate tasks derived from the Combined Arms Training Strategy (CATS) to standard before progressing to the next level of tasks" (Ch 5, "Immediate Retraining"). This is a stop-rule decision frame: certain capabilities are gates, not gradients; failure on a gate task is not a partial score but a stop signal.

6.  **Statistics are a double-edged sword.** "Effective feedback requires participants to measure, collect, and quantify performance during the training exercise. … The danger lies in statistics for statistics' sake. … Statistics and statistics-based charts should *identify critical trends or issues and reinforce teaching points*" (Ch 4, "Statistics"). Decision-makers using post-event metrics should remember the TC's discipline: metrics serve teaching, not grading; if a chart is being used to assign a verdict, it has slipped its proper role.

7.  **OC selection rule: experience beats rank when in conflict.** "If trainers must choose between experience and understanding of current TTPs or rank, they should go with experience" (Ch 2, "Selecting and Training Observers and Controllers"). The TC's worked example: a staff sergeant with platoon-sergeant experience observes a platoon better than a sergeant first class without that experience. Decision-makers staffing reviews or evaluations of any kind should apply this rule when the dimensions are in tension.

8.  **Two-echelons-above evaluator rule.** External evaluations are conducted by the headquarters "two levels above the unit being evaluated" (Ch 2, "After-Action Review Plan"). This is a structural rule for evaluator independence: the immediate next echelon is too close; two echelons up has enough distance for honest assessment and enough proximity to understand the conditions. Useful when designing review structures in non-military contexts.

9.  **Fratricide overrides time-budget discipline.** "All incidents or near incidents of fratricide … will be discussed in detail" (Ch 4, "Fratricide"). The TC normally cautions against dwelling on issues unrelated to mission accomplishment, but fratricide is the explicit exception. Decision-makers should identify their own equivalents: events whose severity overrides normal time-budget rules in a post-event review. Near-misses count.

10.  **Force protection (safety) mentioned every AAR.** "Safety should be specifically addressed in every AAR and discussed in detail when it impacts unit effectiveness or soldier health" (Ch 4, "Discussion of Force Protection"). A standing-agenda discipline: certain topics get touched every review regardless of whether they came up during the event under review.

11.  **Open-ended questions, not yes/no.** The TC's worked contrast — *"What happened when your Bradley crested the hill?"* versus *"Why didn't you engage the enemy tanks to your front?"* (Ch 4, "Summary of Recent Events") — is a question-design rule for surfacing evidence. The first invites narrative; the second invites self-defence. Decision-makers gathering post-event evidence should design their questions to invite narrative.

12.  **The benefit lives in the follow-up.** "The real benefits of AARs come from taking the results and applying them to future training" [V] (Ch 5, "Benefits"). The decision cycle closes only when retraining occurs; an AAR whose output never reaches a retraining decision has not produced its benefit. Decision-makers convening reviews should pre-commit to the next-action stage.

13.  **Pencil-and-paper beats gadgetry.** "He should not get carried away with gadgets when a pencil and paper would do" (Ch 3, "Observe the Training and Take Notes"). "Training aids need not be complicated or fancy to be effective" (App A, "Training Aids"). A tooling-discipline rule: the question is fit-for-purpose, not sophistication.

14.  **Choosing the discussion-organisation technique.** Three options for organising the AAR's substance — chronological order, battlefield operating systems, key events/themes — and the leader can switch between them within a single AAR when systemic issues emerge (Ch 4, "Flexibility"). The decision is a fit-to-question call: chronological for narrative recall; BOS for systemic patterns; key events when time is limited. Decision-makers designing reviews should pick the organising structure that matches the question they need answered.

15.  **AAR planning lead time and duration discipline.** Formal AARs are planned six to eight weeks before execution (Ch 2, "After-Action Review Plan"). Platoon AARs 30–45 minutes; company AARs about one hour; battalion and above about two hours (Ch 2, "Scheduling Stopping Points"). Decision-makers convening reviews should respect the *no rushing* rule but also the *no waste* rule: "Reviewers must fully address all key learning points. They must not waste time on dead-end issues" (Ch 2, "Scheduling Stopping Points").

## Questions to Ask During Decision-Making

### Phase 1: Framing (recognising what kind of decision is being made)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Are we evaluating *the action* or *the actor*? | The AAR rule is to evaluate the action against the standard, not the person against a verdict. If the conversation has drifted into evaluating people, has it slipped from AAR mode into critique mode? |
| Have we framed this as a learning question or a grading question? | If the post-event review is being used to assign blame, performance ratings, or a pass/fail outcome, the AAR mechanism cannot produce its full benefit. Separate the two purposes — and accept that an AAR-shaped review of a graded event will produce less candour. |
| What is the next decision this review must inform? | If the review is not upstream of a specific next decision (retrain, redesign, redeploy), is the review serving any decision at all? |
| Have we identified our equivalent of *fratricide*? | What are the events whose severity overrides normal time-budget discipline and requires deep discussion regardless of how much else needs covering? Name them in advance, not after they happen. |
| Is this a stop-rule decision or a gradient decision? | If a critical gate task is involved, performance below standard is a stop, not a partial score. Has the team understood which capabilities are gates? |

### Phase 2: Bounding (defining the decision space)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| What is the budget of time, attention, and standing for this review? | The TC sets durations (30–45 min platoon, 1 hr company, 2 hr battalion-and-above). Are we budgeting realistically for the depth required, or pretending a 30-minute slot will support a 2-hour question? |
| Who is two echelons above the unit being evaluated? | If the review needs evaluator independence, the immediate next echelon is too close. Where does the two-up rule put us, and is that person convenable? |
| What is the lead time on the review? | The TC's formal-AAR planning starts six to eight weeks before execution. If we are convening last-minute, we should adjust our expectations of formality and aid availability. |
| Have we selected experience over rank for the reviewer? | If we have a choice between a junior person with deep experience in the role being evaluated and a senior person without that experience, the TC's rule is to take experience. |
| Have we identified what *the standard* is? | Without an explicit Army-standard equivalent — a published criterion against which performance can be evaluated — the review will drift toward opinions about what good looks like. |

### Phase 3: Exploring (generating alternatives)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Which organising technique fits the question? | Chronological for *what happened in what order*; BOS-equivalent for *which subsystem failed across phases*; key events for *which moments mattered*. We can switch mid-review when systemic issues emerge. |
| Have we considered the OPFOR perspective? | The TC's rule that the OPFOR commander briefs his plan and intent — what is our equivalent? Whose perspective, when added, would clarify why our action succeeded or failed? Other party in a negotiation, the customer in a product launch, an external auditor, a competitor. |
| Have we considered the *informal* alternative? | The TC's pinecones-and-the-squad-leader worked example is operative: a 15-minute on-the-spot AAR with minimal aids often produces more useful retraining decisions than a fully resourced formal review. Is a formal review really the right scale? |
| Are we using statistics as evidence or as verdict? | If charts are being presented to score the unit's performance rather than to identify trends or reinforce teaching points, they have slipped their proper role. |

### Phase 4: Deciding (analysing and selecting)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply train-to-weakness when allocating retraining resources. | The default allocation is to known weakness, not known strength. If we are about to invest in further development of an already-proficient capability, what is our reason for deviating from the default? |
| Distinguish bad luck from bad decision. | The TC's framing throughout — that there are always weaknesses to improve and strengths to sustain — implies that outcome ≠ decision quality. An action that produced a poor outcome despite competent execution under uncertainty is a different case from an action with poor execution. |
| Stop on critical gate tasks. | If a gate task has not been performed to standard, the decision is to retrain, not to progress. Have we identified our equivalent of gate tasks, and are we honouring the stop? |
| Apply the *no rushing, no waste* discipline. | We should address all key learning points, but not dwell on dead-end issues. Are we doing both? |
| Choose the right discussion-organisation technique for the decision. | If the next decision is *which subsystem needs the most attention*, organise by BOS-equivalent. If the next decision is *which moments to drill into in retraining*, organise by key events. If the next decision is *how the action unfolded in time*, go chronological. |

### Phase 5: Ratifying (committing to and communicating the decision)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Lock in the next-action commitment before the review ends. | The TC's *real-benefits-come-from-applying-results* principle: an AAR that closes without a clear retraining commitment has not produced its benefit. What is the next action, who owns it, and when does it occur? |
| Communicate the retraining commitment even when retraining is delayed. | The TC's rule for delayed retraining: soldiers must understand that the task was not performed to standard and that retraining will occur. The decision-maker's equivalent: if the next action is deferred, the deferral must be visible and timed, not silent. |
| Apply T-P-U assessment honestly. | Trained / Practiced / Untrained is a three-bucket assessment, not a five-star rating. The categorisation drives the future-training concept. Are we using the right grain — three buckets, not a continuous scale? |
| Lock in SOP revisions surfaced by the review. | If the AAR has surfaced SOP problems, the SOP must be revised and the change implemented during future training (Ch 5, "Revised Standing Operating Procedures"). What changes did this review surface, and are they being made before the next iteration? |
| End on a positive note linking to future training. | The TC's closing-comments rule: the AAR leader summarises key points, ends positively, links to next training, then leaves so the unit can discuss in private. The decision-maker's equivalent: communicate the commitment, mark the link to the next action, then create space for the team to process. |

### Phase 6: Monitoring (post-decision review)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Has the retraining decision actually happened? | The benefit lives in the follow-up. Has the next action occurred, on the timeline committed? If not, why not? |
| Has the SOP revision been implemented? | If the review surfaced an SOP problem, has the SOP been revised and used in the next iteration? An unrevised SOP after a review that surfaced a problem is a silent regression. |
| Have we re-checked the T-P-U assessment at the next iteration? | A task that was assessed as Untrained should have moved to Practiced or Trained by the next review. Has the assessment shifted? If not, the retraining has not produced its effect. |
| Have we surfaced events that became fratricide-equivalent? | What near-miss events emerged that should be added to our standing list of severity-overrides-time-budget topics? |
| Is the review producing better next-decisions than the absence of a review would? | The TC's claim is that AARs produce a more cohesive and proficient fighting force. The decision-maker's analogue: is the review-driven cycle producing better decisions than the no-review counterfactual? If not, what is the review doing? |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: The review has drifted into assigning blame.** Signal: participants are defending themselves rather than reconstructing what happened; candour has dried up. Diagnosis: the review has slipped from AAR mode into critique mode — "an AAR does not grade success or failure." Follow-up: reframe explicitly; ask "what happened?" not "why did you do that?"; restore the distinction between evaluating the action and evaluating the person.
- **Pattern: The review closes with no next-action commitment.** Signal: the session ends with observations but no owner, no deadline, no retraining plan. Diagnosis: "the real benefits of AARs come from taking the results and applying them to future training" — without the follow-up decision, the review has not produced its benefit. Follow-up: before the room disperses, name the action, the owner, and the date.
- **Pattern: Known weaknesses are consistently skipped in retraining.** Signal: training cycles invest in areas already assessed as strong; weak areas appear in AAR after AAR without resolution. Diagnosis: train-to-strength bias — the default rule is *train to weakness, not to strength*. Follow-up: invert the allocation; identify the weakest capabilities and concentrate the next training investment there.
- **Pattern: A critical gate-task failure is being treated as a partial score.** Signal: the team has rated a gate capability as "needs improvement" and plans to continue progression. Diagnosis: gate tasks are stop signals, not gradient assessments — "the unit must always retrain and perform critical gate tasks to standard before progressing." Follow-up: halt progression; retrain to standard; retest.
- **Pattern: Statistics are being used to assign verdicts, not identify trends.** Signal: charts are presented to score teams or individuals rather than to surface patterns. Diagnosis: "the danger lies in statistics for statistics' sake" — the TC requires that metrics "identify critical trends or issues and reinforce teaching points." Follow-up: redirect the metric conversation from "what score did we get?" to "what does this trend tell us about what to fix?"
- **Pattern: The reviewer was chosen by rank rather than experience.** Signal: the reviewer lacks direct experience in the role being evaluated. Diagnosis: "if trainers must choose between experience and understanding of current TTPs or rank, they should go with experience." Follow-up: identify a more experienced reviewer, even if more junior in rank.
- **Pattern: A high-severity event got squeezed by time budget.** Signal: a near-miss or fratricide-equivalent event was mentioned briefly but not discussed in depth because "we were running out of time." Diagnosis: the TC's explicit exception — "all incidents or near incidents of fratricide will be discussed in detail" — overrides time-budget discipline. Follow-up: identify severity-override events at the start of the review, not mid-session.

## When to Use This Reference

- A team or unit has completed an operation, project, Sprint, exercise, or training event and needs a structured post-event review to inform the next iteration.
- The review must surface candid multi-perspective evidence, not simply transmit one observer's assessment.
- Retraining allocation decisions must be made after the review (which capabilities to invest in next).
- A stop-vs-proceed decision must be made about whether a critical capability has reached standard.
- A review structure must balance depth on key learning points with realistic time budgets.
- An evaluator for the review must be selected, and the rank-experience trade-off must be resolved.
- The review must remain focused on the action rather than on grading the actor.

## Worked Example

A software-delivery team has completed a painful release cycle: the release was delayed two weeks; a critical defect reached production; the on-call engineer was paged eleven times over three nights. The engineering manager wants to run a post-event review.

Applying the TC's decision framework:

1. *AAR vs critique (Key Concept 2).* The manager resists the urge to deliver a verdict in a team meeting. Instead, she structures a 60-minute session where participants reconstruct the release timeline in sequence. No grades are assigned. The question is "what happened?" not "who is responsible?"

2. *Open-ended questions (Key Concept 11).* She opens with "Walk me through what you saw when the first page came in at 2 a.m." rather than "Why wasn't the monitoring alert configured correctly?" The first question invites narrative; the second invites self-defence.

3. *Fratricide equivalent (Key Concept 9).* The production defect that reached users is the team's fratricide-equivalent: it overrides time-budget discipline. The manager names this at the top of the session. When the discussion runs long on the defect's detection failure, she does not cut it short.

4. *Statistics as evidence, not verdict (Key Concept 6).* The manager presents the eleven-page count as a trend indicator: "This tells us the on-call load concentrated on one person — what conditions produced that?" rather than "This shows our monitoring was inadequate."

5. *Train to weakness (Key Concept 4).* The review surfaces two weaknesses: the team's pre-release checklist was incomplete, and the feature-flag rollback procedure was untested. The team is strong on code review. The manager commits the next Sprint's technical-improvement slot to the checklist and the rollback procedure — not to adding more code-review tooling.

6. *Stop rule (Key Concept 5).* The manager treats the untested rollback procedure as a gate task. Until it is tested to standard in staging, the next release is held. "Not yet ready to proceed" rather than "proceed with caution."

7. *Next-action commitment (Key Concept 12).* Before the session ends: two actions, two owners, two deadlines. The manager verifies both at the next Sprint retrospective.

The TC's contribution is not a new tool but a set of decision rules that prevent the most common failure modes of post-event reviews — drifting into blame, allocating training resources to comfort rather than need, treating severity-override events as one item on a list, and closing without a commitment.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Using a post-event review as a graded verdict on people instead of as evidence input for the next decision.
- Convening a post-event review without identifying the next decision it must inform.
- Training to known strengths because they are visible and rewarding, while known weaknesses go untrained because they are uncomfortable to surface.
- Treating critical gate-task failure as a partial score that the team can work around, rather than as a stop signal.
- Using post-event statistics to assign blame or rate performance rather than to identify trends and reinforce teaching points.
- Choosing reviewers by rank when the rank-experience trade-off favours experience.
- Convening a review at the immediate next echelon up when independence requires two echelons.
- Failing to identify fratricide-equivalent events in advance, then discovering after the fact that the most important thing to discuss got squeezed by time budget.
- Asking yes/no questions when open-ended questions would surface the underlying narrative.
- Closing a review without a next-action commitment and timeline.
- Failing to revise SOPs that the review surfaced as broken, then being surprised when the next iteration repeats the same failure.
- Over-investing in gadgetry and elaborate aids when a notebook, paper, and the leader's attention would do.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The deep reference contains the following `[BT]` citations — doctrine and doctrine-derived frameworks the TC references but does not originate:

- *FM 25-100, Training the Force (November 1988)* — cited as the parent doctrine for training planning, including the requirement to schedule retraining (Ch 5) [BT].
- *FM 25-101, Battle Focused Training (September 1990)* — the TC explicitly supplements and expands FM 25-101 (Preface); Appendix G of FM 25-101 is referenced for additional AAR detail [BT].
- *ARTEP mission training plans (AMTPs) and soldier's manuals* — cited as the source of training-and-evaluation outlines (TEOs) providing tasks, conditions, and standards (Ch 2, Ch 3) [BT].
- *Combined Arms Training Strategy (CATS)* — cited as the source from which critical gate tasks are derived (Ch 5) [BT]. Key Concept 5 traces here.
- *Combat training centers (CTCs)* — cited as the source of professional AAR practice the TC distils (App A) [BT].

**Named limits of the source.** The TC is a 1993 military training circular for Army unit training reviews. Its concepts transfer cleanly to any post-event review context (software retrospectives, clinical case reviews, incident retrospectives), but its worked examples and terminology (OPFOR, BOS, platoon/company/battalion echelons, TTPs) are military-specific. Practitioners mapping TC concepts to non-military contexts must translate the structural rules rather than import the vocabulary directly. The TC does not address psychological safety explicitly, trauma-informed review practices, or the just-culture legal exposure that may be relevant in healthcare or safety-critical industry contexts — those gaps are served by the NHS Just Culture Guide and LFUO 2024 distillations.

**Evidence-marker continuity.** This distillation paraphrases throughout; verbatim passages live in the deep reference (`tc-25-20-army-aar-deep.md`). Key Concepts are `[AP]` (the TC's stated rules and positions) or `[V]` (direct quotations). The TC contains no borrowed-through theoretical frameworks in the sense of named academic authors; its borrowed-through citations are to other Army doctrine documents (FM 25-100, FM 25-101, CATS, AMTPs) that supply the standards the TC's evaluation rules apply against.
