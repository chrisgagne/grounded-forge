## Author's thesis (paraphrased from source)

The Guide's organising claim is that online learning is built by *facilitation*, not by content delivery — and that the facilitator's role is to support discovery rather than to transmit knowledge. Chapter 1 establishes the working definition: facilitation in education is helping learners "understand course content through questioning and suggestions while providing rich cases, complex problems, and opportunities to apply new knowledge in different contexts" rather than "acting as the 'sage on the stage'" [AP] (Ch 1, "What is facilitation in an educational context?"). The Guide cites the Institute of Cultural Affairs – UK's definition of the facilitator as "…in working with a group, enabling and supporting them to achieve their objectives in a way that involves and respects all contributions, builds ownership and releases the potential of the group and its members" [V] [BT] (Ch 1, "The definition"). The Community of Inquiry model is named as the framework that first identified the importance of "facilitation of learning through listening, connecting ideas, and involving learners in '…meaningful and educationally worthwhile learning'" in online contexts [V] [BT] (Ch 1, "Facilitating learning online", quoting Vaughan et al, 2013, p.47).

A second load-bearing claim is that FLO facilitators *model* the practices they teach. Chapter 3 frames this as the through-line of the FLO design: "in addition to talking about online facilitation skills with our participants throughout the course, we also model those same skills. We want our participants to see us continually working on enhancing the skills in ourselves – to model reflective practice as an online facilitator and to bring an authentic presence to our courses" [V] (Ch 3, "Developing FLO facilitation skills"). Modelling appears throughout — synchronous-session design models good facilitation; the Welcome post models good facilitation; the facilitator's Learning Journal modelling models reflective practice; the FLIF that facilitators complete alongside participants models constructive reflection. The mechanism is congruence between what facilitators say and what facilitators do.

A third load-bearing claim is that facilitator presence shifts over the arc of a course. Chapter 3 describes the move: "During the first week of each course, facilitator presence is high as the facilitators take on the roles of a community builder and a guide" — welcoming, posting reminders, clarifying curriculum, troubleshooting, encouraging participation [V] (Ch 3, "Roles and functions of FLO facilitators"). "As the course moves on and learners become more familiar with the course expectations, content and environment, they become more active participants in the co-creation of meaningful learning. FLO facilitators take on the roles of coach and mentor. They step back and encourage learning through the use of different questioning strategies and proven facilitation techniques. They participate in discussions but don't lead; instead, FLO facilitators highlight important concepts, weave together the ideas that participants share, and post frequent summaries to help learners keep track of the flow of conversations" [V] (Ch 3, "Roles and functions of FLO facilitators"). This is the *scaffolding-then-fading* arc: heavy presence early; lighter as the group develops capacity.

A fourth load-bearing claim is that reflective practice is the engine of facilitator development. Throughout the Guide, the facilitator is portrayed as a continuing learner. "Developing skills in online facilitation is a journey, not something that can be accomplished over one iteration of a course! We encourage people interested in facilitating FLO courses to spend some time reading about online facilitation, open education practices and Universal Design for Learning, before facilitating FLO courses" [V] (Ch 3, "Developing FLO facilitation skills"). The mentorship model — apprenticeship under an experienced facilitator, mutual exchange of time and expertise, gradual handoff — is the Guide's recommended pathway: "the best way to learn how to facilitate a FLO course is to co-facilitate with a more experienced facilitator as a mentor" [V] (Ch 3, "Our model").

A fifth load-bearing claim is that *climate* and *community* are operational concerns, not soft skills. The four core skill categories named in Chapter 3 are: Support Diverse Learners Online; Build and Sustain Online Community; Manage Course Online; Model Effective Online Facilitation [AP] (Ch 3, "Developing FLO facilitation skills", "Core FLO Facilitation Skills" table). Two of the four are about community and climate; community building takes the entire first week of Fundamentals; the privacy-and-confidentiality framing ("Vegas rules" — what happens in the course stays in the course) is a community-design choice [V] (Ch 4, "Privacy and Confidentiality"; Ch 5, "Privacy and confidentiality"; Ch 6, "Privacy and confidentiality"). The Guide frames privacy and trust as foundations, not as legal compliance: "our learning relies on the exchange of honest, constructive feedback, and we need to agree on rules that support this foundation of trust" [V] (Ch 4, "Privacy and Confidentiality").

A sixth load-bearing claim is that the course-design choices that shape facilitation are mostly made before the course begins. Each course chapter (4, 5, 6, 7) opens with a Facilitator Task List structured around "Several weeks (or months) before the course begins"; "One to two weeks before the course begins"; "A few days before the course begins"; then week-by-week running. The Guide's directive form throughout: prepare so the course can run lightly, not so the facilitator can run hard during the course.

## Part I: What facilitation is and is not (Ch 1)

### Definitions, framings, and the educational-context lineage

The Oxford English Dictionary's verb-form definition, *make (an action or process) easy or easier*, opens the chapter [V] (Ch 1, "The definition"). The Institute of Cultural Affairs – UK's definition follows: "…in working with a group, enabling and supporting them to achieve their objectives in a way that involves and respects all contributions, builds ownership and releases the potential of the group and its members" [V] [BT] (Ch 1, "The definition").

The Guide then traces the pedagogical shift that produced facilitation-as-method. "As educational philosophies developed and a growing number of adults began to participate in higher education (not only youth or young adults), beliefs and pedagogical practices changed to accommodate the richer experience and knowledge that learners brought to the classroom. Adult learning theory had a profound impact on the way courses were designed and delivered, and the developing ideas around social constructivist learning and humanist teaching approaches influenced many instructors to move from acting as the 'sage on the stage' and instead, to begin supporting or scaffolding the learning that was meaningful for each student (e.g., acting as the 'guide on the side')" [V] (Ch 1, "What is facilitation in an educational context?"). The 21st-century-skills argument follows: "traditional approaches were not successful in developing the 21st-century skills that learners needed (e.g., critical thinking and the ability to communicate effectively, innovate, and solve problems through negotiation and collaboration). Research consistently suggested that collaborative learning and personalized learning strategies were successful in supporting the deeper learning needed" [V] (Ch 1, "What is facilitation in an educational context?", citing UNESCO/Scott 2015 in footnote).

The transition from direct teaching to facilitative teaching is the chapter's move: "Many teachers began to modify their practices to include facilitative teaching rather than direct teaching. While teaching methods will still vary depending on the subject, level of learning or intended outcomes, the focus is generally on helping learners understand course content through questioning and suggestions while providing rich cases, complex problems, and opportunities to apply new knowledge in different contexts" [V] (Ch 1, "What is facilitation in an educational context?").

### Community of Inquiry as the framing model for online

The Community of Inquiry model is named as one of the first frameworks to identify "the increasing role of the learner and the importance of having teachers facilitate social and cognitive interactions. Research collected over more than 18 years demonstrated the importance of facilitation of learning through listening, connecting ideas, and involving learners in '…meaningful and educationally worthwhile learning'" [V] [BT] (Ch 1, "Facilitating learning online", quoting Vaughan et al, 2013, p.47).

## Part II: The FLO Family and what makes it unique (Ch 2)

### The five courses and their durations

The FLO family is "a growing collection of one- to five-week online courses that are intended to enhance the skills required to confidently and effectively facilitate learning online" [V] (Ch 2, "Overview"). The five courses are: **FLO Fundamentals** (5 weeks, introduces research-based adult and online learning concepts; alternates learner and facilitator roles); **FLO Design** (4 weeks, instructional and learning design with weekly studio forums); **FLO Synchronous** (3 weeks, planning and facilitating synchronous online sessions with two-track choice of Reviewing Participant or Practicing Facilitator); **FLO MicroCourses** (1 week, single-topic, hands-on, "emergent" topics) [V] (Ch 2, "The courses"). FLO Fundamentals is the foundation; FLO Design and FLO Synchronous follow; MicroCourses run alongside.

### The FLO story and lineage

"The first Facilitating Learning Online course was called Instructional Skills Workshop Online (ISWO) and was developed by Royal Roads University in 2008 through the BCcampus Online Program Development fund. This course was based on the well-known Instructional Skills Workshop, but focused on facilitating online, rather than in person" [V] (Ch 2, "The FLO story (the short version!)"). FLO Fundamentals as such dates to 2013 (an adaptation of ISWO, revised several times). In 2015, the workshop was renamed from ISWO to FLO. FLO Design and FLO Synchronous emerged later; MicroCourses were added "to appeal to faculty and staff who were unable to commit to a course spanning several weeks" [V] (Ch 2, "The FLO story (the short version!)"). All FLO courses are open educational resources (OER) "available for institutions to implement in-house" [V] (Ch 2, "The FLO story (the short version!)").

### What makes FLO unique (the Guide's contrarian framing)

The list captures the Guide's philosophy and approach. Selected claims (Ch 2, "What makes FLO unique?"):

> "The FLO experience is very different from an independent and self-directed study."

> "Practice, seek advice and feedback, and share learning in a small, supportive community of peers; this is not a MOOC!"

[V] (Ch 2, "What makes FLO unique?"). Courses are licensed CC BY; participants experience the role of learner while learning about and experiencing the role of facilitator; participants experience collaborative teamwork while doing it; participation is full-time throughout — "these courses are a commitment!"; participants are co-located with colleagues from various locations, settings, and academic disciplines; reflective practice is integral. The contrast against MOOCs and against independent study is sharp: FLO is participatory, small-cohort, peer-supported, time-bounded, and facilitated.

## Part III: Facilitating FLO — accessible design, roles, and the development model (Ch 3)

### Accessible design and inclusive facilitation

Chapter 3 opens with Universal Design for Learning (UDL) as the framework: "Many educators point to the Universal Design for Learning framework, which focuses on providing multiple means of: Engagement; Representation; Action and Expression" [V] (Ch 3, "Accessible design, inclusive facilitation", citing CAST 2018 in footnote). Inclusive facilitation is framed as ongoing rather than achieved: "Ensuring accessibility of learning is an ongoing quest, as technologies change and we gain experience in online facilitation of FLO courses" [V] (Ch 3, "Accessible design, inclusive facilitation").

The Guide poses four diagnostic questions for facilitators on accessibility:

> - "Do I have materials (visual, multimedia, documents) that present important concepts or information that not all students may be able to see, hear, or access?
> - Do I use facilitation strategies that may exclude some learners or present barriers to participation?
> - If I utilize new apps or tools to encourage exploration of online teaching and learning, do I have alternatives for those who can't (or don't wish to) use these technologies?
> - As I think about the diversity of my learners, can I imagine how they perceive the learning environment and experience my presentations, comments, interaction with learners?"
> (Ch 3, "Accessible design, inclusive facilitation")

### Roles and functions — the scaffolding-and-fading arc

The Guide's role description sets the developmental arc explicitly. Early in a course, facilitator presence is high; facilitators are "community builder and a guide" who "welcome and engage learners, post frequent reminders, clarify curriculum, troubleshoot problems, and encourage each learner to participate and learn" [V] (Ch 3, "Roles and functions of FLO facilitators"). As the course moves on, "facilitators take on the roles of coach and mentor. They step back and encourage learning through the use of different questioning strategies and proven facilitation techniques. They participate in discussions but don't lead; instead, FLO facilitators highlight important concepts, weave together the ideas that participants share, and post frequent summaries to help learners keep track of the flow of conversations as they move in and out of the course environment" [V] (Ch 3, "Roles and functions of FLO facilitators"). Toward course close, facilitators help participants complete activities, assess their progress, and host a final wrap-up to celebrate achievements [AP] (Ch 3, "Roles and functions of FLO facilitators").

The Guide is explicit that FLO facilitators "strive to identify and respond to the varying needs, abilities and interests of each learner. The courses are developmental in focus. Although FLO facilitators provide frequent and constructive (formative) feedback, they encourage participants to use the self-assessment rubrics and reflective practices embedded in each course to monitor and celebrate their own achievements" [V] (Ch 3, "Roles and functions of FLO facilitators"). Self-assessment is the default mode; facilitator assessment is supplementary.

### Four core skill categories

The Guide identifies four core FLO facilitation skill categories with explicit sub-skills (Ch 3, "Developing FLO facilitation skills"):

1. **Support Diverse Learners Online** — communicate goals and roles, invite cultural and acquired knowledge, support effective online learning strategies, encourage participatory connected learning, encourage risk-taking, promote reflective practice and critical/creative thinking, provide timely formative feedback, assist learners in self-assessment using rubrics [AP].

2. **Build and Sustain Online Community** — build a safe and supportive learning community, use strategies to build rapport, develop guidelines for constructive feedback, model respectful appreciative dialogue, encourage learners to identify norms of behaviour, assist learners in developing positive responses to conflicts, connect with external experts, explore open learning resources or communities, close community by supporting final reflections [AP].

3. **Manage Course Online** — design functional learning spaces easy to navigate, select appropriate tools, share content in varied ways, maintain currency and relevance of references, provide varied ways to participate or complete assignments [AP].

4. **Model Effective Online Facilitation** — model positive professional presence, plan effectively with co-facilitator(s), manage time effectively, seek regular feedback, maintain balanced presence, use varied questioning techniques, develop strategies to monitor participation, engage in and model reflective practice, explore new pedagogical approaches, develop technical skills [AP].

The Guide draws skills from the self-assessment rubric and crowd-sourced ideas from FLO facilitators, with inspiration from the International Association of Facilitators (IAF) Core Competencies [BT] (Ch 3, "Developing FLO facilitation skills").

### The mentorship development model

The Guide's mentorship pathway is four-step (Ch 3, "Our model"):

- **Take FLO courses.** Experience the roles of online learner, collaborative team member, activity designer, and facilitator; complete FLO courses in any sequence. "Experiencing the role of participant in the course you wish to facilitate is one of the best ways to understand the structure, flow and resources that make up the course. You will also experience the value of facilitated learning" [V].
- **Apply what you've learned.** Completing one or more FLO courses provides new ideas for one's own teaching; share with colleagues; consider scholarly research [AP].
- **Become a FLO facilitator.** "The best way to learn how to facilitate a FLO course is to co-facilitate with a more experienced facilitator as a mentor" [V]. Mentees and facilitators negotiate level of commitment and tasks.
- **Adopt FLO / Become a lead facilitator and mentor.** Once an institution has experienced FLO facilitators, the next step is to bring more facilitators on board; experienced facilitators mentor incoming ones [AP].

Two participant testimonials illustrate the pathway: Leonne Beebe ("The FLO/FDO mentoring model for learning to facilitate online makes the learning both real and realizable. As a teacher, you have the concurrent experience of being a student while also being a teacher. Through your experiences, you will develop an awareness and empathy for the learning challenges and the strategies needed for student success. You may also find one time is just the beginning…") [V] [AE] (Ch 3, "Our model"); and Rachel Loganberg, who progressed from an observer role in FLO Design pilot to co-facilitator and lead [AE] (Ch 3, "Our model").

### Tools to support FLO

The Guide's tooling section frames technology as instrumental, not aspirational. FLO courses generally require: registration and login accounts (for privacy and event management); persistent text-based discussion forums (preferably with images, audio, and video); a place to store resources; a synchronous web-conferencing tool (with the ability to assign facilitator-level access for participant practice) [V] (Ch 3, "Foundational or essential technologies"). Additional features that may help: quick surveys and quizzes; learner-created short videos or audio; voting or preference-indication within forums; private or shared group forums [V] (Ch 3, "Important additional technologies").

When considering tools outside what the institution supports, the Guide poses six diagnostic questions:

> - "Why do I want to use this tool? What value do I hope it can provide to the learning experience of my learners?
> - Does my institution have guidelines or rules about the use of social media or the protection of learner privacy?
> - What characteristics or affordances does the tool offer that I believe will benefit the learners?
> - What impact will this have on my learners? Will they need to sign up for an account? Can I use this tool and protect their privacy?
> - Can I provide alternatives to learners who don't want to use this tool?
> - What happens to anything we record or create using this tool? Sometimes free social media tools retain copyright to what you create when you use their tool. It may not matter but you should consider the implications before using a new tool."
> (Ch 3, "Using open source or social media tools")

## Part IV: FLO Fundamentals — the five-week course and the FLIF reflection process (Ch 4)

### Course shape and time commitment

FLO Fundamentals is "mostly asynchronous… delivered over five sequential weeks. Two or three one-hour synchronous sessions are optional but recommended to facilitate community building and to provide valuable exposure to synchronous facilitation" [V] (Ch 4, "What is FLO Fundamentals?"). The facilitator's primary role: "build and sustain the online community: pre-planning the course, communicating regularly with participants, providing supplementary materials, organizing and coaching teams, and supporting participants' experimentation with a variety of online learning tools" [V] (Ch 4, "What is FLO Fundamentals?").

The course structure is participatory: in Week 1 the facilitators get everyone enrolled and introduced and launch the Building Online Community forum; by end of Week 1 participants are organised into four teams; each team facilitates an assigned topic for one of the following weeks; the course facilitators "provide support from the sidelines and are less visible in the discussion" during participant-led weeks [V] (Ch 4, "What is FLO Fundamentals?"). The topics across the five weeks: Week 1 Building Learning Community (course facilitators); Week 2 Diversity of Learners (orange team); Week 3 Responsive Facilitation (blue team); Week 4 Collaboration (yellow team); Week 5 Reflective Practice (green team) [V] (Ch 4, "Course content and layout").

**Time commitments.** Facilitators: 15–25 hours per week during preparation and implementation; first-time hosts may require more; "available to the course on a daily basis" [V] (Ch 4, Synopsis/abstract). Participants: 6–12 hours per week; "more time will be required during the week the participant is facilitating the discussion with their team" [V] (Ch 4, Synopsis/abstract).

**Class size.** "A small class of 10 or fewer participants will make it difficult to sustain rich online conversations. On the other hand, a large class of more than 25 may be more than 2 facilitators can handle. Our experience has shown that a class of 14 – 20 educators is a good size for this course" [V] (Ch 4, "What's the right number of participants for this course?", sidebar).

### Privacy, confidentiality, and Vegas rules

"In all FLO courses, our learning relies on the exchange of honest, constructive feedback, and we need to agree on rules that support this foundation of trust" [V] (Ch 4, "Privacy and Confidentiality"). The Guide proposes:

- Always seek permission to share content posted by participants.
- When using social media or other online services to complete activities, ensure familiarity with privacy levels and storage/sharing.
- If recording synchronous sessions, confirm OK with session participants *before* hitting record.
- Let participants know who else has access to the course (participants, facilitators, assistants, website administrators).
- "You may wish to establish 'Vegas rules,' i.e. what happens in the course, stays in the course" [V] (Ch 4, "Privacy and Confidentiality").

### Welcome post and instructor presence

The Welcome post is portrayed as a facilitator's first move: "this is your first impression. You might like to talk a bit about your teaching and other professional experience. You could share an anecdote or advice from your own online facilitation experience, or use an icebreaker-type format" [V] (Ch 4, "Your Welcome post doesn't have to be just text…"). Tips: model "an attitude of fearless experimentation with communication technologies" (video against backdrop, narrated slideshow); make sure the post sets a good impression — "establishes instructor presence, presents a clear message without being too long or too short"; "be sure your introductory post ends with an invitation for your participants to share their introductions in a similar way" [V] (Ch 4, "Your Welcome post doesn't have to be just text…").

### Week-1 craft: building community

Week 1's facilitator role is to "establish a strong facilitator presence and effectively demonstrate what good online facilitation looks like. Aim to do this as transparently as possible; think out loud to your participants to show them how you plan and facilitate the various activities" [V] (Ch 4, "Week 1: Building Online Community"). The Guide includes an explicit "Be pro-active in the first week" sidebar: "If it doesn't come up spontaneously in the conversation: mention that the first week of an online course is often overwhelming (for both participants and facilitators), that it will get better, and that you will do what you can to mitigate start-up stresses" [V] (Ch 4, sidebar at Week 1 Day 2).

The "Helping participants cope" sidebar names a concrete operational concern: "If your LMS offers forum 'subscriptions' (in which participants automatically receive an email copy of all forum posts) make sure that not all forums are configured this way, and let participants know how to 'unsubscribe' from a forum whenever possible" [V] (Ch 4, sidebar at Week 1).

A participant testimony quoted in the chapter illustrates first-week overwhelm: one participant wrote "I have a deep empathy for my students now on the first week experience. I think when we 'know the material' we forget what it feels like to 'not know.' As a student in my first online course, I now know how it feels to be in that place and will be more understanding of my students" [V] [AE] (Ch 4, "Time management, reflection and empathy"); another quoted Week-1 reading: "When a person is feeling anxious, the likelihood that they will interpret things negatively increases" [V] [AE].

### Sample reflective post — Anxious Annie

Leonne Beebe's Weekly Journal Share is quoted in full as a worked example:

> "Week 1 of any course, especially an online course, is exciting and challenging. Seeing the FLO online community beginning to develop and learning of participants helping each other is motivating and rewarding. However, one of my 'imaginary facilitator friends' known as Anxious Annie has a habit of showing up in Week 1 and hanging around. She keeps asking me, 'What about those who haven't posted by the suggested timeline? What about those who haven't started working on their week's planning yet? What about those who haven't posted their journal yet?' Then, I remember facilitating is not about me; it's about helping the participants as they need and ask for help. There is a saying that RRU folks often refer to called 'Trust the process.' As I gain more experience and confidence with facilitating, the more I am able to trust the process and ignore Annie. Besides, it's only 5:00 Sunday afternoon. There is lots of time left…"
> [V] [AE] (Ch 4, Week 1 end-of-week, "Example of reflective post")

The example is illustrative of the Guide's stance toward facilitator anxiety: name the worry, locate it as one's own pattern rather than as a participant problem, trust the process.

### The shifting role across weeks 2–5

"Weeks 2 through 5 follow a relatively similar pattern: as co-facilitators, you will post a message at the beginning to welcome participants to this week's topic and work, and you will post a summary to close the week. Throughout the week, you will monitor discussions and join in as appropriate. Although your guidance may be more hands-on in Weeks 1 and 2, by Weeks 4 and 5 you should find yourself pulling back and encouraging this week's facilitation team to manage the discussion. Ideally, your participation will always be supportive – but not overwhelming — as you strive to scaffold your participants towards developing good facilitation skills" [V] (Ch 4, "Your shifting role: From facilitator to guide").

### Engagement and pacing — three operating notes

The Guide gives several operating notes for handling participant engagement variance:

> "Are some participants not as engaged as you expect? Although full participation is ideal, the reality is that people have work, family, and other responsibilities that can cause disruptions in their ability to participate. Do what you can to assist individuals in these circumstances. Offer the opportunity to complete the work before or immediately after an absence, or suggest an alternate assignment or activity."
> (Ch 4, sidebar at Week 3)

On weekly overview posts: "Your overview post or weekly bulletin is an excellent way to remind participants about what's happening this week and who's facilitating what topic. You can mention any key deadlines or other events (e.g. a long weekend) that might impact the flow of the course. You can also use this opportunity to highlight things that went well, or to give credit to someone who shared a great resource or moved a discussion forward in a skillful way" [V] (Ch 4, "Keeping participants on-track each week").

Sylvia Currie's post-Thanksgiving overview post is quoted as a worked example of regrouping: "It isn't unusual for the steady rhythm in an online course to take some effort to get back on track. We all know why this happens, but what do you do about it as a facilitator?" — followed by four moves: celebrate initiative; support those struggling; suggest something to focus on; make any necessary adjustments to the plan [V] [AE] (Ch 4, "Example of an overview post").

### Self-assessment rubrics

FLO Fundamentals includes two rubrics. **Team Facilitation Self-Assessment** (facilitator role) uses five criteria — Plans effectively with team member(s); Communicates goals and roles for learning activity; Guides participants through the activity; Demonstrates strategies to encourage community; Engages in reflective practice — across three developmental levels (Beginning / Developing / Accomplished) [V] (Ch 4, "Team Facilitation Self-Assessment (Facilitator Role)"). **Weekly Participation Self-Assessment** (participant role) uses four criteria — Relevance; Fosters the development of online community; Engages in the activities; Reflective practice — across the same three levels, scored each week with a 15-per-week maximum and 60-over-five-weeks maximum [V] (Ch 4, "Weekly Participation Self-Assessment (Participant Role)"). The Guide is explicit about purpose: "This course is designed to be developmental; it is not a graded course in the traditional sense. Unless you decide otherwise, your participants will not be marked or assigned a formal grade on their performance" [V] (Ch 4, "Course assessment strategies"). The rubric "could be used to assign a grade, if needed", but the default mode is self-assessment as developmental signal.

### The FLIF facilitation process (Feel, Like, Improve, Feedback)

The FLIF is the Guide's structured reflection tool for participant facilitation teams. "The FLIF (Feel, Like, Improve, Feedback) survey is a valuable way to help your participant facilitation teams debrief their facilitation experience. As each team completes their facilitation week, you can strongly encourage them to complete the 6-question FLIF survey (see below). You can assist the team with this reflection by modelling the FLIF process yourself" [V] (Ch 4, "The FLIF Facilitation Process").

The six FLIF questions:

> 1. How do I feel about the session? Reflect on the strategies you used to facilitate and their impact on participants. Do you feel participants achieved the learning outcomes? How do you know?
> 2. What do I like about what I did? Think about the aspects of your mini-session that you felt good about.
> 3. What do I want to improve or do differently? Consider what you would like to do better next time you are in a facilitation role like this.
> 4. What were the key points about the feedback I received from my participants about my facilitation? Review the feedback you received from your participants. What conclusions can you synthesize? What are the take-away ideas you'll want to consider for next time?
> 5. How will this facilitation experience impact my teaching? What will you take away from this experience that you can apply to your own courses?
> 6. Are there some additional points I would like feedback on? Are there any specific aspects of your facilitation experience you'd like your course facilitators to respond to?
> (Ch 4, "The FLIF Facilitation Process")

The Guide notes that FLIF responses are private — "shared only with course co-facilitators" — and includes two worked anonymised examples of facilitator responses to illustrate honest reflective practice [V] [AE] (Ch 4, "Explanatory notes for participant facilitators").

The Guide's framing of how feedback reaches facilitating teams in FLO is also worked out in a transcribed short video (Ch 4, "Example of video feedback with transcript"): facilitators support behind the scenes during the week with open-ended questions ("how do you think things are going, and maybe if they think something else needs to be done"), the team asks for feedback at end-of-week through varied feedback structures (modelling), then the FLO facilitator responds individually to each FLIF — "I try to be positive here, but I do try to bring up things that maybe they could have done differently or suggest alternatives. It almost becomes like a little conversation in the FLIF about how things could have gone if they had done something different, and also giving them kudos for what they did that I saw that was going really well" [V] (Ch 4, "Example of video feedback with transcript").

## Part V: FLO Design — the studio-based design course (Ch 5)

### Course shape and the studio-based approach

FLO Design is a four-week course in which "participants are introduced to a wide range of ideas about how people learn, best practices and recommended pedagogies to engage and support online learners, and several respected quality and accessibility frameworks to guide their design efforts" [V] (Ch 5, "What is FLO Design?"). Participants identify a design project and develop a short unit of learning (2–3 weeks of an online course or equivalent) plus a sample prototype learning activity.

The distinctive feature is the *studio*: "FLO Design goes a step further by building in weekly Studio design forums. FLO Facilitators offer expert advice and suggestions, but also acknowledge the diverse range of skills and experiences that learners bring and invite them to help each other clarify and develop their design projects" [V] (Ch 5, "More details"). "A unique aspect of the FLO Design course is the use of weekly collaborative Studio forums to encourage participants to share their emerging designs and to review constructive feedback provided by the FLO Facilitators (and contributed by other participants)" [V] (Ch 5, "A studio-based design approach"). The studio is constituted as a recurring weekly forum thread where each participant posts emerging design progress, the chosen approach, integrations from synchronous sessions, the drawing or visual method, and questions [AP] (Ch 5, "Example: Week 2 studio forum").

**Time commitments.** Facilitators: ~15 hours per week, more in Week 1 and Week 4 (showcase organisation); first-time hosts may need more; daily availability [V] (Ch 5, Synopsis/abstract). Participants: ~6–8 hours per week; more for newcomers; additional time for showcase preparation [V] (Ch 5, Synopsis/abstract).

### Four-week shape

- **Week 1 Getting started.** Use posts to begin developing a sense of community and instructor presence; respond quickly but build independence; reinforce essential tasks (decide on a design project, alone or with another, end the week with understanding of what's ahead); first synchronous session (launch, agreements, monitoring/asking-for-assistance) [AP] (Ch 5, "Week 1: Getting started"). Group agreements are introduced; the Guide cites Seeds for Change for the practice [BT].
- **Week 2 Sparks to design.** Encourage participants to connect and continue building shared purpose; draw attention to important learning theories or design approaches; second synchronous session to spark new ideas (interesting examples, guest presenters); make explicit links from emerging designs to theories and approaches [AP] (Ch 5, "Week 2: Sparks to design").
- **Week 3 Projects under construction.** "Your facilitation this week will be both broad and deep! Connect with any participants who seem to be struggling while celebrating the 'aha' design moments and accomplishments" [V] (Ch 5, "Week 3: Projects under construction"). Take feedback to deeper levels (FLO Design rubric, learning/design theories, quality guidelines); keep discussions learner-focused; encourage visual representations; "stay calm and carry on" — find ways to lessen anxiety; "be open to modifying your approach/expectations to adapt to the needs of your cohort. The purpose is to help them develop, NOT to create a 'do-or-die' atmosphere" [V] (Ch 5, "Week 3: Projects under construction").
- **Week 4 Showcase designs.** Manage Showcase sessions (4–5 presentations per one-hour session, 10–15 minutes each with brief questions and feedback); facilitator and peer feedback; final reflective activity ("Looking Back/Looking Forward" — adapted from FLO foundation workshop); close the course (synchronous celebration if possible) [AP] (Ch 5, "Week 4: Showcase designs").

### Studio-management craft

"Managing the clarity of Studio design posts: Not all participants will recognize the importance of linking back to a core design document and using each week's Studio forum to post new design thinking or responses to constructive feedback. You can scaffold the process by creating links or inserting explanatory posts that encourage each participant to explain their thinking and highlight changes in their designs from week-to-week" [V] (Ch 5, "Week 3"). "Encourage peer-to-peer feedback: Find ways to elicit more meaningful posts; don't accept 'that's great' superficial responses" [V] (Ch 5, "Week 3").

The Guide makes the case for visual design: "The design of each project is changing each week. In addition, the underlying reasons for particular activities, assessment, and approaches are explained in lengthy paragraphs in Studio topic threads. A simple drawing or flowchart can allow participants and facilitators to quickly grasp the overall design and ask cogent questions or provide useful suggestions" [V] (Ch 5, "Why should I draw or map my design?").

### Reflection design choice — focused question vs nuggets

The Guide names a design choice for reflective practice: "Posting a new question mid-week allows you to direct the attention of participants to emerging design or related issues" *or* "Encouraging participants to share nuggets from their personal learning journals can introduce new perspectives and unexpected ideas that stimulate creative thinking" [V] (Ch 5, "Design choices – Weekly reflections"). The FLO Design OER uses a focused-question approach to "deepen the level of reflection and to encourage more meaningful discussions… Each week, a facilitator would pose a 'thought' question to direct each participant's attention to a core concept or important theory/practice. Then they ask participants to NOT question or comment on each other's reflective posts; instead, they asked participants to pursue relevant reflections within the Studio forums so the discussions could directly inform/improve the emerging designs. Each approach has potential benefits; choose what you believe will be of most benefit to your participants" [V] (Ch 5, "Encouraging critical thinking through reflection").

### FLO Design rubric

Five criteria across three levels (Beginning / Developing / Accomplished): Structure of Design Project Plan; Communication of Plan in Studio; Feedback on Plan; Participation in Workshop Events/Activities; Reflective Practice [V] (Ch 5, "FLO Design Self-Assessment Rubric"). The rubric "uses five criteria and a three-stage developmental structure"; FLO Facilitators provide constructive feedback "based on each participants' stated design goals, and they are also guided by a course quality framework (see Week 3 Overview, Elements of Quality)" [V] (Ch 5, "Assessment of learning").

## Part VI: FLO Synchronous — the two-track three-week course (Ch 6)

### Course shape and the two-track choice

FLO Synchronous is a three-week course that "helps people develop synchronous online facilitation skills. Participants learn best practice strategies to plan for, facilitate, and follow up after synchronous online learning events. FLO Synchronous provides participants with opportunities to see and learn from examples of synchronous online facilitation and, if desired, to practice synchronous online facilitation skills in a safe environment" [V] (Ch 6, "What is FLO Synchronous?").

The two tracks: **Reviewing Participant** (focused on reviewing and discussing best practices in synchronous online facilitation and providing feedback) and **Practicing Facilitator** (focused on planning for and facilitating a short synchronous online event and receiving feedback). "This flexibility allows people to choose what kind of learning is best for them at the time they take the course" [V] (Ch 6, "What is FLO Synchronous?"). Participants must choose their track by end of Week 1.

Class size and target distribution: "FLO Synchronous works well with 10 or more participants and can accommodate participant numbers into the 20s, most likely with two facilitators" [V] (Ch 6, "What is FLO Synchronous?"). "Say, for example, that you have a class size of 20. You wouldn't want only two of the 20 participants to choose the Practicing Facilitator track! Ideally 6-8 participants or more would choose to go that route in that size of class" [V] (Ch 6, "Help participants choose which track they want to be on for the course"). Active encouragement of Practicing Facilitator choice is part of the facilitator's job: "part of our role as course facilitators is to encourage participants to take a risk and choose the potentially harder track of Practicing Facilitator, when their natural inclination might be to play it safe on the Reviewing Participants track" [V] (Ch 6, "Help participants choose which track they want to be on for the course").

### Three-week shape

- **Week 1 Preparing for synchronous sessions.** Welcome post; Video Introductions activity (90-second video clips responding to one or two prompts); first synchronous session (technical skills — turning on microphones, using chat, raising hands; community building; track decision); Tech Times (informal exploration of the web-conferencing platform); track choice declaration by end of week [AP] (Ch 6, "Week 1: Preparing for synchronous sessions").
- **Week 2 Facilitating synchronous sessions.** Second synchronous session (Facilitation Session Guide, active participatory environment, moderator perspective, feedback-eliciting activity); the Guide cites Liberating Structures (1-2-4-All, User Experience Fishbowl) as worked examples [BT]; connect with Practicing Facilitators about requirements; connect with Reviewing Participants about expectations; Sample Synchros activity (participants watch short video examples of others facilitating synchronously); remind about Self-Assessment Rubric [AP] (Ch 6, "Week 2: Facilitating synchronous sessions").
- **Week 3 Following up after synchronous sessions.** "The first few days of Week 3 are usually a rush of attending Practicing Facilitator sessions" [V] (Ch 6, "Week 3"). Attend as many Practicing Facilitator sessions as possible (or split with co-facilitator); if missing a session, watch the recording, fill out Facilitation Session Guide, give robust feedback by end of Week 3. Final synchronous session — gather one last time, look back on the experience, reflect on learning; optional co-facilitation by a participant; "do your lesson planning for your final synchronous session in the open by posting the lesson plan in a Google Doc or other shared site… Invite them to make comments in the document" as an additional behind-the-scenes learning opportunity [V] (Ch 6, "Final synchronous session", sidebar). Write closing Open Forum post; respond to self-assessment rubrics after the course; read course feedback forms; debrief with co-facilitators [AP] (Ch 6, "After the course").

### Tracking matrix for engagement equity

The Guide proposes a participant-engagement tracking discipline: "One idea to help you track interactions with your participants to ensure you engage with them fairly equally throughout the course is to use a tracking matrix. This can be a simple spreadsheet with all your participants' names in the first column on the left and many blank columns next to them on the right. Every time you respond to a participant, make a mark in a column next to their name. Over time, you can use this matrix to try to spread out your forum post responses to participants. This may help each participant feel heard by you as their facilitator throughout the course" [V] (Ch 6, "One idea to help you track interactions").

### Counsel-out as a facilitator move

The Guide is explicit that not every registration should proceed: "FLO courses requires a lot of commitment and participation in order to be effective. If you find out a participant is deeply struggling, perhaps from an overwhelming schedule, your role is to help them decide if now is the right time to take the course. We strongly advise against allowing participants to audit FLO courses for many reasons, the least of which is that they will not be able to achieve course learning outcomes if they do" [V] (Ch 6, "Be in touch with participants who haven't logged in/aren't participating").

### Modelling and the 3-2-1 reflection structure

The Guide names modelling as the central craft of FLO Synchronous: "It is important in FLO Synchronous to model the kinds of synchronous online facilitation skills that we hope participants will be able to learn and practice in the course." This includes "Creating and using lesson plans (not just slide decks) to model good design skills"; "Creating sessions that model the kinds of sessions we'd like to see participants design and facilitate in the course, i.e. sessions that are interactive, participatory, etc."; "Creating and including evaluations or feedback-eliciting mechanisms after each of the synchronous sessions to, again, model what we teach in the course" [V] (Ch 6, "Create/update the lesson plan and slide deck").

The 3-2-1 structure appears in the Self-Assessment Rubric for Practicing Facilitators: "Reflected on facilitation of session and used the 3-2-1 structure, inviting at least one piece of feedback from course peers and facilitators" (Met requirements); "Reflected on facilitation of session and identified specific ideas for what could be improved for next time, posting more than the 3-2-1 structure. Asked specific questions of course peers and facilitators to advance own practice forward" (Exceeded requirements) [V] (Ch 6, "Self-Assessment Rubric (Additional items for practicing facilitators)").

### Self-Assessment Rubric

The Synchronous rubric uses a three-point scale ("did not meet requirements" / "met requirements" / "exceeded requirements") across criteria that vary by track. All participants are assessed on: Participation in synchronous sessions facilitated by course facilitators; Participation in weekly discussion forums; Participation in weekly activities other than discussions. Reviewing Participants additionally on: Participation in synchronous sessions of Practicing Facilitators; Giving feedback to Practicing Facilitators. Practicing Facilitators additionally on: Participation in another Practicing Facilitator's session; Giving feedback to Practicing Facilitators; Leading synchronous online session using a web-based platform (≥15 minutes employing skills from the Facilitation Session Guide); Reflecting on facilitation of session (3-2-1 structure inviting at least one piece of feedback) [V] (Ch 6, "FLO Synchronous Self-Assessment Rubric").

## Part VII: FLO MicroCourses — short, single-topic, peer-review centred (Ch 7)

### Course shape

FLO MicroCourses are 5-day "courses that focus on a single topic. Participants engage in a five-step process to create a draft or prototype, and then give and receive feedback. Participants then leave with something practical and useful for their own teaching practice, whether it's of their own creation or borrowed from their peers" [V] (Ch 7, "What are FLO MicroCourses?"). MicroCourses appeal to participants who cannot commit to a 3-, 4-, or 5-week course.

Time commitments: facilitator at least 5 hours during the week (more if providing direct feedback on participants' work); participants ~5 hours [V] (Ch 7, Synopsis/abstract). Class size: 10+ active participants for "sufficient variety in the prototypes and more exposure to new ideas and examples"; "we have not placed a limit on enrolment and fully expect that not all participants will be active. Facilitators do not reach out to participants if they aren't engaging in the course, such as they would to participants of other FLO courses" [V] (Ch 7, "Participants"). This is a deliberate exception to the FLO active-outreach pattern.

### Five-step Activity Packet

The Activity Packet is the core resource. Five steps across three phases (Ch 7, "Activity Packet – 5 steps / 5 days"):

- **Research phase.** Step 1 — What? Why? What have others done?
- **Creation phase.** Step 2 — Draft it! / Step 3 — Post it!
- **Review and feedback phase.** Step 4 — Review and engage. / Step 5 — Give and receive feedback.

"Keep the activity packet concise. It is not meant to be a dumping ground for content related to your topic. Encourage exploration of the subject matter and perhaps link to a few key readings" [V] (Ch 7, "Edit the Activity Packet", sidebar).

### Facilitator stance — light hand, balance, judgment

The MicroCourse facilitator's stance is lighter than in other FLO courses. "What is the facilitator's role when peer review is the centre of the course design? This is an area that requires balance and judgment. The best advice is to:

> 1. Be concerned only if an uncomfortable amount of time goes by without any replies.
> 2. Only offer genuine feedback.
> 3. Resist the urge to be thorough, but be clear that you're only commenting on one or two aspects of the artifact.
> 4. Invite participants to jump in and elaborate, using your curious voice: 'I can't wait to read others' first impressions and advice.'"
> (Ch 7, "Monitor the sharing of drafts and prototypes")

"Jumping in too quickly to offer your advice or feedback, or to respond to questions, might discourage others from engaging" [V] (Ch 7, sidebar).

Tone discipline: "Avoid pressuring participants to comply with course deadlines, or suggesting they are only welcome if they are committed to following the activity plan. This is an informal, adult learning environment; expect that individuals will have their own agenda" [V] (Ch 7, "Write a welcome post").

### Peer Feedback Rubric

Six criteria across three levels (Sophisticated / Competent / Marginal): Is descriptive; Is specific; Is respectful; Is useful; Engages; Is realistic [V] (Ch 7, "FLO MicroCourse Peer Feedback Rubric"). The rubric is itself a self-assessment tool for the quality of feedback being given.

### Two-heads-better, day-stamped posts, harvesting gems

The Guide quotes Robin Leung on co-facilitation: "I learnt that you need a co-facilitator that balances with your life well. I had a more technical background and my co-facilitator had more of the facilitation background. I am a night owl (catching most of the activities at night), and she's the early riser (catching most of the activities in the day time). I think we complemented one another very well" [V] [AE] (Ch 7, "Two heads are better than one").

The Guide proposes day-stamped post conventions: "Establishing a protocol for facilitator posts can help them to be noticed and reinforce the timeframe. For example, the subject heading for this post is Day 1: Rise and Shine. The next day's post was Day 2: Dig In, and so on" [V] (Ch 7, "Write a welcome post"). Sylvia Currie's "Day 1: Rise and Shine" post is quoted as a worked example: "With MicroCourses we just jump right in. Let's do this!" [V] [AE].

"Harvest the gems!" is a separate move: "Throughout the course, it's useful to create a routine that brings together the resources that the participants are sharing. Often this falls on the facilitators, but certainly encourage everyone to compile and organize artifacts" [V] (Ch 7, "Harvest the gems!").

### Closing and summary report

"Facilitators need to gauge the opportune moment to wrap up this five-day course. Since there can still be a lot of buzz on the Friday, Saturday could be better so it doesn't look like you are checking out early" [V] (Ch 7, "Write a closing post"). A facilitator debrief is recommended: "Don't part company with your co-facilitators without scheduling a debrief. It's important to share experiences and ideas about the facilitation process, otherwise you are destined to repeat what could have been improved!" [V] (Ch 7, "Facilitator debrief"). A summary report — including participation data, quotes from the evaluation survey, and forum-post highlights — "can help participants feel a sense of accomplishment, and can also help build interest in upcoming MicroCourses" [V] (Ch 7, "Summary report").

## Part VIII: Cross-cutting facilitation craft

### Modelling as the through-line

Across all four course chapters, the Guide returns to modelling as the load-bearing mechanism. FLO facilitators model good facilitation in Week 1 of Fundamentals so participant teams know what to do in Weeks 2–5 [AP] (Ch 4, "Week 1"). FLO Synchronous facilitators create lesson plans, interactive sessions, and feedback-eliciting activities to model what the course teaches [V] (Ch 6, "Create/update the lesson plan and slide deck"). FLO Design facilitators do their lesson planning *in the open* — posting the lesson plan in a Google Doc and inviting participant comments — to make the planning process visible [V] (Ch 6, sidebar at "Final synchronous session"). FLIF facilitators complete the FLIF themselves to model the reflection process [V] (Ch 4, "The FLIF Facilitation Process"). The pattern is consistent: the facilitator's authentic engagement with the practice is part of the curriculum.

### Question form

The Guide is consistent that good facilitation uses open-ended, invitational, and probing questions rather than closed or directive ones. The video transcript on team feedback names the move: "sometimes I'll post in their planning forum to ask some open-ended questions about how they think things are going" [V] (Ch 4, "Example of video feedback with transcript"). The MicroCourse facilitator's "curious voice" — "I can't wait to read others' first impressions and advice" — is an explicit invitational tone [V] (Ch 7, "Monitor the sharing of drafts and prototypes"). The accessibility self-questions in Chapter 3 are diagnostic open questions ("Do I have materials… Do I use facilitation strategies… can I imagine how they perceive…") [V] (Ch 3, "Accessible design, inclusive facilitation").

### Reflective practice as discipline, not as introspection

The Guide treats reflection as a structured, scheduled, modelled, and shared discipline. Participants maintain Learning Journals throughout each course; they share "nuggets" weekly to the Weekly Journal Share forum; facilitators share their own reflections alongside; the FLIF is a six-question structured reflection; final activities like "Looking Back/Looking Forward" close the course with structured reflective writing. "Evidence has shown that learning is improved when participants become more aware (metacognition) of how and what they are learning. FLO courses integrate structures to promote both individual and collaborative reflective practice" [V] (Ch 5, sidebar at "Encouraging critical thinking through reflection").

### Co-facilitation as a craft

The Guide recommends co-facilitation across all four courses and treats it as a load-bearing design choice. Co-facilitators share the workload, enrich facilitation, and increase facilitation expertise within the institution [AP] (Ch 4, "Several weeks (or months) before the course begins", item 3). The Synchronous chapter notes that "Having a co-facilitator throughout the duration of the course helps for this with a big class!" [V] (Ch 6, "Block time in personal calendars during Week 3"). The MicroCourse chapter's quote from Robin Leung names a complementarity criterion: skill mix, life rhythm, working hours [V] [AE] (Ch 7, "Two heads are better than one"). The MicroCourse chapter also names a co-facilitation plan as a deliberate move: "There is no shortage of ways to spend your time as a facilitator. With a plan for how you will divide up the tasks, you can maintain a manageable workload and avoid overstepping" [V] (Ch 7, "Create a co-facilitation plan").

### Synchronous and asynchronous

The Guide treats the synchronous/asynchronous mix as a design choice, not as a default. FLO Fundamentals is "mostly asynchronous… delivered over five sequential weeks. Two or three one-hour synchronous sessions are optional but recommended" [V] (Ch 4, "What is FLO Fundamentals?"). FLO Synchronous is intentionally a course about the synchronous mode. FLO MicroCourses are asynchronous: "A facilitated asynchronous online course" [V] (Ch 7, Synopsis/abstract). The Guide cites Hrastinski (2008) and Yamagata-Lynch (2014) on when and why to choose each, and on how communication strategies can complement each other [BT] (App 1, Hrastinski and Yamagata-Lynch entries).

### The "informal-but-decisive" sidebars

The Guide deploys explicit sidebars throughout — visually offset short instructional blocks — that name a single facilitator move. Examples: "Helping participants cope who may feel overwhelmed during the first week"; "Be pro-active in the first week"; "Welcome feedback throughout the course"; "Are some participants not as engaged as you expect?"; "Encourage richer, deeper reflection"; "Stay calm and carry on"; "Encouraging critical thinking through reflection". The sidebar form is itself a Guide convention: short, actionable, embedded at the point of decision in the workflow.

## Positions the text explicitly frames against

- **The sage-on-the-stage model of teaching.** The Guide frames the shift from direct teaching to facilitative teaching as the central pedagogical move: from "acting as the 'sage on the stage'" to "supporting or scaffolding the learning that was meaningful for each student (e.g., acting as the 'guide on the side')" [V] (Ch 1, "What is facilitation in an educational context?").
- **MOOCs and self-directed independent study as substitutes for facilitated learning.** "Practice, seek advice and feedback, and share learning in a small, supportive community of peers; this is not a MOOC!" [V] (Ch 2, "What makes FLO unique?"). "The FLO experience is very different from an independent and self-directed study" [V] (Ch 2, "What makes FLO unique?"). The Guide is consistent that FLO is participatory, small-cohort, and facilitated.
- **Auditing FLO courses.** "We strongly advise against allowing participants to audit FLO courses for many reasons, the least of which is that they will not be able to achieve course learning outcomes if they do" [V] (Ch 6, "Be in touch with participants who haven't logged in/aren't participating"). Auditing is incompatible with the participatory design.
- **Side-of-the-desk participation.** "Remind participants that a valuable learning experience cannot be achieved using a side-of-the-desk approach" [V] (Ch 4, Week-1 Day 3, weekly overview post item 3). FLO is a commitment, not a casual addition to existing workload.
- **Heavy-handed direction from the facilitator.** "Now, before the activity, as their FLO facilitator being assigned to support their team, I try to let them know what they are supposed to be doing, but I try not to be too heavy-handed in how to do it" [V] (Ch 4, "Example of video feedback with transcript"). The facilitator supports rather than directs.
- **Jumping in too quickly with answers.** "Jumping in too quickly to offer your advice or feedback, or to respond to questions, might discourage others from engaging" [V] (Ch 7, sidebar at "Monitor the sharing of drafts and prototypes"). Facilitator silence has a function.
- **Pressuring tone and deadline policing.** "Avoid pressuring participants to comply with course deadlines, or suggesting they are only welcome if they are committed to following the activity plan. This is an informal, adult learning environment; expect that individuals will have their own agenda" [V] (Ch 7, "Write a welcome post").
- **Do-or-die atmosphere.** "Be open to modifying your approach/expectations to adapt to the needs of your cohort. The purpose is to help them develop, NOT to create a 'do-or-die' atmosphere" [V] (Ch 5, "Week 3: Projects under construction").
- **Comprehensive facilitator feedback that crowds out peers.** "Resist the urge to be thorough, but be clear that you're only commenting on one or two aspects of the artifact… consider the impact if facilitators only respond to one or two contributions. Will others feel neglected? Quite possibly! So, it's important to establish expectations that you may acknowledge contributions but not provide comprehensive feedback. After all, other participants might have more subject matter expertise than you, and they will provide valuable feedback" [V] (Ch 7, "Monitor the sharing of drafts and prototypes").
- **"That's great" superficial feedback.** "Encourage peer-to-peer feedback: Find ways to elicit more meaningful posts; don't accept 'that's great' superficial responses" [V] (Ch 5, "Week 3").
- **Anxious Annie pattern.** The Guide names and externalises facilitator anxiety as a recognised pattern to be noticed, named, and trusted-past-rather-than-acted-on: "Then, I remember facilitating is not about me; it's about helping the participants as they need and ask for help. There is a saying that RRU folks often refer to called 'Trust the process'" [V] [AE] (Ch 4, "Example of reflective post").
- **Content-dumping into the Activity Packet.** "Keep the activity packet concise. It is not meant to be a dumping ground for content related to your topic. Encourage exploration of the subject matter and perhaps link to a few key readings" [V] (Ch 7, sidebar at "Edit the Activity Packet").
- **Grading by default in developmental courses.** "This course is designed to be developmental; it is not a graded course in the traditional sense. Unless you decide otherwise, your participants will not be marked or assigned a formal grade on their performance" [V] (Ch 4, "Course assessment strategies"). Self-assessment via rubric is the default; grading is the opt-in.
- **Recording without consent.** "If you plan on recording your synchronous sessions, confirm that this is OK with the session participants *before* you hit the record button" [V] (Ch 4, "Privacy and Confidentiality"). Recording is a consent decision, not a default.
- **Adopting tools without privacy assessment.** "Sometimes free social media tools retain copyright to what you create when you use their tool. It may not matter but you should consider the implications before using a new tool" [V] (Ch 3, "Using open source or social media tools").
- **One-iteration mastery.** "Developing skills in online facilitation is a journey, not something that can be accomplished over one iteration of a course!" [V] (Ch 3, "Developing FLO facilitation skills"). Facilitation is a developmental practice, not a credential.
