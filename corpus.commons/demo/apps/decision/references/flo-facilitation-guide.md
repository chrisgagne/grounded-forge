## Thesis (condensed)

The Guide's organising claim is that online learning is built by *facilitation*, not by content delivery — and that the facilitator's job is to support discovery rather than transmit knowledge. Chapter 1 traces the lineage from "sage on the stage" to "guide on the side" and names facilitative teaching as the move. Across all four FLO courses, the through-line is that facilitators *model* the practices they teach: facilitator presence shifts from heavy in Week 1 (community builder, guide) to lighter in later weeks (coach, mentor stepping back) — a scaffolding-and-fading arc.

Reflective practice is treated as the engine of facilitator development, structured through Learning Journals, weekly nuggets, the FLIF (Feel, Like, Improve, Feedback) six-question survey, the 3-2-1 reflection for Practicing Facilitators in FLO Synchronous, and the Looking-Back/Looking-Forward closing activity in FLO Design. Climate and community are operational concerns (not soft skills): two of four core skill categories are explicitly community-focused, and privacy-and-confidentiality ("Vegas rules") is treated as a foundation-of-trust design choice. Course-design choices that shape facilitation are mostly made before the course begins — every course chapter opens with a Facilitator Task List structured around several-weeks-before, one-to-two-weeks-before, and a-few-days-before phases.

## What facilitation is and is not

Facilitative teaching shifted from direct teaching as adult learning theory, social constructivist learning, and humanist approaches gained ground; the focus moved to "helping learners understand course content through questioning and suggestions while providing rich cases, complex problems, and opportunities to apply new knowledge in different contexts" (Ch 1). The Institute of Cultural Affairs – UK's definition of the facilitator's role — enabling and supporting a group to achieve objectives in a way that involves and respects all contributions, builds ownership, and releases the group's potential — frames the work.

The Community of Inquiry model is named as the framing model for online: 18+ years of research demonstrated the importance of facilitation through listening, connecting ideas, and involving learners in meaningful and educationally worthwhile learning (Vaughan et al, 2013; Anderson 2017).

## The FLO family of courses

Five courses, all CC BY licensed and available as OER:

- **FLO Fundamentals** (5 weeks, asynchronous + optional synchronous, 14–20 participants is the sweet spot).
- **FLO Design** (4 weeks, studio-based with weekly Studio forums; participants develop a 2–3-week unit of online learning plus a prototype activity).
- **FLO Synchronous** (3 weeks, two-track choice of Reviewing Participant or Practicing Facilitator; 10–20 participants).
- **FLO MicroCourses** (5 days, single-topic, peer-review centred, 10+ active participants; no upper limit, expect not-all-active).

Lineage: the first FLO course was Instructional Skills Workshop Online (ISWO), developed by Royal Roads University in 2008 through BCcampus's Online Program Development fund; FLO Fundamentals as such dates to 2013 (revised several times); renamed to FLO in 2015; FLO Design and FLO Synchronous followed; MicroCourses were added to appeal to people who could not commit to multi-week courses.

FLO contrasts itself against MOOCs ("this is not a MOOC!") and against independent study; FLO is participatory, small-cohort, peer-supported, time-bounded, and facilitated.

## Facilitating FLO: roles, skills, and the development model

### Scaffolding-and-fading role arc

Early in a course, facilitator presence is high: community builder and guide, welcoming, posting reminders, clarifying curriculum, troubleshooting, encouraging participation, developing connections. As the course moves on, facilitators take on coach and mentor roles: stepping back, using questioning strategies, participating in discussion without leading, highlighting concepts, weaving ideas, posting summaries. Toward the close, facilitators help complete activities, assess progress, and host wrap-up.

### Four core skill categories

The Guide identifies four core FLO facilitation skill categories (drawn from the self-assessment rubric, crowd-sourced facilitator input, and IAF Core Competencies):

1. **Support Diverse Learners Online** — communicate goals/roles, invite cultural and acquired knowledge, support effective online learning strategies, encourage participation and risk-taking, promote reflective practice, provide timely formative feedback, assist with self-assessment.
2. **Build and Sustain Online Community** — build safety and support, build rapport, develop feedback guidelines, model respectful dialogue, encourage norms of behaviour, support positive conflict response, close community at end.
3. **Manage Course Online** — design functional learning spaces, select appropriate tools, share content in varied ways, maintain currency of references, provide varied ways to participate.
4. **Model Effective Online Facilitation** — model positive professional presence, plan with co-facilitator(s), manage time, seek feedback, maintain balanced presence, use varied questioning techniques, monitor participation, engage in and model reflective practice, explore new pedagogical approaches, develop technical skills.

### Mentorship development model

Four-step pathway: Take FLO courses (experience the participant role) → Apply what you've learned in your own teaching → Become a FLO facilitator by co-facilitating with an experienced mentor → Adopt FLO / become a lead facilitator and mentor. The Guide is explicit that "the best way to learn how to facilitate a FLO course is to co-facilitate with a more experienced facilitator as a mentor" and that "developing skills in online facilitation is a journey, not something that can be accomplished over one iteration of a course!"

### Accessible design

Universal Design for Learning (UDL; CAST 2018) is the framework: multiple means of Engagement, Representation, and Action/Expression. The Guide poses four diagnostic accessibility questions for facilitators: do I have inaccessible materials? do I use strategies that exclude? do I have alternatives to new tools? can I imagine how diverse learners perceive the environment?

### Tools

Foundational technologies: registration and login; persistent text-based forums; resource storage; synchronous web conferencing (with facilitator-level access for participant practice). Additional features: quick surveys/quizzes; learner-created short videos; voting in forums; private group forums. When using tools outside institutional support, six diagnostic questions cover purpose, institutional policy, affordances, learner impact, alternatives, and content-ownership.

## FLO Fundamentals (5 weeks)

Mostly asynchronous; two or three optional synchronous sessions. Facilitator's primary role: build and sustain online community. The course structure is participatory: in Week 1, course facilitators establish presence and run the Building Online Community forum; by end of Week 1 participants are organised into four teams; each team facilitates Weeks 2–5 on assigned topics (Diversity of Learners, Responsive Facilitation, Collaboration, Reflective Practice). Course facilitators are "less visible" during participant-led weeks.

Time commitments: facilitator 15–25 hours/week (more first time); participant 6–12 hours/week (more during their facilitation week). Class size sweet spot 14–20.

**Privacy and Vegas rules.** "Our learning relies on the exchange of honest, constructive feedback, and we need to agree on rules that support this foundation of trust." Always seek permission to share participant content; confirm recording-OK before pressing record; let participants know who else has course access; "Vegas rules" — what happens in the course stays in the course.

**Self-Assessment Rubrics.** Team Facilitation (facilitator role): five criteria across three developmental levels (Plans effectively; Communicates goals and roles; Guides participants; Demonstrates community strategies; Engages in reflective practice). Weekly Participation (participant role): four criteria across three levels (Relevance; Fosters community; Engages in activities; Reflective practice). The course is developmental, not graded by default; the rubric can be adapted for grading if needed.

**The FLIF Facilitation Process** (Feel, Like, Improve, Feedback). Six-question structured reflection for participant facilitation teams to debrief their facilitation week, completed privately and shared only with co-facilitators. Facilitators model the FLIF by completing it themselves. Questions: how do I feel; what do I like; what do I want to improve; key points from feedback received; how this impacts my teaching; additional points for feedback.

**Week-1 craft.** Establish strong facilitator presence; demonstrate good online facilitation transparently; "think out loud" to show how to plan and facilitate. Be proactive on first-week overwhelm — name it before participants do. The "Anxious Annie" pattern named: notice facilitator anxiety as a recognisable pattern; "facilitating is not about me; it's about helping the participants as they need and ask for help… Trust the process."

## FLO Design (4 weeks)

Studio-based design course. Participants develop a 2–3-week unit of online learning plus a prototype activity. The distinctive feature is the *weekly Studio forum* where participants share emerging designs and receive constructive feedback from facilitators and peers.

Four-week shape: **Week 1 Getting started** (community building, first synchronous session, design project choice, group agreements). **Week 2 Sparks to design** (theory and design approaches, synchronous session with examples or guest presenters). **Week 3 Projects under construction** (deeper feedback, learner-focused questions, visual representation, "stay calm and carry on"). **Week 4 Showcase designs** (showcase sessions of 4–5 presentations at 10–15 minutes each; Looking-Back/Looking-Forward reflection; closing).

Time commitments: facilitator ~15 hours/week (more in Weeks 1 and 4); participant 6–8 hours/week. Studio-management craft: scaffold link-back to core design documents; encourage richer peer feedback ("don't accept 'that's great' superficial responses"); make visual representation explicit; integrate weekly reflections back into Studio.

Reflection design choice: pose a focused mid-week question, *or* invite shared nuggets from personal learning journals. Each approach has potential benefits; choose by participant context.

FLO Design Rubric: five criteria across three developmental levels (Structure of Design Project Plan; Communication of Plan in Studio; Feedback on Plan; Participation in Workshop Events/Activities; Reflective Practice).

## FLO Synchronous (3 weeks)

Three-week course on synchronous online facilitation skills. Two tracks: **Reviewing Participant** (reviewing and discussing best practices, providing feedback) and **Practicing Facilitator** (planning for, facilitating, and receiving feedback on a short synchronous session ≥15 minutes). Track choice declared by end of Week 1.

Class size: 10+ to into the 20s with two facilitators. In a class of 20, aim for ~6–8 Practicing Facilitators; actively encourage risk-taking onto the Practicing Facilitator track rather than letting natural defaults push everyone to Reviewing Participant.

Three-week shape: **Week 1 Preparing** (welcome, ~90-second Video Introductions activity, first synchronous session on technical skills and community, optional Tech Times, track choice). **Week 2 Facilitating** (second synchronous session with active participation modelling and feedback-eliciting activity — Liberating Structures examples like 1-2-4-All and User Experience Fishbowl; connect with Practicing Facilitators on requirements and with Reviewing Participants on expectations; Sample Synchros activity; remind on Self-Assessment Rubric). **Week 3 Following up** (attend as many Practicing Facilitator sessions as possible; final synchronous session including possible co-facilitation by a participant; lesson planning *in the open* as an additional behind-the-scenes learning opportunity; closing post; respond to rubrics; co-facilitator debrief).

**Tracking matrix.** Simple spreadsheet with participant names and many blank columns; mark when responding to a participant to spread engagement equity across the cohort.

**Counsel-out.** "If you find out a participant is deeply struggling, perhaps from an overwhelming schedule, your role is to help them decide if now is the right time to take the course. We strongly advise against allowing participants to audit FLO courses." Auditing is incompatible with participatory design.

**Modelling as central craft.** Facilitators model the synchronous facilitation skills they want participants to learn: lesson plans (not just slide decks); interactive participatory sessions; feedback-eliciting activities after each synchronous session.

**Self-Assessment Rubric.** Three-level (did not meet / met / exceeded requirements). All participants: participation in facilitator-led synchronous sessions; weekly discussion forums; weekly activities. Reviewing Participants additionally: participation in Practicing Facilitator sessions; giving feedback. Practicing Facilitators additionally: participating in another Practicing Facilitator's session; giving feedback; leading a session ≥15 minutes; reflecting on facilitation using the 3-2-1 structure.

## FLO MicroCourses (5 days)

Short, single-topic, hands-on. Participants engage in a five-step process to create a draft or prototype and give/receive feedback. Class size 10+ active participants; no upper enrolment limit; "facilitators do not reach out to participants if they aren't engaging in the course, such as they would to participants of other FLO courses." This is a deliberate exception to the FLO active-outreach default.

**Five-step Activity Packet** across three phases: Research phase — Step 1 (What? Why? What have others done?); Creation phase — Step 2 Draft it!, Step 3 Post it!; Review and feedback phase — Step 4 Review and engage, Step 5 Give and receive feedback.

**Facilitator stance: light hand.** "What is the facilitator's role when peer review is the centre of the course design? This is an area that requires balance and judgment." Four moves: be concerned only if uncomfortable time goes by without replies; only offer genuine feedback; resist thoroughness — comment on one or two aspects only; invite participants to elaborate using a curious voice. "Jumping in too quickly to offer your advice or feedback, or to respond to questions, might discourage others from engaging."

**Tone discipline.** "Avoid pressuring participants to comply with course deadlines, or suggesting they are only welcome if they are committed to following the activity plan. This is an informal, adult learning environment; expect that individuals will have their own agenda."

**Day-stamped posts.** Establish a posting protocol for visibility: "Day 1: Rise and Shine"; "Day 2: Dig In"; and so on.

**Harvest the gems.** A facilitator routine of bringing together resources participants are sharing; can be delegated but typically falls on the facilitators.

**Peer Feedback Rubric.** Six criteria across three levels (Sophisticated / Competent / Marginal): Is descriptive; Is specific; Is respectful; Is useful; Engages; Is realistic.

## Cross-cutting facilitation craft

**Modelling as through-line.** Facilitator authenticity with the practice is part of the curriculum. Welcome post models good facilitation; first-week presence models what participant teams will do later in Fundamentals; FLIF facilitators complete the FLIF themselves; FLO Design facilitators plan in the open in shared Google Docs; FLO Synchronous facilitators design sessions that demonstrate what they're teaching.

**Question form.** Open-ended, invitational, and probing. The video transcript on team feedback names the move: "post in their planning forum to ask some open-ended questions about how they think things are going." The MicroCourse facilitator's "curious voice" — "I can't wait to read others' first impressions and advice" — is an explicit invitational tone.

**Reflective practice as discipline.** Learning Journals throughout; weekly nuggets shared; facilitators share their own; FLIF as structured reflection; Looking-Back/Looking-Forward closing activity. "Evidence has shown that learning is improved when participants become more aware (metacognition) of how and what they are learning."

**Co-facilitation as design choice.** Recommended across all four courses. Co-facilitators share workload, enrich facilitation, and increase institutional facilitation expertise. Complementarity matters: skill mix, life rhythm, working hours. A written co-facilitation plan avoids overstepping and duplicate work.

**Sidebar form.** The Guide deploys named short instructional blocks at point-of-decision in the workflow: "Be pro-active in the first week"; "Welcome feedback throughout the course"; "Encourage richer, deeper reflection"; "Stay calm and carry on". The sidebar form is itself a Guide convention.

## Key connections

- **Community of Inquiry model (Vaughan, Cleveland-Innes & Garrison 2013; Anderson 2017; Richardson et al. 2015).** The framing model for online learning facilitation; three-presence framework (social, cognitive, teaching).
- **Universal Design for Learning (CAST 2018).** Accessibility framework — multiple means of Engagement, Representation, Action/Expression.
- **Institute of Cultural Affairs – UK.** Working definition of the facilitator's role.
- **International Association of Facilitators (IAF) Core Competencies.** Inspiration for the Core FLO Facilitation Skills list.
- **Liberating Structures.** 1-2-4-All and User Experience Fishbowl as worked synchronous facilitation activities.
- **Hew (2015).** Conditions for instructor versus peer facilitation preference in asynchronous discussion.
- **Hrastinski (2008).** Asynchronous vs synchronous e-learning design choice.
- **Kauffman (2015).** Four learner-satisfaction factors: structured, interactive, relevant, instructor-facilitated.
- **Mor & Mogilevsky (2013); Brown, Karle & Kelly (2015).** Studio-based learning approach that FLO Design adopts.
- **Wiggins & McTighe (2007).** Three teacher roles (direct instruction, facilitation of understanding, coaching of performance); facilitative-teaching definition.
- **Pratt (1998).** Teaching-perspectives framework FLO Fundamentals often uses for participant self-assessment.
- **Bates (2015).** *Teaching in a Digital Age* — influential open textbook on online learning.
- **UNESCO 2002 OER definition; Scott 2015 (UNESCO Futures of Learning 3).** OER framing and 21st-century pedagogy claim.

## Signature contrarian positions

1. **Facilitate, don't transmit.** Shift from "sage on the stage" to "guide on the side" is the load-bearing move; facilitative teaching is the goal, not directive teaching.
2. **Facilitators model the practices they teach.** Modelling is the through-line; congruence between what facilitators say and what facilitators do is the mechanism.
3. **Scaffolding-and-fading role arc.** High presence early as community builder and guide; lighter later as coach and mentor stepping back; facilitator participation always supportive but never overwhelming.
4. **Climate and community are operational.** Two of four core skill categories are explicitly community-focused; privacy and trust are foundations, not soft skills.
5. **This is not a MOOC and not self-directed study.** FLO is small-cohort, participatory, time-bounded, and facilitated. Auditing is incompatible with the design.
6. **No side-of-the-desk participation.** FLO is a commitment, not a casual addition to existing workload.
7. **Developmental, not graded by default.** Self-assessment rubrics are the primary mode; grading is the opt-in.
8. **Co-facilitation is recommended across all four courses.** Workload, enrichment, institutional capacity-building. Complementarity (skill mix, life rhythm, working hours) matters.
9. **Open-ended invitational questions.** "How do you think things are going?" "I can't wait to read others' first impressions" — the facilitator's curious voice is part of the craft.
10. **Resist the urge to be thorough in your feedback.** Facilitator feedback that crowds out peers degrades the design. Comment on one or two aspects; invite participants to elaborate.
11. **Heavy-handed direction is wrong.** "I try not to be too heavy-handed in how to do it or all of the minute details of what they're going to be up to."
12. **Anxious-Annie pattern.** Notice facilitator anxiety as one's own pattern, not as a participant problem; trust the process.
13. **No do-or-die atmosphere.** Adapt to cohort needs; the purpose is development, not selection.
14. **Stay-calm-and-carry-on in design weeks.** Find ways to lessen participant anxiety in Week 3 of Design rather than to amplify it.
15. **Pencil-and-paper-style tooling discipline.** Tools serve the learning, not the other way around; the Activity Packet is not a content-dumping ground; new tools are vetted on privacy, alternatives, and content-ownership.
16. **Recording is consent, not default.** Confirm OK with participants before pressing record.
17. **Three-level developmental rubrics, not five-level performance ratings.** Beginning/Developing/Accomplished, or did-not-meet/met/exceeded — coarse-grain developmental signal rather than fine-grain performance grading.
18. **One iteration is not enough.** "Developing skills in online facilitation is a journey." The mentorship model assumes progressive practice over multiple courses.
19. **Counsel-out is part of the role.** Some registrations should not proceed; helping a struggling participant decide whether now is the right time is a facilitator move.
20. **Design choices made before the course begins.** Every course chapter opens with several-weeks-before, one-to-two-weeks-before, and a-few-days-before task lists; the in-course running depends on the pre-course design.
