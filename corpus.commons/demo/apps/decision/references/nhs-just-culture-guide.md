
# NHS Improvement, A Just Culture Guide (light reference)

**Source:** NHS Improvement (2018). *A Just Culture Guide — Supporting consistent, constructive and fair evaluation of the actions of staff involved in patient safety incidents*. **Structure:** Single-page A3 landscape poster. Four "Please note" caveats; three-paragraph preamble framing purpose and use; five sequential tests (deliberate harm; health; foresight; substitution; mitigating circumstances) with eleven sub-questions across them; seven distinct recommendation end-states distributed across the branches. Complete coverage; nothing omitted.

## Author's Thesis (condensed)

The appropriate response to a member of staff involved in a patient safety incident should differ according to the *circumstances in which the error was made* rather than the *outcome that occurred*. The poster operationalises this through a five-test decision tree designed to support a conversation between managers about whether a staff member requires specific individual support or intervention to work safely. The prior, stated explicitly: "action singling out an individual is rarely appropriate — most patient safety issues have deeper causes and require wider action".

Four structural claims anchor the guide. First, the guide is a *conversation aid, not a substitute* for investigation or HR advice — the four "Please note" caveats fix the scope (not a replacement for investigation; re-entrant as new information emerges; not a replacement for HR advice; one action at a time). Second, the tests are *sequential and gated* — each test conditions the next, and only one action (or failure to act) is taken through the tree at a time. Third, *making the approach explainable* is itself part of a just culture; the guide is positioned as a communication tool for managers, staff, patients, and families. Fourth, every recommendation end-state pairs its operational guidance with a directive about *wider investigation* — patient-safety outcomes are not exhausted by the individual response, even on the deliberate-harm branch.

## The decision tree shape

Five tests are applied in order. At each test the answer (Yes / No) routes either to a recommendation that exits the tree or to the next test:

1. **Q1 deliberate harm.** Single sub-question (intention to cause harm?). Yes → exit. No → Q2.
2. **Q2 health.** Three sub-questions (substance abuse; physical ill health; mental ill health). Yes to any → exit. No to all → Q3.
3. **Q3 foresight.** Three sub-questions (agreed protocols / accepted practice in place; workable and in routine use; knowingly departed from). Yes to all → Q4. No to any → exit.
4. **Q4 substitution.** Three sub-questions (peers would have done the same; training was missed; senior supervision absent). Yes to any → exit. No to all → Q5.
5. **Q5 mitigating circumstances.** Single sub-question (significant mitigating circumstances?). Yes → exit to softer recommendation. No → exit to management action.

Three recommendations route away from singling out the individual (D, E, F), two route toward management action directed at the individual (A and G), and two route toward health-affecting-work pathways (B and C). Of seven end-states, only A (deliberate harm) and G (the residual after all filters have routed elsewhere) carry the strongest individual-management response.

## The seven recommendation end-states

- **A — Deliberate harm (Q1 Yes).** Organisational guidance for appropriate management action — possibly including regulatory bodies, suspension, and police / disciplinary processes. Wider investigation still needed.
- **B — Substance abuse (Q2 Yes to 2a).** Organisational substance-abuse-at-work guidance; wider investigation still needed to understand whether substance abuse could have been recognised earlier.
- **C — Health issue (Q2 Yes to 2b or 2c).** Organisational guidance for health issues affecting work, likely occupational-health referral; wider investigation still needed.
- **D — Not appropriate to single out, foresight branch (Q3 No to any).** "Action singling out the individual is unlikely to be appropriate"; wider actions may include the individual but are not limited to them.
- **E — Not appropriate to single out, substitution branch (Q4 Yes to any).** Textually identical to D. Same default response, different gating test.
- **F — Mitigating circumstances apply (Q5 Yes).** "Action directed at the individual may not be appropriate"; senior HR advice on degree of mitigation; wider actions for future patients.
- **G — Residual: management action plus wider investigation (Q5 No).** Graduated response — individual training, performance management, competency assessments, changes to role or increased supervision — before reaching regulatory bodies, suspension, and disciplinary processes. Wider investigation preserved as a parallel obligation.

## The five tests in plain terms

The **deliberate harm** test isolates the (rare) case of intent — the only test in the tree that addresses intent. Every test after Q1 addresses conditions and context surrounding an act done without intent to harm.

The **health** test treats impaired-capacity contributors as conditions to be addressed through occupational-health or substance-abuse-at-work guidance, not as individual culpability. The three sub-questions cover substance abuse, physical ill health, and mental ill health.

The **foresight** test is the load-bearing test for whether the system held up its side of the contract. Only the conjunction of all three sub-answers (yes there are agreed protocols; yes they are workable and in routine use; yes the individual knowingly departed from them) advances toward potential individual accountability. Any No at this test routes to "not appropriate to single out".

The **substitution** test asks whether substituting another comparable individual into the same situation would have produced a different outcome. The three sub-questions probe peer behaviour, training gaps, and supervision failure. Any Yes routes to "not appropriate to single out".

The **mitigating circumstances** test is the residual after all prior filters have routed elsewhere. At that point the question is whether *significant mitigating circumstances* attach to the case, and the routing turns on that single answer.

## Caveats on use (Preamble)

Four explicit caveats fix the guide's scope:

1. Not a replacement for an investigation; only a full investigation can identify the underlying causes.
2. Re-entrant — can be used at any point but may need to be revisited as more information emerges.
3. Does not replace HR advice; use in conjunction with organisational policy.
4. One action (or failure to act) at a time; multiple actions in one incident must be considered separately.

## Use as communication tool

The guide is positioned for use by all parties — managers, staff, patients, families. It functions as a reference point for organisational HR and incident reporting policies, and as a communication tool to help all parties understand how the appropriate response can and should differ according to the circumstances in which an error was made. The dual purpose is stated: "protecting staff from unfair targeting" and protecting patients "by removing the tendency to treat wider patient safety issues as individual issues".

## Signature Contrarian Positions

1. **Default to system, not to individual.** "Action singling out an individual is rarely appropriate — most patient safety issues have deeper causes and require wider action." The decision tree is structured so that four of seven end-states route away from singling out the individual.
2. **Even deliberate harm preserves the system question.** The deliberate-harm branch (Recommendation A) explicitly preserves "wider investigation is still needed to understand how and why patients were not protected from the actions of the individual" alongside the individual-management response. The system question is never extinguished by any finding about the individual.
3. **Outcome is not the test of culpability; circumstances are.** The guide explicitly frames the appropriate response as differing "according to the circumstances in which an error was made", not according to the outcome that followed. This is the source's most explicit framing-against position, though it is not attached to a named opposing position.
