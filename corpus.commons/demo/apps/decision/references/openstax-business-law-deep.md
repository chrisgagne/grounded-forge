## Author's thesis (paraphrased from source)

The book presents American business law as a body of rules and standards "developed with the purpose of establishing standards for acceptable conduct, prescribing punishment for violations as a deterrent, establishing systems for enforcement, and peacefully resolving disputes," whose ultimate goal is "promotion of the common good" [V] (Ch 1.1, "Basic American Legal Principles"). Business law is described as "a very expansive area" that "primarily addresses issues related to the creation of new businesses, which arise as existing companies deal with the public, government, and other companies" — composed of contracts, tax law, corporate law, intellectual property, real estate, sales, immigration, employment, bankruptcy, and others [V] (Ch 1.3, "Important Business Laws and Regulations").

A second organising claim is that American law derives from multiple sources that interact through a hierarchical structure: the U.S. Constitution as supreme law, federal and state statutes (legislative law), case law (judicial law / common law), and administrative law from agencies, with federal law preempting conflicting state law within the limited powers granted by the Constitution [AP] (Ch 1.2, "Sources and Types of Law"; Ch 4.1, "Commerce Clause"). The Commerce Clause is treated as the central authorising mechanism by which federal regulation reaches across state lines, with the 1937 *NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel Corp.* decision marking the inflection from a literal interpretation to a "close and substantial relationship to interstate commerce" test [V] (Ch 4.1, "The Commerce Clause and The Affordable Care Act").

A third organising claim is that disputes can — and increasingly should — be resolved through mechanisms that avoid full litigation. Negotiation, mediation, and arbitration are presented as a tiered system of alternative dispute resolution (ADR), each adding more structure than the last, with negotiation framed as "process by which parties with nonidentical preferences allocate resources through interpersonal activity and joint decision making" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Negotiation"). The Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument (TKI) is the text's framing apparatus for negotiation styles: forcing, collaborating, compromising, avoiding, yielding (Ch 2.1, "Negotiation Style").

A fourth organising claim is that legal rules in business operate against an ethical and stakeholder backdrop. Ch 3 frames business ethics as "the blueprint for building a successful organization" whose absence produces "poor company reputation," "negative employee relations," "recruitment and retention problems," and "company credibility lost" [V] (Ch 3.1, "Why Is Corporate Ethics So Important in Business?"). Corporate social responsibility (CSR) is presented as "the idea that a business is given the opportunity and privilege to make the world a better place" [V] (Ch 3.2, "What Is Corporate Responsibility?"), measured against a triple-bottom-line frame of "profits, people, and the planet" [V] (Ch 3.2, "The Financial Impact of the Triple Bottom Line").

## Part I: The American legal system and sources of law (Ch 1)

### Five purposes of the American legal system

The book opens with five purposes: establishing standards, promoting consistency, maintaining order, resolving disputes, and protecting liberties and rights [AP] (Ch 1.1). Federal laws are framed as those "all United States citizens are expected to follow," while "state and local laws may often be similar to federal laws, but they may also differ quite a bit, and only govern the state's citizens" [V] (Ch 1.1, "Establishing Standards"). The American system is described as following "the British Common Law system, which is designed to leverage past judicial reasoning, while also promoting fairness through consistency"; the body of past decisions is **case law**, on which judges rely as **precedent** [V] (Ch 1.1, "Promoting Consistency").

The text notes a structural exception: "All U.S. states, except Louisiana, have enacted 'reception statutes,' stating that the judge-made common law of England is the law of the state to the extent that it does not conflict with the state's current laws" [V] (Ch 1.1, "Promoting Consistency"). However, "American cases rarely cite English materials, except for a British classic or a famous old case. Additionally, foreign law is not cited as binding precedent" (Ch 1.1).

### Four sources of American law

American law is described as drawing from four sources: constitutional law, common law (judicial law from courts), legislative law (state and federal statutes), and administrative law from governmental agencies [AP] (Ch 1.2, "Sources and Types of Law"). The Constitution is "the foremost law of the land," with "the first ten amendments referred to as the Bill of Rights, which offers specific protections of individual liberty and justice" [V] (Ch 1.2, "The Constitution"). It empowers federal lawmaking by giving Congress the power "to enact statutes for certain limited purposes, like regulating interstate commerce"; the United States Code (USC) "officially compiles and codifies the federal statutes" [V] (Ch 1.2).

Federal preemption is bounded: "federal law preempts conflicting state and local laws. However, federal preemption is not without limits, insofar as states each have their own constitution and are considered sovereign. Therefore, federal law may only preempt state law if it is enacted within the limited powers that are enumerated and granted to Congress in the Constitution" [V] (Ch 1.2, "Federal Law"). The text gives the example of *State v. Wayfair Inc.* (2017 SD 56) as case-law illustration, which the South Dakota Supreme Court used to hold a state law on internet retailer sales tax unconstitutional [AE] (Ch 1.1, "Promoting Consistency").

### Distinguishing laws from regulations

Laws are passed by Congress or state legislatures; regulations are "standards and rules adopted by administrative agencies that govern how laws will be enforced" and "normally carry the force of law, as long as they demonstrate a reasonable interpretation of the relevant statutes" [V] (Ch 1.2, "Regulations"). The Administrative Procedure Act (APA) "enables the adoption of regulations, which are codified and incorporated into the Code of Federal Regulations (CFR)" (Ch 1.2). Persuasive but not binding outputs include "forms, manuals, policy statements, letters, and rulings" (Ch 1.2).

### Survey of business law's component disciplines

The remainder of Ch 1 surveys the sub-disciplines covered in the book: disputes and dispute settlement; business ethics and social responsibility; constitutional issues; criminal liability; torts; contracts; employment and labour law; antitrust; unfair trade practices; international law; and securities regulation [AP] (Ch 1.3). Each is given a one-paragraph orientation that the later chapters expand.

## Part II: Alternative dispute resolution (Ch 2)

### Negotiation as the foundation of ADR

Negotiation is "considered to be the simplest [form of ADR] because it does not require outside parties" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Negotiation"). The text adopts a definition from *Organization Behavior and Human Decision Processes*: "process by which parties with nonidentical preferences allocate resources through interpersonal activity and joint decision making" [V, BT] (Ch 2.1, "Negotiation"). Two structural distinctions: **dyadic** versus **group** negotiation (by number of parties); and **relational** versus **outcome** goals — relational goals "are focused on building, maintaining, or repairing a partnership, connection, or rapport with another party," while outcome goals "concentrate on achieving certain end results" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Negotiation Types and Objectives").

Future-of-relationship considerations shape goal selection: "when a business negotiates with a supply company that it intends to do business with in the foreseeable future, it will try to focus on 'win-win' solutions that provide the most value for each party. In contrast, if an interaction is of a one-time nature, that same company might approach a supplier with a 'win-lose' mentality, viewing its objective as maximizing its own value at the expense of the other party's value" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Negotiation Types and Objectives"). Win-lose is named **zero-sum** or **distributive**; win-win is named **integrative** (Ch 2.1).

### Five negotiation styles (Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument)

The Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument (TKI) "is a questionnaire that provides a systematic framework for categorizing five broad negotiation styles. It is closely associated with work done by conflict resolution experts Dean Pruitt and Jeffrey Rubin" [V, BT] (Ch 2.1, "Negotiation Style"). The five styles, framed by level of self-interest:

1. **Forcing** — high self-concern, low concern for the other; competitive; prone to zero-sum thinking; "often undermines the party's long-term success" (Ch 2.1).
2. **Collaborating** — high concern for both; "seeks to maximum the gain for both"; recognises mutual interests "create greater value and synergies" (Ch 2.1).
3. **Compromising** — moderate concern for both; parties "find certain points that are more important to one versus the other" (Ch 2.1).
4. **Avoiding** — low concern for both; the party "will often try to avoid negotiation completely" (Ch 2.1).
5. **Yielding** — low self-concern, high concern for the other; the party "will yield to demands that may not be in its own best interest"; the text flags this style as a possible signal of "an unfair power differential" (Ch 2.1).

The Apple-China warranty case is the named worked example: Apple's one-year warranties (instead of the legally required two) "rankled its customers"; Tim Cook's public apology "diffuse[d] a contentious situation that might have resulted in costly litigation" [AE] (Ch 2.1, "Negotiation Styles in Practice").

### Negotiation laws

Negotiations sit under federal and state laws, prominently the **Federal Arbitration Act (FAA)** and **Uniform Arbitration Act**. The FAA "is a national policy that favors arbitration and enforces situations in which parties have contractually agreed to participate in arbitration. Parties who have decided to be subject to binding arbitration relinquish their constitutional right to settle their dispute in court" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Negotiation Laws"). The Uniform Arbitration Act has been "adopted in some form by about 35 states" (Ch 2.1).

### Mediation: solution-focused, future-oriented

A **mediator** is "someone who has been trained in conflict resolution, though often, he or she does not have any expertise in the subject matter that is being disputed" [V] (Ch 2.2, "Court or Agency-Connected Mediation"). Mediation is "distinguished by its focus on solutions. Instead of focusing on discoveries, testimonies, and expert witnesses to assess what has happened in the past, it is future-oriented. Mediators focus on discovering ways to solve the dispute in a way that will appease both parties" [V] (Ch 2.2).

The Chevron internal mediation programme is the named cost-savings example: one dispute was resolved at $25,000, "far less than the estimated $700,000 it would have incurred through the use of outside legal services" and the "estimated $2.5 million" of going to court [AE] (Ch 2.2, "Court or Agency-Connected Mediation").

Three benefits of mediation are flagged (Ch 2.2, "Other Benefits of Mediation"): **confidentiality** (court proceedings become public record); **creativity** (mediators can apply outside-the-box thinking and recognise cultural differences influencing the parties); **control** (parties retain more control than in trial). The Harvard Law School Program on Negotiation reports a study by mediator Peter Adler in which participants remembered mediators as "opening the room, making coffee, and getting everyone introduced," underscoring "the need for mediators to play a role beyond mere administrative functions" [V, BT] (Ch 2.2, "Role of the Mediator").

### Six steps of mediation

Citing nolo.com, six steps are named (Ch 2.2, "Steps of Mediation"):

1. Mediator's opening statement.
2. Opening statements of plaintiff and defendant (no interruption).
3. Joint discussion (mediator guides parties to speak to each other).
4. Private caucus (each party privately with mediator).
5. Joint negotiation (parties rejoin; mediator presents new insight).
6. Closure (agreement reached, or escalation options reviewed).

### Ethical framing: consequentialist vs deontological

Mediators face ethical decisions framed by two competing positions. **Consequentialist ethics** (also called situational ethics) "is a way of looking at difficult decisions by considering their implications" [V] (Ch 2.2, "Ethical Issues"). **Deontologist ethics** "bases its decision on whether the action itself is right or wrong, regardless of its consequences" [V] (Ch 2.2). Kant's **categorical imperative** is quoted: "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can, at the same time, will that it should become a universal law" [V, BT] (Ch 2.2). The text observes: "In real life, it is very difficult to adopt a 100% deontological viewpoint for dispute resolution" (Ch 2.2).

### E-mediation and canine-assisted mediation

Two emerging directions are flagged. **E-mediation** is useful when "parties are geographically far apart, or the transaction in dispute took place online"; eBay uses it to handle "the sheer volume of misunderstandings between parties." A noted benefit: "it allows people the time needed to 'cool down' when they have to explain their feelings in an email" [V] (Ch 2.2, "Future Directions in Mediation"). Canine-assisted mediation (CAM) draws on findings that "the presence of dogs has a positive impact on many of the neurophysiological stress markers in humans" (Ch 2.2).

### Arbitration: structure, types, and procedures

The American Bar Association's definition: arbitration is "the 'private process where disputing parties agree that one or several individuals can make a decision about the dispute after receiving evidence and hearing arguments'" [V, BT] (Ch 2.3, "Arbitration"). The arbitrator "acts more like a judge in a court trial and often has legal expertise" (Ch 2.3); awards "can be a monetary payment" or non-monetary (e.g., requiring a business to stop a practice) (Ch 2.3).

Two structural splits: **voluntary** versus **involuntary** arbitration; **binding** versus **non-binding**. In binding arbitration, "the decision of the arbitrator(s) is final, and except in rare circumstances, neither party can appeal the decision through the court system." In non-binding, "the arbitrator's award can be thought of as a recommendation; it is only finalized if both parties agree" [V] (Ch 2.3, "Types of Arbitration Agreements").

### Three major business applications of arbitration

(Ch 2.3, "Issues Covered by Arbitration Agreements"):

- **Labour.** Two sub-types: **interest arbitration** (disagreements about new contract terms) and **grievance arbitration** (disputes about implementing existing agreements).
- **Business transactions.** Both B2B and B2C; the named example is the Jessica Simpson fitness-video arbitration case.
- **Property disputes.** Physical property (boundary disputes) or intellectual property (trade secrets, inventions, artistic works).

### Forced-arbitration clauses: ethical concern

Forced-arbitration clauses are flagged as a stakeholder-fairness concern: "consumers agree to settle all disputes through arbitration, effectively waiving their right to sue a company in court," and some "cause the other party to forfeit their right to appeal an arbitration decision or participate in any kind of class action lawsuit" [V] (Ch 2.3, "Ethics of Commercial Arbitration Clauses"). The 2006 Enron class action ($7.2 billion award) is offered as a class-action exemplar, with the explicit normative point that arbitration clauses "should not be used as a way to keep wrongdoings 'quiet' or to limit consumers' abilities to obtain rightful retribution" (Ch 2.3).

### Baseball arbitration

Baseball arbitration is presented as a structural fix for arbitrators lacking subject-matter expertise: "each party would submit a lease renewal figure to an arbitrator… the arbitrator chooses one offer or the other, without modification. This type of arbitration incentivizes both parties to be fair in their dealings with one another because to do otherwise would be to their own detriment" [V] (Ch 2.3, "Arbitration Procedures").

### Arbitration awards: bare-bones, reasoned, and enforcement

A **bare-bones** award states the decision; a **reasoned** award lists rationale (Ch 2.3, "Arbitration Awards"). Three enforcement mechanisms are listed (Ch 2.3, "Judicial Enforcement of Arbitration Awards"):

- **Writ of Execution** — Cornell Law School: "A court order that directs law enforcement personnel to take action in an attempt to satisfy a judgment won by the plaintiff" [V, BT].
- **Garnishment** — court order seizing money (typically wages); Social Security Disability Income (SSDI) and similar are exempt; thresholds (e.g., "$1,600 gross/month") may apply.
- **Liens** — entitlement to seize property; commonly placed on real estate, automobiles, boats, with state-law value thresholds (e.g., "vehicle…market value of over $7,500").

## Part III: Business ethics and social responsibility (Ch 3)

### Ethics as the blueprint for organisational success

"Business ethics are considered to be the blueprint for building a successful organization. If an organization is built on socially responsible values, it will be stronger than an organization that is built on profit alone" [V] (Ch 3.1, "Business Ethics"). The text takes the position that "the more a company lives by its set of ethics, the more likely it is to be successful" [V] (Ch 3.1, "Why Is Corporate Ethics So Important in Business?").

The Gravity Payments-Dan Price case is the named positive exemplar: in 2015, the CEO "voluntarily took a huge pay cut and vowed to raise his employees' wages to $70,000" with reported outcomes of skyrocketing revenues and "a 91 percent employee retention rate over the last few years" [AE] (Ch 3.1, "Why Is Corporate Ethics So Important in Business?").

### Four named consequences of unethical action

Unethical decisions, the text argues, produce four downstream effects (Ch 3.1, with bolded paragraph leads in source):

1. **Poor company reputation** — "unethical decisions made by businesspeople become permanent stains on the company" (Ch 3.1).
2. **Negative employee relations** — discrepancies between expectations and leadership behaviour produce disengagement; "the entire company becomes less productive, less responsive to customers, and less profitable" (Ch 3.1).
3. **Recruitment and retention problems** — "Disengaged employees who grow tired of the double standards will leave," followed by customer impact through less experienced replacements (Ch 3.1).
4. **Company credibility lost** — "Customers are savvy enough to follow what's going on from an ethics standpoint" (Ch 3.1).

### Six-step ethical decision-making model (Michigan State University)

(Ch 3.1, "Ethical Decision-Making Policies", citing Michigan State University):

1. Make sure leaders understand the issue and have gathered all related facts.
2. List facts known and assumptions being made (kept separated).
3. Note all concerns: people involved, applicable laws, corporate or professional ethical guidelines.
4. Construct a potential solution.
5. Evaluate the proposed solution against the ethical aspects in step 3.
6. Recommend the solution and required actions.

### Code of conduct: four elements

Citing Kimberlee Leonard of the *Houston Chronicle*, four elements of a code of conduct are catalogued (Ch 3.1, "Establishing a Code of Conduct"):

- **Legal considerations** — anticipating lawsuit risk; defining sexual harassment and the response.
- **Value-based ethics** — commitments to green practices, carbon footprint reduction, charitable giving.
- **Regulatory ethics** — industry-specific standards such as data privacy.
- **Professional behaviours** — conduct in office, while travelling, during meetings, after hours.

A whistleblower policy is the named complement: "violators are identified and action is taken… Retaliation should never be tolerated when it comes to ethics violations" [V] (Ch 3.1, "What to Do When Something Goes Wrong"). Third-party investigative firms are recommended for executive-level cases.

### Corporate social responsibility (CSR) defined

CSR is "the idea that a business is given the opportunity and privilege to make the world a better place," through donations, volunteerism, and "implementation of environmentally friendly policies" [V] (Ch 3.2, "What Is Corporate Responsibility?"). The text traces the idea to *Corporate Responsibility of the Businessman* (1953) as the earliest published book on the topic (Ch 3.2, "Where Did the Concept Originate?"). Volunteerism is quantified: "some 63 million Americans volunteer each year, which is worth around $175 billion in worker hours annually," and U.S. corporations give "over $18 billion to charities each year through fundraisers and employee-employer matching programs" [V] (Ch 3.2).

### Five direct CSR benefits (Kellogg School of Management)

Drawing on Kellogg School research, five benefits are catalogued (Ch 3.2, "How Does Corporate Responsibility Benefit a Business?"):

1. **Improved perception by investors** — exceeding investor expectations on CSR signals financial standing, producing positive stock returns.
2. **Enhanced performance for going green** — operational performance improves into year two; collaboration with industry standard-setting associations (e.g., LEED) amplifies it.
3. **Contracting for success** — tying CEO salary to CSR results increases company value while maintaining bottom line.
4. **The benevolent halo effect** — consumer perception improves; "Customers actually perceive the company and its products in a different way because they expect a better experience" (Ch 3.2).
5. **Consistency of efforts and partnerships** — sustained focus on issues important to employees and customers produces better results.

Two indirect benefits are flagged: **talent attraction** and **consumer influence** (Ch 3.2). Cone Communications data: "60 percent more millennials will engage with brands that discuss and market to social issues"; Adweek estimates millennials represent "around $2.45 trillion in spending per year" [V] (Ch 3.2, "Promoting Corporate Responsibility with Marketing").

### CSR is not social marketing

The text takes a clear position: "one should not use corporate social responsibility as a marketing pitch for a company. Consumers will quickly pick up on this tactic, and it can damage the brand" [V] (Ch 3.2, "Promoting Corporate Responsibility with Marketing"). Bernard Okhakume is quoted on success factors: "the project needs to be sustainable, its topic and practice abide by ethical standards, sensitive to society's needs, embraced and supported by the company's employees, create the aimed effect on the target audience, and every year, and the project needs to be evaluated to see how beneficial it is" [V, BT] (Ch 3.2).

### The triple bottom line (TBL)

TBL "essentially measures the sustainability of an organization's social responsibility efforts," covering "profits, people, and the planet" [V] (Ch 3.2, "The Financial Impact of the Triple Bottom Line"). Andrew Savitz is quoted: TBL "captures the essence of sustainability by measuring the impact of an organization's activities on the world ... including both its profitability and shareholder values and its social, human and environmental capital" [V, BT] (Ch 3.2). The text concedes a measurement difficulty: "while profits can be measured in dollars, and people can be measured in numbers, it can be difficult to measure the impact of social responsibility" (Ch 3.2).

## Part IV: Constitutional law and business (Ch 4)

### Federalism, supremacy, and preemption

Federalism is "the political system that divides authority to govern between the state and federal governments" and is established in the Constitution [V] (Ch 4.1, "The Constitution and the Law"). The Tenth Amendment "states that any area over which the federal government is not granted authority through the Constitution is reserved for the state" (Ch 4.1). The supremacy clause (Article VI) operates such that "if a state law conflicts with the U.S. Constitution, the state law is declared invalid"; the prior step is a court analysis of whether Congress intended to preempt — if yes, federal law prevails; if no, state-law claims may proceed [AP] (Ch 4.1, "Federal Preemption").

### The Commerce Clause: 1937 inflection

The Commerce Clause (Article I, Section 8) grants Congress the power "to 'regulate Commerce with foreign Nations, and among the several States, and with the Indian Tribes'" [V] (Ch 4.1, "The Commerce Clause and The Affordable Care Act"). It "serves to simultaneously empower the federal government, while limiting state power" (Ch 4.1).

The 1937 case *NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel Corp.* is treated as the doctrinal turning point: the prior year's *Carter v. Carter Coal Co.* had invalidated New Deal coal-labour regulation as "local"; *NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel Corp.* held that "Congress could regulate employment practices at a steel plant because any stoppages at that plant would have a serious, detrimental impact on interstate commerce" [AP] (Ch 4.1). The court's verbatim conclusion is blockquoted in the source: "Although activities may be intrastate in character when separately considered, if they have such a close and substantial relationship to interstate commerce that their control is essential or appropriate to protect that commerce from burdens or obstructions, Congress cannot be denied the power to exercise that control" [V, BT] (Ch 4.1, citing *NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel Corp.*, 301 U.S. 1, 1937).

The text quantifies the Commerce Clause's reach: "there are more than 700 legislative provisions that explicitly refer to foreign or interstate commerce" [V] (Ch 4.1, "Challenges to and Reinterpretations of the Commerce Clause"). Named statutes invoking it include the Controlled Substances Act, Federal Mine Safety and Health Act, Civil Rights Act, Americans with Disabilities Act, and Indian Child Welfare Act (Ch 4.1).

The 1995 *United States v. Lopez* is the named limit: the Gun-Free School Zone Act was struck because possession of guns near schools "had 'nothing to do with commerce, or any sort of enterprise'" [V] (Ch 4.1).

### Police power and the dormant Commerce Clause

**Police power** is "the residual powers granted to each state to safeguard the welfare of their inhabitants" — zoning, building codes, sanitation [V] (Ch 4.1, "Police Power and the Dormant Commerce Clause"). The **dormant Commerce Clause** is "the limitation on the authority of states to regulate in areas that impact interstate commerce" [V] (Ch 4.1). The Chicago spray-paint ordinance is the named example, upheld as legitimate-purpose graffiti reduction.

### Four areas of Commerce Clause regulation (Table 4.1)

(Ch 4.1, Table 4.1):

1. **Channels** of interstate commerce — airways, waterways, roadways.
2. **Instrumentalities** — machines, equipment, vehicles, personnel.
3. **Cross-state-line tangibles and intangibles** — goods, electronic databases (named example: Driver's Privacy Protection Act regulating DMV records).
4. **Substantial impact** on interstate commerce — economic activity with significant effect (the *Lopez* limit applies here).

### Bill of Rights as applied to commercial activity

The Bill of Rights summary table covers the first ten amendments (Ch 4.2, Table 4.2). The Fourteenth Amendment "extends the provisions set out in the Bill of Rights to the states" (Ch 4.2). The chapter then surveys application to corporations.

**First Amendment.** Three speech categories are distinguished (Ch 4.2, "The First Amendment"):

- **Corporate political speech.** Protected since *First National Bank of Boston v. Bellotti* (1978).
- **Unprotected speech.** Per *Chaplinsky v. New Hampshire* (1942), speech that could "inflict injury or incite an immediate breach of the peace" is unprotected — obscenities, defamation, slanderous speech.
- **Commercial speech.** The four-part **Central Hudson Test** (1980, *Hudson Gas & Electric Corp v. Public Service Commission of New York*) governs (Ch 4.2).

The **free exercise clause** prohibits laws that prohibit the free exercise of religion; workplace religious-practice cases are noted as recurrent (Ch 4.2).

**Fourth Amendment.** Search-warrant requirements and probable-cause apply to "individual organizations and places of business, as well as residences." The **pervasive-regulation exception** allows warrantless searches of businesses in industries with "a long history of pervasive regulation" — public-health agencies and stone quarries under the Federal Mine Safety and Health Act of 1977 (Ch 4.2, "The Fourth Amendment").

**Fifth Amendment.** Two types of due process and the takings clause are introduced (Ch 4.2, "The Fifth Amendment"):

- **Substantive due process** — laws affecting life, liberty, or property must be "fair and not arbitrary"; the **rational-basis test** requires that the law "bears a rational relationship to a legitimate state interest." Banking regulations, minimum wage laws, and unfair-trade regulations have all been tested against it.
- **Procedural due process** — fair procedures when depriving an individual of life, liberty, or property; applies to government employer dismissal and driver's-licence suspension.
- **Takings clause** — "when the government seizes private property for public use, it is required that the government pay the owner just compensation" (equivalent to market value). Significant regulatory restrictions on land use can constitute a taking.
- **Privilege against self-incrimination** — applies "only" to sole proprietorships not legally distinct from the owner; corporate custodians and agents do not enjoy it (Ch 4.2).

## Part V: Criminal liability and white-collar crime (Ch 5)

### Constitutional authority for federal crime regulation

Congress's power to "regulate Commerce with foreign Nations, and among the several States, and with the Indian Tribes" is the constitutional basis. While "federal courts initially interpreted the commerce power narrowly," over time they "decided that the commerce clause gives the federal government broad powers to regulate commerce, not only on an interstate (between the states) level, but also on an intrastate (within each state) level, as long as some economic transaction is involved" [V] (Ch 5.1, "Constitutional Authority to Regulate Business").

### White-collar crime defined

"White collar crimes are characterized by deceit, concealment, or violation of trust. They are committed by business professionals. They generally involve fraud, and the employees committing the crimes are motivated by the desire for financial gains or fear of losing business standing, money, or property. Fraud is the intentional misrepresentation of material facts for monetary gain. This type of crime is not dependent on threats or violence" [V] (Ch 5.1, "White Collar Crime"). The Hand brothers' 2018 pump-and-dump conviction (9-30 month sentences) is the named exemplar (Ch 5.1).

### Three high-profile white-collar crime types

(Ch 5.1, "Types of Business Crimes"):

- **Ponzi (pyramid) schemes** — "investing scams that promise investors low-risk investment opportunities with a high rate of return," paid to old investors with money from new ones [V]. Bernie Madoff's "20-year Ponzi scheme" is the named exemplar, who "was serving a more than 100-year sentence in prison; however, he died in 2021 while incarcerated" [V] (Ch 5.1).
- **Larceny and embezzlement.** Larceny "occurs when a person unlawfully takes the personal property of another person or a business"; embezzlement "occurs when a person has been entrusted with an item of value and then refuses to return it or does not return the item" [V] (Ch 5.1). The Sue Sachdeva case at Koss Corporation ($34 million embezzled over 5 years; 11 years federal prison) is the named exemplar.
- **Environmental crimes.** Five named federal statutes carrying criminal penalties: Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act, Resource Conservation and Recovery Act, CERCLA, Endangered Species Act (Ch 5.1). The International Petroleum Corporation of Delaware (IPC) Clean Water Act case (1992-2012 falsified water samples) is the named exemplar.

### Other business crimes

Five additional categories are named (Ch 5.1, "Other Types of Business Crime"):

- **Antitrust violations.** Antitrust laws "do not allow activities that restrain trade or promote market domination," with the goal of avoiding **monopolies**. The Sherman Antitrust Act includes criminal provisions.
- **Racketeering.** Includes loan-sharking, money laundering, blackmailing. RICO (Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act) is the federal vehicle: "RICO is now used against insurance companies, stock brokerages, tobacco companies, banks, and other large commercial enterprises" [V, BT, citing Schodolski 2018] (Ch 5.1).
- **Bribery.** "Monetary payments, goods, services, information, or anything of value… exchanged for favorable or desired actions." The Foreign Corrupt Practices Act prohibits bribery payments by U.S. companies to foreign government officials [V] (Ch 5.1).
- **Money laundering.** "Taking 'dirty' money… and passing it through otherwise legitimate businesses so that it appears 'clean'" [V] (Ch 5.1).
- **Spamming.** Illegal under state legislation (Washington state cited as model) prohibiting misrepresentation of sender, hidden identity, and misleading subject lines.

### Criminal procedure: components of crime

Two components must be proven by the prosecutor (Ch 5.2, "Components of Crime"):

- **Mens rea** — "the criminal, or guilty, or 'wrongful' mind."
- **Actus reus** — "the criminal, or guilty, or 'wrongful' act."

Each crime statute should describe the required mental state plus a description of conduct, plus the category (felony, misdemeanour, gross misdemeanour) (Ch 5.2).

### Procedural stages

(Ch 5.2, "Criminal Procedures"):

1. The **information** is the first pleading filed by the prosecutor (the criminal counterpart to a civil "complaint").
2. **Arraignment** — defendant identifies, hears charges, enters plea.
3. Discovery and trial — conviction requires "beyond a reasonable doubt" and unanimous verdict.

### Civil vs criminal: five differentiating axes

(Ch 5.2, "Initiation and Roles"):

- **Initiation.** Criminal cases are initiated by federal/state government (the prosecution); civil cases by private parties (plaintiff/complainant).
- **Burden of proof.** Criminal: "beyond a reasonable doubt." Civil: "preponderance of the evidence."
- **Right to attorney.** Criminal defendants are entitled to one (appointed if necessary); civil parties secure their own.
- **Terminology.** "Liability" in civil; "guilt" in criminal.
- **Consequences.** Civil: typically money damages or injunctive relief. Criminal: incarceration and/or fines.

The *Liebeck v. McDonald's* hot-coffee case is the named civil exemplar (compensatory verdict $160,000); the 2017 Oliver Schmidt VW emissions case is the named corporate criminal exemplar (7-year sentence) (Ch 5.2).

### Professional negligence (malpractice)

Malpractice involves "the degree of care, skill, diligence, and knowledge commonly possessed and exercised by a reasonable, careful, and prudent professional of the same type in the state (or sometimes in the community)" [V] (Ch 5.2, "Professional Negligence"). Compensatory damages include "money for medical bills, lost wages, loss of future earning capacity, pain and suffering, emotional distress, property damage, etc." (Ch 5.2).

## Part VI: The tort system (Ch 6)

### Tort defined

"The word 'tort' means 'wrong' in French. Thus, torts are wrongs committed against others who suffer some form of damage as a result" [V] (Ch 6.1, "Torts"). Tort law "address[es] this damage based on the circumstances of the issue, which is based on **fault**" (Ch 6.1). The standard of proof is lower than in criminal cases. The tortfeasor is the actor; the plaintiff is the injured party; the offender becomes the defendant (Ch 6.1).

### Judge-jury division of labour (Table 6.1)

The judge decides issues of law: "The duty of the defendant to the plaintiff, if any"; "The elements of the defense"; "Application of legal rules." The jury decides questions of fact: "What happened"; "Legal consequences of what happened"; "The damages suffered by the plaintiff" (Ch 6.1, Table 6.1, citing Kionka 2013).

### Two basic elements of torts

"Damages and compensation" — tort law "compensate[s] persons who have suffered damages at the hands of another" [V, BT, citing Baime 2018] (Ch 6.1, "Harm").

### Four named intentional torts

(Ch 6.1, "Intentional Torts"):

- **Assault** — "an intentional act that is designed to cause harm to himself or herself, or to another person."
- **Malicious prosecution** — "files groundless complaints to initiate a criminal matter against another."
- **Defamation** — "intentionally creates and promotes malicious falsehoods about another," with two forms: **slander** (spoken) and **libel** (written or recorded).
- **Invasion of privacy** — "unwanted production of negative public information."

Excusable circumstances include permission from the injured party and defence of property, self, or another (Ch 6.1).

### Four elements of negligence

Per Kionka (2013) (Ch 6.1, "Negligence"):

- A duty by the defendant to either act or refrain from acting.
- A breach of that duty, based on a failure to conform to the standard of care.
- A causal connection between the defendant's action or inaction and the injury.
- Measurable harm that can be remedied in monetary damages.

The **reasonable person** is "someone who must exercise reasonable care based on what he or she knows about the situation, how much experience he or she has with the situation, and how he or she perceives the situation" [V, BT, citing Kionka 2013] (Ch 6.1, "Negligence"). **Special relationships** (e.g., nurse to patient, lifeguard to swimmer) imply a duty of care; passersby do not have a duty to aid, but if they intervene, they are responsible for acting responsibly (Ch 6.1).

### Foreseeability

Negligence depends on whether harm "was reasonably foreseeable, which means that the result was fairly obvious before it occurred" [V] (Ch 6.1, "Foreseeability"). The intoxicated-driver assistance scenario is the named worked example.

### Strict liability and abnormally dangerous activity

Strict liability "determines liability, or harm, based on reasons other than fault" [V, BT, citing CCBC Legal Studies] (Ch 6.2, "Strict Liability"). The **abnormally dangerous activity standard** assigns responsibility for engaging in dangerous activity even if reasonable care was taken. To assign strict liability, the court must establish "at least four of the following six factors" (Ch 6.2):

- Activity poses a high degree of risk of harm to a person, land, or property.
- Resulting harm would likely be substantial.
- Reasonable care would not eliminate the risk.
- Activity is not a matter of common usage.
- Activity is not appropriate for the place where it occurs.
- Danger overshadows benefit to the community.

Trespass is named as a partial exception (the property owner does not have the duty to make premises safe for trespassers), with five sub-exceptions: common-trespass areas, owner aware of trespasser, trespasser needs aid, trespasser is a child and the activity is an **attractive nuisance**, etc. (Ch 6.2, "Trespass").

### Product liability and res ipsa loquitor

"It is not always possible to conclusively prove that an act or omission was responsible for the harm" [V, BT, citing Baime 2018], so courts developed **res ipsa loquitor** ("whatever it is speaks for itself"), shifting the burden of proof from plaintiff to defendant. The plaintiff must first establish three factors (Ch 6.2, "Product Liability"):

- Defendant had control over the product during manufacture.
- Under normal use, the product would not cause damage, but harm has occurred.
- Plaintiff's behaviour did not significantly contribute to the harm.

The **unreasonably dangerous product standard** identifies three sources of danger (Ch 6.2, "The Unreasonably Dangerous Product Standard"):

- Manufacturing flaw — failure to exercise proper care during manufacturing.
- Design defect — dangerous design where safer alternatives are available and economically feasible.
- Insufficient warnings or instructions.

### Defences to product liability

(Ch 6.2, "Defenses"):

- **Contributory negligence** — when the court finds it, "prevents any recovery of damages by the plaintiff." Two forms: **assumption of risk** and **misuse**.
- **Comparative negligence** — plaintiff's actions contributed but plaintiff may still receive damages adjusted by the percentage of fault (named example: 30% / 70% allocation).

## Part VII: Contract law (Ch 7)

### Definition and elements

"A contract is defined as an agreement between two or more parties that is enforceable by law" [V] (Ch 7.1, "Agreement, Consideration, and Promissory Estoppel"). To be enforceable, a contract must contain: offer and acceptance, genuine agreement, consideration, capacity, and legality (Ch 7.1).

The text develops each element:

- **Offer and acceptance.** Offers must be made with intent, must be definite and certain, must be communicated to the offeree. Acceptance must demonstrate "willingness to consent to all of the terms of the offer."
- **Genuine agreement** ("a meeting of the minds") can be destroyed by fraud, misrepresentation, mistake, duress, or undue influence.
- **Consideration.** "A thing of value promised in exchange for something else of value. This mutual exchange binds the parties together." Consideration "need not be pecuniary."
- **Capacity.** "The law presumes that anyone entering a contract has the legal capacity to do so. Minors are generally excused from contractual responsibility, as are mentally incompetent and drugged or drunk individuals."
- **Legality.** Illegal-purpose or against-public-policy contracts are unenforceable.

### Promissory estoppel: five elements

The promissory estoppel doctrine "is an exception to the requirement of consideration for contracts. Promissory estoppel is triggered when one party acts on the other party's promise" [V] (Ch 7.1, "Promissory Estoppel"). Five elements (Ch 7.1):

1. Legal relationship existed between the parties.
2. A promise was made.
3. Reliance on the promise that caused one party to act before consideration was exchanged.
4. Substantial and measurable detriment occurred from failure to perform.
5. Unconscionable result or gross injustice from the broken promise.

If satisfied, the court awards "reliance damages to restore the aggrieved party to the position they were in prior to the broken promise. Expectation damages are not usually available if promissory estoppel is being claimed" [V] (Ch 7.1). The Bob-MegaCorp Co. relocation case is the named worked example (Ch 7.1).

### Three classes presumed to lack capacity

(Ch 7.2, "Capacity and Legality"):

- **Minors.** "In most states, minors under the age of 18 lack the capacity to make a contract and may therefore either honor an agreement or void the contract." Exception: contracts for **necessities** (food, clothing) cannot be voided. The Mary endorsement case is named (cannot void at age 20).
- **Mental incapacity.** Measured by the **cognitive standard**: whether the party "understood its meaning and effect." The Mr. Williams patent-sale case is named: California Court of Appeals refused to terminate the contract because his manic state "may have impaired his judgment but not his understanding."
- **Voluntary intoxication.** "Courts generally do not find lack of capacity to contract for people who are voluntarily intoxicated" — to prevent side-stepping obligations. But severe inebriation impairing understanding can void; the late-1900s stock-binge case is named (sold at 1.5% of value while bank-aware-third-party engaged).

### Material vs minor breach

"When something substantially different from what was expected under the terms of the contract is delivered, the breach will be considered material" [V] (Ch 7.3, "Material vs. Minor Breach"). Material breach gives the non-breaching party "the right to all remedies for breach of the entire contract" and discharge of obligations. Minor breach (e.g., late delivery without "time of the essence") still requires performance, with damages possible.

Court considerations for materiality: benefit received by non-breaching party, adequacy of compensation, extent of performance by breaching party, hardship to breaching party, negligence or intent of breaching party, possibility of completion (Ch 7.3).

### Five remedies

(Ch 7.3, "Remedies"):

- **Money damages** — compensation for financial losses.
- **Restitution** — restores the injured party "to status quo or the position they had prior to the formation of the contract"; returns money or property.
- **Rescission** — "terminates the duties of both parties under the contract."
- **Reformation** — "allows courts to equitably change the contract's substance."
- **Specific performance** — "compels one party to perform the promises stated in the contract"; only when money damages are inadequate; "Personal service, however, may not be used to compel specific performance, since doing so would constitute forced labor, i.e. slavery, which is in violation of the U.S. Constitution" [V] (Ch 7.3, "Remedies").

## Part VIII: Sales contracts and the UCC (Ch 8)

### Six features of sales contracts

A sales contract is "a specific type of contract in which one party is obligated to deliver and transfer ownership of a good to a second party, who in turn is obligated to pay for the good in money, or its equivalent" [V] (Ch 8.1, "Features of Sales Contracts"). The vendor is the seller; the vendee is the buyer. Six features (Ch 8.1):

1. **Consensual** — perfected by mere consent.
2. **Bilateral** — both parties bound to reciprocal obligations.
3. **Onerous** — good and price exchanged in mutual consideration.
4. **Commutative** — good and price are equivalents.
5. **Nominate** — special designation (sale).
6. **Principal** — validity does not depend on other contracts.

### The UCC: history and three categories

State common and statutory law governed sales until 1952, when "the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) was created to govern business transactions. All 50 states have adopted the Code, but each has the power to modify it, in line with the wishes of the state legislature" [V] (Ch 8.1, "Sources of Law for Sales Contracts").

UCC categorises items as (Ch 8.1, "The Uniform Commercial Code"):

- **Goods** — Section 2-105: tangible items "which are movable at the time of identification to the contract for sale" [V].
- **Services** — movable but not tangible (e.g., accounting).
- **Realty** — non-good items that are tangible but not movable.

Article 2 specifically governs sales of goods. The 2008 *Crown Castle Inc. et al. v. Fred Nudd Corporation et al.* case is the named exemplar of the goods/realty boundary (cell phone towers ruled goods). **Trade fixtures** (e.g., counters used in business) are treated as goods. Software licences have been treated variously as goods, **mixed sales**, and pure services.

### Four ways to be classified as a merchant (Table 8.1)

(Ch 8.1, "The Uniform Commercial Code"):

1. An agent who regularly sells goods in business or trade (online auction seller).
2. An individual who employs others to sell goods (clothing-store owner).
3. A person who works for a person who sells goods (grocery-retail employee).
4. Any entity who self-identifies as a merchant in corporate documents.

### Open terms and the quantity exception

The UCC modifies basic-contract definiteness "with an overlay of 'reasonableness'" [V] (Ch 8.1, "Formation of Sales Contracts under the UCC"). Default rules (Table 8.2):

- Price → "reasonable price" (UCC 2-305(1)).
- Payment → "due at the time and place at which the buyer is to receive the goods" (UCC 2-310(a)).
- Delivery → "buyer normally takes delivery at the seller's place of business" (UCC 2-308(a)).
- Duration of an ongoing contract → "reasonable time" (UCC 2-309).

"The only term that really cannot be left open is the **quantity** term" [V]. Two exceptions: **requirements contracts** ("as much as I need") and **output contracts** ("as much as you can produce"); both are technically illusory but allowed under UCC 2-306 with good-faith limits (Ch 8.1).

### Firm offers and the absent mirror-image rule

Under UCC Section 2-205, merchants' written offers explicitly stating irrevocability are **firm offers**, with a default three-month irrevocability period if unspecified. Acceptance can be in any reasonable manner; the **mirror-image rule does not apply** under the UCC — non-mirroring acceptance is treated as counteroffer with no contract. Sale-of-goods contracts $500 or more must be in writing. Modifications must be in good faith; new consideration is not required. **Unconscionable** terms may render a contract or its provisions unenforceable [AP] (Ch 8.1).

### Three types of title

(Ch 8.1, "Title"):

- **Good title** — from someone who owns goods free and clear.
- **Void title** — from someone who does not legitimately own; "good faith is irrelevant when a void title is acquired." Exception: when an owner **entrusts** goods to a merchant who deals in those goods, a good-faith buyer acquires good title (motorcycle-repair shop named example).
- **Voidable title** — buyer deceit about identity, buyer is a minor, bad cheque, etc.

### Four contract types and risk-of-loss rules

The UCC treats four scenarios separately (Ch 8.1, "Issues Associated with Title"):

- **Simple delivery contract** — title transfers at execution; risk transfers at possession unless the seller is not a merchant (then risk remains with buyer under **tender of delivery**).
- **Common-carrier delivery contract.** Two sub-types:
  - **Shipment contract** — title passes at time of shipment; buyer bears risk of loss.
  - **Destination contract** — title transfers when goods are delivered; seller bears risk until then.
- **Goods-in-bailment contract** — **negotiable** documents transfer title and risk together upon endorsement; **non-negotiable** documents transfer title at endorsement but risk only when custodian is notified.
- **Conditional sales contract** — sale dependent on approval (sale-or-return).

### International sale: CISG

The **United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods (CISG)** "broadly covers the same topics as the UCC, but it preempts the UCC if there is a problem with an international sale" [V] (Ch 8.1, "The International Sale of Goods").

### Warranties: express, implied, and three classes

A **warranty** is "a guarantee on the good that comes as part of the sales contract" treated as an additional binding contract; if the standard is unmet, "the contract provides a specific remedy, such as a replacement or repair" [V] (Ch 8.2, "Warranties"). UCC 1-203 requires **good faith** ("honesty in fact and the observance of reasonable commercial standards of fair dealing") and, between merchants, **commercial reasonableness** [V] (Ch 8.2).

**Express warranties** are seller statements (oral or written) about quality. Examples in source: "Wrinkle-free shirt"; "Lifetime guarantee"; "Made in the USA"; "This orange juice is not from concentrate"; "24k gold." Puffery ("this sandwich is the best in the world") is lawful and "cannot ordinarily be treated as a legally binding warranty"; courts apply a **reasonableness test of reliance** (Ch 8.2, "Express and Implied Warranties").

**Implied warranties** arise automatically from sale. Two key implied warranties (Ch 8.2, "Implied Warranties"):

- **Warranty against infringement** — goods sold by merchants carry implied protection from third-party trademark/patent/IP infringement claims.
- **Warranty of fitness for normal use** — goods must be fit for the ordinary purposes for which they are sold.

The implied **warranty of merchantability** generally prevails over inconsistent express warranties unless the express and implied can be construed as **consistent and cumulative** (Ch 8.2). Defects defining breach: design defects, manufacturing defects, inadequate instructions, inadequate warnings (Ch 8.2, "Breaches of Warranty").

Three special warranty cases (Table 8.3): second-hand goods (held to lower standard); buyer-designed goods (no fitness-for-purpose warranty); food and drink (implied warranty of fitness for human consumption) (Ch 8.2).

### Warranty of title

By the act of selling, "the vendor implies a warranty that the title is good and that the transfer of title is lawful," with goods delivered free of liens unknown to buyer. Exceptions: representative-capacity sellers (e.g., estate executors) (Ch 8.2, "Warranty of Title").

### Four buyer remedies under the UCC (Table 8.4)

(Ch 8.2, "Remedies to Buyers under the UCC"):

- Cancel the contract for nonconforming goods.
- **Obtain cover** — substitute goods, "reasonable, acquired without delay, and obtained in good faith."
- Obtain specific performance for unique goods.
- Sue for consequential, incidental, liquidated, or punitive damages.

## Part IX: Employment and labour law (Ch 9)

### At-will employment and its limits

"Up until 1959… employers had the right to fire a worker without giving any reason. This concept, which was known as **at-will employment**, was applicable in all states. The concept of at-will employment does, however, continue today, and all employees are considered to be at-will unless they are employed under a collective bargaining agreement, or under a contract for a set duration. Employers can still fire employees for any reason, but they cannot be fired for illegal reasons, as set out in the U.S. or state constitutions, federal law, state statutes, or public policy" [V] (Ch 9.1, "Employment, Worker Protection, and Immigration Law").

### Health and safety: OSHA

The **Occupational Safety and Health Act** (1970) established **OSHA** to "assure safe and healthy working conditions for working men and women by setting and enforcing standards and by providing training, outreach, education and assistance" [V, BT] (Ch 9.1, "Health and Safety"). OSHA covers private employers and federal agencies (most states' self-employed and state/local government workers excluded). Two standard types (Table 9.1): **specific duty standards** (specific work, procedures, equipment) and **general duty standards** (apply to all employers; protection from known hazards).

### Workers' compensation

**Workers' Compensation Acts** require insurance or self-insurance against compensation claims. Three-step claim process (Ch 9.1):

1. Worker files claim with state agency.
2. Agency establishes legitimacy.
3. Compensation paid if legitimate.

Workers' compensation is an **exclusive remedy** — "workers cannot sue the employer in court for further damages beyond that which is paid out under the compensation claim. An exception is made when the employer intentionally injures the worker" [V] (Ch 9.1). The *Chad A. Kelley v. Marsha P. Ryan… Coca-Cola Enterprises* "horseplay" canoe-trip case is the named exemplar, where the appellate court ruled the employer responsible.

### Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA)

Three categories (Ch 9.1, "Fair Labor Standards Act"):

- **Child labour.** Minimum non-agricultural work age is 14. Minors under 14 may deliver newspapers, babysit, perform chores, work in family businesses (non-hazardous), and perform in television/radio/movie/theatrical productions. Child labour regulations end at 18.
- **Minimum wage.** Federal minimum wage was raised by Congress in 2009 to $7.25 an hour, "the first [increase] in almost a decade" [V]. President Obama's 2014 executive order raised it to $10.10 for new federal contracts.
- **Overtime pay.** "More than 40 hours in a week should receive overtime pay that is equal to at least one and one-half times their regular wage" [V]. Excluded: executives, administrative employees, professional employees, outside salespersons.

### Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA)

The 1993 FMLA "guarantees all eligible workers up to 12 weeks of unpaid leave during any 12-month period for family and medical emergencies" [V] (Ch 9.1, "Family and Medical Leave Act"). Applies to public/private employers with 50+ employees; eligibility requires one year tenure and 25 hours/week for 12 months prior. Five qualifying events: birth of child; adoption; foster placement; care of seriously ill spouse/parent/child; serious health condition preventing essential job functions. Same or equivalent position must be restored.

### Ending employment: COBRA, ERISA, unemployment compensation

(Ch 9.1, Table 9.2):

- **COBRA** — terminated employees can continue group health insurance at group rate.
- **ERISA** — covers employer-offered pension plans; provides for **vesting** (nonforfeitable right to receive pension benefits).
- **Unemployment compensation** — temporarily unemployed workers; funded by employer taxes; quitting voluntarily or termination for bad conduct disqualifies; applicants must be available for work.

Immigration law's main employment touchpoints: USCIS administration; EB-1 visa for "extraordinary ability"; Immigration Reform and Control Act (IRCA) requires employer evidence-of-identity examination, with serious financial and criminal penalties for knowingly hiring undocumented workers (Ch 9.1, "Immigration Law").

### Labour law: trade unions

A trade union "is an organized group of workers who come together to lobby employers about conditions affecting their work. There currently are around 60 unions representing 14 million workers across the United States" [V] (Ch 9.2, "What Is a Trade Union?").

### Timeline of US labour law

(Ch 9.2, "Timeline of Developments in Labor Law"):

- **1886** — American Federation of Labor formed in Columbus, Ohio (craft-dominated until C20).
- **1932** — Norris-LaGuardia Act prohibits **yellow-dog contracts** (which prevented joining unions); barred federal court injunctions against boycotts/strikes/picketing.
- **1935** — Congress of Industrial Organizations established (semi-skilled and unskilled welcomed).
- **1935** — Wagner Act (**National Labor Relations Act**), the major statute of US labour law: rights to form, assist, join unions; engage in collective bargaining; engage in concerted activity.
- **1947** — Taft-Hartley Act (Labor-Management Relations Act) imposed restrictions on union power; six unfair-practice amendments (Table 9.3): protection from union discrimination; no refusal to hire on union grounds (with 30-day union-security agreements permitted); good-faith bargaining required of unions; secondary boycotts prohibited; ban on excessive dues and pay-for-no-work; employer free speech protected.
- **1959** — Landrum-Griffin Act regulates internal union affairs and officials' relationships with employers.
- **1988** — WARN Act requires 60-day notice for layoffs/plant closings (employers with 100+).

### NLRB and elections

The **National Labor Relations Board** has three functions (Ch 9.2, "The National Labor Relations Board"):

1. Monitor union and employer conduct during representation elections.
2. Remedy and prevent unfair labour practices.
3. Establish rules interpreting the NLRA.

Excluded from coverage: government employees, transportation workers (Railway Labor Act), agricultural workers, confidential employees, independent contractors, and those employed by a spouse or parent.

Three election types (Ch 9.2, "Organizing a Union"):

- **Consent election** — no substantial issues; pre-election hearing waived.
- **Contest election** — employer-contested; NLRB supervises.
- **Decertification election** — employees vote out the union or join another.

The **inaccessibility exception** allows union solicitation on the firm's property when no other access exists (Ch 9.2).

Three union security agreement types (Ch 9.2):

- **Closed shop** — union membership required for employment.
- **Union shop** — must join within a specified number of days of hire.
- **Agency shop** — fees required, but membership not required.

### Collective bargaining and strikes

Collective bargaining concerns "wages and salaries, hours, and the terms and conditions of employment" [V] (Ch 9.2, "Collective Bargaining"). Strikes are permitted but subject to a 60-day "mandatory cooling off period." Four illegal strike types: violent, sit-down, wildcat (unauthorised), and intermittent/partial. Picketing is lawful unless it involves violence, prevents customer entry, prevents non-striker entry, or prevents deliveries/pickups.

**Secondary boycott picketing** (against the employer's customers or suppliers) is "legal if it is product picketing, but illegal if the picket is directed against a neutral business" [V] (Ch 9.2, "Collective Bargaining").

### Equal opportunity in employment

The Ann Hopkins / Price Waterhouse case (1982) is the chapter's named landmark exemplar: rejected for partner despite billing "more than $34 million in consulting contracts," she was awarded $371,000 in back pay and partnership after suing on grounds the partners said she should "walk more femininely, talk more femininely, dress more femininely, wear makeup, have her hair styled and wear jewelry" [V] (Ch 9.3, "A Landmark Case").

### Title VII of the Civil Rights Act

Title VII "bans employers from discriminating against employees in their hiring, firing, and promotion practices on the basis of sex, national origin, color, religion, or race" and covers all commercial employers with 15+ employees for 20 consecutive weeks/year [AP] (Ch 9.3, "Civil Rights Act of 1964 – Title VII"). Two ways discrimination can be proven (Ch 9.3):

- **Disparate treatment** — three-step process: plaintiff demonstrates *prima facie* case; employer must show legitimate non-discriminatory reasons; plaintiff must demonstrate the reason is mere pretext.
- **Disparate impact** — unintentional discrimination; plaintiff must statistically establish disproportionate injurious impact on a protected class. *Griggs v. Duke Power Co.* is the named exemplar (high school diploma + intelligence test produced disparate impact: 12% black men with diplomas vs 34% white; 6% black vs 58% white passing tests) (Ch 9.3).

### Sexual harassment

Two recognised types (Ch 9.3):

- **Quid pro quo** — manager makes a sexual demand perceived as a condition of employment.
- **Hostile work environment** — pervasive harassing conduct.

*Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services Inc.* (1997) established same-sex sexual harassment as actionable. The **Pregnancy Discrimination Act** (1987) expanded sex discrimination to include pregnancy, childbirth, and related medical conditions (Ch 9.3).

### Three Title VII defences (Table 9.5)

(Ch 9.3, "Defenses to Title VII Claims"):

- **Bona Fide Occupational Qualification (BFOQ)** — discrimination "necessary for the performance of the job." Must be based on actual qualifications, not stereotypes. No BFOQs for race or colour.
- **Merit defence** — decisions based on test scores, validated to professional standards, manifestly job-related.
- **Seniority defence system** — preferential treatment for tenure; lawful if not rooted in discrimination and applies equally.

### Equal Pay Act and ADA / ADEA

The Equal Pay Act requires equal pay for jobs demanding "equal skill, effort, and responsibility, and which are performed under similar working conditions" [V] (Ch 9.3, "The Equal Pay Act"). Four affirmative defences: seniority system; merit system; production-quality/quantity system (e.g., piece rates); "any other factor other than sex" (the latter "has been the subject of intense debate").

**Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)** — prevents disability-based discrimination; requires reasonable accommodations not imposing undue burden. Three plaintiff requirements: has a disability; suffered adverse employment decision because of it; was otherwise qualified (Ch 9.3).

**Age Discrimination Act** (1967) — prohibits discriminatory employment decisions against people 40+ (Ch 9.3, "Age Discrimination Act"). Applies to employers with 20+ employees.

## Part X: Administrative law and regulatory agencies (Ch 10)

### Administrative law defined

"Administrative law is also referred to as **regulatory** and **public law**. It is the law that is related to administrative agencies. Administrative agencies are established by statutes and governed by rules, regulations and orders, court decisions, judicial orders, and decisions" [V] (Ch 10.1, "Administrative Law"). Federal agencies are created by act of Congress through an **organic statute** that "lays out the purpose and structure of the agency" [V] (Ch 10.1).

### Industrialisation as expansion driver

The number of agencies expanded with industrialisation, including the FDA (1906, Pure Food and Drugs Act), FTC (regulate trade), and Federal Reserve System (regulate banks) (Ch 10.1, "Industrialization"). Examples developed: the FDA's EpiPen actions (2018: 4-month expiration extension; first generic approval) (Ch 10.1).

### Delegation, executive branch, and ALJs

"Assigning this authority to agencies is called **delegation**" [V] (Ch 10.2, "Regulatory Agencies"). Most administrative agencies sit in the executive branch; leaders are typically appointed by the president. Administrative law judges (ALJs) "have two primary duties": oversee procedural aspects (depositions, witness review) and determine whether agency rules were broken. They can "award money, other benefits, and punish those found guilty" but jurisdiction is limited to agency-rule evaluation (Ch 10.2).

### FTC structure: three bureaus

The FTC is "organized into bureaus" with three (Ch 10.2, "Agency Structure"):

- **Bureau of Consumer Protection** — unfair and deceptive business practices; complaints, investigations, lawsuits.
- **Bureau of Competition** — antitrust laws; supports lower prices and consumer choice.
- **Bureau of Economics** — consumer-protection investigation, rulemaking, economic-impact analysis.

### Administrative Procedure Act (APA) constraints

The 1946 APA "set up a full system for the execution of administrative law by administrative agencies for the federal government" [V] (Ch 10.2, "Administrative Procedure Act (APA)"). Three named operational constraints: due process requirements, no violation of constitutional rights or civil liberties, public-comment opportunity. Agencies are bounded by "the Constitution, span of authority, statutory limitations, and other restrictions" (Ch 10.2). The APA "organizes administrative functions into rulemaking and adjudication" (Ch 10.2).

## Part XI: Antitrust law (Ch 11)

### Origins: late-1800s industrial consolidation

In the late 1800s, "the United States became concerned about the development of corporate monopolies dominating the manufacturing and mining industries" [V, BT, citing Jurist] (Ch 11.1, "Business Trusts"). Post-Civil-War industrialisation produced supply outpacing demand; companies sought to reduce competitors through "**restraint of trade** such as price-fixing, monopolies, and mergers" [V, BT, citing West] (Ch 11.1).

A **business trust** is "a trust agreement that allows businesses to maintain profits as beneficiaries, but legal ownership and management of the company's property is maintained through the power of trustees" [V, BT, citing West] (Ch 11.1). Trusts allowed coordinated price-fixing and predatory pricing that drove smaller competitors out of business (Ch 11.1, "Unfair Business Practices").

### Rule of reason: naked vs ancillary

The **rule of reason** distinguishes (Ch 11.1, "Rule of Reason"):

- **Naked restraint** — contracts promote general restraint of competition; long-term, unbounded; courts disapprove.
- **Ancillary restraint** — limited in time and geography; courts treat inconsistently.

### Three federal antitrust statutes

(Ch 11.2, "Antitrust Laws"):

- **Sherman Antitrust Act (1890)** — first antitrust law. A criminal statute; prohibits unreasonable restraints. Violations may include "agreements with competitors to set prices." Penalties up to $100 million for corporations, $1 million for individuals, up to 10 years in prison; if gain or loss exceeds $100 million, fine may be doubled (Ch 11.2). Limitation: "did not provide clear and specific language, which left the courts to make decisions on a case-by-case basis, without any consistent precedent" (Ch 11.2).
- **Clayton Act (1914)** — civil statute; focuses on unfair mergers and acquisitions. Four illegal acts (Ch 11.2):
  1. **Price discrimination** — same product sold to different buyers at different prices.
  2. **Exclusive dealing contracts** — buyers required to purchase only from one business.
  3. **Corporate mergers** — acquisition of competing companies.
  4. **Interlocking directorates** — boards with common members across competitors.

  Acts are illegal "only when they create monopolies or substantially lessen competition" [V, BT, citing West] (Ch 11.2). Unions excluded ("Congress did not wish to treat human labor as a commodity"). 1976 amendment requires advance notification and authorisation for larger mergers; victims can sue for "triple damages" (Ch 11.2).
- **Federal Trade Commission Act (1914)** — focuses on "unfair methods of competition and deceptive acts or practices that impact commerce" [V, BT, citing West]. "All acts that violate the Sherman Act also violate the FTC Act" (Ch 11.2). Two named remedies: **divestiture** (forces giving up operating functions) and **dissolution** (terminates a partnership's right to exist) (Ch 11.2).

### Four antitrust exemptions

(Ch 11.2, "Exemptions"):

- **Labour** — unions can organise and bargain unless combining with non-labour groups.
- **Agriculture and fisheries** — collective co-ops if no restraint of trade.
- **Foreign trade** — cooperative export activities allowed unless restraining domestic trade.
- **Cooperative research and production** — small businesses can collaborate on research joint ventures.

## Part XII: Unfair trade practices and the FTC (Ch 12)

### Section 5(a) of the FTC Act

The FTC Act "prohibits 'unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce'" [V] (Ch 12.1). Per the rule, **unfair practices** are those that cause or are likely to cause consumer injury, are unavoidable by consumers, and where the benefit does not outweigh the deception. **Deceptive practices** are those where "the seller misrepresents or misleads the consumer, and the misleading practice is substantial" [V] (Ch 12.1).

### Six categories of unfair trade practices

(Ch 12.1, "Unfair Trade Practices and Examples"):

1. **Product guarantees and false endorsements** — companies must honour advertised guarantees; cannot fabricate testimonials.
2. **Unfair advertising** — misrepresentation of product, service, or price; **bait and switch** (advertising one item, selling another, typically more expensive). Other examples: incorrect pricing, fake endorsements, deceptive guarantees, false statements, exaggerated descriptions. Ivan's Amazon-curtains case is the named worked example.
3. **Taking advantage of customers** — targeting vulnerable populations (e.g., seniors, non-English speakers). The Devin telemarketing case is the named exemplar.
4. **Misrepresenting a product** — technical-term restrictions; "the word 'new' may only be used to refer to a product that is less than six months old"; "a sweater should not be called 'wool' unless that is its complete composition" [V] (Ch 12.1).
5. **Giving misleading price information** — false sales (false "limited time offer," false "Going Out of Business"). The Black Friday phone deal hidden plan-price example is named.
6. **Failing to disclose pertinent information** — withholding facts that would influence consumer decisions; sellers must disclose full price before payment.

Plaintiffs "do not have to prove intent. Showing that the practice itself was unfair or deceptive is sufficient" [V] (Ch 12.1).

### FTC: history and Wheeler-Lea

The FTC was created in 1914 to address monopolies and trusts. The **Wheeler-Lea Act** (1938) gave the FTC "the power to combat false advertising for any foods, drugs, medical devices, or cosmetics" [V] (Ch 12.2, "The Federal Trade Commission"); "the FTC did not formally have a consumer protection mission until" then.

### FTC three bureaus and four enforcement options

The Bureau of Consumer Protection (advertising/marketing, privacy, financial products, identity protection, and the National Do Not Call Registry); Bureau of Competition (antitrust enforcement, shared with DOJ); Bureau of Economics (subject-matter expertise on economic impacts) (Ch 12.2, "Bureaus of the FTC").

If FTC concludes unlawful conduct, recourse options (Ch 12.2, "FTC Activities"):

- Voluntary compliance through a consent order.
- Filing of administrative complaints.
- Initiation of federal action and litigation.

The FTC also creates **trade rules** for "widespread industry practices" (Ch 12.2).

## Part XIII: International law (Ch 13)

### International law framing

"International law relates to the policies and procedures that govern relationships among nations" [V, BT, citing Clarkson, Miller & Cross 2018] (Ch 13.1). The text observes "there is not a single authoritative legislative source for global business affairs, nor a single world court responsible for interpreting international law… There is also not a global executive branch that enforces international law" [V, BT, citing Cheeseman 2016] (Ch 13.1). Enforcement remedies when persuasion fails include "economic sanctions, severance of diplomatic relations, boycotts, or even war" [V, BT, citing Clarkson et al. 2018] (Ch 13.1).

### Two US Constitution clauses on international law

(Ch 13.1, "U.S. Constitutional Clauses"):

- **Foreign Commerce Clause** — empowers Congress to "regulate commerce with foreign nations." States cannot "unduly burden foreign commerce." General Motors example: cannot suggest a 50% tax on foreign vehicles only, but Michigan can tax all vehicles 10% to offset trade costs.
- **Treaty Clause** — president has the power "by and with the advice and consent of the senate" to create treaties. States cannot enter treaties. "Any treaties established with other countries become U.S. law, and any conflicting law is null and void."

### Three primary sources of international law

(Ch 13.1, "Primary Sources of International Law"):

- **International customs.** "Accepted as law" per the Statute of the International Court of Justice; **customary international law (CIL)** is "not written, nor does it require ratification to become binding"; provides guidelines for business affairs. Ambassador protection is the named example.
- **International treaties.** **Bilateral** (two nations; named example Peru-United States Trade Promotion Agreement, 2006/2007) and **multilateral** (named example NAFTA, 1994).
- **International organisations.** "The U.S. participates in more than 120 bilateral and multilateral organizations." Two named exemplars: the United Nations and the European Union.

### United Nations

The UN was created as a multilateral treaty in 1945; goals include "maintaining global peace and security, promoting economic and social cooperation, and protecting human rights, especially related to women and children" [V, BT, citing Cheeseman] (Ch 13.1, "United Nations"). The General Assembly includes representatives from each member nation. As of 2018, "the UN acknowledges 195 sovereign states, with all but two participating as full members"; observer states are Palestine and Vatican City. Six countries are not UN members but recognised by at least one UN member: Abkhazia, Kosovo, Northern Cypress, South Ossetia, Taiwan, Western Sahara.

The Security Council includes five permanent members (China, France, Russia, the United Kingdom, the United States) and 10 selected by the General Assembly for two-year terms (Ch 13.1).

The World Bank, headquartered in Washington DC, provides money to developing countries for projects relieving suffering through grants and long-term low-interest-rate loans, with debt relief possible (Ch 13.1).

The United Nations Commission on International Trade Law established the **CISG** in 1980 (Ch 13.1).

### European Union

The EU is "a regional international organization that includes many countries in Europe" [V, BT, citing Cheeseman] established for peace and economic, social, and cultural development. As of 2018, 28 member countries (with the UK in the process of withdrawal). The EU represents "more than 500 million people and a gross community product that exceeds that of the United States, Canada, and Mexico combined" [V, BT, citing Cheeseman] (Ch 13.1, "European Union").

### Sovereignty and the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act

National sovereignty allows nations to enter treaties and honour territorial boundaries. The text traces the shift from "absolute sovereignty" of the 1800s to "doctrines of limited immunity" of the 1940s (Ch 13.1, "Sovereignty"). The **doctrine of sovereign immunity** "states that countries are granted immunity from lawsuits in courts of other countries" [V, BT]. The US adapted to qualified immunity in 1952; the **Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976** allows US governance of foreign-state lawsuits when immunity has been waived or when the activity "causes a direct effect in the United States" (Ch 13.1).

### CISG: ratification scope

By January 2018, "84 countries had adopted CISG, including the countries that account for more than two-thirds of all global trade," including the US, Canada, China, Japan, Mexico, Argentina, Brazil, and most European countries [V, BT, citing Clarkson et al.] (Ch 13.2, "Sources of International Law"). The CISG "is enforced whenever international transactions occur without the presence of written contracts to govern those transactions"; it does not apply to consumer sales or service contracts.

### Three principles and doctrines

(Ch 13.2, "International Principles and Doctrines"):

- **Principle of Comity** — "nations will defer to the laws and decrees of other nations when those laws are consistent with their own, essentially upholding reciprocity between nations with similar laws" [V, BT, citing Cross & Miller].
- **Act of State Doctrine** — "applicable in England and the United States. It states that these two nations will not pass legal judgement on public acts committed by a recognized government if those acts occur within that government's own territory." Petrobras oil pricing in Brazil is the named example.
- **Doctrine of Sovereign Immunity** — exception applies when foreign country conducts US commercial activity (Ch 13.2).

### Common vs civil law systems

Common law systems "operate independently by developing their own rules… The United States has a common law system. One-third of all people in the world live in nations in which common law is practiced." Civil law systems "base their legislation on Roman civil law, which utilizes statutory codes as the primary source of law" [V, BT, citing Cross & Miller] (Ch 13.2, "International Law Enforcement"). Table 13.1 lists country examples: common law (Australia, Bangladesh, Canada, Ghana, India, Israel, Jamaica, Kenya, Malaysia, New Zealand, Nigeria, Singapore, United Kingdom, United States, Zambia); civil law (Argentina, Austria, Brazil, Chile, China, Egypt, Finland, France, Germany, Greece, Indonesia, Iran, Italy, Japan, Mexico, Poland, South Korea, Sweden, Tunisia, Venezuela).

### Three international enforcement methods

(Ch 13.2, "Impact on International Trade"):

- **Collective action** — businesses work collectively. The 2018 UN Conference on Trade and Development Secretary-General quoted on the value of "harness[ing] energy that will not fragment the [international trade] system" [V, BT].
- **Reciprocity** — countries lower import duties in exchange for mutual concessions; can be punitive. The text notes that in 2018 China "accused the United States of launching the 'largest trade war in economic history'" [V, BT, citing BBC 2018].
- **Shaming** — "deliberate attempt to negatively impact a state, regime, or governmental leader's reputation by publicizing and targeting violations of international laws" [V, BT, citing Gopalan & Fuller 2014]. Effectiveness without concrete measures is contested (Klymak 2017 on child/forced labour goods imports continuing despite shaming).

## Part XIV: Securities regulation (Ch 14)

### The 1929 crash and the 1934 Act

In 1929 "the United States stock market crashed and lost $25 billion, which would be approximately $319 billion today. The Stock Market crash of 1929 was one cause of the American Great Depression of the 1930s, which caused the failure of nearly half of American banks and created unemployment rates of almost 25 percent by 1933" [V] (Ch 14.2, "The Securities Exchange Act of 1934"). The 1934 California Law Review is quoted on the period: "Artificial prices of securities were the rule rather than the exception.… The result was vast economic power, with all that implies in a democracy, in the hand of men whose ethical standards were substantially those of gangsters" [V, BT] (Ch 14.2).

The Securities Exchange Act of 1934 "represented a broad shift in the social and economic paradigms and legal frameworks of the United States" — a rejection of **laissez-faire** economic policy as "popularized by Scottish economist Adam Smith and British philosopher Herbert Spencer" [V, BT] (Ch 14.2).

### The SEC: structure and jurisdiction

Section 4 of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 created the **SEC** as "an independent agency of the United States federal government." Joseph P. Kennedy was the first chairperson [V] (Ch 14.2, "The SEC"). Five divisions: Corporation Finance; Investment Management; Trading and Markets; Enforcement; Economic and Risk Analysis.

The SEC oversees **self-regulatory organizations (SROs)** including NYSE, NASDAQ, and the Chicago Board of Options. Per Section 12(g), "companies with total assets exceeding $10 million and with 500 or more owners of any class of securities must register with the SEC unless they meets exemption requirements" [V] (Ch 14.2). The SEC also makes rules for emerging technologies, e.g., Title III of the JOBS Act 2012 Section 4(a)(6) on **crowdfunding**, exempt from registration when not exceeding $1,070,000 in 12 months (Ch 14.2).

### Primary vs secondary markets

The Act governs **secondary markets** ("the stock market"). **Primary markets** involve initial security sales (e.g., IPO) at preset prices; secondary markets involve subsequent buyers and sellers at constantly changing valuations driven by supply, demand, and investor expectations [AP] (Ch 14.2, "Secondary Markets"). The Facebook IPO at $38/share in May 2012, with NASDAQ technical issues, is the named exemplar; Facebook later traded at >4× IPO valuation.

### Reporting requirements

Section 12 requires registration of certain securities; required information includes "the nature of the business, its financial structure, the different classes of securities, the names of officers and directors along with their salaries and bonus arrangements, and financial statements" [V] (Ch 14.2, "Reporting Requirements"). Section 15 requires brokers and dealers to register; traders are not subject. Section 15(d) requires registered companies to file periodic reports.

### Insider trading: definition and the Martha Stewart case

Insider trading per the SEC: "buying or selling a security, in breach of a fiduciary duty or other relationship of trust and confidence, on the basis of material, nonpublic information about the security" [V, BT] (Ch 14.1, "Insider Trading"). "Material nonpublic information" is "information of any kind that would impact the market price of securities that has not been disclosed to the public" [V] (Ch 14.1).

The Martha Stewart / ImClone case (2003) is the named exemplar: Stewart sold shares before a 16% drop, avoiding "a $45,673 loss." Stephen M. Cutler, SEC Director of Enforcement, is quoted: "It is fundamentally unfair for someone to have an edge on the market just because she has a stockbroker who is willing to break the rules and give her an illegal tip. It's worse still when the individual engaging in the insider trading is the Chairman and CEO of a public company" [V, BT] (Ch 14.1).

### Pre-arranged trading and safe harbour

SEC Rule 10b5-1 permits executives to transact securities under pre-arranged plans on predetermined dates and pre-set amounts; **safe harbour** is "exemption from insider trading charges for compliant pre-arranged equity trades" [V] (Ch 14.1, "Insider Trading").

### Schedule 13D and corporate insiders

The 1968 Williams Act amended the Securities Exchange Act so investors could have "advance warning of possible corporate takeovers." Becoming a **beneficial owner** of more than 5% of a company's stock requires filing a Schedule 13D within 10 days. "A beneficial owner is anyone with 'voting and investment power over their shares.'" Qualified **institutional investors** (insurance companies, state employee benefits plans, investment companies) report at year-end (Ch 14.1, "Schedule 13D"). **Corporate insiders** are officers, directors, and beneficial owners with more than 10% of a class of securities under Section 12 (Ch 14.1, "Insider Transactions").

### Four required filings

(Ch 14.1, "Reporting Requirements"):

- **Form 10-K** — annual report; **audited** financial statements (reviewed by independent CPAs against GAAP). Must disclose financial condition, market risks, internal controls, legal proceedings, defaults. Annual reports may include **management discussion** of operations and economy (Berkshire Hathaway / Buffett-Munger named).
- **Form 10-Q** — quarterly **unaudited** statements; less expensive but with less independent assurance.
- **Form 8-K** — for events such as officer changes, mergers, bankruptcy declarations; filed within four business days.
- **Proxy statements** — required to inform shareholders for stockholder meetings; sent at least 40 days before meetings (mailed or via "Notice of Internet Availability of Proxy Materials").

### Blue sky laws

In 1911, Kansas bank commissioner J.N. Dolley lobbied for the "first 'comprehensive' securities law" because investors lost money to swindles, and these investments were "backed by nothing except the blue skies of Kansas." State-level securities laws aimed at fraud are **blue sky laws**; the SEC does not enforce them [V, BT] (Ch 14.2, "Blue Sky Laws").

## Positions the author explicitly frames against

- *Forced-arbitration clauses used to silence consumers.* The text explicitly frames against using arbitration clauses "as a way to keep wrongdoings 'quiet' or to limit consumers' abilities to obtain rightful retribution" (Ch 2.3, "Ethics of Commercial Arbitration Clauses").
- *Profit-only foundations for organisations.* The text states an organisation "built on socially responsible values… will be stronger than an organization that is built on profit alone" (Ch 3.1).
- *CSR as marketing pitch.* "One should not use corporate social responsibility as a marketing pitch for a company. Consumers will quickly pick up on this tactic, and it can damage the brand" (Ch 3.2).
- *Treating decisions purely on consequentialist or deontological grounds.* The text takes the position that pure deontological ethics is impractical for dispute resolution: "In real life, it is very difficult to adopt a 100% deontological viewpoint" (Ch 2.2).
- *Pure laissez-faire.* The 1934 Securities Exchange Act is presented as "a rejection of laissez faire" and "part of a larger social shift that opposed the long hours, unsafe working conditions, and child labor" of the Industrial Revolution (Ch 14.2).
- *Compelled personal-service performance.* "Personal service…may not be used to compel specific performance, since doing so would constitute forced labor, i.e. slavery, which is in violation of the U.S. Constitution" (Ch 7.3).
- *Stereotype-based BFOQs.* The text notes BFOQs must be based on "actual qualifications, rather than stereotypes about the abilities of a certain class. For example, an employer is not expected to hire a man as a model for women's clothes," and explicitly that "there are no BFOQs for discrimination on the basis of race or color" (Ch 9.3).
