## Author's thesis (condensed)

American business law is presented as a body of rules whose purposes are establishing standards, promoting consistency, maintaining order, resolving disputes, and protecting liberties and rights. Business law itself is described as expansive, drawing on contracts, tax, corporate, IP, real estate, sales, immigration, employment, and bankruptcy law. American law derives from four sources interacting in a hierarchy: the U.S. Constitution as supreme; federal and state statutes; case law (common law from courts); and administrative law from agencies. Federal law preempts conflicting state law within the limited powers granted by the Constitution.

Three organising commitments run through the text. First, disputes can and increasingly should be resolved through tiered alternative dispute resolution (negotiation, mediation, arbitration) rather than litigation. Second, legal rules operate against an ethical and stakeholder backdrop: business ethics is "the blueprint for building a successful organization," and corporate social responsibility is measured against a triple bottom line of profits, people, and the planet. Third, the Commerce Clause and post-1937 jurisprudence (after *NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel*) is the central federal authorising mechanism, with the Bill of Rights extended to corporations through 14th Amendment incorporation.

## The American legal system and sources of law

American law follows the British common law tradition, with judges relying on case law and precedent. All states except Louisiana have enacted reception statutes for English common law, but American cases now rarely cite English materials. Four sources of law operate together: constitutional, common (judicial), legislative (statutes), and administrative (agency regulations). The Constitution is supreme; federal preemption applies only within Congress's enumerated powers. Laws are passed by legislatures; regulations are agency rules adopted under statutory authority and codified in the Code of Federal Regulations.

## Alternative dispute resolution: negotiation, mediation, arbitration

Negotiation is the simplest ADR form, defined as a process by which parties with non-identical preferences allocate resources through joint decision-making. Two structural distinctions: dyadic versus group negotiation; relational versus outcome goals. Future-of-relationship considerations drive integrative (win-win) versus distributive (zero-sum) framing. The Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument names five styles by self-interest level: forcing, collaborating, compromising, avoiding, yielding. The Federal Arbitration Act and Uniform Arbitration Act anchor US negotiation law.

Mediation uses a trained, often non-subject-matter-expert third party in a future-oriented, solution-focused process. Six steps: opening statement, party opening statements, joint discussion, private caucus, joint negotiation, closure. Three core benefits: confidentiality, creativity, control. Mediators face ethical decisions framed by consequentialist (situational) versus deontological (Kantian categorical imperative) positions; the text treats pure deontology as impractical for real disputes.

Arbitration uses a judge-like arbitrator who issues an award. Voluntary versus involuntary, binding versus non-binding axes structure the practice. Three major business applications: labour (interest and grievance arbitration), business transactions, property disputes. Forced-arbitration clauses raise stakeholder-fairness concerns and should not silence consumers. Baseball arbitration (the arbitrator picks one party's number unmodified) is presented as a structural fix for arbitrators lacking subject-matter expertise. Awards can be bare-bones or reasoned; enforcement runs through writ of execution, garnishment, or liens.

## Business ethics and corporate social responsibility

Business ethics is presented as foundational to organisational success. Four named consequences of unethical action: poor company reputation, negative employee relations, recruitment and retention problems, lost company credibility. The Michigan State University six-step ethical decision-making model: understand the issue and gather facts; separate facts from assumptions; note people, laws, and ethical guidelines; construct a solution; evaluate against ethical aspects; recommend with required actions. A code of conduct should cover legal considerations, value-based ethics, regulatory ethics, and professional behaviours, paired with a whistleblower policy that prohibits retaliation.

Corporate social responsibility (CSR) is the idea that a business is given the opportunity to make the world better through donations, volunteerism, and environmentally friendly policies. Five named CSR benefits (Kellogg research): improved investor perception, enhanced operational performance from green practices, contracting CEO compensation to CSR results, the benevolent halo effect, and consistency of efforts and partnerships. Two indirect benefits: talent attraction and consumer influence. The text takes a clear position that CSR should not be used as marketing — consumers detect inauthenticity. The triple bottom line (TBL) measures social-responsibility sustainability across profits, people, and planet.

## Constitutional law and business

Federalism divides governance between federal and state governments; the Tenth Amendment reserves un-enumerated powers to states; the supremacy clause (Article VI) makes federal law preempt conflicting state law within Congress's enumerated powers. The Commerce Clause (Article I, Section 8) is the central federal authorising mechanism. The 1937 *NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel Corp.* decision marks the inflection point from a literal interstate-only interpretation to a "close and substantial relationship to interstate commerce" test, expanding federal reach to over 700 statutes including the Civil Rights Act, ADA, Controlled Substances Act, and Federal Mine Safety and Health Act. The 1995 *United States v. Lopez* (Gun-Free School Zone Act) marks the limit. Police power is the residual state power for inhabitant welfare; the dormant Commerce Clause limits state regulation impacting interstate commerce.

The Bill of Rights extends to corporations and commercial activity (with the Fourteenth Amendment incorporating against the states). First Amendment: corporate political speech (post-*Bellotti* 1978), unprotected speech (per *Chaplinsky*), commercial speech (governed by the Central Hudson four-part test). Fourth Amendment: search-warrant requirements with the pervasive-regulation exception for long-regulated industries. Fifth Amendment: substantive due process (rational-basis test), procedural due process, takings clause (just compensation at market value), and the privilege against self-incrimination (sole proprietors only — not corporations).

## Criminal liability and white-collar crime

White-collar crimes are characterised by deceit, concealment, or violation of trust, committed by business professionals motivated by financial gain or fear of loss. Three high-profile types: Ponzi schemes (Madoff), larceny and embezzlement (Sue Sachdeva at Koss), environmental crimes (named federal statutes: Clean Air, Clean Water, RCRA, CERCLA, Endangered Species). Other types: antitrust violations, racketeering (RICO), bribery (Foreign Corrupt Practices Act), money laundering, spamming.

Two components must be proven: mens rea (the wrongful mind) and actus reus (the wrongful act). Procedural stages: information (prosecutor's pleading), arraignment, discovery and trial. Civil and criminal cases differ on five axes: initiator, burden of proof (beyond reasonable doubt vs preponderance of evidence), right to attorney (appointed in criminal only), terminology (guilt vs liability), and consequences (incarceration vs damages). Professional negligence (malpractice) requires the degree of care of a reasonable, prudent professional of the same type in the same state or community.

## The tort system

A tort is a wrong; tort law is fault-based and compensates injured parties. Two basic elements: damages and compensation. Two main types: intentional torts and negligence. Four named intentional torts: assault, malicious prosecution, defamation (slander when spoken, libel when written), invasion of privacy. Excusable circumstances include permission, defence of self/others/property.

Negligence has four elements (Kionka): a duty by the defendant to act or refrain; a breach of that duty; a causal connection; measurable harm. The reasonable-person standard considers what the actor knew, experienced, and perceived. Special relationships (e.g., nurse-patient) imply a duty of care. Foreseeability — whether harm was reasonably foreseeable — drives liability decisions.

Strict liability assigns responsibility without fault. The abnormally dangerous activity standard requires four of six factors. Trespass is a partial exception with five sub-exceptions including the attractive nuisance doctrine for child trespassers. Product liability uses res ipsa loquitor ("speaks for itself") to shift the burden once the plaintiff establishes three prerequisites (defendant control during manufacture, normal-use damage, no significant plaintiff contribution). The unreasonably dangerous product standard names three sources: manufacturing flaw, design defect, insufficient warnings or instructions. Defences: contributory negligence (with assumption of risk and misuse) blocks recovery; comparative negligence reduces damages by fault percentage.

## Contract law

A contract is an enforceable agreement between two or more parties. Five required elements: offer and acceptance, genuine agreement (a meeting of the minds, destroyable by fraud, misrepresentation, mistake, duress, or undue influence), consideration (a thing of value, need not be monetary), capacity, and legality. Promissory estoppel is an exception to the consideration requirement when one party reasonably acts on a promise; remedies are reliance damages, not expectation damages.

Three classes presumed to lack capacity: minors (with a necessities exception), the mentally incompetent (cognitive standard: did the party understand meaning and effect), and voluntarily intoxicated parties (only where inebriation severely impairs understanding). Illegal-purpose contracts are unenforceable. Material breach (substantially different from what was promised) discharges the non-breaching party's obligations; minor breach still requires performance with possible damages. Five remedies: money damages, restitution, rescission, reformation, specific performance. Specific performance cannot be used to compel personal service (which would be forced labour under the Constitution).

## Sales contracts and the UCC

A sales contract obligates one party to deliver and transfer ownership of a good for money or its equivalent (vendor and vendee). Six features: consensual, bilateral, onerous, commutative, nominate, principal. The Uniform Commercial Code (UCC, 1952) was created for state-law uniformity in sales transactions and has been adopted by all 50 states. Article 2 governs sales of goods. Three categories: goods (movable, tangible), services (movable but not tangible), realty (tangible but not movable). Four merchant classifications.

The UCC modifies general contract definiteness with reasonableness defaults: price, payment, delivery, and duration can be open with default rules. Quantity cannot be open, with two exceptions: requirements contracts and output contracts. UCC Section 2-205 firm offers (in writing, three-month default irrevocability). Mirror-image rule does not apply. Sales of $500 or more must be in writing. Three title types: good, void, voidable (with the entrustment exception). Four contract scenarios with distinct title and risk-of-loss timing: simple delivery, common-carrier delivery (shipment vs destination), goods-in-bailment (negotiable vs non-negotiable documents), conditional sales. The CISG governs international sales and preempts the UCC.

Warranties are guarantees treated as additional contracts. Express warranties (statements about quality), implied warranties (against infringement; of fitness for normal use; of merchantability). Express and implied warranties should be consistent and cumulative; otherwise express prevails except for the merchantability warranty. Three special cases: second-hand goods, buyer-designed goods, food and drink. Four buyer remedies: cancel, obtain cover, specific performance, sue.

## Employment and labour law

Until 1959 employers could fire workers for any reason; at-will employment continues today, but employees cannot be fired for illegal reasons under federal/state constitutions, statutes, or public policy. OSHA (1970) governs workplace health and safety with specific-duty and general-duty standards. Workers' compensation is the exclusive remedy for work injuries (with intentional-injury exception); claims process: file, validate, pay. The FLSA covers child labour (minimum age 14 with exceptions), minimum wage ($7.25 federal since 2009; Obama 2014 EO raised federal-contract wage to $10.10), and overtime (1.5× over 40 hours, with executive/administrative/professional/outside-sales exclusions). FMLA (1993) guarantees 12 weeks unpaid leave for five qualifying family-medical events at 50+ employee firms with one-year/25-hour eligibility. COBRA, ERISA (vesting), and unemployment compensation cover ending employment. IRCA imposes documentation requirements with penalties for knowingly hiring undocumented workers.

Trade unions (~60 unions, 14 million workers) lobby employers about working conditions. Labour-law timeline: 1886 AFL; 1932 Norris-LaGuardia (banned yellow-dog contracts and federal injunctions); 1935 Wagner Act / NLRA (right to organise and bargain); 1947 Taft-Hartley (six restrictive amendments); 1959 Landrum-Griffin (internal union affairs); 1988 WARN (60-day layoff notice). NLRB has three functions: monitor elections, prevent unfair labour practices, establish rules. Three election types: consent, contest, decertification. Three union security agreement types: closed shop, union shop, agency shop. Strikes require a 60-day cooling off period; four illegal strike types (violent, sit-down, wildcat, intermittent). Picketing is lawful unless it involves violence or blocks customers, non-strikers, or deliveries. Secondary boycott picketing is legal for product picketing only.

Title VII of the Civil Rights Act bans hiring/firing/promotion discrimination by sex, national origin, colour, religion, race for employers with 15+ employees for 20 consecutive weeks/year. Two proof routes: disparate treatment (three-step burden shifting) and disparate impact (statistical demonstration; *Griggs v. Duke Power* exemplar). Two sexual harassment types: quid pro quo and hostile work environment. The Pregnancy Discrimination Act (1987) extended sex discrimination to pregnancy and childbirth. Three Title VII defences: BFOQ (no race or colour BFOQs), merit, seniority. The Equal Pay Act, ADA, and ADEA (40+ at 20+ employee firms) extend protection.

## Administrative law and regulatory agencies

Administrative law (also regulatory and public law) governs federal agencies created by act of Congress through organic statutes. Industrialisation drove agency expansion (FDA 1906, FTC, Federal Reserve System). Delegation assigns authority. Most agencies sit in the executive branch with presidentially appointed leaders. Administrative law judges (ALJs) oversee procedural aspects and decide whether agency rules were broken; jurisdiction is limited. The FTC is structured into three bureaus: consumer protection, competition, and economics. The Administrative Procedure Act (APA, 1946) imposes due-process, civil-liberties, and public-comment constraints, organising functions into rulemaking and adjudication.

## Antitrust law

Late-1800s industrial consolidation produced business trusts that fixed prices and drove competitors out, prompting federal regulation. The rule of reason distinguished naked restraint (long-term, unbounded) from ancillary restraint (limited in time and geography); courts disapprove of the former more consistently. Three federal antitrust statutes:

- Sherman Antitrust Act (1890): criminal statute against unreasonable restraints; penalties up to $100M corporations / $1M individuals / 10 years.
- Clayton Act (1914): civil statute against four practices — price discrimination, exclusive dealing contracts, corporate mergers (creating monopoly or substantially lessening competition), interlocking directorates. 1976 amendment: pre-merger notification and triple damages.
- Federal Trade Commission Act (1914): unfair methods of competition; created the FTC; remedies include divestiture and dissolution.

Four exemptions: labour (unions alone), agriculture and fisheries co-ops, foreign-trade cooperative export, cooperative research.

## Unfair trade practices and the FTC

FTC Act Section 5(a) prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce." Unfair: causes consumer injury, unavoidable, benefits do not outweigh deception. Deceptive: substantial misrepresentation. Six categories: product guarantees and false endorsements, unfair advertising (including bait and switch), taking advantage of customers (vulnerable populations), misrepresenting a product (technical-term restrictions: "new" means under six months; "wool" means complete composition), giving misleading price information, failing to disclose pertinent information. Plaintiffs do not need to prove intent.

The FTC was created in 1914 to address monopolies and trusts; the 1938 Wheeler-Lea Act gave it false-advertising jurisdiction over foods, drugs, medical devices, and cosmetics. Three bureaus: consumer protection (advertising, privacy, financial products, identity protection, Do Not Call Registry); competition (antitrust shared with DOJ); economics (subject-matter expertise). Four enforcement options: voluntary compliance through consent order, administrative complaints, federal litigation, and trade rules for systemic issues.

## International law

International law governs nation-state relationships without a single legislative source, world court, or executive enforcement body. Two US Constitution clauses: Foreign Commerce Clause (Congress regulates foreign commerce; states cannot unduly burden it) and Treaty Clause (presidential treaty-making with Senate advice and consent; states cannot enter treaties; treaties become US law preempting conflicts). Three primary sources: international customs (customary international law, CIL — not written, not requiring ratification, but binding); international treaties (bilateral and multilateral); international organisations (US in 120+).

The UN was created as a multilateral treaty in 1945, with goals of peace, economic and social cooperation, and human rights protection. The General Assembly includes representatives from each member nation; 195 sovereign states are acknowledged. The Security Council has five permanent members (China, France, Russia, UK, US) and 10 selected. The World Bank funds developing-country projects. The UN Commission on International Trade Law established the CISG (1980), which 84 countries had ratified by January 2018.

The EU is a regional organisation of 28 (UK withdrawing) representing 500+ million people and a GCP exceeding US-Canada-Mexico combined.

Three principles: Principle of Comity (deference to foreign laws when consistent), Act of State Doctrine (England and US do not judge public acts within other governments' territories), Doctrine of Sovereign Immunity (qualified after 1952; Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976 allows US jurisdiction with waiver or US-direct-effect commercial activity).

Common law systems (US, UK, India, Canada, Australia, etc. — about one-third of the world's population) develop their own rules; civil law systems (France, Germany, China, Japan, etc.) base legislation on Roman civil law and statutory codes. Three international enforcement methods: collective action, reciprocity, shaming (mixed effectiveness).

## Securities regulation

The 1929 stock market crash ($25 billion / ~$319 billion today; one cause of the Great Depression) prompted the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, representing a rejection of laissez-faire economic policy. Section 4 created the SEC (independent agency, five divisions, first chair Joseph P. Kennedy). The SEC oversees self-regulatory organisations (NYSE, NASDAQ, Chicago Board of Options). Companies with $10M+ assets and 500+ owners must register. The Act governs secondary markets (the stock market with constantly changing valuations) as opposed to primary markets (initial sale through IPO at preset prices). Section 12 requires registration of certain securities; Section 15 requires broker/dealer registration; Section 15(d) requires periodic reports.

Insider trading (per the SEC) is buying or selling securities in breach of fiduciary duty on the basis of material nonpublic information. The Martha Stewart / ImClone case (2003) is the named exemplar. SEC Rule 10b5-1 provides safe harbour for pre-arranged trading plans. The Williams Act (1968) requires Schedule 13D filing within 10 days of becoming a 5% beneficial owner. Corporate insiders own more than 10% of a class of securities and must file statements of ownership.

Four required filings: Form 10-K (annual, audited), Form 10-Q (quarterly, unaudited), Form 8-K (event-driven, four business days), proxy statements (40+ days before stockholder meeting). Blue sky laws are state-level securities laws aimed at fraud (named for J.N. Dolley's 1911 Kansas lobbying); the SEC does not enforce them.

## Signature contrarian positions

1. **Profit-only foundations are weaker than ethics-grounded ones.** "An organization is built on socially responsible values, it will be stronger than an organization that is built on profit alone."
2. **CSR should not be marketing.** "One should not use corporate social responsibility as a marketing pitch for a company. Consumers will quickly pick up on this tactic, and it can damage the brand."
3. **Forced-arbitration clauses should not be used to silence consumers.** Specifically not "as a way to keep wrongdoings 'quiet' or to limit consumers' abilities to obtain rightful retribution."
4. **Pure deontological ethics is impractical for real disputes.** "In real life, it is very difficult to adopt a 100% deontological viewpoint for dispute resolution."
5. **Personal-service specific performance would be unconstitutional forced labour.** Specific performance "may not be used to compel… personal service, since doing so would constitute forced labor, i.e. slavery, which is in violation of the U.S. Constitution."
6. **No BFOQs based on race or colour.** Stereotype-based BFOQs are explicitly excluded; race and colour BFOQs do not exist.
7. **Pure laissez-faire was rejected after 1929.** The 1934 Securities Exchange Act represents "a rejection of laissez faire" tied to the broader social shift against industrial-revolution working conditions.
8. **Win-lose negotiation undermines long-term success.** Forcing-style negotiation "often undermines the party's long-term success" through reputational damage and lost referrals.
