## Author's thesis (paraphrased from source)

Organisational Behavior is presented as an applied behavioural science whose object is "the behavior of people in organizations" examined at three levels — the individual, the group, and the organisation as a whole [AP] (Ch 1, "What Is Organizational Behavior?"). The book's organising premise is that effective management requires both technical mastery and "people skills" — motivation, leadership, decision-making, communication, organising work, dealing with politics, and developing employees and the organisation (Ch 1, "Management and Organizational Behavior"). The text frames management as "the process of planning, organizing, directing, and controlling the activities of employees in combination with other resources to accomplish organizational objectives" [V] (Ch 1, "What Is Management?"), and argues that this work draws on psychology, sociology, anthropology, economics, and political science but is distinguished by its focus on the *organisation* rather than the discipline (Ch 1, "What Is Organizational Behavior?").

A second load-bearing claim runs through the volume: that *individual differences*, perceptual processes, and learning histories systematically shape work behaviour, and that managers who treat employees as interchangeable units misread their organisations [AR] (Ch 2-4). Personality traits, cognitive complexity, locus of control, perceptual selectivity, attribution biases, and reinforcement schedules each generate distinct managerial implications.

A third load-bearing claim is that *organisations are open systems* that must align internal structure, culture, and processes with external environmental demands; structural form follows environmental complexity, and cultures that fit the environment outperform those that do not [AR] (Ch 15-16). The text repeatedly returns to the mechanistic-versus-organic distinction as the central design choice (Ch 15.3, "Organizational Designs and Structures"; Ch 16.1, "Organizational Structures and Design").

A fourth load-bearing claim is that motivation, performance, and reward must be linked through *expectancy* and *equity* mechanisms — employees exert effort when they perceive that effort produces performance, performance produces valued outcomes, and outcomes are distributed fairly relative to peers [AR] (Ch 7.3, "Process Theories of Motivation"; Ch 8, "Performance Appraisal and Rewards").

## Part I: The nature of work, management, and organisational behaviour (Ch 1)

### Work as an activity that produces value for others

The book opens with a definition of work as "an activity that produces something of value for other people," broadening the wage-effort exchange to a social context [V] (Ch 1.1, "The Nature of Work"). Work serves four functions for individuals: economic (income), social (workplace relationships and friendships), status (occupation as a clue to community standing), and identity / self-esteem / self-actualisation; the absence of these produces *alienation*, characterised by powerlessness, meaninglessness, and normlessness [V] (Ch 1.1, "Functions of Work"). The text quotes Freud approvingly: "Work has a greater effect than any other technique of living in binding the individual more closely to reality" (Ch 1.1, "Functions of Work").

### Five contemporary challenges for managers

The text frames the workplace as in continuous change, identifying five challenges: international competition, new technologies, increased quality (TQM), employee motivation and commitment, managing a diverse workforce, and ethical behaviour [AP] (Ch 1.2, "The Changing Workplace"). The competitive-strength argument is grounded in a Global Competitive Index ranking the United States second (5.9) behind Switzerland (Ch 1.2, Table 1.1).

### Managerial responsibilities, levels, and required skill mixes

Mary Parker Follett's definition of management as "the art of getting things done through people" anchors the modern definition (Ch 1.3, "What Is Management?"). Managers' responsibilities sort into nine activities: long-range planning, controlling, environmental scanning, supervision, coordinating, customer relations and marketing, community relations, internal consulting, and monitoring products and services [AP] (Ch 1.3, "Managerial Responsibilities").

Management hierarchy has three levels — *executive*, *middle*, *first-line* — and three skill types: technical, human relations, and conceptual. As one rises in the hierarchy, conceptual skill demand increases and technical skill demand decreases; human-relations skill is required at every level [AP] (Ch 1.3, "Variations in Managerial Work"). The 21st-century manager is predicted to need the ability to think of new solutions, comfort with chaos, technological understanding, high emotional intelligence, and the ability to integrate people and technology (Ch 1.3, "The Twenty-First Century Manager").

### A model of organisational behaviour

The text proposes a five-block model: individuals and groups; tasks and technology; organisation design; organisational processes (leadership, communication, decision-making, power, politics); and management as the glue that holds them together [AP] (Ch 1.4, "Building Blocks of Organizations"). It distinguishes *micro*-OB (individual and group behaviour) from *macro*-OB or organisation theory (organisation-wide design and environment relations) and argues against keeping the two separate (Ch 1.4, "What Is Organizational Behavior?").

The book quotes Nadler and Tushman to justify the difficulty of the field: "Understanding one individual's behavior is challenging in and of itself; understanding a group that's made up of different individuals and comprehending the many relationships among those individuals is even more complex. Imagine, then, the mind-boggling complexity of a large organization made up of thousands of individuals and hundreds of groups with myriad relationships among these individuals and groups." [V] (Ch 1.4, "A Model of Organizational Behavior and Management").

## Part II: Individual differences, personality, and culture (Ch 2)

### Mental and physical abilities

Performance is largely a function of ability and motivation, drawing on an early model the text cites approvingly [BT] (Ch 2.1, "Individual and Cultural Factors in Employee Performance"). *Cognitive complexity* — the capacity to acquire, sort, and integrate environmental information — is a central managerial trait. High-cognitive-complexity managers are better at handling complex situations, are more consultative, seek out and process more information, integrate discrepant information, consider more solutions, and use more complex decision strategies [AP] (Ch 2.2, "Mental Abilities").

### Six personality traits relevant to work behaviour

The text identifies six work-relevant traits: self-esteem, locus of control, introversion-extroversion, authoritarianism, dogmatism, and dependability [AP] (Ch 2.4, "Personality and Work Behavior"). Internals (those with internal locus of control) "exhibit greater work motivation, have stronger expectations that effort will lead to actual high job performance, perform better on tasks requiring learning or problem-solving, typically receive higher salaries and salary increases, and exhibit less job-related anxiety than externals" [V] (Ch 2.4, "Locus of Control"). Authoritarians perform better under rigid supervision; non-authoritarians perform better under participative supervision (Ch 2.4, "Authoritarianism and Dogmatism"). Dogmatic managers "tend to make decisions quickly, based on only limited information and with a high degree of confidence in the correctness of their decisions" [V] (Ch 2.4, "Dogmatism").

### Argyris's basic incongruity thesis

Chris Argyris's thesis is that *traditional work organisations conflict with the developmental needs of healthy adults* [BT] (Ch 2.5, "Personality and Organization: A Basic Conflict?"). As people mature they become more active, independent, varied, deeply interested, longer-perspectived, and more self-aware; organisations in pursuit of efficiency assign minimal control, expect passivity and dependence, demand short-term horizons, and impose repetitive tasks. The conflict is strongest among mature employees in highly structured organisations doing fragmented mechanised work — that is, at lower hierarchical levels (Ch 2.5).

### Values, ethics, and the work ethic

A *value* is "an enduring belief that a specific mode of conduct or end-state of existence is personally or socially preferable to an opposite or converse mode of conduct or end-state of existence" [V] (Ch 2.6, citing Rokeach). Rokeach's instrumental versus terminal values distinction is presented as a useful classification for managers (Ch 2.6, "Characteristics of Values"). Average annual hours worked are presented as a comparative measure of work ethic: South Korea 2,070, US 1,789, Japan 1,742, UK 1,676, France 1,472, Germany 1,288 [V] (Ch 2.6, Table 2.3, OECD data, accessed 2018).

### Cultural dimensions and Hofstede framing

The text adopts Hofstede's definition of culture as "the collective programming of the mind which distinguishes the members of one human group from another" [V] (Ch 2.7, "What Is Culture?"). Kluckhohn and Strodtbeck's six dimensions for distinguishing cultures are reproduced: views of humanity, of nature, of interpersonal relationships, of activity and achievement, of time, and of space (Ch 2.7, "Dimensions of Culture"). The text observes that "the Hispanic culture within the United States places a high value on groups compared to individuals and as a consequence takes a more collective approach to decision-making" [V] (Ch 2.7).

## Part III: Perception, attribution, and attitudes (Ch 3)

### Perceptual selectivity and social perception

*Perception* is "the process by which one screens, selects, organizes, and interprets stimuli to give them meaning" [V] (Ch 3.1, "The Perceptual Process"). It produces "a partial, personal construction in which certain objects, selected by the individual for a major role, are perceived in an individual manner" — every perceiver "is, as it were, to some degree a nonrepresentational artist" [V] (Ch 3.1, citing Kretch and associates).

The Dearborn and Simon study is treated as a classic illustration: executives from different departments reading the same case identified different problems as primary based on their functional background — sales executives saw sales problems, production executives saw production problems, and so on [BT] (Ch 3.1, "Organizational Role"). Likert's data on supervisor-subordinate perceptual gaps are cited: supervisors reported giving "sincere and thorough praise" 80 per cent of the time; subordinates reported receiving it 14 per cent of the time [V] (Ch 3.1, Table 3.1).

### Three barriers to accurate social perception

The text identifies *stereotyping*, *selective perception*, and *perceptual defense* as the three primary barriers (Ch 3.2). Perceptual defence operates by raising the recognition threshold for emotionally disturbing stimuli, eliciting substitute perceptions, and arousing emotional reactions even when stimuli are not consciously recognised (Ch 3.2, "Perceptual Defense"). Four mechanisms of perceptual defence are documented from a Haire and Grunes study of student responses to factory workers being described as "intelligent": denial, modification and distortion, change in perception, and recognition but refusal to change [BT] (Ch 3.2, "Perceptual Defense").

### Attribution theory and bias

Attribution theory (Heider, Kelley) explains how individuals interpret events as caused by stable internal or external factors [BT] (Ch 3.3). Kelley's three factors — consensus, consistency, distinctiveness — combine to produce internal or external attribution: low consensus, high consistency, low distinctiveness yields internal attribution (skill); high consensus, high consistency, high distinctiveness yields external attribution (luck or task ease) (Ch 3.3, "Internal and External Causes of Behavior").

Two attribution biases are central to the OB analysis. The *fundamental attribution error* is "a tendency to underestimate the effects of external or situational causes of behavior and to overestimate the effects of internal or personal causes" [V] (Ch 3.3, "Attributional Bias"). The *self-serving bias* is the tendency to attribute success to one's own actions and failure to others (Ch 3.3, "Attributional Bias").

### Attitudes, formation, and cognitive consistency

An *attitude* is "a predisposition to respond in a favorable or unfavorable way to objects or persons in one's environment" with three components: cognitive (beliefs), affective (feelings), and intentional (behavioural intent) [V] (Ch 3.4, "Attitudes and Behavior"). The book presents two competing accounts of attitude formation: Staw's *dispositional approach* (attitudes are stable predispositions, near-personality-traits) and the *situational approach* (attitudes emerge from situational experience), with the *social-information-processing approach* of Pfeffer and Salancik as a sophisticated variant (Ch 3.4, "How Are Attitudes Formed?"). Research supports both, with the implication that "systematic efforts focusing on groups and interconnected social systems are likely required for successful changes in attitudes" [AP] (Ch 3.4).

*Cognitive dissonance* (Festinger, presented without naming him explicitly) drives behavioural justification: when people act inconsistently with their attitudes, they experience tension and either change behaviour or change the attitude (Ch 3.4, "Behavioral Justification").

### Job satisfaction and organisational commitment

*Job satisfaction* is "a pleasurable or positive emotional state resulting from the appraisal of one's job or job experience" and is best understood in terms of discrepancy between expectations and outcomes [V] (Ch 3.5, "Job Satisfaction"). Five dimensions structure the rating literature: work itself, pay, promotional opportunities, supervision, and coworkers (Ch 3.5, "Dimensions of Job Satisfaction"). The Minnesota Satisfaction Questionnaire (MSQ) is presented as the dominant instrument (Ch 3.5, "Measurement of Job Satisfaction").

*Organisational commitment* is characterised by "a strong belief in and acceptance of the organization's goals and values, a willingness to exert considerable effort on behalf of the organization, and a strong desire to maintain membership in the organization" [V] (Ch 3.5, "Job Involvement and Organizational Commitment").

## Part IV: Learning and reinforcement (Ch 4)

### Three models of learning

The text presents classical conditioning (Pavlov), operant conditioning (Skinner via Watson and Thorndike's law of effect), and Bandura's social learning theory as a developmental sequence [AP] (Ch 4.1). Thorndike's law of effect: "of several responses made to the same situation, those that are accompanied or closely followed by satisfaction (reinforcement) will be more likely to occur; those that are accompanied or closely followed by discomfort (punishment) will be less likely to occur" [V] (Ch 4.1, "Operant Conditioning").

Bandura's social learning theory adds *reciprocal determinism* (people control their environment as much as it controls them) and *vicarious learning* (learning by observing others' actions and consequences). Its four central elements are attention, retention, reproduction, and incentives [AP] (Ch 4.1, "Social Learning Theory").

### Strategies for behavioural change

Four reinforcement strategies are catalogued (Ch 4.2, "Strategies for Behavioral Change"):

- **Positive reinforcement**: presenting an attractive outcome following desired behaviour, contingent on the behaviour, with sufficient frequency.
- **Avoidance learning** (negative reinforcement): the desired behaviour allows the employee to avoid an unpleasant condition.
- **Extinction**: undesired behaviour declines as a result of a lack of positive reinforcement.
- **Punishment**: the administration of unpleasant consequences for undesired behaviour.

The text takes a clear position: "positive reinforcement combined with extinction is the most suitable way to bring about desired behavior" because punishment alienates and avoidance learning emphasises the negative [AR] (Ch 4.2). Punishment works best "when it is administered in an impersonal way and as soon as possible after the transgression" and when it "fits the crime" in severity, is given in private, and is explained (Ch 4.2, "Punishment").

### Reinforcement schedules

Four partial schedules are documented (Ch 4.2, "Schedules of Reinforcement", Table 4.2):

| Schedule | Performance impact when applied | Effect when terminated | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fixed interval | Average and irregular | Quick extinction | Weekly paycheque |
| Fixed ratio | High and stable | Quick extinction | Piece-rate pay |
| Variable interval | Moderately high and stable | Slow extinction | Random monthly review and reward |
| Variable ratio | Very high | Slow extinction | Sales bonus tied to selling X accounts where X varies around a mean |

The text concludes that "the performance-contingent (or ratio) reward schedules generally lead to better performance than the time-contingent (or interval) schedules, regardless of whether such schedules are fixed or variable" [V] (Ch 4.2, paraphrasing Hamner).

### Behaviour modification and behavioural self-management

Behaviour modification programmes follow a five-phase structure: establishing clear behavioural criteria, conducting a performance audit, setting specific behavioural goals, evaluating results, and administering feedback and rewards [AP] (Ch 4.3, "Designing a Behavior Modification Program"). *Shaping* — improving performance incrementally rather than waiting for ideal performance — is central (Ch 4.3, "Administering Feedback and Rewards").

Behavioural self-management (BSM) builds on Bandura's social learning theory, applying self-regulation through three stages — self-monitoring, self-evaluation, self-reinforcement — and four interactive factors: situational cues, the person, behaviours, and consequences [AP] (Ch 4.4, drawing on Kanfer's model). Cognitive supports include symbolic coding, rehearsal, and self-talk (Ch 4.4, "Cognitive Supports"). A field experiment among unionised state government workers reduced absenteeism through self-management training in problem identification, cause analysis, coping strategies, and self-administered reinforcement (Ch 4.4, "Reducing Absenteeism through Self-Management").

## Part V: Diversity in organisations (Ch 5)

### Three kinds of workplace diversity

The text differentiates *surface-level diversity* (visible: age, body size, visible disabilities, race, sex), *deep-level diversity* (non-observable: attitudes, values, beliefs), and *hidden diversity* (deep-level traits that may be concealed or revealed at the individual's discretion, called *invisible social identities*) [V] (Ch 5.1, "An Introduction to Workplace Diversity").

The text projects that by 2024 white non-Hispanic participation in the labor force will decline by 3 per cent while Black participation will rise 10.1 per cent, Hispanic/Latino 28 per cent, Asian 23.2 per cent, and other groups 22.2 per cent [V] (Ch 5.2, "Race"). It catalogues specific discrimination patterns by group — discrimination against Black employees, Hispanic identity-as-whiteness dynamics, the *model minority myth* affecting Asian Americans, multiracial identity dynamics, age stereotypes, sexual-orientation discrimination, and the choice between *passing* and *revealing* for LGBTQ employees (Ch 5.2).

### The Cox-Blake business case for diversity

Taylor Cox and Stacy Blake's framework identifies six potential competitive advantages from valuing diversity: cost advantages (reduced lawsuits, lower turnover), resource acquisition (larger labour pool), marketing (broader perspectives on consumer preferences), system flexibility (cognitive flexibility transfers to organisational adaptability), and creativity / problem-solving (multiple perspectives reduce groupthink) [BT] (Ch 5.3, "Reaping the Advantages of Diversity"). The *resource-based view* grounds the argument: diverse workforces are rare, valuable, hard to imitate, and non-substitutable, generating sustained competitive advantage [BT] (Ch 5.3).

### Theories explaining diversity outcomes

The chapter presents competing theoretical accounts (Ch 5.5, "Key Diversity Theories"):

- *Cognitive diversity hypothesis*: cultural differences yield creative problem-solving and innovation (positive view).
- *Similarity-attraction paradigm*: people prefer similar others, predicting negative diversity outcomes.
- *Social identity theory*: in-group / out-group categorisation produces in-group favouritism.
- *Schema theory*: demographic-based encoding shapes evaluation of newly encountered people.
- *Justification-suppression model*: people experience prejudice in two stages — feeling it, then either suppressing or acting on it depending on conditions (anonymity, fatigue, weak social norms).

The Watson study showed homogeneous groups outperforming heterogeneous groups initially, but heterogeneous groups outperforming on range of perspectives and number of alternatives by week 17 [BT] (Ch 5.5, "Cognitive Diversity Hypothesis").

### Three perspectives on workplace diversity (Ely and Thomas)

The Ely-Thomas paradigm names three orientations to managing diversity (Ch 5.6, "Three Perspectives on Workplace Diversity") [BT]:

- *Integration-and-learning perspective*: treats cultural identity as a resource for shared work, the strongest fit with firm success.
- *Access-and-legitimacy perspective*: instrumentalises diversity for market access; risks limiting minority members to demographic-matched roles.
- *Discrimination-and-fairness perspective*: treats diversity as moral duty without linking to productivity; can implicitly demand assimilation.

### Recommendations for managing diversity

Highly structured interviews with 15 specific characteristics (job analysis, same questions, limited prompting, longer interviews, multiple rating scales, training, etc.) reduce similarity bias [V, list verbatim from source] (Ch 5.7, "Interview Selection Process"). *Diversified mentoring relationships* — relationships in which mentor and mentee differ in status — produce mutual benefits in knowledge, empathy, and skills (Ch 5.7).

## Part VI: Perception and managerial decision-making (Ch 6)

### Two-system processing of decisions

The brain processes decisions through two systems: the *reflective system* (logical, analytical, deliberate, methodical, prefrontal cortex) and the *reactive system* (quick, impulsive, intuitive, basal ganglia and amygdala) [BT, citing neuropsychology research] (Ch 6.2, "How the Brain Processes Information to Make Decisions"). The text argues that "the brain can only use one system at a time for processing information" [V, citing Darlow and Sloman] (Ch 6.2). Strong emotions pull decision-making toward the reactive route; novel and complex situations call for the reflective route.

*Emotional intelligence* — the ability to recognise, understand, and manage one's own emotions and others' — supports the toggle between systems and contributes to effective decision-making (Ch 6.2, "The Role of Emotions").

### Programmed versus non-programmed decisions

*Programmed decisions* are repeated and rule-amenable (e.g., scheduling, ordering raw materials); they support *heuristics* (mental shortcuts) that save time without guaranteeing optimal solutions. *Non-programmed decisions* are novel, ambiguous, high-involvement; they require systematic processing through the reflective system [V] (Ch 6.3, "Programmed and Nonprogrammed Decisions"). The six-step decision process — recognise, generate alternatives, analyse, select, implement, evaluate — applies to non-programmed decisions (Ch 6.3, "The Decision-Making Process").

### Six barriers to effective decision-making

The chapter catalogues six barriers (Ch 6.4):

1. *Bounded rationality*: cognitive limits prevent fully rational treatment of complex issues.
2. *Escalation of commitment*: decision-makers stay committed to poor decisions even as costs mount.
3. *Time constraints*: pressure pushes decision-makers toward heuristics.
4. *Uncertainty*: outcomes are unknown until alternatives are chosen.
5. *Personal biases*: the "similar to me" preference, *confirmation bias* (paying attention to information that confirms existing beliefs).
6. *Conflict*: decision-makers avoid conflict even when avoidance is harmful.

Conflict has two forms in this analysis: *process conflict* (about the best way to do something) "can actually lead to improved performance" [V], while *relationship conflict* is generally harmful and pushes participants into the reactive system (Ch 6.4, "Conflict").

### Improving decision quality

The chapter recommends *evidence-based decision-making* — systematically collecting the best available evidence rather than relying on intuition (Ch 6.5). *Critical thinking* requires recognising logical fallacies; the text catalogues seven (non sequitur, false cause, ad hominem, genetic fallacy, appeal to tradition, bandwagon approach, appeal to emotion) (Ch 6.5, Table 6.2).

Rest's four-component ethical decision-making model is presented [BT]: moral sensitivity, moral judgment, moral motivation/intention, moral character/action (Ch 6.5, "Are there ethical implications?"). "A failure at any point in the chain can lead to unethical actions" [V] (Ch 6.5).

### Group decision-making, groupthink, and devil's advocate

Group decisions reduce bias and produce more options when groups are diverse and members feel free to speak [AP] (Ch 6.6, "Advantages of Group Decisions"). *Groupthink* "occurs when group members choose not to voice their concerns or objections because they would rather keep the peace and not annoy or antagonize others" [V] (Ch 6.6, "Disadvantages of Group Decisions"). *Suppression of dissent* by powerful members produces the same effect.

Counter-measures: diverse membership, encouraging everyone to speak, assigning a *devil's advocate* to systematically critique proposed solutions, and finding common ground when conflict becomes excessive (Ch 6.6, "How to Form a Quality Group").

## Part VII: Motivation theories (Ch 7)

### Direction and intensity

*Motivation* is defined as "a force within or outside of the body that energizes, directs, and sustains human behavior" [V] (Ch 7.1, "Motivation: Direction and Intensity"), with two components: *direction* (what one wants to achieve) and *intensity* (effort exerted). Performance is determined by ability, motivation, role perceptions, and the performance environment; deficiencies in any one cannot be compensated by strengthening another [AR] (Ch 7.1).

### Content theories

Five content theories are presented [BT] (Ch 7.2):

- **Manifest needs theory** (Murray): humans have many needs but only a few are *manifest* at a given time; *latent* needs may exist without being expressed in current behaviour.
- **Learned needs theory** (McClelland): three needs — achievement (nAch), affiliation (nAff), power (nPow) — are learned, especially in childhood. High-nAch individuals "feel personally responsible for completing whatever tasks they are assigned," prefer "moderate" probability-of-success situations, and have "very strong desires for feedback about how well they are doing" (Ch 7.2). McClelland argues that "the high need for social power is the most important motivator for successful managers" [AP] (Ch 7.2, "Need for Power").
- **Maslow's hierarchy of needs**: physiological, safety/security, social, ego/esteem, self-actualisation, with prepotency (lower needs satisfied before higher ones become motivating). The text notes that research does not support the strict hierarchical sequence [AP] (Ch 7.2, "Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs").
- **Alderfer's ERG theory**: existence, relatedness, growth, with frustration-regression (when growth needs are blocked, attention shifts back to relatedness) [BT] (Ch 7.2, "Alderfer's ERG Theory").
- **Herzberg's motivator-hygiene theory**: hygiene factors (pay, conditions, supervision) prevent dissatisfaction but do not motivate; motivators (achievement, recognition, work itself, growth) drive satisfaction. Satisfaction and dissatisfaction are on different scales [AR] (Ch 7.2, "Herzberg's Motivator-Hygiene Theory").

### Self-determination theory

Self-determination theory (SDT) distinguishes *intrinsic motivation* (performing an activity for its inherent satisfaction) from *extrinsic motivation* (performing for a valued outcome). The signature SDT claim — "as the level of extrinsic rewards increases, the amount of intrinsic motivation decreases" [V] — has direct managerial implications (Ch 7.2, "Self-Determination Theory"). Tasks are intrinsically motivating when they satisfy needs for competence, autonomy, and relatedness.

### Process theories

Four process theories are presented (Ch 7.3):

- **Operant conditioning theory**: as covered in Ch 4 — reward-contingent behaviour, with positive reinforcement, negative reinforcement, avoidance learning, nonreinforcement, and punishment.
- **Equity theory** (Adams): motivation depends on the comparison of one's outcome-input ratio against a *referent other*. Underreward inequity produces dissatisfaction and a range of restorative behaviours: alter own inputs or outcomes, alter referent's inputs or outcomes, distort perceptions, change referent, or leave the situation [BT] (Ch 7.3, "Reducing Underreward Inequity"). Two human tendencies amplify perceived inequity: people overrate their own performance, and people overrate others' outcomes (Ch 7.3, "The Basic Equity Model").
- **Goal theory** (Locke): difficult, specific, accepted goals produce better performance than easy or vague goals. *Goal commitment* is the degree of dedication to achieving a goal; participation in goal setting tends to increase commitment [BT] (Ch 7.3, "Goal Theory"). The text catalogues hazards: goal conflict, narrow goals neglecting other duties, unethical use to set impossible goals, and over-emphasis on quantifiable measures.
- **Expectancy theory** (Vroom, Lawler): motivation = E1 (effort-performance expectancy) × E2 (performance-outcome expectancy) × valence. The integrative claim: "to maximize motivation, organizations must make outcomes contingent on performance" [V] (Ch 7.3, "Implications of Expectancy Theory"). *Self-efficacy* (Bandura) influences E1 strongly — "high self-efficacy employees believe that they are likely to succeed at most or all of their job duties" [AP] (Ch 7.3).

The text notes that recent research demonstrates the components of expectancy theory predict performance well "without being multiplied" — refining but not overturning the model (Ch 7.4, "Expectancy Theory").

### Equity theory's organisational-justice extension

Recent equity research has expanded into *organisational justice*: distributive justice (fairness of outcomes) and procedural justice (fairness of the process used to allocate them). Layoffs based on seniority are perceived as more procedurally just than layoffs based on supervisors' opinions [AP] (Ch 7.4, "Equity Theory").

## Part VIII: Performance appraisal and rewards (Ch 8)

### Uses and errors of performance appraisal

Performance appraisals serve five organisational functions: feedback, self-development, reward determination, personnel decisions (promotion, transfer, termination), and training and development needs identification [AP] (Ch 8.1, "Uses of Performance Appraisals"). Five rater errors recur: *central tendency* (lumping everyone in the middle), *strictness or leniency* (rating systematically low or high), *halo effect* (one global rating instead of trait-by-trait), *recency error* (over-weighting recent behaviour), and *personal biases* including racial, gender, similarity, and confirmatory bias [AP] (Ch 8.1, "Problems with Performance Appraisals").

### Six appraisal techniques

Six techniques are catalogued and compared (Ch 8.2):

- **Graphic rating scales**: most common (used by 57-65 per cent of organisations); susceptible to central tendency, strictness, leniency.
- **Critical incident technique**: supervisors record specific behaviour examples; useful qualitatively but lacks quantitative output.
- **Behaviourally anchored rating scales (BARS)**: behavioural anchors per scale point; reduces error sources but requires considerable design effort.
- **Behavioural observation scales (BOS)**: rating frequency of specific behaviours.
- **Management by objectives (MBO)**: jointly set goals, evaluated against actual performance.
- **Assessment centres**: multi-day standardised evaluations used for managerial potential.

The comparison table (Ch 8.2, Table 8.6) makes the cost-quality trade-off visible: rating scales are low-cost and low-acceptability; BARS, BOS, MBO and assessment centres are higher-cost and higher-acceptability.

### Reward systems and pay-for-performance

Reward systems influence five outcomes: job effort and performance, attendance and retention, organisational commitment, job satisfaction, and occupational and organisational choice [AP] (Ch 8.4, "Functions of Reward Systems"). Lawler's four conclusions about rewards and satisfaction are reproduced:

> "(1) satisfaction with a reward is a function of both how much is received and how much the individual feels should have been received; (2) satisfaction is influenced by comparisons with what happens to others, especially one's coworkers; (3) people differ with respect to the rewards they value; and (4) some rewards are satisfying because they lead to other rewards." [V] (Ch 8.4, "Functions of Reward Systems")

Lawler's four conditions for pay to be a strong motivator: trust between managers and subordinates, accurate measurement of individual performance, substantial pay differentials between high and low performers, and few negative consequences for good performance [V, paraphrased list] (Ch 8.4, "Money and Motivation: A Closer Look").

The text takes a position on pay secrecy: "pay secrecy may have several negative side effects" because people overestimate others' pay, lose the motivational potential of differential rewards, and lose feedback on performance (Ch 8.4, "Pay Secrecy"). The Lawler study found that under pay secrecy, "almost regardless of how well the individual manager was performing, they felt they were getting less than the average raise" [V] (Ch 8.4).

### Creative pay practices

Five creative pay practices are presented with advantages and drawbacks (Ch 8.5, Table 8.7): gain-sharing (Scanlon, IMPROSHARE, Rucker, Lincoln Electric), skills-based incentives, lump-sum pay increases, participative pay decisions, and flexible benefits. The five-question test for incentive effectiveness — does it capture attention, is it understood, does it improve communication, does it pay out when it should, is the company performing better — is offered as a diagnostic [V, list] (Ch 8.5, "Guidelines for Effective Incentive Programs").

## Part IX: Group and intergroup relations (Ch 9)

### Group definition, types, and joining motives

A group is "a collection of individuals who share a common set of norms, generally have differentiated roles among themselves, and interact with one another toward the joint pursuit of common goals" [AP] (Ch 9.1, "What Is a Group?"). The 2×2 typology (formal/informal × permanent/temporary) yields four types: command groups (permanent formal), task groups (temporary formal), friendship groups (permanent informal), interest groups (temporary informal) (Ch 9.1, Table 9.1).

People join groups for six reasons: security, social needs, self-esteem, economic self-interest, mutual interest, and physical proximity [AP] (Ch 9.1, "Reasons for Joining Groups"). The text notes that office layout influences group formation: clustered offices around a central area produce more interaction than parallel rows of offices (Ch 9.1).

### Tuckman's four-stage model

Groups develop through forming, storming, norming, performing [BT, Tuckman's model] (Ch 9.1, "Stages in Group Development"). The text emphasises that groups can regress to earlier stages when membership changes or scope changes.

### Work group structure: roles, norms, status, cohesiveness

Work roles split into task-oriented, relations-oriented, and self-oriented [AP] (Ch 9.2, "Work Roles"). A *role episode* has four stages: expected role (group expectations), sent role (communicated expectations), perceived role (individual's interpretation), enacted role (actual behaviour). The role set comprises all roles assigned to one person. Role conflict, role ambiguity, and role overload are discussed at length in Ch 18.

Group size effects on group dynamics (Ch 9.2, Table 9.2): smaller groups have higher group interaction, cohesiveness, satisfaction, and lower absenteeism, turnover, and social loafing. The relationship between group size and productivity is unclear without considering task interdependencies. *Social loafing* — individual reduction of effort on group tasks — is more prevalent in larger groups when members see their task as unimportant or simple, output as unidentifiable, or expect coworkers to loaf (Ch 9.2, "Productivity").

Work group norms have five characteristics: they summarise and simplify group influence; they apply only to behaviour, not to thoughts; they develop only for behaviours the group considers important; they develop gradually; and they may not apply to all members [AP] (Ch 9.2, "Characteristics of Work Group Norms"). Group norms serve four functions: facilitating group survival, simplifying expected behaviours, avoiding embarrassing situations, and identifying the group to others (Ch 9.2, "Functions of Work Group Norms").

The Asch conformity experiment is presented as a classic finding: in over a third of trials, subjects denied the evidence of their own senses to agree with a unanimous (confederate) group [BT] (Ch 9.2, "Conformity and Deviance"). Conformity is influenced by personality (lower in high-self-identity individuals), stimulus ambiguity, and group characteristics (pressure, identification, prior success).

Status systems serve four purposes: motivation, identification, dignification, and stabilisation [AP] (Ch 9.2, "Status Systems"). *Status incongruence* — when high status on one dimension contradicts characteristics or context — produces interpersonal hostility.

Group cohesiveness has seven determinants — homogeneity, maturity, size, frequency of interaction, clear group goals, competition or external threat, and prior success — and consequences include maintenance of membership, group power over members, participation and loyalty, and satisfaction. Cohesiveness's relationship with productivity is moderated by acceptance of organisational goals: "high performance is most likely to result when highly cohesive teams accept the goals of the organization" [V, paraphrased] (Ch 9.2, "Consequences of Group Cohesiveness").

### Intergroup behaviour: Hackman's effectiveness model

Group effectiveness is determined by group effort, group knowledge and skill, and task performance strategies — the *intermediate criteria* — themselves shaped by environmental context, design factors, and interpersonal processes [BT, Hackman's model] (Ch 9.3, "What Is Work Group Effectiveness?").

Intergroup performance depends on three interaction requirements: *interdependence* (pooled, sequential, reciprocal — Thompson's typology in all but name), *information flow* (driven by task uncertainty), and *integration* (collaboration despite different goals and time orientations) [AP] (Ch 9.4, "Determinants of Intergroup Performance"). Five techniques manage intergroup coordination: rules and procedures, member exchange, linking roles, task forces, and decoupling (Ch 9.4, "Managing Intergroup Behavior and Performance").

## Part X: Teams (Ch 10)

### Team versus working group

Katzenbach and Smith's definition of a team — "people organized to function cooperatively as a group" — is presented with five elements: common commitment and purpose, specific performance goals, complementary skills, commitment to how the work gets done, and mutual accountability [V] (Ch 10.1, "Teamwork in the Workplace"). A team is differentiated from a working group: the team has shared leadership, individual *and* mutual accountabilities, and measures collective work products; the working group has a focused leader, individual accountabilities, and a group purpose that mirrors the broader organisational mission.

Eight practices make teams effective: establish urgency and direction, select for skill rather than personality, attend to first impressions, set rules of behaviour, set immediate performance-oriented goals, challenge with fresh information, spend time together, and exploit positive feedback [BT, Katzenbach and Smith] (Ch 10.1).

### Team development

The Tuckman four-stage model recurs (forming, storming, norming, performing), with adjourning added in 1977 (Ch 10.2). The Katzenbach-Smith *team performance curve* tracks the journey from working group to high-performing team.

### Managing teams as paradox

Linda Hill's framing — managing a team means managing paradox [BT] (Ch 10.3, "Things to Consider When Managing Teams"): managing both individual and collective identities and goals; fostering both support and confrontation; balancing performance and learning; balancing managerial authority with team discretion; balancing the manager-team-individual relationship triangle. A core managerial responsibility is *managing the team's boundaries* — buffering external pressure while integrating useful external forces.

### Conflict, diversity, and multicultural teams

Lencioni's *Five Dysfunctions* model is presented as the framework for thinking about team trust and conflict: absence of trust prevents conflict, which prevents commitment, which prevents accountability, which prevents results [BT] (Ch 10.4, "Opportunities and Challenges to Team Building"). *Mining* and *real-time permission* are techniques to surface conflict productively.

Diverse teams outperform homogeneous teams on decision-making, problem-solving, and innovation according to multiple cited studies (McKinsey 2015 — top quartile for ethnic diversity 35 per cent more likely to have above-mean returns; BCG-TUM study — diversity in management correlates with innovation revenue when women exceed 20 per cent of management positions) [BT] (Ch 10.5, "Team Diversity"). The mechanism is that diverse team membership "alters the behaviour of a group's social majority in ways that lead to improved and more accurate group thinking" [V] (Ch 10.5).

Multicultural teams face four common conflict sources: direct versus indirect communication, accent and fluency differences, differing attitudes toward hierarchy, and conflicting decision-making norms [BT] (Ch 10.6, "Multicultural Teams"). Four interventions: adaptation, structural intervention, managerial intervention (last resort), and exit (very last resort). Cultural intelligence is presented through the head-body-heart framework (cognitive, physical, emotional/motivational) (Ch 10.6).

## Part XI: Communication (Ch 11)

### The communication model and feedback types

The basic model — communicator encodes a message, receiver decodes it — names *encoding* (translating ideas into symbols), *decoding* (interpreting), and *noise* (factors distorting clarity) (Ch 11.1, "The Process of Managerial Communication"). Three feedback types are catalogued: informational (non-evaluative), corrective (challenging), and reinforcing (confirming) [AP] (Ch 11.1, "Feedback").

### Mintzberg's three managerial role clusters

Three role clusters from Mintzberg's seminal study [BT] (Ch 11.3, "The Roles Managers Play"):

- *Interpersonal*: figurehead, leader, liaison.
- *Informational*: monitor, disseminator, spokesperson.
- *Decisional*: entrepreneur, disturbance/crisis handler, resource allocator, negotiator.

Stewart's 1967 finding (and Guest's 1956 corroboration) that managers spend 47 per cent of their time with peers, 41 per cent within their unit, and only 12 per cent with superiors anchors the importance of liaison [BT] (Ch 11.3, "Interpersonal Roles").

### Communication as invention

The chapter advances the claim that "communication is a process of invention" — managers literally create meaning through communication. "A company, for example, is not in default until a team of auditors sits down to examine the books and review the matter. Only after extended discussion do the accountants conclude that the company is, in fact, in default. It is their discussion that creates the outcome." [V] (Ch 11.5, "Communication Is Invention"). Information never speaks for itself; context drives meaning; a messenger always accompanies a message [AR].

The Larkin and Larkin observation is reproduced: "Deep down, managers believe they are communicating effectively. In ten years of management consulting, we have never had a manager say to us that he or she was a poor communicator." [V] (Ch 11.5, "Managers' Greatest Challenge").

## Part XII: Leadership (Ch 12)

### Leadership versus management

Leadership is "a social (interpersonal) influence relationship between two or more persons who depend on each other to attain certain mutual goals in a group situation" [V] (Ch 12.1, "The Nature of Leadership"). Leadership and management differ on definition (leadership is a relationship; management is a process of planning, organising, directing, controlling), origin (managers appointed; leaders accepted), power source (managers from organisational position; leaders from followers' perception of expertise, attractiveness, working relationship), and motivation type (management mostly extrinsic; leadership mostly intrinsic) (Ch 12.1, "Leader versus Manager"). Grace Hopper's distinction — "You don't manage people, you manage things. You lead people." — is reproduced [V] (Ch 12.3, "Paths to Leadership").

### Leader emergence and the five bases of power

Leaders emerge from the dynamics of need, expertise, and idiosyncrasy credits in the group [BT, Hollander, Salancik & Pfeffer] (Ch 12.3). French and Raven's five bases of power (Ch 12.3, "Leadership as an Exercise of Influence"):

- *Reward power*: people believe the holder can bestow desired outcomes.
- *Coercive power*: people believe the holder can punish.
- *Referent power*: people want to associate with or be accepted by the holder; *charismatic* power is its near-synonym.
- *Expert power*: people believe the holder has and shares needed expertise.
- *Legitimate power*: people believe the holder has the right to influence and ought to be obeyed.

Different power types produce different responses (Exhibit 12.5): reward power elicits measured response and tit-for-tat thinking; coercive power produces alienation and resistance; referent power produces identification; rationality, expertise, and moralistic appeal produce commitment and internalisation. Research suggests rationality is the most effective influence tactic for follower commitment, motivation, performance, satisfaction, and group effectiveness [BT] (Ch 12.3).

### Trait approach and the great-man theory

Locke's core trait list for leaders [BT] (Ch 12.4, "Leader Trait Research"):

- Drive: high effort, achievement orientation, ambition, energy, tenacity, initiative.
- Leadership motivation: intense desire to lead.
- Honesty and integrity: commitment to truth.
- Self-confidence: assurance in self, ideas, and capability.
- Cognitive ability: conceptual skill, judgment, analytical capacity.
- Knowledge of the business: industry and technical understanding.
- Other: charisma, creativity, flexibility.

The text reports that men emerge as leaders more frequently than women, but argues "gender role is more predictive than sex" — individuals with masculine characteristics (assertive, aggressive, competitive) emerge in leadership roles, regardless of sex; androgynous individuals (combining masculine and feminine characteristics) are as likely to emerge as those with only masculine characteristics [BT] (Ch 12.4, "Sex and Gender Role"). Style differences: men are somewhat more task-oriented and more autocratic; women somewhat more interpersonally oriented and more democratic/participative.

### Behavioural approaches: Ohio State and Michigan

The Ohio State studies identified two primary behaviour dimensions [BT, Stogdill]: *consideration* (relationship-oriented behaviours: support, friendliness, openness, recognising ideas) and *initiating structure* (task-oriented behaviours: scheduling, deciding, providing direction, planning, coordinating, problem-solving, maintaining standards) (Ch 12.5, "The Ohio State University Studies"). The Michigan studies identified job-centred and organisational-member-centred behaviours, corresponding closely to initiating structure and consideration. The Blake-Mouton Leadership Grid® positions leadership styles on concern-for-results × concern-for-people dimensions; the 9,9 (high-high) style has been advocated as universally best, but the text notes there is no universally effective style [AP] (Ch 12.5, "The Leadership Grid").

### Situational approaches: Fiedler's contingency model

Fiedler's *contingency theory of leadership* assesses the leader through the *least-preferred coworker (LPC)* score (high LPC = relationship-oriented; low LPC = task-oriented) and the situation through three factors in order of importance: leader-member relations, task structure, and position power [BT] (Ch 12.6, "Fiedler's Contingency Model"). High-LPC (relationship-oriented) leaders are most effective in moderate-favourability situations; low-LPC (task-oriented) leaders are most effective in highly favourable or highly unfavourable situations.

McGregor's Theory X (people dislike work, lack self-direction, require control) versus Theory Y (people have creative capacity, can self-direct, want to participate) sets the philosophical anchor for the involvement-versus-control style continuum [BT] (Ch 12.3, "Theory X and Theory Y Leaders"). A study of 3,600 managers from 14 countries found most held Theory-X assumptions even when publicly endorsing participatory management.

### Substitutes for leadership and transformational leadership

The chapter proceeds through substitutes-for-leadership theory and transformational/charismatic/visionary leadership (Ch 12.7-12.8, sections present but not fully covered in the read).

## Part XIII: Power and politics (Ch 13)

### Power, authority, leadership distinguished

*Power* is "the probability that one actor within a social relationship will be in a position to carry out his own will despite resistance" [V, Weber] (Ch 13.1, "What Is Power?"). The text proposes that power is "an interpersonal relationship in which one individual (or group) has the ability to cause another individual (or group) to take an action that would not be taken otherwise" [V] (Ch 13.1).

Three types of influence are distinguished: power (ability), authority (right backed by legitimacy), leadership (eliciting voluntary response). Etzioni's typology — coercive, utilitarian, normative — classifies organisations [BT] (Ch 13.1, "Types of Power"). The French-Raven five bases recur from Ch 12.

### Power dependencies and counterpower

The extent to which influence attempts succeed depends on the target's *power dependencies*: the target's values (whether the offered outcomes matter), the nature of the relationship, and *counterpower* (the target's other sources of power that buffer the influencer) [BT] (Ch 13.1, "Power Dependencies"). The matrix at Exhibit 13.4 cross-classifies power level with dependency to predict response (compliance, bargaining, cooperation, rejection-aggression, ignoring).

### Common political tactics

Seven political tactics are catalogued (Ch 13.2, "Common Power Tactics in Organizations"):

- Controlling access to information.
- Controlling access to persons.
- Selective use of objective criteria — selecting decision criteria to shape decisions, citing Simon: "if an individual is permitted to select decision criteria, they needn't care who actually makes the decision" [AP] (Ch 13.2).
- Controlling the agenda.
- Using outside experts (selectively).
- Bureaucratic gamesmanship — including *work to rule* (diligently following every rule and policy until the organisation grinds to a halt).
- Coalitions and alliances.

### Resource dependence and strategic contingencies

The Salancik-Pfeffer *resource dependence* model: subunit power accrues to those that control critical resources others need [BT] (Ch 13.3, "Power and the Control of Critical Resources"). The Hickson et al. *strategic contingencies* model identifies three sources of subunit power: ability to cope with uncertainty (forestalling, providing information, absorbing pressure), nonsubstitutability of the unit's contribution, and centrality (workflow pervasiveness and immediacy) (Ch 13.3, "Power and the Control of Strategic Activities"). The Lawrence and Lorsch study of three industries (containers, food processing, plastics) showed that successful firms aligned power distribution with strategic contingencies — power resided in the units most central to the firm's competitive challenges [BT] (Ch 13.3).

### Politics in organisations

*Politics* is "those activities taken within organizations to acquire, develop, and use power and other resources to obtain one's preferred outcomes in a situation in which there is uncertainty or dissensus about choices" [V, Pfeffer] (Ch 13.3, "What Is Politics?"). Five conditions facilitate political behaviour: ambiguous goals, limited resources, changing technology and environment, non-programmed decisions, organisational change [AP] (Ch 13.3, "Reasons for Political Behavior"). Politics is most intense for interdepartmental coordination, promotions and transfers, and delegation of authority; least intense for personnel policies, hiring, and disciplinary procedures (Ch 13.3, "Intensity of Political Behavior").

## Part XIV: Conflict and negotiation (Ch 14)

### Types and levels of conflict

Conflict is "the process by which individuals or groups react to other entities that have frustrated, or are about to frustrate, their plans, goals, beliefs, or activities" [V] (Ch 14.1, "Conflict in Organizations: Basic Considerations"). Four types: goal, cognitive, affective, behavioural. Four levels: intrapersonal, interpersonal, intergroup, interorganisational. The conflict-intensity-outcomes relationship is inverted-U: too little or too much conflict produces negative outcomes; moderate conflict can stimulate innovation, creativity, and motivation [AP] (Ch 14.1, "The Positive and Negative Sides of Conflict").

### Causes and the Thomas process model

Seven antecedents of conflict: task interdependencies, status inconsistencies, jurisdictional ambiguities, communication problems, dependence on common resource pool, lack of common performance standards, individual differences [BT, Miles] (Ch 14.2, "Why Organizations Have So Much Conflict"). The Thomas four-stage process model: frustration, conceptualisation, behaviour, outcome [BT] (Ch 14.2, "A Model of the Conflict Process").

### Five conflict-resolution modes (Thomas)

The two-axis model — assertiveness × cooperativeness — yields five modes [BT] (Ch 14.2, Exhibit 14.4):

- *Competing* (high assertiveness, low cooperativeness): for emergencies, unpopular but vital actions.
- *Collaborating* (high both): when goals matter, integrative solution needed.
- *Compromising* (medium both): when goals are important but not worth disruption.
- *Avoiding* (low both): when issue is trivial or other priorities pressing.
- *Accommodating* (low assertiveness, high cooperativeness): when issues matter more to others, when minimising loss when outmatched.

The text reports an executive study finding that executives "typically described themselves as using collaboration or compromise to resolve conflict, whereas these same executives typically described their opponents as using a competitive mode almost exclusively" — a self-serving asymmetry [V] (Ch 14.2).

### Conflict-prevention and reduction strategies

Five ineffective resolution techniques: nonaction, administrative orbiting, due process nonaction, secrecy, and character assassination [AP] (Ch 14.3, "Common Strategies that Seldom Work"). Four prevention strategies: emphasise organisation-wide goals, structure tasks well, facilitate intergroup communication, avoid win-lose situations.

Nine reduction strategies on a behaviour-change-to-attitude-change continuum (Ch 14.3, Exhibit 14.5): physical separation, rules and regulations, limiting intergroup interaction, integrators, confrontation and negotiation, third-party consultation, rotation of members, identification of interdependent tasks and superordinate goals, intergroup training.

### Negotiation: stages, strategies, and BATNA

Four negotiation stages: non-task time, information exchange, influence and persuasion, closing [AP] (Ch 14.4, "Stages of Negotiation"). Cultural differences in stage emphasis are significant: in Mexico or South Korea, the non-task stage is longer because it establishes the relationship; in China, a signed contract may symbolise the *beginning* of final-points negotiation rather than closure.

Two bargaining strategies: *distributive* (win-lose, fixed resources, short-term) and *integrative* (win-win, variable resources, joint maximisation, long-term) [V] (Ch 14.4, "Bargaining Strategies"). Skilled negotiators differ from average negotiators in considering 5.1 versus 2.6 options per issue, spending 39 per cent versus 11 per cent of time on areas of agreement, asking 21 per cent versus 10 per cent questions, and spending 1 per cent versus 6 per cent attacking opponents [V] (Ch 14.4, Table 14.4).

The *BATNA* concept ("Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement") from Fisher and Ury's *Getting to Yes* anchors *principled negotiation* — finding objective standards based on existing precedents [BT] (Ch 14.4, "Concluding Thoughts about Conflict and Negotiations"). "In general, the party with the more attractive BATNA gets the better of the deal. If both parties have attractive BATNAs, the best course of action may be not to reach an agreement at all" [V] (Ch 14.4).

Cross-cultural negotiation differences are documented: Americans, Arabs, and Russians differ significantly in primary negotiating style (factual / affective / axiomatic), conflict response (objective facts / subjective feelings / asserted ideals), concession-making, and deadline orientation [BT] (Ch 14.4, "Cultural Differences in International Negotiations", Table 14.5).

## Part XV: External and internal organisational environments and corporate culture (Ch 15)

### External environment forces

Five macro-environmental force categories: sociocultural, technological, economic, government and political, natural disasters and human-induced problems [AP] (Ch 15.1, "The Organization's External Environment"). The text catalogues current forces shaping organisations: digital technologies and AI, blockchain, sharing-economy business models (Airbnb, Uber), shifts in learning and credentialing (online courses), ethics and corporate social responsibility, climate change.

### Environment-industry-organisation fit (the 2×2)

Following Duncan and Daft, four cells based on environmental complexity (simple/complex) × environmental change (stable/unstable) [BT] (Ch 15.2, Exhibit 15.4):

- Simple-stable: soft drinks, beer, food processing, container manufacturers — low uncertainty.
- Complex-stable: universities, appliance manufacturers, chemical companies, insurance — moderate uncertainty.
- Simple-unstable: e-commerce, music, fashion clothing — high uncertainty.
- Complex-unstable: computer, aerospace, airlines, telecommunications — highest uncertainty.

### Mechanistic versus organic structures

Mechanistic structures fit stable, simple environments: top-down hierarchy of control, centralised authority, formal rules, narrow span of control, rigid departmentalisation [AP] (Ch 15.3, "Organizational Designs and Structures"). Organic structures fit unstable, complex environments: flat hierarchy, participatory communication, distributed decision-making, broader span of control, fluid task definitions.

Six organisational structures along the mechanistic-to-organic continuum (Ch 15.3, Exhibit 15.6): functional → divisional → geographic → matrix → networked team → virtual.

The matrix structure is described historically: it originated in 1960s US aerospace contracting where employees had to have dual reporting relationships with the government and the aerospace company [AP] (Ch 15.3). Matrix structures have weaknesses around divided loyalty and dual-authority confusion (Ch 15.3).

### Open systems and internal-external alignment

Katz and Kahn's open systems theory (input → through-put → output → feedback loop) frames organisations as systems exchanging resources with their environment [BT] (Ch 15.4, "The Internal Organization and External Environments"). Closed systems are insensitive to environmental changes; open systems are responsive.

The McKinsey 7-S model — strategy, structure, systems, skills, staff, style around shared values (culture) — is used as a framework for internal alignment [BT] (Ch 15.4).

### Corporate culture and the Competing Values Framework

Drucker's "culture eats strategy for breakfast" is reproduced as the framing claim [V] (Ch 15.5, "Corporate Cultures"). Culture serves two purposes: helping the organisation adapt to and integrate with its external environment by adopting appropriate values, and creating internal unity for shared goal pursuit.

The Cameron-Quinn *Competing Values Framework* (CVF) yields four cultural types on the external/internal × flexibility/control axes [BT] (Ch 15.5, Exhibit 15.21):

- *Adhocracy Culture* (external × flexibility): innovation, risk-taking, entrepreneurship — fits Facebook, Google, fast-paced industries.
- *Clan Culture* (internal × flexibility): relationships, team-building, mentoring — fits Tom's of Maine.
- *Hierarchy Culture* (internal × control): efficiency, process control, careful decision-making — fits the US Postal Service, military.
- *Market Culture* (external × control): delivering value, competing, results-driven — fits Larry Ellison's Oracle.

Amazon is presented as an instructive mixed case: an Adhocracy Culture externally (Bezos's leadership, expansion) combined with a Hierarchy Culture internally (tight control over employees at lower levels, criticised as toxic) (Ch 15.5).

## Part XVI: Organisational structure and change (Ch 16)

### Bureaucracy and the mechanistic-organic continuum

Weber's five bureaucratic elements — specialisation, command-and-control, span of control, centralisation, formalisation — are presented as the foundation for organisational design [BT] (Ch 16.1, "Bureaucracy"). The mechanistic / organic distinction is reframed (Ch 16.1, Table 16.1): high standardisation, narrow specialisation, centralised, functional departmentalisation = mechanistic; the opposites = organic.

### Three types of change and the organisational life cycle

Three types of organisational change: structural (formal relationships), technological (new technologies), and culture (most difficult, requires reshaping core identity) [AP] (Ch 16.2, "Types of Change").

The four-phase organisational life cycle: entrepreneurship (loose, organic) → survival and early success (structures emerge) → sustained success / maturity (mechanistic structures strengthen, functional structures introduced) → renewal or decline (further-flung operations, autonomy, possible restructuring) [AP] (Ch 16.2, "The Organizational Life Cycle"). The implication: structural needs evolve over time; the ideal structure changes.

### Three dimensions of change

Three dimensions for assessing change (Ch 16.2, "Dimensions of Change"):

- *Scope*: incremental (small refinements, LEAN, Six Sigma) versus transformational (significant, disruptive).
- *Level*: individual / group / organisation.
- *Intentionality*: planned versus unplanned (emergent).

### Four common change models

Four change models with different assumptions [BT] (Ch 16.3):

- **Lewin's three-stage model**: unfreeze → change → refreeze. Best for stable contexts.
- **Kotter's eight-step model**: establish urgency → form guiding coalition → create vision → communicate vision → remove obstacles → create small wins → consolidate improvements → anchor changes. Mechanistic, top-down, deficit-based assumptions; high-control situations.
- **Cooperrider's Appreciative Inquiry (AI)**: 5-D cycle (define, discover, dream, design, destiny). Abundance-based, bottom-up, positive. Uses appreciative conversations to shift organisational narratives.
- **Olson and Eoyang Complex Adaptive Systems (CAS)**: organisations are emergent, self-organising. Three influence levers: empower change agents, strengthen connections among people, ensure information and energy flow through connections. Self-organising structures can be altered through boundary conditions, disturbances, and connection-and-flow attention.

The text argues that the perception of the organisation's state (deficit-based versus abundance-based), the mechanism of change (top-down versus bottom-up), and the mindset toward people (conventional versus appreciative) jointly shape which model fits (Ch 16.3, "Basic Assumptions about Change").

## Part XVII: Human resource management (Ch 17)

### Four waves of HR

Ulrich, Younger, Brockbank, and Younger's four-wave evolution of HR work [BT] (Ch 17.1, "An Introduction to Human Resource Management"):

- Wave 1: administrative work (terms and conditions, regulatory compliance).
- Wave 2: design of innovative HR practices (compensation, learning, communication, sourcing).
- Wave 3: integration of HR strategy with business strategy.
- Wave 4: HR as a competitive practice responding to external customer, investor, and community demands.

### Performance management evolution and current debates

The performance management process traces back to the US military's WWI "merit rating" system, expanded by GE under Jack Welch in the 1980s into forced-ranking ("A players" rewarded, "C players" dismissed) [BT] (Ch 17.3, "Performance Management"). The text reports widespread dissatisfaction: 58 per cent of executives surveyed by Deloitte believed their performance management approach drove neither engagement nor high performance.

Deloitte's "radical redesign" replaced traditional ratings with four forward-looking questions about each individual: highest possible compensation increase, always want this person on the team, at risk for low performance, ready for promotion today (Ch 17.3, "Performance Management").

### Pay-for-performance and total rewards

The 2011 SHRM study found four drivers for moving to pay-for-performance: recognise and reward high performers (46.9%), increase likelihood of achieving corporate goals (32.5%), improve productivity (7.8%), move away from entitlement culture (7.8%) [V, list with percentages] (Ch 17.4).

WorldatWork's six total-rewards elements: compensation, benefits, work-life effectiveness, recognition, performance management, talent development [V, list] (Ch 17.4, "Influencing Employee Performance and Motivation"). The Nohria-Groysberg-Lee four-drives model maps drives (acquire, bond, comprehend, defend) to organisational levers (reward systems, culture, job design, performance management and resource allocation) [BT] (Ch 17.4).

### Talent acquisition and succession planning

The Fernandez-Araoz / Groysberg / Nohria seven-step recruiting framework — anticipate, specify the job, develop the pool, assess candidates, close the deal, integrate the newcomer, review the process [BT] (Ch 17.5).

The 9-box talent review template (performance × potential) anchors talent reviews, with potential defined as "an employee's ability to demonstrate the behaviors necessary to be successful at the next highest level within the company" [V] (Ch 17.6, "Talent Development and Succession Planning"). Succession planning identifies short-term (<1 year), mid-term (1-3 years), and long-term (3+ years) successor candidates per role. Follow-up development tools: training, stretch assignments, individual assessments (including 360-degree feedback), individual development plans.

## Part XVIII: Stress and well-being (Ch 18)

### Stress definition, general adaptation syndrome, and intensity-J-curve

Stress is "a physical and emotional reaction to potentially threatening aspects of the environment" — a poor person-environment fit [V] (Ch 18.1, "Work-Related Stress"). The damage from stress is *strain*. Selye's general adaptation syndrome: alarm → resistance → exhaustion [BT] (Ch 18.1, "General Adaptation Syndrome").

Stress and performance follow an inverted-J: low or moderate stress can stimulate motivation and performance; very high stress consumes attention and energy, dropping performance markedly [AP] (Ch 18.3, "Stress and Job Performance"). Some stress is necessary for psychological growth, creative activity, and skill acquisition.

### Two stress types

*Frustration* is "a psychological reaction to an obstruction or impediment to goal-oriented behavior" — what happens when a person wants to do something but is prevented [V] (Ch 18.1, "Types of Stress: Frustration and Anxiety"). *Anxiety* is "a feeling of inability to deal with anticipated harm" — what happens when one lacks plans or coping responses for anticipated problems.

### Organisational influences on stress

Four major organisational stressors (Ch 18.2):

- Occupational differences: high-stress occupations are typified by little control, relentless time pressures, threatening physical conditions, or major responsibility for human or financial resources. Top of high-stress list: firefighter, race car driver, astronaut, surgeon, NFL football player. Low-stress: musical instrument repairperson, medical records technician, librarian (Ch 18.2, Table 18.1).
- Role ambiguity: inadequate information about one's role; documented in 35-60 per cent of employees in two surveys; produces psychological strain, dissatisfaction, underutilisation, futility.
- Role conflict: simultaneous incompatible expectations.
- Role overload (quantitative or qualitative) and role underutilisation: visualised as a continuum where the optimum is a person-environment fit (Ch 18.2, Exhibit 18.4).

### Personal influences and Type A personality

Three personal stress factors (Ch 18.2, "Personal Influences on Stress"):

- *Personal control*: extent to which the employee has control over factors affecting performance. Internal locus of control intensifies the negative response to constrained control; externals are less affected.
- *Type A personality* (Friedman and Rosenman): impatience, restlessness, aggressiveness, competitiveness, polyphasic activities, time pressure. The Rosenman-Friedman 8.5-year study of 3,500 men found Type A individuals "twice as prone to heart disease, five times as prone to a second heart attack, and twice as prone to fatal heart attacks" compared to Type B [V] (Ch 18.2, "Type A Personality"). Approximately 60 per cent of managers in one study were Type A; only 12 per cent clearly Type B. The text notes the irony: Type A behaviour is helpful for promotion to the top but can be detrimental once there.
- *Rate of life change* (Holmes and Rahe): higher rates of life change are correlated with stress and illness onset.

### Buffering effects: social support and hardiness

*Social support* — peer trust and concern — buffers stress effects [BT, citing medical research] (Ch 18.3, "Social Support"). *Hardiness* — a collection of personality characteristics including commitment, internal locus of control, and a sense of life challenge — moderates stress; "people characterized by hardiness have a clear sense of where they are going and are not easily deterred by hurdles" [V] (Ch 18.3, "Hardiness").

### Consequences of stress

High stress contributes to: heart disease, peptic ulcers, arthritis, mental illness; counterproductive behaviours including turnover, absenteeism, alcoholism, drug abuse, aggression, and sabotage; performance decline; and burnout [AP] (Ch 18.3, "Consequenses of Work Related Stress"). *Burnout* is "a general feeling of exhaustion that can develop when a person simultaneously experiences too much pressure to perform and too few sources of satisfaction" [V] (Ch 18.3, "Stress and Burnout").

## Part XIX: Entrepreneurship (Ch 19)

### Six types of entrepreneurship

Entrepreneurs are "individuals who recognize and pursue opportunities, take on risk, and convert these opportunities into value-added ventures that can survive in a competitive marketplace" [V] (Ch 19, Introduction). Six types are catalogued (Ch 19.1):

- *Social entrepreneurship*: innovative solutions to social or environmental problems.
- *Corporate entrepreneurship*: new products, processes, ventures within large organisations.
- *Family entrepreneurship*: business owned and managed by multiple family members across generations.
- *Serial or habitual entrepreneurship*: starting multiple businesses.
- *Lifestyle entrepreneurship*: ventures suiting a personal lifestyle.
- *High-technology entrepreneurship*: ICT ventures, typically high growth expectations.

### GEM stages and the Total Entrepreneurial Activity (TEA) rate

The Global Entrepreneurship Monitor identifies four entrepreneurial stages: potential (capacity and intention), nascent (<3 months), new business owner (3 months to 3 years), established business owner (3.5+ years) [AP] (Ch 19.1, "Global Entrepreneurship Prevalence"). TEA varies from <5 per cent in Bulgaria, Bosnia & Herzegovina, Italy, Japan to ~20 per cent in Ecuador.

### Entrepreneurial personality traits

Nine entrepreneurial traits: ambitious, independent, self-confident, risk-taking, visionary, creative, energetic, passionate, committed [AP] (Ch 19.2, "Entrepreneurial Personality"). The text notes that managerial ability and technical knowledge are equally necessary.

### The business model canvas

Osterwalder's business model canvas covers four areas — customers, offering, infrastructure, financial viability — through nine building blocks [BT] (Ch 19.3, "Business Model Canvas"):

- Customer segments.
- Value propositions.
- Channels.
- Customer relationships.
- Revenue streams.
- Key resources.
- Key activities.
- Key partnerships.
- Cost structure.

The Apple example illustrates: mass-market customer segment, seamless music experience as value proposition, retail/online channels, lovemark customer relationship, hardware revenue stream, brand-and-content key resources, marketing and design key activities, OEM partnerships, manufacturing-and-marketing cost structure (Ch 19.3, Table 19.2).

### First mover versus second mover and design thinking

First movers can define category characteristics, capture name recognition, lock in distribution channels, set technology standards. Second movers learn from and improve on first-mover efforts. "Research on the battle between first and second movers indicates that they are equally likely to win the market" [V] (Ch 19.3).

*Design thinking* uses elements of play, empathy, reflection, creation, and experimentation across three phases: inspiration (the design challenge), ideation (creative solving), implementation (turning ideas into actions) [AP] (Ch 19.5). Convergent thinking narrows from broad to specific; divergent thinking opens new possibilities.

### Government support for entrepreneurship

Government can support entrepreneurship through reducing regulatory barriers, protecting intellectual property and capital, providing commercialisable technology, increasing positive incentives (subsidies, training programmes), or providing special benefits to favoured industries (cronyism — comes at a political and economic cost) [AP] (Ch 19.6).

## Appendix A: Scientific method in OB research

Five research designs in increasing rigor and cost [AP] (Appendix A, "Basic Research Designs"):

- Naturalistic observation (authoritative opinions, case studies): low rigor, low cost, no a priori hypotheses, qualitative.
- Survey research: low control, high external validity, low cost, qualitative and quantitative.
- Field study: medium control, high external validity, medium cost, quantitative.
- Field experiment: high control, high external validity, high cost, quantitative.
- Laboratory experiment: high control, low external validity, high cost, quantitative.

Five criteria for evaluating a theory (Kaplan): internal consistency, external consistency, scientific parsimony, generalisability, verification [BT] (Appendix A, "What Is a Good Theory?"). The text quotes Lewin: "There is nothing so practical as a good theory" [V] (Appendix A, "Uses of a Theory").

## Positions the authors explicitly frame against

- *Punishment as primary reinforcer*: the text argues against, recommending positive reinforcement combined with extinction [AR] (Ch 4.2).
- *Strict Maslow hierarchical sequencing*: research does not support the strict prepotency claim [AR] (Ch 7.2, "Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs").
- *Treating employees identically (equality) regardless of performance*: equity theory grounds the argument that *equity* (proportionality) is what employees expect, not equality [AR] (Ch 7.4).
- *Pay secrecy*: the text takes a clear position that secrecy reduces the motivational impact of differential rewards and produces miscalibrated perceptions [AR] (Ch 8.4).
- *Conflict as inherently destructive*: the text argues that moderate conflict can be productive and that process conflict (about how to do something) can improve performance, in contrast to relationship conflict [AR] (Ch 6.4, Ch 14.1).
- *Stereotyping as factual baseline*: stereotypes and similarity bias are framed as cognitive shortcuts that systematically misrepresent (and discriminate against) individuals [AR] (Ch 5.5, Ch 8.1).
- *Forced ranking as a long-term performance management tool*: the text notes GE backed away from it after Welch retired and many companies followed because it "fostered internal competition and undermined collaboration and teamwork" [V] (Ch 17.3).
- *Top-down change as universally appropriate*: the text contrasts conventional top-down change with bottom-up appreciative and complex-adaptive approaches, arguing that "the more complex the potential change, the greater the need to involve employees" [AP] (Ch 16.3).
- *Treating culture as soft / non-strategic*: Drucker's "culture eats strategy for breakfast" is cited approvingly to frame culture as the more influential force [V] (Ch 15.5).
- *The single-system view of decision-making*: the dual-system (reflective vs reactive) model is presented as more accurate than uniform models of cognition [AR] (Ch 6.2).
