## Author's thesis (paraphrased from source)

Principles of Management is presented as a traditional introductory management text organised around the **leading, planning, organising, and controlling** functions, with the stated purpose of addressing two themes: the variables that affect how, when, where, and why managers perform their jobs, and the theories and techniques that successful managers use across organisational levels to achieve and exceed objectives [AR] (Preface, "About Principles of Management"). The text frames management itself as "the process of planning, organizing, directing, and controlling the activities of employees in combination with other resources to accomplish organizational goals" [V] (Ch 1.3, "Major Characteristics of the Manager's Job").

A first load-bearing claim is that managerial work is *fragmented, conversational, and interactive*. Drawing on Mintzberg, Kotter, and Stewart, the text argues that managers spend two-thirds to three-quarters of their time in verbal interaction, switch tasks frequently, are constantly interrupted, and accomplish their work primarily through brief, unplanned conversations rather than through scheduled "big decisions" [AP] (Ch 1, "1.1 What Do Managers Do?"). Hannaway's observation that the standard textbook list of managerial activities (planning, organising, staffing, directing, coordinating, reporting, controlling) "do not, in fact, describe what managers do" anchors the chapter's argument that the real world of management is a "messy and hectic stream of ongoing activity" [V] (Ch 1, opening).

A second load-bearing claim is that *managerial decision-making is a dual-system cognitive process* and that effective managers must learn when to engage which system. The brain processes decisions through one of two routes — a reflective system (logical, analytical, deliberate, methodical) and a reactive system (quick, impulsive, intuitive) — and "the brain can only use one system at a time for processing information" [V, citing Darlow & Sloman] (Ch 2, "How the Brain Processes Information to Make Decisions"). The text categorises decisions as programmed (rule-amenable, supports heuristics) or non-programmed (novel, ambiguous, requires the systematic six-step process: recognise, generate alternatives, analyse, select, implement, evaluate) [AR] (Ch 2, "Programmed and Nonprogrammed Decisions").

A third load-bearing claim is that *organisations are open systems whose internal structure, culture, and processes must align with external environmental demands*. The mechanistic-organic continuum, the environmental-uncertainty 2×2 (simple/complex × stable/unstable), the McKinsey 7-S model, and the Cameron-Quinn Competing Values Framework recur as design tools [AR] (Ch 4, Ch 10). Drucker's "culture eats strategy for breakfast" anchors the cultural-fit argument: culture is "more influential than strategy in terms of motivating employees' beliefs, behaviors, relationships, and ways they work since culture is based on values" [V] (Ch 4.5, "Corporate Cultures").

A fourth load-bearing claim is that *strategic success requires systematic external and internal analysis* feeding into deliberate strategic positioning. Chapters 8 and 9 develop this through SWOT (strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats), PESTEL (political, economic, sociocultural, technological, environmental, legal), Porter's Five Forces (industry rivalry, threat of new entrants, threat of substitutes, supplier power, buyer power), and VRIO (value, rarity, imitation, organisation), arguing that "a firm's strategic position should try to be unique in some way that competitors cannot imitate quickly or easily" [V] (Ch 8.7, "Strategic Positioning"). The strategic management process is a cycle: vision and mission → strategic analysis → strategic objectives → strategy formulation → implementation → measurement and evaluation [AR] (Ch 9.1, "Strategic Management").

A fifth load-bearing claim is that *motivation, performance, and reward must be linked through expectancy and equity mechanisms*, with the parallel claim that *content and process theories of motivation each capture part of the picture but no single theory is universal* [AR] (Ch 14). The text argues that "to maximize motivation, organizations must make outcomes contingent on performance" [V] (Ch 14.3, "Process Theories of Motivation: Implications of Expectancy Theory") and that goal theory's premises (difficult, specific, accepted, committed-to goals produce better performance) are "one of the strongest theories in organizational behavior" [V] (Ch 14.3, "Goal Theory").

## Part I: The nature of managerial work (Ch 1)

### Managerial work as fragmented, conversational, and interactive

Mintzberg's CEO observations found managers averaged 36 written and 16 verbal contacts per day, "almost every one of them dealing with a distinct or different issue", with most activities lasting less than nine minutes [V] (Ch 1.1, "What Do Managers Do?"). Kotter's five-year study of general managers found managers spent only 25 per cent of their time alone, mostly at home, on airplanes, or commuting; few spent less than 70 per cent of their time with others, some up to 90 per cent [V] (Ch 1.1). Sproull's study found managers engaged in 58 different activities per day with an average duration of nine minutes [BT] (Ch 1.1). Stewart found her studied managers could work uninterrupted for half an hour only nine times across four weeks [BT] (Ch 1.1). The text quotes Mintzberg: "Unlike other workers, the manager does not leave the telephone or the meeting to get back to work. Rather, these contacts are his work" [V] (Ch 1.1).

Verbal conversations are, per Eccles and Nohria, the means by which managers "gather information, stay on top of things, identify problems, negotiate shared meanings, develop plans, put things in motion, give orders, assert authority, develop relationships, and spread gossip" [V] (Ch 1.1).

### Mintzberg's three role clusters

Mintzberg's seminal study identified three core role clusters [BT] (Ch 1.2, "The Roles Managers Play"; restated in Ch 16.3):

- *Interpersonal* roles arising from formal authority: figurehead, leader, liaison. Mintzberg's chief executives spent 12 per cent of contact time on ceremonial duties; 17 per cent of incoming mail dealt with acknowledgments and status-related requests [V] (Ch 1.2; Ch 16.3).
- *Informational* roles: monitor (scanning the environment for information), disseminator (passing privileged information to subordinates), spokesperson (sending information to people outside the organisation).
- *Decisional* roles: entrepreneur (initiating change), disturbance/crisis handler (involuntarily reacting to conditions), resource allocator (deciding "who gets what, how much, when, and why"), negotiator.

Stewart's 1967 study of 160 British middle and top managers found they spent 47 per cent of their time with peers, 41 per cent with people inside their unit, and only 12 per cent with superiors; Guest's 1956 study of US manufacturing supervisors revealed similar findings, anchoring the importance of the liaison role [V] (Ch 1.2; Ch 16.3).

### Characteristics of managerial work

Time is fragmented; values compete and roles are in tension; the job is overloaded; efficiency is the core skill of the 21st-century manager [AP] (Ch 1.3, "Major Characteristics of the Manager's Job"). Classical management theory's seven-direct-reports limit has given way to spans of 20-30 direct reports under modern information technology (Ch 1.3).

### Nine managerial responsibilities, three levels, three skill types

Managers' responsibilities aggregate into nine activities [AP] (Ch 1.3, "Managerial Responsibilities"): long-range planning; controlling; environmental scanning; supervision; coordinating; customer relations and marketing; community relations; internal consulting; and monitoring products and services.

The three management levels are *executive*, *middle*, and *first-line*; the three skill types are technical, human relations, and conceptual. As one rises in the hierarchy, conceptual skill demand increases and technical skill demand decreases; human-relations skill is required at every level [AP] (Ch 1.3, "Variations in Managerial Work"). The 21st-century manager will need to be "a global strategist, a master of technology, a good politician, and a premier leader-motivator" [V] (Ch 1, "Summary of Learning Outcomes 1.3" — appears in chapter overview prose as well).

## Part II: Managerial decision-making (Ch 2)

### Decision-making and stakeholders defined

*Decision-making* is "the action or process of thinking through possible options and selecting one" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Overview of Managerial Decision-Making"). *Stakeholders* are "all the individuals or groups that are affected by an organization (such as customers, employees, shareholders, etc.)" [V] (Ch 2.1). Top-management decisions affect organisational survival; lower-level decisions affect productivity, expense, and morale (Ch 2.1).

The text takes a position against "maximizing shareholder wealth" as the dominant decision criterion: "Maximizing shareholder wealth is often a short-sighted decision … because it can harm the organization's financial viability in the future. Bad publicity, customers boycotting the organization, and government fines are all possible long-term outcomes" [V] (Ch 2.1, "What's the Right (Ethical) Answer?"). "More importantly, increasing the wealth of shareholders is not an acceptable reason for causing harm to others" [V] (Ch 2.1).

### Two-system processing of decisions

The brain processes decisions through one of two routes: the *reflective system* (logical, analytical, deliberate, methodical, prefrontal cortex) and the *reactive system* (quick, impulsive, intuitive, basal ganglia and amygdala) [BT, citing neuropsychology research, Darlow & Sloman] (Ch 2.2, "How the Brain Processes Information to Make Decisions"). "Research in neuropsychology suggests that the brain can only use one system at a time for processing information" [V] (Ch 2.2). Strong emotions push decision-making toward the reactive route; novel and complex situations call for the reflective route. *Emotional intelligence* — the ability to recognise, understand, pay attention to, and manage one's own emotions and others' — supports the toggle between systems, involves self-awareness, self-regulation, empathy, and social skills (Ch 2.2, "The Role of Emotions").

### Programmed and non-programmed decisions

*Programmed decisions* are "those that are repeated over time and for which an existing set of rules can be developed to guide the process" [V] (Ch 2.3, "Programmed and Nonprogrammed Decisions"). They support *heuristics* — "mental shortcuts, to help reach a decision" — that "are efficient — they save time for the decision maker by generating an adequate solution quickly" but "don't necessarily yield the optimal solution" [V] (Ch 2.3).

*Non-programmed decisions* are "novel, unstructured decisions that are generally based on criteria that are not well-defined" [V] (Ch 2.3). They require the systematic six-step decision-making process: (1) recognise that a decision needs to be made, (2) generate multiple alternatives, (3) analyse the alternatives, (4) select an alternative, (5) implement the selected alternative, (6) evaluate its effectiveness (Ch 2.3, "The Decision-Making Process").

The text aligns the dual systems with the two decision types: programmed decisions can use the quick, reactive system; non-programmed decisions need the reflective system (Ch 2.3).

### Six barriers to effective decision-making

The chapter catalogues six barriers (Ch 2.4):

1. *Bounded rationality* — "the idea that for complex issues we cannot be completely rational because we cannot fully grasp all the possible alternatives, nor can we understand all the implications of every possible alternative" [V] (Ch 2.4).
2. *Escalation of commitment* — "the tendency of decision makers to remain committed to poor decision, even when doing so leads to increasingly negative outcomes" [V] (Ch 2.4). "Effective managers recognize that progress down the wrong path isn't really progress, and they are willing to reevaluate decisions and change direction when appropriate" [V] (Ch 2.4).
3. *Time constraints* — "Time pressures can cause us to rely on heuristics rather than engage in deep processing" [AP] (Ch 2.4).
4. *Uncertainty* — outcomes are unknown until alternatives are chosen.
5. *Personal biases*, including the "similar to me" preference and *confirmation bias* — "the tendency to pay attention to information that confirms our existing beliefs and to ignore or discount information that conflicts with our existing beliefs" [V] (Ch 2.4).
6. *Conflict*, with the explicit distinction between *process conflict* — "conflict about the best way to do something" — which "can actually lead to improved performance, as individuals explore various options together in order to identify superior solutions" [V], and *relationship conflict* — "conflict between individuals that is more personal and involves attacks on a person rather than an idea" — which "is generally harmful and should be quelled when possible" [V] (Ch 2.4, "Conflict"). Relationship conflict pushes participants toward the reactive system.

### Improving decision quality

The chapter recommends *evidence-based decision-making* — "an approach to decision-making that states that managers should systematically collect the best evidence available to help them make effective decisions" [V] (Ch 2.5, "Improving the Quality of Decision-Making"). Evidence includes the decision-maker's own expertise, external evidence (other stakeholders, contextual factors, costs, benefits) — particularly beneficial "for new managers or for experienced managers who are starting something new" [V] (Ch 2.5).

*Critical thinking* is "a disciplined process of evaluating the quality of information, especially data collected from other sources and arguments made by other people, to determine whether the source should be trusted or whether the argument is valid" [V] (Ch 2.5). Seven common logical fallacies are catalogued in Table 2.2: non sequitur (does not follow); false cause; ad hominem (attack the person); genetic fallacy (origin-based dismissal, often stereotype-based); appeal to tradition; bandwagon approach (majority justifies it); appeal to emotion (Ch 2.5).

Ethical decision-making is grounded in James Rest's four-component model [BT] (Ch 2.5, "Are there ethical implications?"):

1. Moral sensitivity — recognising that the issue has a moral component.
2. Moral judgment — determining which actions are right vs wrong.
3. Moral motivation/intention — deciding to do the right thing.
4. Moral character/action — actually doing what is right.

"A failure at any point in the chain can lead to unethical actions!" [V] (Ch 2.5). The "front-page-of-a-newspaper" test is offered for moral judgment: "if you would feel guilty or ashamed, don't do it!" [V, paraphrased] (Ch 2.5).

The selection step warns against decision paralysis: "Effective managers recognize that they will not always make optimal (best possible) decisions because they don't have complete information and/or don't have the time or resources to gather and process all the possible information" [V] (Ch 2.5, "Recognize that perfection is unattainable").

*Satisficing* — "Choosing the first acceptable solution to minimize time spent on a decision" [V] (Ch 2 Key Terms, restated in Ch 2.5) — is the Simon-derived heuristic for programmed decisions.

### Group decision-making, groupthink, and devil's advocate

Group decisions can incorporate diverse perspectives and reduce bias, "provided the group members reflect different perspectives" [V] (Ch 2.6, "Group Decision-Making"). Diverse groups generate more options through *brainstorming* — "a process of generating as many solutions or options as possible" [V].

The disadvantages: *groupthink* "occurs when group members choose not to voice their concerns or objections because they would rather keep the peace and not annoy or antagonize others. Sometimes groupthink occurs because the group has a positive team spirit and camaraderie, and individual group members don't want that to change by introducing conflict" [V] (Ch 2.6, "Disadvantages of Group Decisions"). *Suppression of dissent* occurs when "one individual in the group has more power or exerts more influence than others and discourages those with differing opinions from speaking up" [V].

Counter-measures: form groups with diverse members; encourage everyone to speak; assign a *devil's advocate* — "A group member who intentionally takes on the role of being critical of the group's ideas in order to discourage groupthink and encourage deep thought and discussion about issues prior to making decisions" [V] (Ch 2.6, Ch 2 Key Terms); and find common ground when conflict becomes excessive (Ch 2.6, "How to Form a Quality Group").

## Part III: The history of management thought (Ch 3)

### Ancient and pre-industrial origins

Management's earliest origins trace through Sumer (writing and trade), Hammurabi's Code (1810 BC-1750 BC) of 282 laws regulating "business dealings, personal behavior, interpersonal relations, and punishments" — Law 104 being "one of the first instances of accounting and of the need for formal rules for managers and owners" [V] (Ch 3.1, "The Early Origins of Management"). Nebuchadnezzar (605 BC-c. 562 BC) gave incentives to cloth weavers, paying more food for more cloth produced. The Egyptians pioneered division of labor, coordination, specialisation, and span of control (an ideal of ten workers per supervisor). The Han dynasty (206 BC-220 AD) developed Chinese bureaucracy with Confucian-trained scholars and meritocratic selection. The Greeks (Plato's recognition of human diversity, Socrates on managerial skills) and Romans (standardisation of measures, weights, coins; the corporation; stocks sold to the public) added further pieces (Ch 3.1).

### The Italian Renaissance and the Industrial Revolution

The Italian Renaissance saw the emergence of double-entry bookkeeping, the corporation, and the first multinationals (the Florence Company of Bardi making loans to kings) [BT, citing Muldoon and Marin] (Ch 3.2). The word "management" entered English through John Florio, an Anglo-Italian member of Queen Elizabeth's court (Ch 3.3). Adam Smith's *The Wealth of Nations* contributed specialisation and division of labor, with three outcomes: drastically reduced cost, drastically reduced training need, and the increased need for management to coordinate specialised work [BT] (Ch 3.3).

The Industrial Revolution (1760-1900) shifted production from family-led home settings to factories. Pre-revolution conditions in factories included no concern for workers' physical or mental health, no breaks, vicious management-worker contention, and "soldiering" — Frank Gilbreth's 1885 apprenticeship as a bricklayer revealed he was taught three ways to lay bricks "even though there was no need for more than one method" [AP] (Ch 3.3, "The Industrial Revolution"; Ch 3.4, "Taylor-Made Management"). Drucker is cited claiming the development of management was "one of the United States' primary contributions to the world, along with the Declaration of Independence" [V] (Ch 3.4).

### Taylor and scientific management

Frederick Winslow Taylor (1856-1915), "the father of scientific management", coined the term "soldiering" for the deliberate reduction of productivity by workers (Ch 3.4). Taylorism rested on **four principles of management** [BT] (Ch 3.4):

1. Develop a rule of science for each aspect of a job (objective data, not rules of thumb).
2. Scientifically select and train each worker.
3. Management and workforce should work together cooperatively, not as enemies.
4. Work and responsibility should be equally divided between management and workers.

Taylor pioneered time studies, division of labor based on function, cost-control systems, written instructions, planning, and standardised equipment. He stressed *piecework* and *differential piecework* (paying more for production above a threshold). His concept of *first-class work* held that workers should do as much as they are physically and mentally capable of, and those incapable should be reassigned (Ch 3.4).

Taylor's acolytes contributed: Carl G. Barth (slide rule for steel cutting); Henry Gantt (the Gantt chart and the employee bonus system); Frank and Lillian Gilbreth (motion studies, reducing all hand motions to 17 basic combinations; Lillian later designed the modern kitchen) (Ch 3.4, "Taylor's Acolytes").

Taylor's shortcomings: he was a "monomaniac" who alienated unions, workers, and management; he treated workers as machine tools; and he ignored strategy, implementation, and human factors. "Taylorism, despite its claims, was not an overall theory of management, but a management system designed for frontline managers" [AP] (Ch 3.4, "Taylor's Shortcomings").

### Fayol's administrative theory and Weber's bureaucracy

Henri Fayol (1841-1925) focused on top managers; Max Weber (1864-1920) focused on middle managers; both complemented Taylor's frontline focus [AP] (Ch 3.5, "Administrative and Bureaucratic Management").

Fayol's three principal ideas were: *unity of command* ("each worker should have only one supervisor"); *esprit de corps* (cohesion, commitment, pride from organisational membership, built through communication); and the importance of *justice* in organisational decision-making [BT] (Ch 3.5). Fayolism comprises the **14 principles of management**: division of work; authority; discipline; unity of command; unity of direction; subordination of individual interest; remuneration; centralisation; scalar chain; order; equity; stability of tenure of personnel; initiative; esprit de corps. The **five functions of management**: planning, organising, staffing, controlling, directing (Ch 3.5).

Weber identified three types of leadership domination — charismatic, traditional, legal — and developed the *modern bureaucracy* in which decisions are made on a formal basis through rules rather than whim. Weber's principles of the ideal bureaucracy: specialised roles; recruitment based on merit; uniform principles of placement, promotion, and transfer; careerism with systematic salary structure; hierarchy, responsibility, accountability; subjection of official conduct to strict discipline; supremacy of abstract rules; impersonal authority [BT] (Ch 3.5).

Robert K. Merton's critique of bureaucracy — that "rules could become more important than actual goals" and that "the very elements which conduce toward efficiency in general produce inefficiency in specific instances" [V] — is reproduced (Ch 3.5).

### The human relations movement: Mayo, Barnard, Follett

The Hawthorne studies (1924-1932 at Western Electric's Hawthorne plant in Cicero, Illinois) are presented as "the most influential, misunderstood, and criticized research experiment in all of the social sciences" [V] (Ch 3.6, "Human Relations Movement"). The illumination phase showed productivity rose regardless of light level. The relay-assembly-test-room phase (six young female workers) showed productivity rose regardless of variable manipulated, with social factors (camaraderie, suggestion-making freedom, perceived special status) implicated. The bank wiring room study (1931-1932) revealed group norms restricting output to 6,000-6,600 connections/day, with workers ostracising or hitting those who produced too much. George Homans is quoted contrasting the two: "Both groups developed an informal social organization, but while the Bank Wiremen were organized in opposition to management, the Relay Assemblers were organized in cooperation with management in the pursuit of a common purpose" [V] (Ch 3.6).

Elton Mayo's interpretation — that supervisor attention and group attitudes drive productivity — became canonical despite later criticism that the studies were "scientifically worthless" because of methodological problems. The lasting contributions: focus on workers' actual work life, generation of subsequent research showing attitudes matter, recognition of supervisor role in worker behaviour and emotion, and the principle that work motivation is multifactorial (pay, social relationships, meaning, interests, attitudes) (Ch 3.6).

Chester Barnard (1886-1961), president of New Jersey Bell Telephone Company, contributed *The Functions of the Executive* and the concept of the "**zone of indifference**": "workers will comply with orders if they are indifferent to them … workers need merely to be indifferent to an order to follow it" [V] (Ch 3.6, "Barnard and the 'Zone of Indifference'"). Compliance is contingent on four factors: ability to comply; understanding of the order; consistency with organisational goals; non-violation of personal beliefs. Barnard's executive incentives spanned monetary, nonmaterial recognition, desirable working conditions, and pride/meaning in work (Ch 3.6).

Mary Parker Follett (1868-1933) bridged scientific management and human relations [BT, citing Wren and Bedeian] (Ch 3.6, "Follett and Conflict Resolution"). Follett identified three ways to resolve conflict: *dominance* (one party dictates terms); *compromise* (neither party gets what it wants); and *integration* (both parties state preferences and reach a creative agreement that satisfies both) [V terms] (Ch 3.6). Her famous library window example illustrates integration: "In the Harvard Library one day, in one of the smaller rooms, someone wanted the window open. I wanted it shut. We opened the window in the next room where no one was sitting" [V] (Ch 3.6). Follett also argued power should be *with* the worker, not *over* the worker, and that power should be based on knowledge and expertise.

### Contingency and systems schools

The systems school (Boulding, Katz, Kahn, von Bertalanffy) framed firms as *open systems* exchanging resources with their environment [BT] (Ch 3.7, "Contingency and System Management"). Open systems are responsive to environmental change; closed systems are not.

The contingency school (Joan Woodward in 1950s-1960s Britain) replaced the search for one-best-way with the principle that "there are no universal rules in management. External and internal factors create unique situations, and each situation requires a different response. … The key statement of the contingency school is 'it depends.'" [V] (Ch 3.7).

Modern management since the 1970s emphasises theory development and *evidence-based management* (Pfeffer), bringing the discipline back toward Taylor's scientific aspirations (Ch 3.7, "Modern Management").

## Part IV: External and internal organisational environments and corporate culture (Ch 4)

### External environment forces

Five categories of macro-environmental forces shape organisations: sociocultural, technological, economic, government and political, natural disasters and human-induced problems [AP] (Ch 4.1, "The Organization's External Environment"). Globalization is "a combination of external forces shaping environments of organizations", defined as "the development of an integrated global economy and characterized by free trade, capital flows, communications, and cheaper foreign labor markets" [V] (Ch 4.1).

Current external trends shaping organisations include digital technologies and artificial intelligence (a survey showed 59 per cent of organisations were collecting information to develop AI strategies); blockchain ("an architecture that allows disparate users to make transactions and then creates an unchangeable record of those transactions" [V]); sharing-economy business models (Airbnb, Uber); shifts in learning and credentialing; ethics, CSR, and sustainability; environmental and climate change [AP] (Ch 4 introduction; Ch 4.6).

### Environment-industry-organisation 2×2 fit

Following Duncan and Daft, four cells based on environmental complexity (simple/complex) × environmental change (stable/unstable) [BT] (Ch 4.2, "External Environments and Industries"):

- *Simple-stable*: soft drink bottlers, beer distributors, food processors, container manufacturers — low uncertainty.
- *Complex-stable*: universities, appliance manufacturers, chemical companies, insurance — moderate uncertainty.
- *Simple-unstable*: e-commerce, music, fashion clothing — high uncertainty.
- *Complex-unstable*: computer, aerospace, airlines, telecommunications — highest uncertainty.

The text observes that domains once stable (toys, public utilities, US Postal Service, higher education) have become more complex and unstable (Ch 4.4).

### Mechanistic versus organic structures and six structural types

*Mechanistic organizational structures* are "best suited for environments that range from stable and simple to low-moderate uncertainty … characterized by top-down hierarchies of control that are rule-based. The chain of command is highly centralized and uses formal authority; tasks are clearly defined and differentiated to be executed by specific specialized experts. Bosses and supervisors have fewer people working directly under them (i.e., a narrow span of control), and the organization is governed by rigid departmentalization" [V] (Ch 4.3, "Organizational Designs and Structures"). *Organic organizational structures* "have opposite characteristics from mechanistic ones. … these organizational forms work best in unstable, complex, changing environments. Their structures are flatter, with participatory communication and decision-making flowing in different directions. There is more fluidity and less-rigid ways of performing tasks; there may also be fewer rules. Tasks are more generalized and shared; there is a wider span of control" [V] (Ch 4.3).

Six organisational structure types span the mechanistic-organic continuum (Ch 4.3, Exhibit 4.6):

- *Functional structure* — earliest and most-used; hierarchical pyramid organised by functional departments (R&D, production, accounting, HR). Strengths: specialisation, simple reporting, economies of scale, scalable. Weaknesses: silos, slow decisions, power competition.
- *Divisional structure* — multiple functional groups under a division head, by markets, geographies, or products. Strengths: focus, accountability, customer responsiveness, decentralised decision-making. Weaknesses: division insularity, system incompatibility.
- *Geographic structure* — organised by customer locations; suits national/international/global operations. Similar advantages and disadvantages to divisional.
- *Matrix structure* — originated in 1960s US aerospace contracting where employees had dual reporting (government plus aerospace company) [AP]. Combines vertical functional structure with horizontal team-based structure. Weakness: confusion and conflict from dual reporting; "divided loyalties" [V]. Variants: cross-functional, functional, global (Ch 4.3).
- *Networked team structure* — "naturally forms after being initially assigned … connected together by informal networks and the demands of the task, rather than a formal organizational structure. The network organization prioritizes its 'soft structure' of relationships, networks, teams, groups and communities rather than reporting lines" [V] (Ch 4.3, citing the 2017 Global Human Capital Trend study). Weaknesses: communication clarity, technology dependence, lack of central physical location.
- *Virtual structure* — emerged 1990s for flexibility, on-demand solutions, dispersed expertise; "boundaryless"; examples include Uber, Airbnb, Amazon, Reebok, Nike, Puma, Dell. Advantages include cost savings, faster response, environmental impact reduction (Ch 4.3).

### Open systems and internal-external alignment

Katz and Kahn's open systems theory: input → through-put → output → feedback loop [BT] (Ch 4.4, "The Internal Organization and External Environments"). The McKinsey 7-S model — strategy, structure, systems, skills, staff, style around shared values (culture) — is presented as a framework for internal alignment [BT] (Ch 4.4).

Lewin and Stephens's unifying framework integrates external scanning with SWOT analysis, then domain definition, then vision/mission/goals/strategy, then culture/structure/systems alignment [BT] (Ch 4.4, Exhibit 4.18).

### Corporate culture and the Competing Values Framework

Drucker's "culture eats strategy for breakfast" is reproduced as the framing claim [V] (Ch 4.5, "Corporate Cultures"). Culture serves two purposes: helping the organisation adapt to and integrate with its external environment by adopting appropriate values, and creating internal unity for shared goal pursuit. "Organizational cultures are generally framed and influenced by the top-level leader or founder. This individual's vision, values, and mission set the 'tone at the top'" [V] (Ch 4.5).

The Cameron-Quinn *Competing Values Framework* (CVF) yields four cultural types on external/internal × flexibility/control axes [BT] (Ch 4.5, Exhibit 4.21):

- *Adhocracy Culture* (external × flexibility): innovation, risk-taking, entrepreneurship — Facebook, Google.
- *Clan Culture* (internal × flexibility): relationships, team-building, mentoring — Tom's of Maine.
- *Hierarchy Culture* (internal × control): efficiency, process control, careful decision-making — US Postal Service, military.
- *Market Culture* (external × control): delivering value, competing, results-driven — Larry Ellison's Oracle.

Amazon is presented as a mixed case: "Amazon blends a high-performance Adhocracy Culture with regard to its external expansion and Bezos's leadership style; at the same time, Amazon resembles a Hierarchy Culture internally with regard to its tight control over employees at lower levels" [V] (Ch 4.5).

## Part V: Ethics, corporate responsibility, and sustainability (Ch 5)

### Ethics defined; terminal and instrumental values

*Ethics* "essentially involves how we act, live, lead our lives, and treat others" [V] (Ch 5.1, "Ethics and Business Ethics Defined"). *Normative ethics* concerns "how should and ought we live and act" [V]. *Business ethics* is "applied ethics that focuses on real-world situations and the context and environment in which transactions occur — How should we apply our values to the way we conduct business?" [V].

The Goodpaster-Nash framework identifies three levels of ethical analysis: individual, organisational, societal/global [BT] (Ch 5.2, "Dimensions of Ethics: The Individual Level"). At the individual level, *terminal values* are "desired goals, objectives, or end states that individuals wish to pursue" — e.g., freedom, security, pleasure, social recognition, friendship, accomplishment, comfort, adventure, equality, wisdom, happiness [V]. *Instrumental values* are "preferred means of behavior used to obtain those goals" — e.g., being helpful, honest, courageous, independent, polite, responsible, capable, ambitious, loving, self-contained, forgiving [V] (Ch 5.2).

The text takes a strong position on Wells Fargo: high-pressure unrealistic sales goals (terminal value: aggressive achievement; instrumental value: aggressive achievement) created undue pressure that produced lying, pressure, and customer misleading. "Ultimately, the CEO was pressured to resign, 5,300 employees were fired, and several lawsuits ensued" [V] (Ch 5.2). The lessons: be aware of underlying values of organisational directives; discover your own values; familiarise yourself with ethical principles. Caroucci's five organisational provocations of unethical choices: psychological unsafety to speak up; excessive performance pressure; conflicting goals and unfairness; ethics talk only after scandal; lack of positive examples [BT] (Ch 5.2).

### Seven ethical principles

The chapter presents seven ethical principles for moral decision-making (Ch 5.3, "Ethical Principles and Responsible Decision-Making") [BT]:

1. *Utilitarianism* (consequentialist, ends-justifies-means): the greatest good for the greatest number; net benefits over costs are greatest. Limits: ignores individuals; "good for all concerned" is contested; costs/benefits hard to measure.
2. *Universalism* (duty-based): considers welfare and risks of all parties, takes human values seriously. Cooper, Santora, and Sarros: "Universalism is the outward expression of leadership character and is made manifest by respectfulness for others, fairness, cooperativeness, compassion, spiritual respect, and humility" [V] (Ch 5.3).
3. *Rights* (legal and moral entitlements): legal rights from constitutions/statutes; moral rights universal across societies (life, liberty, freedom of speech, work).
4. *Justice* (procedures, compensation, retribution): equal treatment; equal opportunity; fair procedures; punishment of harm-doers and compensation of harmed parties. Five test questions: Is it fair? Is it right? Who gets hurt? Who pays for consequences? Do I want to assume responsibility? (Ch 5.3, "Justice").
5. *Virtue ethics* (character-based): truthfulness, practical wisdom, happiness, flourishing, well-being; "What would my 'best self' do in this situation?" [V].
6. *Common good*: actions take the broader physical, cultural, and social environment into account.
7. *Ethical relativism* (self-interest, cultural relativism): "people set their own moral standards for judging their actions" [V]; "When in Rome, do as the Romans do" [V]. Limits: blind spots, dictatorships, enabling abuses (e.g., apartheid).

### Ethical leadership at the organisational level

Barnard's 1939 values-based leadership approach inspires "cooperative personal decisions by creating faith in common understanding, faith in the probability of success, faith in the ultimate satisfaction of personal motives, and faith in the integrity of common purpose" [V] (Ch 5.4, "Leadership: Ethics at the Organizational Level").

Leadership is defined as "the ability to influence followers to achieve common goals through shared purposes" [V]. The "tone at the top" sets the ethical climate. Ethisphere's five EQ criteria: ethics and compliance program (35 per cent); culture; corporate citizenship and responsibility; corporate governance; leadership, innovation, reputation [BT] (Ch 5.4).

Five virtue-related leadership behaviours: show respect for others; treat all stakeholders fairly; work toward a common good; build community; be honest. Cialdini, Petrova, and Goldstein found that "dishonest organizations suffer from tarnished reputations, decreased worker productivity, and various damages related to increased surveillance" with "the costs of organizational dishonesty greatly outweigh any short-term gains from such behavior" [BT] (Ch 5.4).

*Stewardship* "is concerned with empowering followers to make decisions and gain control over their work" [V]. *Servant leadership* (Greenleaf) "involves selflessly working with followers to achieve shared goals that improve collective, rather than individual, welfare" [V]. The five aspects of servant leadership: placing service before self-interest; listening to others; inspiring through trust; working toward feasible goals; helping others whenever possible (Ch 5.4, "Stewardship and Servant Leadership Styles").

Seven symptoms of failed ethical leadership ("dark side") [BT] (Ch 5.4): ethical blindness; ethical muteness; ethical incoherence (espoused values inconsistent with practice); ethical paralysis; ethical hypocrisy; ethical schizophrenia (different values at home vs work); ethical complacency.

### Ethics, culture, and compliance

Schein defines organisational culture as "a pattern of shared tacit assumptions learned or developed by a group as it solves its problems of external adaptation and internal integration that have worked well enough to be considered valid and, therefore, to be taught to new members as the correct way to perceive, think, and feel in relation to those problems" [V] (Ch 5.5, "Ethics, Corporate Culture, and Compliance"). Heskett: culture "can account for 20-30% of the differential in corporate performance when compared with 'culturally unremarkable' competitors" [V].

The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 set new accountability for CEOs and CFOs, requiring written certification of financial statements with 20-year prison terms and $5 million fines for knowingly false statements (Ch 5.5).

### Corporate social responsibility and stakeholder management

CSR is "a belief that corporations have a social responsibility beyond pure profit" [V] (Ch 5.6, "Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)"). The 3 Ps / *triple bottom line* (TBL/3BL) — profit, people, planet — measures financial, social, and environmental performance (Ch 5.6).

Cone Communications' 2015 Millennial CSR Study: "More than nine-in-10 Millennials would switch brands to one associated with a cause (91% vs. 85% U.S. average), and two-thirds use social media to engage around CSR (66% vs. 53% U.S. average)" [V] (Ch 5.6). A meta-analysis of 52 studies (sample 33,878) suggested "corporate virtue in the form of social responsibility and, to a lesser extent, environmental responsibility, is likely to pay off" [V] (Ch 5.6).

*Stakeholder theory* "argues that corporations should treat all their constituencies fairly and that doing so can strengthen companies' reputations, customer relations, and performance in the marketplace" [V, citing Freeman] (Ch 5.6, "Stakeholder Management"). A *stakeholder* is "any group or individual who can affect or is affected by an organization's strategies, major transactions, and activities. Stakeholders include employees, suppliers, customers, shareholders, the government, media, and others" [V] (Ch 5.6). Eight stakeholder analysis questions are offered: Who are the stakeholders? What are their stakes? What do supporters/resisters stand to gain/lose? What types of power do they have? What strategies retain support / win over resistance? (Ch 5.6, Exhibit 5.8).

### Ethics around the globe

The Foreign Corrupt Practices Act prohibits American firms from accepting/offering bribes to foreign officials, with fines up to $2 million for companies and $100,000 plus five years' imprisonment for individuals (Ch 5.7). The UN Global Compact's ten principles guide international firms in LDCs, covering human rights, labour, environment, and anti-corruption (Ch 5.7).

The Levi Strauss/Bangladesh case illustrates negotiated values: children under 14 were working in two suppliers, violating Levi's norms but not local norms; firing them would deny education and family income; Levi's negotiated: suppliers pay regular wages while children attend school, then hire them at age 15; Levi's pays for tuition, books, uniforms [AE] (Ch 5.7, "Globalization").

### Moral entrepreneur

Brown and Trevino's *moral entrepreneur* is someone who creates a new ethical norm or enforces a new norm through "the demonstration of normatively appropriate conduct through personal actions and interpersonal relationships, and the promotion of such conduct to followers through two-way communication, reinforcement, and decision-making" [V] (Ch 5.8, "Emerging Trends in Ethics, CSR, and Compliance"; "Moral Entrepreneur"). Studies show that exposure to a moral entrepreneur makes people more likely to become one, and personality traits of agreeableness and conscientiousness positively relate to ethical leadership [BT].

## Part VI: International management (Ch 6)

### Globalisation factors

Three factors fuel globalisation [AP] (Ch 6.1, "Importance of International Management"): (1) lowering trade barriers through trade agreements (the WTO, with 164 members as of July 2016, provides the global mechanism for negotiating, monitoring, and enforcing them); (2) foreign direct investment, with 2017 global FDI flows of USD $1.52 trillion, and the rise of *emerging market multinationals* such as CEMEX, Shoprite, WIPRO, Infosys; (3) the Internet, with 3.8 billion users (50 per cent of world population) and dramatic growth in Africa (>8000 per cent 2000-2017), Latin America (2137 per cent), Middle East (4374 per cent) (Ch 6.1).

### Hofstede's four cultural dimensions

Hofstede defines culture as "the collective programming of the mind which distinguishes the member of one group or category of people from another" [V] (Ch 6.2, "Hofstede's Cultural Framework"). His IBM-employee survey (88,000 employees in 72 countries) yielded four dimensions [BT] (Ch 6.2):

- *Power distance*: degree to which members accept differences in power and authority. High power distance societies (India, Brazil, Mexico, China, France) accept inequality, expect hierarchical authority, send senior/older managers as negotiators. Low power distance (US, UK, Canada, Scandinavia) treats all as equals.
- *Individualism vs collectivism*: degree of focus on individual vs group. High individualism (US, UK, Canada, Australia, Germany, Denmark, Finland) values individual achievement, autonomous decisions, equity-based reward. Low individualism / high collectivism values in-group loyalty, group decisions, equality-based reward, hiring relatives.
- *Uncertainty avoidance*: comfort with risk and unpredictability. High UA (Mexico, France, Spain, Greece, Japan) wants stability, written rules, large companies, long-term employment. Low UA (Anglo and Scandinavian countries) tolerates ambiguity, smaller companies, shorter tenure.
- *Masculinity*: emphasis on traditional masculine qualities (advancement, earnings). High masculinity (US, UK, Canada, Germany, Australia, Japan) prioritises work over leisure, pay incentives, fewer women in management. Low masculinity (France, Spain, Scandinavia) prioritises quality of life, work-life balance.

### The GLOBE framework and cluster-based leadership preferences

The GLOBE project (170 researchers, 17,000 managers, 62 countries) added two new dimensions to Hofstede's: *performance orientation* and *humane orientation* [BT] (Ch 6.3, "The GLOBE Framework"). GLOBE clusters relevant for international managers: Anglo, Confucian Asia, Germanic Europe, Latin America, Nordic Europe, Middle East, Sub-Saharan Africa.

Six leadership profiles are rated by cluster preferences (Ch 6.3, Table 6.7):

- *Charismatic*: high in Anglo, Germanic Europe, Latin America, Nordic Europe; low in Middle East.
- *Team-oriented*: high in Latin America; low in Middle East.
- *Participative*: high in Anglo, Germanic Europe, Nordic Europe, Sub-Saharan; low in Confucian Asia, Middle East.
- *Humane-oriented*: high in Anglo; medium-high in Confucian Asia.
- *Autonomous*: high in Germanic Europe; low in Latin America, Sub-Saharan.
- *Self-protective*: high in Confucian Asia, Middle East; low in Anglo, Germanic Europe, Nordic Europe.

Universally admired traits across cultures: trustworthy, dependable, intelligent, just, honest, decisive, plans ahead, effective bargainer, encouraging, win-win problem solver, positive, skilled administrator, dynamic, communicator, motivator, informed, confidence builder, team builder. Universally disliked traits: loner, egocentric, antisocial, ruthless, not cooperative, dictatorial, nonexplicit (Ch 6.3, Table 6.8).

### Cultural paradox, stereotyping, and social institutions

A *cultural paradox* occurs "where the insights from an understanding of culture may not necessarily coincide with reality" [V] (Ch 6.4, "Cultural Stereotyping and Social Institutions"). The example: American managers are seen as egalitarian but in practice make unilateral decisions; Japanese managers are seen as autocratic but make consensus-based decisions. The Suntory-Jim Beam acquisition illustrated the resulting confusion when a Jim Beam manager presented a proposal to a Japanese manager assuming individual authority, only to learn the decision had been made by consensus.

*Cultural stereotyping* "occurs when one assumes that all people within a culture act, think, and behave the same way" [V]. Recent research shows 80 per cent of variation in cultural values is *within* countries, not between them, suggesting other factors matter (Ch 6.4).

Three relevant *social institutions*: *social stratification* (degree to which social benefits are unequally distributed and patterns are perpetuated); *education* (socialising experiences preparing individuals to act in society); *religion* (Christianity 31 per cent, Islam 24.1 per cent, Hindu 15.1 per cent, Buddhist 6.9 per cent, Judaism 0.2 per cent of world population) [V] (Ch 6.4). Christianity's (especially Protestantism's) link to capitalism and entrepreneurship; Islam's prohibition of interest, requirement for prayer accommodation, and Ramadan considerations; Hinduism's caste system (illegal in modern India but socially active) and reverence for cows are presented as examples of how religion affects business (Ch 6.4).

### Cultural intelligence and cross-cultural training

*Cultural intelligence* refers to "individuals' capabilities to function and manage effectively in culturally diverse settings" [V] (Ch 6.5, "Cross-Cultural Assignments"). Four dimensions [BT]: *cognitive* (knowledge of values and practices); *meta-cognitive* (using cross-cultural knowledge to adapt); *motivational* (desire to learn new cultures); *behavioural* (exhibiting appropriate verbal/nonverbal behaviours).

*Low-rigor* training (lectures, books, case studies) is cost-effective but doesn't teach how to deal with differences. *High-rigor* training (classroom language training, sensitivity training, role-playing, simulations, field experiences, on-the-job training) is more expensive but more effective for longer/complex assignments. *Predeparture* training (ideally 3-5 weeks before assignment) prepares expatriates; *postarrival* training (8-12 weeks after arrival) addresses real-time issues; both have positive effects on adjustment to work, general environment, and interaction with locals [BT] (Ch 6.5).

### Three internationalisation strategies and entry modes

Three main strategies [AP] (Ch 6.6, "Strategies for Expanding Globally"):

- *Global strategy*: operations and activities managed similarly worldwide; works for homogeneous global products (Ford's "global electrification strategy", Canon, Coca-Cola, IBM, Intel, Sony — only nine Fortune Global 500 companies were truly global by sales distribution).
- *Regional strategy*: activities adapted to regional requirements (Toyota organises by region with regional offices independent of Japanese HQ).
- *Local strategy*: products adapted to specific countries (McDonald's in India: McAloo Tikki potato-and-pea burgers, McVeggie, McSpicy Paneer; Masala Grill chicken).

Entry modes (Ch 6.7):

- *Exporting*: cheapest entry; companies that export are 17 per cent more profitable than non-exporters [BT].
- *Licensing*: contractual right to use trademark/know-how/technology for a fee.
- *International franchising*: licensing the complete business model.
- *International strategic alliances*: joint business activities; key example: Nissan-Renault 1999 alliance (Nissan's North American presence and engineering; Renault's cash and design capability).
- *Foreign direct investment (FDI)*: most control, most capital intensive; political risk exposure (e.g., Venezuelan appropriation of US oil company assets).
- The *Uppsala model*: incremental internationalisation as firms learn — domestic base → exports to psychically close countries → exports to more distant ones → FDI. *Born globals* (e.g., M-PESA in Kenya, 2007) operate internationally from inception.

## Part VII: Entrepreneurship (Ch 7)

### Entrepreneurs vs small-business owners; types

*Entrepreneurs* are "people with vision, drive, and creativity, who are willing to take the risk of starting and managing a business to make a profit" [V] (Ch 7.1, "Entrepreneurship"). Small-business owners are "managers or people with technical expertise who started a business or bought an existing business and made a conscious decision to stay small" [V]. Bezos is an entrepreneur (built a new model, web-based book retailing); the proprietor of an independent bookstore is a small-business owner.

Three entrepreneur types: *classic* (risk-takers starting their own companies; subdivided into *micropreneurs* who start small and stay small, and *growth-oriented* entrepreneurs); *multipreneurs* (start a series of companies); *intrapreneurs* (apply creativity and risk-taking within a large corporation, with employer-provided seed funds and salary) [AP] (Ch 7.1).

### Nine entrepreneurial traits

Nine entrepreneurial personality characteristics [AP] (Ch 7.2, "Characteristics of Successful Entrepreneurs"): ambitious; independent; self-confident; risk-takers (preferring moderate risk where they control outcomes); visionary; creative; energetic; passionate; committed. Successful entrepreneurs also need managerial ability and technical knowledge.

### Small business: definitions, role, financing

US small businesses (under 500 employees, independently managed, locally based, not industry-dominant) employ about half the private-sector workforce, produce about half of US economic output, and added 1.4 million net new jobs in 2016 with 57.9 million employees (48 per cent of workforce) (Ch 7.3, Ch 7.8).

Women-owned firms grew 114 per cent 2007-2017 (vs 44 per cent overall), 2.5 times the national average; 11.6 million women-owned businesses generating $1.7 trillion revenue [V] (Ch 7.8). Minority-owned businesses increased 38 per cent. The Kauffman Foundation runs three index series: Startup Activity, Main Street Entrepreneurship, Growth Entrepreneurship (Ch 7.3).

### Starting up: business plan, financing, exporting

The first steps include self-assessment, finding the idea (80 per cent of Inc. 500 executives got their idea while working in the same/related industry), and writing a business plan (Ch 7.4). Financing options include debt and equity (venture capital, angel investors, SBA-backed loans). The Small Business Administration (SBA) "speaks on behalf of small business" and backed more than $25 billion in loans to nearly 68,000 small businesses in fiscal 2017, including $9 billion to minority-owned firms and $7.5 billion to women-owned businesses; more than 300 SBA-licensed Small Business Investment Companies (SBICs) provide about $6 billion annually in long-term financing (Ch 7.7). SCORE (Service Corps of Retired Executives) provides free management consulting (Ch 7.7).

Small businesses can export through Export Trading Companies (ETCs), Export Management Companies (EMCs, taking 5-15 per cent of gross sales), or directly. About 30 per cent of all US exporters in 2005 had 19 employees or less [BT] (Ch 7.5).

## Part VIII: Strategic analysis (Ch 8)

### SWOT and PESTEL

*Strategic analysis* is "the process that firms use to study and understand the many different layers and aspects of their competitive environment" [V] (Ch 8.1, "Gaining Advantages by Understanding the Competitive Environment"). *Environmental scanning* is "the systematic and intentional analysis of both a firm's internal state and its external, competitive environment" [V].

*SWOT* identifies *strengths* (firm capabilities and assets, e.g., Nike's sports marketing); *weaknesses* (gaps in capabilities, addressable through outsourcing such as Paychex); *opportunities* (external positive potentials, e.g., Daimler's Car2Go for urban non-car-owning millennials); *threats* (external risks). Limitations: SWOT identifies factors broadly but may overlook key issues (Ch 8.2).

*PESTEL* examines the *macro environment* — "elements that can impact the firm but are generally beyond its direct control" [V] — across six categories (Ch 8.3, "A Firm's External Macro Environment: PESTEL"):

- *Political*: taxation, tariffs, trade agreements, labour regulations, environmental regulations.
- *Economic*: unemployment rates, interest rates, exchange rates, oil prices, inflation, recession.
- *Sociocultural*: demographics (income, education, age, ethnicity), values, fashion trends, environmental awareness, fitness, social media.
- *Technological*: Internet, social media, automation, MRP systems, GPS, big data.
- *Environmental*: natural resource scarcity, pollution, climate change. Example: London, Barcelona, Paris bans on internal-combustion engines.
- *Legal*: licensing fees, intellectual property, patents, online piracy.

### Porter's Five Forces

*Porter's Five Forces* model (Ch 8.4, "A Firm's Micro Environment: Porter's Five Forces") evaluates the *micro environment* of an industry through five interconnected forces [BT]:

- *Industry rivalry*: how hard firms must fight for customers; strong rivalry reduces profit potential.
- *Threat of new entrants*: incumbent firms may face new competitors entering; *barriers to entry* include cost, brand loyalty, industry growth (high barriers in airlines).
- *Threat of substitutes*: products from other industries that satisfy the same need (grocery stores substitute for fast food).
- *Supplier power*: balance in supplier relationships; high when suppliers offer specialised products or control rare resources (Sony's PlayStation chip supplier); low for commodity inputs.
- *Buyer power*: balance with customers; *switching costs* (financial or practical) protect firms from buyer power (iPhone ecosystem lock-in).

### VRIO and the value chain

The *value chain* is "the progression of activities [a firm] undertakes to create a product or service that consumers will pay for" [V] (Ch 8.5, "The Internal Environment"). Walmart's value chain example shows procurement, inbound logistics, IT, and merchandising adding value through specific activities. *Primary activities* directly create/sell/service the product; *support activities* (accounting, HR) sustain the firm without directly creating output (Ch 8.5).

*VRIO* evaluates resources and capabilities [BT] (Ch 8.5, "Using Resources and Capabilities to Build an Advantage over Rivals"):

- *Value*: does the resource/capability help create value?
- *Rarity*: is it rare?
- *Imitation*: is it difficult to imitate?
- *Organization*: is the firm organised to capture its value?

If all four are yes, the resource/capability is a sustained competitive advantage. Starbucks's brand and customer service pass VRIO; "thousands of locations worldwide" passes value but fails rarity (McDonald's and Subway also have many locations) (Ch 8.5).

### Generic competitive strategies and strategic positioning

A *competitive advantage* exists when a firm "successfully attracts more customers, earns more profit, or returns more value to its shareholders than rival firms do" [V] (Ch 8.6, "Competition, Strategy, and Competitive Advantage").

Porter's three generic business-level strategies [BT] (Ch 8.6):

- *Cost leadership*: tightly control costs throughout the value chain to offer low prices (Walmart).
- *Differentiation*: add value to charge premium prices through innovation, customer service, brand (Starbucks).
- *Focus*: target a smaller demographic or geographic market with either cost-leadership or differentiation (Flux's custom snowboard bindings).

"Trying to combine [cost leadership and differentiation], Porter suggests, can lead to a firm being stuck in the middle" [V] (Ch 8.6).

*Strategic groups* are firms in the same industry following similar generic strategies; rivalry is fiercest within strategic groups (Walmart-Target compete more than Walmart-Neiman Marcus). *Strategic positioning* combines understanding of competitive environment, resources, capabilities, generic strategy, target market, and geography to stake out a unique, hard-to-imitate position (Ch 8.7).

## Part IX: The strategic management process (Ch 9)

### Strategic management cycle, vision, and mission

The *strategic management process* is "the set of activities that firm managers undertake in order to try to put their firms in the best possible position to compete successfully in the marketplace" [V] (Ch 9.1, "Strategic Management"). The strategy cycle (Exhibit 9.3) integrates: vision and mission → strategic analysis → developing objectives → creating, choosing, implementing strategy → measuring and evaluating performance — with all activities running simultaneously, not strictly sequentially (Ch 9.1).

A *vision statement* is "an expression of what a business's founders want that business to accomplish" [V] (Ch 9.2, "Firm Vision and Mission") — broad, aspirational, often does not mention specific products. A *mission statement* "takes the why of a vision statement and gives a broad description of how the firm will try to make its vision a reality" [V] — describing products, target markets, but not exact strategies (Ch 9.2).

### Strategic objectives, levels of strategy, grand strategies

*Strategic objectives* are "the big-picture goals for the company: they describe what the company will do to try to fulfill its mission" [V] (Ch 9.4, "Strategic Objectives and Levels of Strategy"). Three levels of strategy [AP]:

- *Business-level strategy*: framework for organising activities for a single product/business unit; cost leadership or differentiation.
- *Corporate strategy*: managing a portfolio of businesses; uses the *BCG Matrix* (stars, cash cows, dogs, question marks) to evaluate business units.
- *International strategy*: managing operations across countries.

Three grand strategies [AP]:

- *Growth*: develop plans to increase size in revenue, market share, or geographic reach (Walmart acquisitions: Shoebuy, Jet, ModCloth, Flipkart, Parcel).
- *Stability*: maintain current income, market share, or geographic reach.
- *Defensive*: shrink operations, restructure, or rethink offerings under challenge (Macy's brick-and-mortar reductions; Ford ceasing US sedan sales).

### SMART goals and the planning cycle

Good goals follow the *SMART framework*: **s**pecific, **m**easurable, **a**chievable, **r**elevant, **t**ime-bound (Ch 9.5, "Planning Firm Actions to Implement Strategies"). Time horizons divide into *short-term strategic plans* (one year or less) and *long-term strategic plans* (over a year), with multi-year mega-projects like Tesla's Gigafactory illustrating long-term planning.

Planning levels [AP] (Ch 9.5, "Scale Levels of Planning"):

- *Strategic planning*: high-level, by executives, connects to vision/mission.
- *Tactical planning*: mid-level, by division managers, broad ideas to pursue mission.
- *Operational planning*: front-line, daily/short-term actions by employees.

### Performance measurement and benchmarking

*Performance measurement* "comes in many forms, from financial reports to quality measures like defect rates" [V] (Ch 9.6, "Measuring and Evaluating Strategic Performance"). *Benchmarking* uses another firm's superior performance as the standard (Disney for theme park customer satisfaction). Performance evaluation closes the strategy cycle by feeding back into strategic analysis.

## Part X: Organisational structure and change (Ch 10)

### Formal and informal organisation; bureaucratic principles

*Formal organisation* is "an officially defined set of relationships, responsibilities, and connections that exist across an organization" [V] (Ch 10.1, "Organizational Structures and Design"). *Informal organisation* is "the invisible network of interpersonal relationships that shape how people actually connect with one another to carry out their activities" — emergent, complex, impossible to control [V].

Weber's bureaucratic model rests on five elements [BT] (Ch 10.1, "Bureaucracy"):

- *Specialization*: degree of subunit organisation by expertise.
- *Command-and-control*: reporting and oversight structure.
- *Span of control*: scope of any one person's accountability.
- *Centralization*: concentration of resources and decision authority.
- *Formalization*: degree of role definition.

These principles position organisations on a continuum between *mechanistic bureaucratic structure* (highly formalised, narrow specialisation, centralised, functional, vertical/tall) and *organic bureaucratic structure* (low standardisation, broad specialisation, decentralised, divisional, horizontal/flat) (Ch 10.1, Table 10.1).

### Organisational change, life cycle, and dimensions

Three change types: *structural*, *technological*, *culture* — with culture change "among the most difficult kinds of changes to create within an organizational system. It often involves reshaping and reimagining the core identity of the organization" [V] (Ch 10.2, "Organizational Change").

Four organisational life cycle phases [AP] (Ch 10.2, "The Organizational Life Cycle"):

- *Entrepreneurship*: small, agile, organic, informal.
- *Survival and early success*: scaling, specialisation, formal structures emerge.
- *Sustained success/maturity*: deepening hierarchy, lower-level managers given responsibility, mechanistic structures with functional units.
- *Renewal or decline*: far-flung operations, autonomous subunits, restructuring needed.

Three dimensions of change (Ch 10.2, "Dimensions of Change"):

- *Scope*: incremental (LEAN, Six Sigma small refinements) vs transformational (significant disruption).
- *Level*: individual / group / organisation.
- *Intentionality*: planned (deliberate) vs unplanned (emergent, byproduct of planned change or spontaneous).

### Four common change models

Four change methodology models with different assumptions [BT] (Ch 10.3, "Managing Change"):

- *Lewin's three-stage model*: unfreeze → change → refreeze. Best for stable contexts; assumes stability returns after change.
- *Kotter's eight-step model*: establish urgency ("burning platform") → form guiding coalition → create vision of change → communicate vision → remove obstacles → create small wins → consolidate improvements → anchor changes. Mechanistic, top-down, deficit-based; high-control situations.
- *Cooperrider's Appreciative Inquiry (AI)* 5-D cycle: define → discover ("who are we when we are at our best?") → dream ("what could be?") → design (prioritising ideas) → destiny (implementation). Abundance-based, bottom-up, positive; uses appreciative conversations to shift dominant narratives. Examples: British Airways turning a baggage-claim problem into excellent customer service exploration; Avon turning a sexual harassment problem into employee engagement exploration (Ch 10.3).
- *Olson and Eoyang Complex Adaptive Systems (CAS)* model: organisations are emergent, self-organising. Three influence levers: empower change agents; strengthen connections; ensure information and energy flow through connections. Self-organising structures can be altered through *boundary conditions*, *disturbances*, and connection-and-flow attention. Example: Open Space Technology (Ch 10.3, "Complex Adaptive Systems").

The text argues the choice of model depends on three questions: (1) Is the organisation in deficit or abundance? (2) Top-down or bottom-up mechanism? (3) Conventional (resistance assumed) or appreciative mindset (people embrace change when respected)? "The more complex the potential change, the greater the need to involve employees in the process of planning and implementing change" [V] (Ch 10.3).

## Part XI: Human resource management (Ch 11)

### Four waves of HR work

Ulrich, Younger, Brockbank, and Younger's four-wave evolution of HR [BT] (Ch 11.1, "An Introduction to Human Resource Management"):

- Wave 1: administrative work (terms and conditions, HR services, regulatory compliance).
- Wave 2: design of innovative HR practices (compensation, learning, communication, sourcing).
- Wave 3: integration of HR strategy with business strategy.
- Wave 4: HR as a competitive practice responding to external customer, investor, and community demands.

### HR compliance

Major US laws governing the employer-employee relationship [BT] (Ch 11.2):

- *Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA, 1967)*: protects ages 40+.
- *Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA, 1990)*: prohibits disability discrimination; requires reasonable accommodation.
- *Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA, 1938)*: minimum wage, overtime, recordkeeping, youth employment.
- *Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA, 1993)*: 12 weeks unpaid leave for specified reasons.
- *National Labor Relations Act (NLRA, 1935)*: rights to organise and bargain collectively.
- *Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act (WARN, 1988)*: 60 days advance notice for plant closings/mass layoffs (employers with 100+ employees).

Best practices: education and training; documentation (employee handbook); regular HR compliance audits.

### Performance management evolution

Performance management traces back to the WWI US military "merit rating" system; by 1960s, 90 per cent of US companies used performance appraisals [BT] (Ch 11.3, "Performance Management"). Welch's GE forced-ranking system (A players rewarded, C players dismissed) was widely imitated until "GE backed away from the practice as well. Companies, GE included, saw that it negatively fostered internal competition and undermined collaboration and teamwork" [V] (Ch 11.3).

Deloitte's survey: "58% of executives surveyed believed that their current performance management approach drives neither employee engagement nor high performance" [V] (Ch 11.3). Deloitte's "radical redesign" replaces ratings with four forward-looking questions: highest possible compensation increase; always want this person on the team; at risk for low performance; ready for promotion today.

Five performance management challenges (Ch 11.3): aligning individual and company goals; rewarding performance (pay-for-performance); identifying poor performers; avoiding legal troubles; managing the feedback firehose (apps from GE, IBM).

### Pay-for-performance and total rewards

The 2011 SHRM study found four drivers for moving to pay-for-performance: "Recognize and reward high performers (46.9%); Increase the likelihood of achieving corporate goals (32.5%); Improve productivity (7.8%); Move away from an entitlement culture (7.8%)" [V] (Ch 11.4, "Influencing Employee Performance and Motivation"). The 2015 World at Work report: 72 per cent of companies tie pay increases to performance; 67 per cent give top performers raises at least 1.5 times those of average performers (Ch 11.4).

WorldatWork's six total rewards elements [V] (Ch 11.4): compensation; benefits; work-life effectiveness; recognition; performance management; talent development.

The Nohria-Groysberg-Lee four-drives model maps drives to organisational levers (Ch 11.4, Table 11.2) [BT]:

- *Drive to acquire* → reward systems → differentiate performance, link to rewards, pay competitively.
- *Drive to bond* → culture → mutual reliance, friendships, collaboration, teamwork.
- *Drive to comprehend* → job design → distinct, important, meaningful roles.
- *Drive to defend* → performance management and resource-allocation processes → transparency, fairness, trust.

### Talent acquisition and succession planning

The Fernandez-Araoz/Groysberg/Nohria seven-step recruiting framework [BT] (Ch 11.5, "Building an Organization for the Future"): anticipate future leadership needs; specify the job (job-based, team-based, firm-based competencies); develop the pool; assess candidates (behavioural event interview); close the deal; integrate the newcomer.

The 9-box talent review template plots *performance* (current performance management rating, low/medium/high) against *potential* — defined as "an employee's ability to demonstrate the behaviors necessary to be successful at the next highest level within the company" [V] (Ch 11.6, "Talent Development and Succession Planning"). Higher-potential employees display: business acumen, strategic thinking, leadership skills, people skills, learning agility, technology skills.

The talent review *calibration process* (Effron and Ort) brings managers together to discuss each direct report, presenting performance × potential ratings and reaching consensus. Outputs: identification of high-potential employees, development action plans, talent gap insights, succession planning input.

Succession planning identifies short-term (<1 year), mid-term (1-3 years), and long-term (3+ years) successor candidates per role; "EXTERNAL" is recorded if no internal candidate exists. Development tools: training; stretch assignments; individual assessments (including 360 assessments — feedback from manager, peers, direct reports, customers); individual development plans (Ch 11.6).

## Part XII: Diversity in organisations (Ch 12)

### Three kinds of workplace diversity

The text distinguishes *surface-level diversity* (visible: age, body size, visible disabilities, race, sex), *deep-level diversity* (non-observable: attitudes, values, beliefs), and *hidden diversity* (deep-level traits that may be concealed or revealed at the individual's discretion, called *invisible social identities*) [V] (Ch 12.1, "An Introduction to Workplace Diversity"). Hidden diversity may include sexual orientation, hidden disability, mixed racial heritage, or socioeconomic status.

### Workforce demographics

Workforce composition (2016): 17 per cent Hispanic/Latino, 12 per cent Black, 6 per cent Asian, 78 per cent White; 47 per cent women, 53 per cent men [V] (Ch 12.2, "Diversity and the Workforce"). Projected 2014-2024 changes: White non-Hispanic share -3 per cent; Black +10.1 per cent; Hispanic/Latino +28 per cent; Asian +23.2 per cent; Other +22.2 per cent (Ch 12.2).

The *glass ceiling* is "an invisible barrier based on the prejudicial beliefs that underlie organizational decisions that prevent women from moving beyond certain levels within a company" [V] (Ch 12.2). The *Equal Pay Act of 1963* attempts to address pay inequities; women still earn about 80 cents per dollar earned by men in similar work (Ch 12.4).

The *model minority myth* portrays Asian men/women as obedient and successful, contrasted against stereotypes of other minority groups, justifying socioeconomic disparities and limiting Asian/Asian-American leadership opportunities [V] (Ch 12.2). LGBTQ employees face the choice between *passing* (not disclosing invisible social identity) and *revealing*; both carry risks and trade-offs (Ch 12.2).

### Cox-Blake business case for diversity

Six potential competitive advantages [BT] (Ch 12.3, "Diversity and Its Impact on Companies"): cost advantages (reduced lawsuits, lower turnover among women and minorities); resource acquisition (larger labour pool); marketing (broader perspectives on consumer preferences); system flexibility (cognitive flexibility transfers to organisational adaptability); creativity and problem-solving (multiple perspectives reduce groupthink).

The *resource-based view* grounds the diversity argument: diverse workforces are valuable, rare, hard to imitate, non-substitutable — all four VRIO criteria — and thus a sustained competitive advantage [BT] (Ch 12.3). The *strategic human resource management (SHRM)* framework integrates HR vertically with business strategy and horizontally across functional areas to engage employees as a competitive resource.

### Five key diversity theories

Five competing theoretical accounts (Ch 12.5, "Key Diversity Theories"):

- *Cognitive diversity hypothesis*: "multiple perspectives stemming from the cultural differences between group or organizational members result in creative problem solving and innovation" [V]. Watson study: homogeneous groups outperformed heterogeneous groups initially, but heterogeneous groups outperformed on range of perspectives and number of alternatives over 17 weeks (Ch 12.5).
- *Similarity-attraction paradigm*: "individuals are attracted to others with whom they share attitude similarity" [V] — predicts negative diversity outcomes.
- *Social cognitive theory*: people use categorisation/stereotyping to simplify large amounts of information.
- *Social identity theory*: in-group/out-group categorisation produces in-group favouritism, with out-group members seen as homogeneous.
- *Schema theory*: demographic-based encoding shapes evaluation of newly encountered people.
- *Justification-suppression model*: prejudice operates in two stages — feeling it, then suppressing or acting on it depending on conditions (anonymity, fatigue, weak social norms).

### Three perspectives on workplace diversity (Ely-Thomas)

The Ely-Thomas paradigm names three orientations [BT] (Ch 12.6, "Three Perspectives on Workplace Diversity"):

- *Integration-and-learning perspective*: cultural identity is a resource for shared work; strongest fit with firm success.
- *Access-and-legitimacy perspective*: instrumentalises diversity for market access; risks limiting minority members to demographic-matched roles.
- *Discrimination-and-fairness perspective*: treats diversity as moral duty without linking to productivity; can implicitly demand assimilation.

### Recommendations for managing diversity

*Highly structured interviews* with 15 specific characteristics reduce similarity bias [V, list verbatim from source] (Ch 12.7, "Interview Selection Process"): job analysis; same questions; limited prompting; better questions; longer interviews; control of ancillary information; limited questions from candidates; multiple rating scales; anchored rating scales; detailed notes; multiple interviewers; consistent interviewers; no discussion between interviews; training; statistical prediction.

*Diversified mentoring relationships* — where mentor and mentee differ in status (race, gender, class, disability, sexual orientation) — produce mutual benefits in knowledge, empathy, and skills (Ch 12.7).

## Part XIII: Leadership (Ch 13)

### Leadership defined; leader vs manager

*Leadership* is "frequently defined as a social (interpersonal) influence relationship between two or more persons who depend on each other to attain certain mutual goals in a group situation" [V] (Ch 13.1, "The Nature of Leadership"). Leadership and management differ on definition (leadership is a relationship; management is a process of planning, organising, directing, controlling), origin (managers appointed; leaders accepted by followers), power source (managers from organisational position; leaders from followers' perception of expertise, attractiveness, working relationship), and motivation type (management mostly extrinsic; leadership mostly intrinsic) (Ch 13.1, "Leader versus Manager"). Grace Hopper's distinction is reproduced: "You don't manage people, you manage things. You lead people" [V] (Ch 13.3, "Paths to Leadership").

### The leadership process: leader, follower, context, consequences

Five components of the leadership working relationship [AR] (Ch 13.2, "The Leadership Process"): leader; follower; context (situation); leadership process per se; consequences.

The *follower* is "the most critical factor in any leadership event" [V, citing Hollander]. Follower personality and readiness shape effective leadership style: internal locus of control responds to participative styles; authoritarian personality to directive acts; high self-esteem to participative leadership; low self-esteem to supportive leadership; low readiness to directive leadership.

The *leader-member exchange (LMX)* theory frames leadership as dyadic relationships of varying quality. High-quality exchange relationships produce in-group members with higher performance, commitment, and satisfaction; low-quality produces out-group dynamics [BT] (Ch 13.2, "The Consequences").

### Leader emergence, French-Raven, and influence styles

Leaders emerge through dynamics including the *idiosyncrasy credits* concept — competency-based status earned through contributions [BT, citing Hollander, Salancik & Pfeffer] (Ch 13.3, "Leader Emergence").

French and Raven's five bases of power [BT] (Ch 13.3, "Leadership as an Exercise of Influence"):

- *Reward power*: people believe the holder can bestow desired outcomes.
- *Coercive power*: people believe the holder can punish.
- *Referent power*: people want to associate with or be accepted by the holder; close cousin of charismatic power.
- *Expert power*: people believe the holder has and shares needed expertise.
- *Legitimate power*: people believe the holder has the right to influence and ought to be obeyed; can derive from tradition, charisma, laws, institutional roles, moralistic appeal, rationality.

Different powers produce different responses (Ch 13.3, Exhibit 13.5): coercive power often alienates; reward power produces measured, tit-for-tat thinking; referent power produces identification; rationality, expertise, and moralistic appeal produce commitment and internalisation. "Research suggests that rationality is the most effective influence tactic in terms of its impact on follower commitment, motivation, performance, satisfaction, and group effectiveness" [V] (Ch 13.3).

The Tannenbaum-Schmidt continuum (1950s) ranges from authoritarian (boss-centred) to free-rein (member-determined) leadership, with participative in between [BT] (Ch 13.3). McGregor's *Theory X* (people dislike work, lack self-direction, require control) versus *Theory Y* (people have creative capacity, can self-direct, want to participate) sets the philosophical anchor [BT]. A study of 3,600 managers from 14 countries found most held Theory-X assumptions even when publicly endorsing participatory management (Ch 13.3, "Theory X and Theory Y Leaders").

The Muczyk-Reimann four leadership styles cross power retention with implementation discretion [BT] (Ch 13.3, "Directive/Permissive Leadership Styles"): *directive autocrat* (retains power, supervises closely); *permissive autocrat* (retains decision power, lets workers execute with discretion); *directive democrat* (participative decisions, directive on execution); *permissive democrat* (participative on both decisions and execution).

### Trait approach: Locke's core list

Locke's core trait list for effective leaders [BT] (Ch 13.4, "Leader Trait Research"):

- *Drive*: high effort, achievement orientation, ambition, energy, tenacity, initiative.
- *Leadership motivation*: intense desire to lead.
- *Honesty and integrity*: commitment to truth.
- *Self-confidence*: assurance in self, ideas, and capability.
- *Cognitive ability*: conceptual skill, judgment, analytical capacity.
- *Knowledge of the business*: industry and technical understanding.
- *Other*: charisma, creativity, flexibility.

Sex and gender role: "men emerge as leaders more frequently than women, but argues 'gender role is more predictive than sex'" [AP] — individuals with masculine characteristics (assertive, aggressive, competitive) emerge in leadership roles regardless of sex; androgynous individuals emerge as readily as those with only masculine characteristics [BT] (Ch 13.4, "Sex and Gender Role"). Style differences: men slightly more autocratic/directive, women slightly more democratic/participative; no major task-vs-relationship orientation difference (Ch 13.4).

### Behavioural approaches: Ohio State, Michigan, Blake-Mouton

The Ohio State studies identified two primary behaviour dimensions [BT, Stogdill] (Ch 13.5, "Behavioral Approaches to Leadership"): *consideration* (relationship-oriented behaviours: support, friendliness, openness, recognising ideas) and *initiating structure* (task-oriented behaviours: scheduling, deciding, providing direction, planning, coordinating, problem-solving, maintaining standards). The Michigan studies identified job-centred and organisational-member-centred behaviours, corresponding closely to initiating structure and consideration. The Blake-Mouton Leadership Grid® positions leadership styles on concern-for-results × concern-for-people; the 9,9 (high-high) style has been advocated as universally best, but the text notes there is no universally effective style [AP] (Ch 13.5).

### Situational approaches: Fiedler and Path-Goal

Fiedler's *contingency theory of leadership* assesses the leader through the *least-preferred coworker (LPC)* score (high LPC = relationship-oriented; low LPC = task-oriented) and the situation through three factors in order of importance: leader-member relations, task structure, position power [BT] (Ch 13.6, "Situational (Contingency) Approaches to Leadership"). High-LPC (relationship-oriented) leaders are most effective in moderate-favourability situations; low-LPC (task-oriented) leaders are most effective in highly favourable or highly unfavourable situations.

The House-Evans *path-goal theory* posits that effective leaders provide a path to valued goals by clarifying it, reducing roadblocks, and increasing personal-satisfaction opportunities [BT] (Ch 13.6, "Path-Goal Theory"). Four leader behaviours, each suited to different situations: *supportive* (boring/stressful tasks; low self-esteem followers); *directive* (high role ambiguity); *participative* (unstructured tasks); *achievement-oriented* (capitalises on need for achievement). The cross-cultural caveat: empowerment effects vary — negative for job satisfaction in India, positive in US/Poland/Mexico [BT] (Ch 13.6, "Cross-Cultural Context").

### Substitutes for leadership; transformational and charismatic leadership

Substitutes for and neutralisers of leader behaviour exist in subordinate characteristics (experience, professional orientation, indifference to organisational rewards), task characteristics (structured/routine tasks, task-provided feedback, intrinsically satisfying tasks), and organisation characteristics (cohesive work group, low position power, formalisation, inflexibility, leader spatially separated) [BT] (Ch 13.7, "Substitutes for and Neutralizers of Leadership", Table 13.1).

*Transformational leaders* "inspire others to action through their personal values, vision, passion, and belief in and commitment to the mission" [V] (Ch 13.8, "Transformational, Visionary, and Charismatic Leadership"). They use idealised influence (charisma), individualised consideration, intellectual stimulation, and inspirational motivation. *Visionary leaders* "influence others through an emotional and/or intellectual attraction to the leader's dreams of what 'can be'" [V]. *Charismatic leaders* possess "legitimate power that arises from 'exceptional sanctity, heroism, or exemplary character'" [V, citing Weber]. Charismatic leadership has produced both Iacocca-style turnarounds and David Koresh / Waco disasters.

## Part XIV: Work motivation for performance (Ch 14)

### Motivation defined; ability, role perceptions, environment

Performance is a function of ability, motivation, role perceptions, and the performance environment; deficiencies in any one cannot be compensated by strengthening another [AR] (Ch 14.1, "Motivation: Direction and Intensity"). *Motivation* is "a force within or outside of the body that energizes, directs, and sustains human behavior" [V], with two components: *direction* (what to achieve) and *intensity* (effort exerted).

### Content theories

Five content theories presented [BT] (Ch 14.2, "Content Theories of Motivation"):

- **Manifest needs theory** (Murray): humans have many needs but only a few are *manifest* at a given time; *latent* needs may exist without being expressed in current behaviour. Murray condensed the 6,000+ identified human needs into a manageable set of *primary* (instinctive: food, water, sex, urination) and *secondary* (learned: achievement, love, affiliation, power) needs.
- **Learned needs theory** (McClelland): three needs — achievement (nAch), affiliation (nAff), power (nPow) — are learned, especially in childhood. High-nAch individuals "feel personally responsible for completing whatever tasks they are assigned"; prefer "moderate" probability-of-success situations; have "very strong desires for feedback about how well they are doing" [V] (Ch 14.2). McClelland argues that "the high need for social power is the most important motivator for successful managers" [V] (Ch 14.2, "Need for Power"). Two faces of power: *personal power seekers* dominate others for self; *social power seekers* influence others to achieve group goals.
- **Maslow's hierarchy of needs**: physiological, safety/security, social, ego/esteem, self-actualization, with prepotency (lower needs satisfied before higher ones become motivating). Self-actualization: "a desire for self-fulfillment, 'to become more and more what one is, to become everything that one is capable of becoming'" [V] (Ch 14.2). The text notes that research does not support the strict hierarchical sequence, "but, on the other hand, there is some evidence that people satisfy the lower-order needs before they attempt to satisfy higher-order needs" [V] (Ch 14.2).
- **Alderfer's ERG theory**: existence, relatedness, growth, with *frustration regression* (when growth needs are blocked, attention shifts back to relatedness). Four components: satisfaction progression; frustration; frustration regression; aspiration (the more we satisfy growth, the more important it becomes). ERG has stronger empirical support than Maslow's hierarchy [BT] (Ch 14.2, "Alderfer's ERG Theory").
- **Herzberg's motivator-hygiene theory**: hygiene factors (pay, conditions, supervision, security) prevent dissatisfaction but do not motivate; motivators (achievement, recognition, work itself, growth) drive satisfaction. Satisfaction and dissatisfaction are on different scales [AR] (Ch 14.2, "Herzberg's Motivator-Hygiene Theory").

### Self-determination theory

Self-determination theory (SDT) distinguishes *intrinsic motivation* (performing an activity for its inherent satisfaction) from *extrinsic motivation* (performing for a valued outcome). The signature SDT claim: "as the level of extrinsic rewards increases, the amount of intrinsic motivation decreases" [V] (Ch 14.2, "Self-Determination Theory"). Tasks are intrinsically motivating when they satisfy needs for *competence*, *autonomy*, and *relatedness*. SDT implication: for uninteresting jobs, make extrinsic rewards contingent on performance; for interesting jobs, focus on opportunities for competence, autonomy, and relatedness rather than extrinsic rewards.

### Process theories

Four process theories [BT] (Ch 14.3, "Process Theories of Motivation"):

- **Operant conditioning theory**: learning from consequences. Three ways to make response more likely: positive reinforcement (desirable consequence), negative reinforcement (removal of unpleasant condition), avoidance learning (learn to avoid undesired consequence). Two ways to make less likely: nonreinforcement, punishment. Schedules: continuous (good for shaping new behaviours but quick extinction); fixed ratio (high stable output); variable ratio (very high output, slow extinction); fixed interval (irregular output, quick extinction); variable interval (moderately high stable output). "In general, continuous reinforcement is best while employees are learning their jobs or new duties. After that, variable-ratio reinforcement schedules are superior" [V] (Ch 14.3).

- **Equity theory** (Adams): motivation depends on comparison of one's outcome-input ratio to a *referent other's*. Three states: equity, *overreward inequity*, *underreward inequity*. Underreward responses: alter own inputs, alter own outcomes, alter referent's inputs/outcomes, distort perceptions, change referent, leave the situation [BT]. Two human tendencies amplify perceived inequity: people overrate their own performance (one author study: average self-rating was 6.2 of 7, where average should be ~4); people overrate others' outcomes [BT] (Ch 14.3, "The Basic Equity Model"). Recent equity research has expanded into *organisational justice*: distributive justice (fairness of outcomes); procedural justice (fairness of process). "Layoffs based on seniority may be perceived as more fair than layoffs based on supervisors' opinions" [AP] (Ch 14.4).

- **Goal theory** (Locke): difficult, specific, accepted goals produce better performance than easy or vague goals. Goal acceptance ("they need to feel that it is also their goal") and *goal commitment* (degree of dedication to a specific goal among many) are critical. Participation in goal-setting tends to increase commitment [BT] (Ch 14.3, "Goal Theory"). Hazards: goal conflict, narrow goals neglecting other duties, unethical use to set impossible goals, over-emphasis on quantifiable measures, individual goals harming teamwork.

- **Expectancy theory** (Vroom, Lawler): motivation = E1 (effort-performance expectancy) × E2 (performance-outcome expectancy) × valence. The integrative claim: "to maximize motivation, organizations must make outcomes contingent on performance" [V] (Ch 14.3, "Implications of Expectancy Theory"). *Self-efficacy* (Bandura) is a strong determinant of E1: "high self-efficacy employees believe that they are likely to succeed at most or all of their job duties" [AP]. Weak E1s arise from insufficient resources or inaccurate performance ratings; weak E2s arise from equality-focused organisations that do not differentiate rewards based on performance. Recent research finds "the components of expectancy theory predict performance just as well, without being multiplied" [V] (Ch 14.4).

## Part XV: Managing teams (Ch 15)

### Team vs working group; team effectiveness

Katzenbach and Smith's HBR definition of a team — "people organized to function cooperatively as a group" — has five elements [V] (Ch 15.1, "Teamwork in the Workplace"):

- Common commitment and purpose.
- Specific performance goals.
- Complementary skills.
- Commitment to how the work gets done.
- Mutual accountability.

A team differs from a *working group*: teams have shared leadership, individual *and* mutual accountabilities, and measure collective work products; working groups have a focused leader, individual accountabilities, and a group purpose mirroring broader organisational mission (Ch 15.1).

Eight practices for effective teams [BT, Katzenbach and Smith] (Ch 15.1):

- Establish urgency, demanding performance standards, and direction.
- Select members for skill and skill potential, not personality.
- Pay particular attention to first meetings and actions.
- Set some clear rules of behaviour.
- Set and seize upon a few immediate performance-oriented tasks and goals.
- Challenge the group regularly with fresh facts and information.
- Spend lots of time together.
- Exploit positive feedback.

### Team development: Tuckman's stages and the performance curve

Tuckman's stages of group development (1965; adjourning added 1977) [BT] (Ch 15.2, "Team Development Over Time"):

- *Forming*: polite stage; introductions; focus on similarities; informal pecking order develops.
- *Storming*: members vie for leadership; win-lose stage; people choose sides; frustration around goals, tasks, progress.
- *Norming*: working together emerges; group goals accepted; ground rules established; mutual respect.
- *Performing*: self-directed; little management direction needed; congruence of vision, team, self; high-performing teams maximise both task and relational performance.

The Katzenbach-Smith *team performance curve* tracks the journey from working group to high-performing team. Team development is non-linear: adding/changing members, scope changes, or new tasks can cause regression to earlier stages (Ch 15.2).

### Managing teams as paradox

Linda Hill's framing — managing a team means managing paradox [BT] (Ch 15.3, "Things to Consider When Managing Teams"):

- Individual *and* collective identities and goals.
- Fostering both support *and* confrontation among team members.
- Balancing performance *and* learning.
- Balancing managerial authority *and* team member discretion.
- Balancing the manager-team-individual relationship triangle.

A core managerial responsibility is *managing the team's boundaries* — buffering external pressure while integrating useful external forces. "A strong team manager needs [to] manage these 'boundaries' with the organization to help the team navigate through and with the organizational complexities, goals, nuances, and egos that are a part of any organization" [V] (Ch 15.3).

### Lencioni's Five Dysfunctions

Patrick Lencioni's five dysfunctions of a team [BT] (Ch 15.4, "Opportunities and Challenges to Team Building"):

1. Absence of trust (unwillingness to be vulnerable).
2. Fear of conflict.
3. Lack of commitment.
4. Avoidance of accountability.
5. Inattention to results.

"The first dysfunction is an absence of trust among team members. Essentially, this stems from their unwillingness to be vulnerable within the group. … This failure to build trust is damaging because it sets the tone for the second dysfunction: fear of conflict" [V] (Ch 15.4).

Two techniques to surface conflict productively: *mining* — "extracts buried disagreements within the team and sheds the light of day on them" [V]; *real-time permission* — "recognize when the people engaged in conflict are becoming uncomfortable with the level of discord, and then interrupt to remind them that what they are doing is necessary" [V] (Ch 15.4).

The Capobianco-Davis-Kraus 2×2 of conflict responses identifies active vs passive and constructive vs destructive responses [BT] (Ch 15.4, Exhibit 15.7): active-constructive (perspective taking, creating solutions, expressing emotions, reaching out); passive-constructive (reflective thinking, delay responding, adapting); active-destructive and passive-destructive responses are catalogued as well.

### Team diversity

The 2015 McKinsey report on 366 public companies: "those in the top quartile for ethnic and racial diversity in management were 35% more likely to have financial returns above their industry mean, and those in the top quartile for gender diversity were 15% more likely to have returns above the industry mean" [V] (Ch 15.5, "Team Diversity"). The Credit Suisse global analysis: organisations with at least one female board member had higher ROE and higher net income growth than those without.

The Journal of Personality and Social Psychology study: people from diverse backgrounds "might actually alter the behavior of a group's social majority in ways that lead to improved and more accurate group thinking" [V]. Diverse panels raised more facts, made fewer factual errors, were "more likely to constantly reexamine facts and remain objective", and prevented groupthink [V] (Ch 15.5).

The BCG-TUM study: a positive, statistically significant relationship between management diversity and innovation; gender diversity boosts innovation only when "more than 20% women in management positions"; tokenism (low percentages) does not produce the diversity benefit [BT] (Ch 15.5).

### Multicultural teams: four conflict sources, four interventions, three CQ sources

Four common conflict sources in multicultural teams [BT] (Ch 15.6, "Multicultural Teams"): direct vs indirect communication; trouble with accents and fluency; differing attitudes toward hierarchy; conflicting decision-making norms.

Four interventions: *adaptation* (work with or around differences); *structural intervention* (reorganise to reduce friction); *managerial intervention* (decisions made by management without team involvement, used sparingly); *exit* (last resort) (Ch 15.6).

*Cultural intelligence* develops through three sources [V] (Ch 15.6, "Cultural Intelligence"): *head* (cognitive: training-based knowledge of beliefs, customs, taboos); *body* (physical: demeanor, eye contact, posture, accent — physical experimentation with new culture); *heart* (emotional/motivational: confidence and motivation to adapt). Best practices for cross-cultural skills: broaden your mind; develop skills through practice; boost cultural metacognition; develop cognitive complexity (Ch 15.6).

## Part XVI: Managerial communication (Ch 16)

### The communication model and feedback types

The basic model — communicator encodes a message, receiver decodes it — names *encoding* (translating ideas into symbols), *decoding* (interpreting), and *noise* (factors distorting clarity) (Ch 16.1, "The Process of Managerial Communication"). Three feedback types [AP]: *informational* (non-evaluative); *corrective* (challenging); *reinforcing* (confirming) (Ch 16.1, "Feedback").

### Communication types and influences

Three communication types: oral (most prevalent); written (e-mail, reports, memos; often preferred for important decisions and as documented record); nonverbal (traffic lights, body language, office size and placement) (Ch 16.2, "Types of Communications in Organizations").

Major influences on interpersonal communication [AP] (Ch 16.2): social influences (status barriers, prevailing norms and roles); perception (perception of source affects message reception); interaction involvement (responsiveness, perceptiveness, attentiveness); organisational design (decentralisation enhances participative communication; messages distorted travelling through multiple levels).

### Mintzberg's role clusters in communication context

The Ch 16.3 restatement of Mintzberg's three role clusters anchors the communication chapter [BT] (Ch 16.3, "Factors Affecting Communications and the Roles of Managers"): interpersonal (figurehead, leader, liaison); informational (monitor, disseminator, spokesperson); decisional (entrepreneur, disturbance/crisis handler, resource allocator, negotiator). Stewart's 1967 finding (and Guest's 1956 corroboration) that managers spend 47 per cent of their time with peers, 41 per cent within their unit, and only 12 per cent with superiors anchors the importance of liaison [BT] (Ch 16.3, "Interpersonal Roles").

### Managerial communication and corporate reputation

Drucker is quoted: "Managers have to learn to know language, to understand what words are and what they mean. Perhaps most important, they have to acquire respect for language as [our] most precious gift and heritage. The manager must understand the meaning of the old definition of rhetoric as 'the art which draws men's hearts to the love of true knowledge'" [V] (Ch 16.4, "Managerial Communication and Corporate Reputation"). Eccles and Nohria reframe: "To see management in its proper light, managers need first to take language seriously" [V]; "the essence of what management is all about [is] the effective use of language to get things done" [V] (Ch 16.4).

### Communication channels and writing's role

Talking, listening, reading, and writing are the four major channels of management communication. Borden's research: managers across industries spend about 75 per cent of time in verbal interaction [BT] (Ch 16.5, "The Major Channels of Management Communication"). Werner: "North American adults spend more than 78% of their communication time either talking or listening to others who are talking" [V].

Writing is "a career sifter. If managers demonstrate their inability to put ideas on paper in a clear, unambiguous fashion, they're not likely to last" [V] (Ch 16.5, "The Role of Writing"). Procter & Gamble: "brand managers cannot raise a work-related issue in a team meeting unless the ideas are first circulated in writing" [V]. "Documents take on lives of their own. … When [managers] sign a letter and put it in the mail, it's no longer their letter — it's the property of the person or organization it was sent to" [V] (Ch 16.5).

### Communication is invention

The chapter advances the central claim that "communication is a process of invention. Managers literally create meaning through communication. A company, for example, is not in default until a team of auditors sits down to examine the books and review the matter. Only after extended discussion do the accountants conclude that the company is, in fact, in default. It is their discussion that creates the outcome" [V] (Ch 16.5, "Communication Is Invention").

Four principles for socially constructed information [AR] (Ch 16.5, "Information Is Socially Constructed"):

- Information is created, shared, and interpreted by people; meaning is a human phenomenon.
- Information never speaks for itself; "the numbers" require interpretation, explanation, context.
- Context always drives meaning.
- A messenger always accompanies a message; recipients judge the source as well as the content.

The Larkin and Larkin observation is reproduced: "Deep down, managers believe they are communicating effectively. In ten years of management consulting, we have never had a manager say to us that he or she was a poor communicator. They admit to the occasional screw-up, but overall, everyone, without exception, believes he or she is basically a good communicator" [V] (Ch 16.5, "Managers' Greatest Challenge").

## Part XVII: Organisational planning and controlling (Ch 17)

### Why plan; the planning process

*Planning* is "the process by which managers establish goals and specify how these goals are to be attained" [V] (Ch 17.1, "Is Planning Important"). Plans have two components: *outcome/goal statements* and *action statements*. Planning is an intellectual activity that should be ongoing, not a one-off retreat exercise.

Five reasons managers plan [AP] (Ch 17.1, "Why Should Managers Plan?"): (1) offset uncertainty and change; (2) focus organisational activity on objectives; (3) provide a coordinated, systematic road map; (4) increase economic efficiency; (5) facilitate control by establishing standards.

The Koontz-O'Donnell five-step planning process [BT] (Ch 17.2, "The Planning Process"):

1. *Developing awareness of the present state*: the foundation; understand history, strengths, weaknesses, current commitments.
2. *Establishing outcome statements*: setting goals.
3. *Premising*: establishing the assumptions on which the plan rests.
4. *Determining a course of action*: developing alternatives via research, experimentation, experience; evaluating them; selecting based on merit.
5. *Formulating supportive plans*: derivative plans to bolster the basic plan.

### Goal vs domain vs hybrid planning

In *goal planning*, people set specific goals and create action statements [V] (Ch 17.2, "Goal vs. Domain Planning"). In *domain/directional planning*, managers develop a course of action that moves an organisation toward an identified domain. Domain planning suits situations where: flexibility is needed; people cannot agree on goals; the external environment is unstable and uncertain; the organisation is starting up or transitioning.

The 3M Post-it example illustrates domain planning: research aimed at strong cohesives produced an unintended low-cohesion material; Arthur Fry's hymn-book frustration sparked the Post-it innovation [AE] (Ch 17.2). *Hybrid planning* couples the two: managers commit to a domain initially and transition to goal planning as learning accumulates.

Goal planning produces higher performance than domain planning; goals motivate planning by encouraging systematic thinking about how to proceed [BT] (Ch 17.2, "Consequences of Goal, Domain, and Hybrid Planning").

### Deming/Shewhart cycle and integration with controlling

Total Quality Management's *Deming cycle* (also Shewhart cycle) integrates organisational learning into planning: Plan → Do → Check → Act → return to Plan [BT] (Ch 17.2, "Planning, Implementation, and Controlling", Exhibit 17.6). The cycle ensures continuous improvement rather than implement-and-forget plans.

### Types of plans

Five categories of plans (Ch 17.3, "Types of Plans", Table 17.1):

- *Hierarchical*: strategic (institutional level, long-term vision), administrative (integrate strategic and operating), operating (technical core).
- *Frequency-of-use*: standing plans (rules, policies, procedures, SOPs for repeated issues); single-use plans (programs, projects, budgets for unique situations).
- *Time-frame*: short, medium, long-range; with longer-range plans being less specific, less detailed, more flexible. (Konosuke Matsushita's 250-year plan illustrates extreme long-range planning.)
- *Organisational scope*: entire organisation vs operating unit subset.
- *Contingency*: scenario / "what if" planning for events that might disrupt the planned course (terrorism, strikes, natural disasters, economic shifts).

### Goal types and the goal hierarchy

*Official goals* are "an organization's general aims as expressed in public statements" [V] (Ch 17.4, "Goals or Outcome Statements") — usually ambiguous, oriented toward acceptance by constituencies. *Operational goals* are "the concrete goals that organization members are to pursue" [V].

Drucker's eight-area goal framework: market standing; innovation; productivity; profitability; physical and financial resources; manager performance and development; employee performance and attitude; public responsibility [BT] (Ch 17.4, "Multiple Goals and the Goal Hierarchy"). Hewlett-Packard's seven corporate goals exemplify Drucker's framework. Managers must integrate networks of goals across organisational levels and resolve conflicts (e.g., R&D's innovation goal vs production's efficiency goal).

### Planning practice and effectiveness

By the late 1960s, 83 per cent of major US firms used long-range planning; today nearly all US corporations with sales over $100 million prepare formal long-range plans, mostly five-year horizons [V] (Ch 17.5, "Formal Organizational Planning in Practice"). Studies suggest planning pays off: median ROI of 17.1 per cent over five years for organisations engaged in strategic planning vs 5.9 per cent for those not [V] (Ch 17.5, "Does Planning Really Pay Off?").

### Goal characteristics that motivate performance and MBO

Two primary goal attributes that enhance motivating potential: *goal specificity* and *goal difficulty* [BT] (Ch 17.6, "Employees' Responses to Planning"). The Weyerhaeuser truck-driver case: instructing drivers to load to 94 per cent of legal weight capacity (rather than "do their best") yielded approximately $250,000 in savings over nine months as drivers competed against the specific goal [V] (Ch 17.6).

*Management by Objectives (MBO)* is the participatory goal-setting and management technique grounded in goal theory (Ch 17.7, "Management by Objectives: A Planning and Control Technique").

The control-oriented vs involvement-oriented approaches to planning and controlling are contrasted (Ch 17.8): control-oriented approaches emphasise top-down direction and tight monitoring; involvement-oriented approaches emphasise participatory goal-setting and self-managing work groups (Tavistock Institute, sociotechnical model). Many organisations (John Lewis Partnership, Volvo, Motorola) have had successful experiences with employee involvement in planning and controlling.

## Part XVIII: Management of technology and innovation (Ch 18)

### MTI defined

*Technology* is defined as "the processes within the organization that help to convert inputs into outputs as well as the supporting evaluation and control mechanisms" [V] (Ch 18.1, "MTI — Its Importance Now and In the Future"). *Management of technology* is "the planning, implementation, evaluation, and control of the organization's resources and capabilities in order to create value and competitive advantage" [V]. Activities: technology strategy; technology forecasting; technology roadmapping; technology project portfolio.

*Innovation* includes "newness in the development and use of products and/or processes within a firm and within an industry. Invention, new product development, and process-improvement methods are all examples" [V]. *Management of innovation* requires: casting a wide net to track changes; creating newness with existing products (3M tape and Post-it variants); creating a culture open to newness; communicating knowledge throughout the firm (Post-it glue's tackiness was reframed from a failure of strong-glue R&D); changing with courage to avoid *strategic inertia*. Kodak's bankruptcy and IBM's "who wants a computer on their desk?" CEO quotation are presented as cautionary examples (Ch 18.1).

Six societal/business areas requiring good MTI [BT] (Ch 18.1): human resources management; cooperative model expansion; internationalisation; environmental concerns; growth of service industries; intellectual property rights as strategic resource.

### Three creation methods

Three basic organisational processes for technology/innovation creation [AP] (Ch 18.2, "Developing Technology and Innovation"):

- *External processes* (buying and partnering): mergers and acquisitions, joint ventures, contractual relationships, cross-organisational projects, informal relationships. Advantages: quicker, blending rather than discovering, often less costly. Disadvantages: cultural conflicts, winners-and-losers perception, not-invented-here syndrome.
- *Internal processes*: research and development. Advantages: clear ownership, stronger legal protections (patents/trademarks). Disadvantages: slower, costlier, disrupted by key personnel departures (Steve Jobs's death and Apple).
- *New business / entrepreneurship*: exploiting an environmental space. Advantages: agility, dedicated leadership. Disadvantages: highest risk, lack of complementary skills, very little slack.

Five common external process types [AP] (Ch 18.3, "External Sources of Technology and Innovation"):

- Mergers/acquisitions (M&A): ownership changes, new combined entity.
- Joint ventures: long-term alliances creating a new entity for specific innovations; risk of *strategic drift*.
- Franchise agreements: long-term, sharing known technology (McDonald's franchisor model).
- Licensing agreements: technology acquisition without R&D (Dolby noise-reduction technology to sound-equipment producers; over 80 per cent of Dolby revenues from licensing).
- Formal and informal contracts: technology sharing between firms.

### Internal sources, lean start-up, and required skills

R&D, the most common internal process, suits firms wanting ownership and legal protection but accepting cost and slowness (Ch 18.4, "Internal Sources of Technology and Innovation").

Entrepreneurial activities and the *value proposition* (how the firm makes money; how it positions in the marketplace) drive new business entities. The *lean start-up* method works where development costs are low and revisions are inexpensive (e.g., mobile phone applications). Entrepreneurs' "agility" is their defining advantage (Ch 18.5, "Management Entrepreneurship Skills for Technology and Innovation"). Google and 3M employee-time policies illustrate large-firm entrepreneurial flexibility.

Required organisational skills: managing learning and knowledge processes; analysing and forecasting future trends. *Organisational learning* is "the acquisition of knowledge through the collection of data that is analyzed to gather information, which is then transferred and shared through communication among members of the organization" [V] (Ch 18.6, "Skills Needed for MTI"). Two knowledge types: *explicit knowledge* (codified, written rules); *tacit knowledge* (emerging from individual experience; sometimes uncodifiable, as Bessemer's steel-making expertise).

Required individual skills: leadership/followship and creative thinking. *Leadership* shifts within the new-product-development process (engineer in design, engineer/production in prototype, marketing in launch). *Followship* is "the mirror image of leadership … the success of organizations is more the result of good followship than of great leadership. Leadership is influencing others, and followship is seeking or accepting influence" [V] (Ch 18.6, "Skills Needed for MTI"). Five characteristics of good followers: truthful; supportive (not blaming the boss); giving the leader benefit of knowledge and experience; taking initiative to provide solutions; keeping the leader informed (Ch 18.6).

Three managing-now-for-future questions [V] (Ch 18.7, "Managing Now for Future Technology and Innovation"): Where are we now? Where do we want to be? What do we need to move from here to there?

## Positions the authors explicitly frame against

- *Maximising shareholder wealth as primary decision criterion*: the text takes a clear position against this. "Maximizing shareholder wealth is often a short-sighted decision … [I]ncreasing the wealth of shareholders is not an acceptable reason for causing harm to others" [V] (Ch 2.1).
- *Treating decisions as rational and deliberate*: the dual-system (reflective vs reactive) model is presented as more accurate than uniform-rational models of decision-making [AR] (Ch 2.2).
- *Ignoring conflict to keep peace*: process conflict is productive; relationship conflict is harmful; the blanket "conflict is bad" framing misses the distinction [AR] (Ch 2.4).
- *Punishment as primary reinforcer*: "punishment effectively tells a person what not to do and stops the undesired behavior, [but] it does not tell them what they should do" [V] (Ch 14.3); positive reinforcement and reward should dominate.
- *Strict Maslow hierarchical sequencing*: research does not support the strict prepotency claim [AR] (Ch 14.2, "Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs").
- *Treating employees identically (equality) regardless of performance*: equity theory grounds the argument that proportionality (equity) is what employees expect, not equality. "Equality-focused organizations" produce weak E2s and demotivate top performers [AR] (Ch 14.3).
- *Forced ranking as a long-term performance management tool*: "GE backed away from the practice as well. Companies, GE included, saw that it negatively fostered internal competition and undermined collaboration and teamwork" [V] (Ch 11.3).
- *Conflict avoidance in teams*: "Teams often have a fear of conflict so as not to hurt any team members' feelings. The downside of this avoidance is that conflicts still exist under the surface and may resurface in more insidious and back-channel ways that can derail a team" [V] (Ch 15.4).
- *Top-down change as universally appropriate*: "the more complex the potential change, the greater the need to involve employees in the process of planning and implementing change" [V] (Ch 10.3).
- *Treating culture as soft / non-strategic*: Drucker's "culture eats strategy for breakfast" is cited approvingly to frame culture as more influential than strategy [V] (Ch 4.5).
- *Cultural stereotyping*: "broad generalizations may not necessarily be helpful" — 80 per cent of cultural variation is within countries [V/AP] (Ch 6.4).
- *Single-system view of decision-making*: dual-system reflective/reactive is presented as more accurate (Ch 2.2).
- *Generic competitive strategies as combinable*: "Trying to combine [cost leadership and differentiation], Porter suggests, can lead to a firm being stuck in the middle" [V] (Ch 8.6) — the text reproduces Porter's position favourably.
- *Pure rationality without emotion in management*: "Effective decision-making, then, relies on both logic and emotions" [V] (Ch 2.2).
- *Short-term-only profit focus*: "A focus on immediate, short-term outcomes — with little consideration for the future — can cause problems" [V] (Ch 2.5).
- *Treating diversity as moral duty alone*: the discrimination-and-fairness perspective alone (without integration-and-learning) implicitly demands assimilation and undervalues cultural identity [BT/AP] (Ch 12.6).
- *Hiring family/friends as default for startups*: "the long-term hazards (per current research) outweigh the benefits. Family and friends may think too similarly, and the team misses the benefit of other perspectives and connections" [V] (Ch 15.2).
- *Communication as transmission*: managers create meaning through communication; "the numbers" never speak for themselves; communication is invention, not transmission [AR] (Ch 16.5).
