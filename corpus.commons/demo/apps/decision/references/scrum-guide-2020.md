
# Schwaber & Sutherland, The Scrum Guide (light reference)

**Source:** Schwaber, K. & Sutherland, J. (2020). *The Scrum Guide: The Definitive Guide to Scrum: The Rules of the Game*. November 2020 edition. scrumguides.org. Licence: CC BY-SA 4.0. https://scrumguides.org/docs/scrumguide/v2020/2020-Scrum-Guide-US.pdf.
**Structure:** Single 14-page guide with eight top-level sections — Purpose; Scrum Definition; Scrum Theory (Transparency, Inspection, Adaptation); Scrum Values (Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, Courage); Scrum Team (Developers, Product Owner, Scrum Master); Scrum Events (Sprint, Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective); Scrum Artifacts (Product Backlog + Product Goal, Sprint Backlog + Sprint Goal, Increment + Definition of Done); End Note. Canonical definition of Scrum since 2010, latest revision November 2020.

## Author's Thesis (condensed)

Scrum is "a lightweight framework that helps people, teams and organizations generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems." It is deliberately incomplete — defining only what is required to implement Scrum theory — and immutable: implementing only parts of Scrum is possible, but the result is not Scrum. The framework's foundation is empiricism (knowledge from experience, decisions from observation) and lean thinking (waste reduction, focus on essentials); these are operationalised through three pillars (Transparency, Inspection, Adaptation) that the five Scrum events implement.

Scrum is fundamentally about people. Success depends on a small (typically 10 or fewer), cross-functional, self-managing Scrum Team becoming proficient in five values — Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, Courage. The values are not aspirational add-ons but conditions for empiricism to function. The framework applies beyond software ("essentially complex work"), and the retained term *developers* is preserved deliberately as an inclusive label for whoever creates the Increment.

## Scrum Theory: empiricism, lean thinking, and three pillars

Scrum is founded on empiricism and lean thinking, operationalised through three pillars in a causal chain: Transparency enables Inspection ("inspection without transparency is misleading and wasteful"); Inspection enables Adaptation ("inspection without adaptation is considered pointless"); Adaptation must be prompt ("the adjustment must be made as soon as possible to minimize further deviation"). Scrum events are designed to provoke change; failure to operate any event as prescribed results in lost opportunities to inspect and adapt. Adaptation requires authority: it "becomes more difficult when the people involved are not empowered or self-managing."

## The Scrum Team: three accountabilities, no hierarchy

The fundamental unit of Scrum is a small team — one Scrum Master, one Product Owner, and Developers — with no sub-teams or hierarchies, typically 10 or fewer people, cross-functional and self-managing. The team is responsible for all product-related activities and is accountable for creating a valuable, useful Increment every Sprint.

**Developers** are committed to creating any aspect of a usable Increment each Sprint and are accountable for: creating the Sprint Backlog; instilling quality by adhering to the Definition of Done; adapting their plan each day toward the Sprint Goal; and holding each other accountable as professionals.

**Product Owner** is accountable for maximising the value of the product and for effective Product Backlog management (developing the Product Goal, creating and ordering Product Backlog items, ensuring transparency). May delegate but remains accountable. The Product Owner is one person, not a committee. The whole organisation must respect Product Owner decisions for the role to work.

**Scrum Master** is accountable for establishing Scrum as defined in the Guide and for the Scrum Team's effectiveness. Scrum Masters are "true leaders who serve the Scrum Team and the larger organization" — serving the Scrum Team (coaching, focus on Increments, impediment removal, event facilitation), the Product Owner (techniques for Product Goal definition and Product Backlog management, stakeholder collaboration), and the organisation (training and coaching in Scrum adoption, removing barriers between stakeholders and Scrum Teams).

## The Five Scrum Events: cadence for inspection and adaptation

**The Sprint** is the containing event — "the heartbeat of Scrum, where ideas are turned into value." Fixed length, one month or less, with a new Sprint starting immediately after the previous one. All other events happen within the Sprint. During the Sprint: no Sprint-Goal-endangering changes; quality does not decrease; Product Backlog is refined as needed; scope may be clarified and renegotiated with the Product Owner. Shorter Sprints generate more learning cycles and limit risk. Forecasting tools (burn-downs, burn-ups, cumulative flows) are useful but do not replace empiricism. Only the Product Owner has authority to cancel a Sprint.

**Sprint Planning** initiates the Sprint by laying out the work. Created collaboratively by the entire Scrum Team. Organised around three Topics: Why is this Sprint valuable? (Product Owner proposes, team defines Sprint Goal); What can be Done this Sprint? (Developers select Product Backlog items); How will the chosen work get done? (Developers plan the work necessary to meet Definition of Done). The Sprint Goal, selected Product Backlog items, and the delivery plan together form the Sprint Backlog. Timeboxed to a maximum of eight hours for a one-month Sprint.

**Daily Scrum** inspects progress toward the Sprint Goal and adapts the Sprint Backlog. 15-minute event for the Developers, held at the same time and place every working day. If Product Owner or Scrum Master are working on Sprint Backlog items, they participate as Developers. Developers select whatever structure and techniques they want, as long as the meeting focuses on Sprint Goal progress and produces an actionable plan for the next day. Not the only time Developers may adjust the plan — more detailed discussions happen throughout the day.

**Sprint Review** inspects the outcome of the Sprint and determines future adaptations. The Scrum Team presents results to key stakeholders and discusses progress toward the Product Goal; the Product Backlog may be adjusted. A working session, not a presentation. Timeboxed to a maximum of four hours for a one-month Sprint.

**Sprint Retrospective** plans ways to increase quality and effectiveness. The Scrum Team inspects how the last Sprint went with regards to individuals, interactions, processes, tools, and Definition of Done. Assumptions that led the team astray are identified and their origins explored. The team discusses what went well, what problems it encountered, and how those problems were (or were not) solved. The most impactful improvements are addressed as soon as possible and may be added to the next Sprint's Backlog. Concludes the Sprint; timeboxed to a maximum of three hours for a one-month Sprint.

## The Three Scrum Artifacts: each with a Commitment

Scrum's artifacts represent work or value and are designed to maximise transparency. Each carries a commitment that anchors measurable progress.

**Product Backlog** is an emergent, ordered list of what is needed to improve the product — the single source of work for the Scrum Team. Refinement is the act of breaking down and further defining items into smaller, more precise items. Developers are responsible for sizing. Commitment: **Product Goal** — a future state of the product that serves as a target for the Scrum Team to plan against; the long-term objective; one is fulfilled (or abandoned) before taking the next. The Guide defines "product" inline: "A product is a vehicle to deliver value. It has a clear boundary, known stakeholders, well-defined users or customers."

**Sprint Backlog** is composed of the Sprint Goal (why), Product Backlog items selected for the Sprint (what), and an actionable plan for delivering the Increment (how). A plan by and for the Developers; updated throughout the Sprint as more is learned. Commitment: **Sprint Goal** — the single objective for the Sprint. A Developer commitment that provides flexibility about the exact work needed to achieve it; created during Sprint Planning. If the work turns out different than expected, Developers collaborate with the Product Owner to negotiate Sprint Backlog scope without affecting the Sprint Goal.

**Increment** is a concrete stepping stone toward the Product Goal. Each Increment is additive to all prior Increments and thoroughly verified. Multiple Increments may be created within a Sprint; an Increment may be delivered to stakeholders prior to the end of the Sprint ("the Sprint Review should never be considered a gate to releasing value"). Commitment: **Definition of Done** — a formal description of the state of the Increment when it meets the product's quality measures. "The moment a Product Backlog item meets the Definition of Done, an Increment is born." If an item does not meet the Definition of Done, it cannot be released or presented at the Sprint Review and returns to the Product Backlog. Organisational DoD is a minimum standard if it exists; otherwise the team creates its own.

## Key Connections

The Scrum Guide is a self-contained framework definition. The body text makes no external citations beyond the licence reference to Creative Commons. The End Note's provenance section names early collaborators and adopters:

- *Jeff McKenna and John Scumniotales* worked with Jeff Sutherland; *Mike Smith and Chris Martin* worked with Ken Schwaber (End Note).
- First co-presentation of Scrum at the *OOPSLA Conference, 1995*.
- Early adopting organisations: *Individual Inc., Newspage, Fidelity Investments, and IDX (now GE Medical)*.

The Guide is itself cited by Letaw (2024) *Handbook of Software Engineering Methods* as the primary source for Scrum-related framework definitions used throughout Letaw's Chapter 1.

## Signature Contrarian Positions

1. **Scrum is immutable.** "The Scrum framework, as outlined herein, is immutable. While implementing only parts of Scrum is possible, the result is not Scrum." Partial implementation is permitted but the authors refuse to call it Scrum.
2. **Transparency precedes inspection; inspection precedes adaptation.** Each pillar is necessary for the next: "inspection without transparency is misleading and wasteful"; "inspection without adaptation is considered pointless." Adaptation must be immediate to minimise further deviation.
3. **No sub-teams or hierarchies inside the Scrum Team.** Cross-functional, self-managing, flat. If the team grows beyond ~10, split into multiple cohesive teams sharing the same Product Goal, Product Backlog, and Product Owner — do not stratify.
4. **The Product Owner is one person, not a committee.** Single-throat-to-choke ownership of the Product Backlog. Stakeholders wanting Product Backlog changes must convince the Product Owner.
5. **Sprint Review is a working session, not a presentation.** Scrum Team and stakeholders collaborate on what to do next; the Product Backlog may be adjusted in the room.
6. **The Increment may be delivered before the Sprint Review.** "The Sprint Review should never be considered a gate to releasing value." Value-delivery cadence is independent of the inspection cadence.
7. **Only the Product Owner can cancel a Sprint.** And only if the Sprint Goal becomes obsolete.
8. **Forecasting practices do not replace empiricism.** Burn-downs, burn-ups, and cumulative flows are useful, but "in complex environments, what will happen is unknown. Only what has already happened may be used for forward-looking decision making."
9. **The Daily Scrum is for the Developers, and they decide its format.** 15 minutes, same time and place daily, focus on Sprint Goal progress; structure and techniques are at the Developers' discretion.
10. **Definition of Done is the gate at the Increment boundary.** Work failing the Definition of Done cannot even be presented at the Sprint Review — it returns to the Product Backlog.
11. **Scrum applies beyond software.** "Essentially complex work" is the qualifier; "developers" is preserved as a deliberately inclusive term — researchers, analysts, scientists, and other specialists are included.
12. **The Scrum Values are conditions for empiricism, not decorations.** When Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, and Courage are embodied, "the empirical Scrum pillars of transparency, inspection, and adaptation come to life building trust."
