# US Forest Service LFUO 2024, Retrospective Distillation

**Source:** US Forest Service, Risk Management Council & Office of Safety & Occupational Health (2024). *Learning From Unintended Outcomes and Learning Review Implementation Guide*. Revised April 2024. **Licence:** Public domain (17 USC §105 — US federal-employee work). **Scope:** open. Carries borrowed-through citations to Dekker, Hollnagel, Weick, Klein, and Reason — the full HOP canon — which the demo does not hold directly.

## Retrospective Relevance

The LFUO guide's primary domain is the AAR / FLA / Learning Review ladder for accident-class events. Its retro relevance is narrower but durable: the counterfactual prohibition, the cause-as-construction framing, the Five Hows / Lessons Learned Analysis, the work-as-imagined vs work-as-done diagnostic, and the experiment-review discipline all transfer intact to the iterative team-internal retro context. In retro work, LFUO fires most sharply on Phase 0.5 (were last retro's experiments learned from, not just done?), Phase 3 (refuse root cause; refuse counterfactual; decompose the recurring problem through the Five Hows), and the background of Phase 0 (the administrative-firewall logic scales down to the retro's psychological-safety precondition). The guide's borrowed-through Dekker / Hollnagel / Weick / Klein / Reason lineage is named here so practitioners know the epistemic source without overstating what the demo holds. Frame to bring: LFUO's retro contribution is primarily a *refusal set* — what not to say and why — plus the Five Hows as the structured alternative when the team reaches for "root cause."

## Key Concepts for Retrospective

1.  **Cause is constructed, not discovered.** "Cause isn't something investigators 'find' or 'discover'; cause is always something we create by recreating the event showing how certain anomalies led to the accident" (Appendix B, "A Deeper Dive into Causal Factors"). In retro, whenever the team reaches for "root cause," redirect: what are the conditions — networked, not linear — that collectively shaped this outcome?

2.  **Networked causality as the replacement vocabulary.** "The combined total of those conditions that collectively influenced the event or outcome" (Glossary, "Networked causality"). The vocabulary shift matters because it changes what the team looks for. Linear cause points to one fix; networked causality points to a system.

3.  **Counterfactual reasoning is forbidden.** "FLA teams must guard against making counterfactual arguments such as: 'If this person had done X, then the outcome would have been Y and the accident would not have occurred.' The FLA is only useful when it learns why people did what they actually did" (Part 9, "Avoid Counterfactual Arguments"). In retro, state the rule aloud at the opening when blame language or counterfactual framing is anticipated.

4.  **Five Hows as local-rationality reconstruction.** The Lessons Learned Analysis applies the Five Hows to each key decision in an event: how did the participant develop their situational awareness, and why did the action make sense to them in context at the time? (Part 7, "The Five Hows — A Sensemaking Tool"). Scaled to retro, the Five Hows apply to recurring problems: how did the team develop its understanding of the situation that led to the repeated pattern?

5.  **Beliefs, perceptions, expectations, paradigms decomposition.** Each key recurring problem can be decomposed into the beliefs, perceptions, expectations, and paradigms the team held before the Sprint that turned out to be wrong, inaccurate, or misleading (Part 7, step 4). This four-category decomposition surfaces what is invisible in a surface-level data-gathering pass.

6.  **Work-as-imagined vs work-as-done as the diagnostic frame.** "How were we actually completing the work vs how managers were expecting the work to be completed?" (Part 1, "Systems Thinking"). In retro, this is the recurring-problem diagnostic: where is the gap between what the team's process says should happen and what the team actually does?

7.  **Multiple improbable events as the pattern in complex systems.** "Most often, the Lessons Learned Analysis will reveal that multiple improbable events were necessary for the accident to occur" (Part 7). Scaled to retro: a recurring problem that has not yielded to a single fix is almost certainly the product of multiple interacting conditions, not a single cause that has been missed.

8.  **Just Culture framing as the substrate for blame-free retrospection.** "Employees make mistakes, but most of the time (almost continuously) they are actually *creating safety*" (Part 1, "Part 1 — Foundational Principles"). In retro: human performance variability is "not only normal, it's the rule!" (Part 1, "Learning"). This framing supports the Prime Directive's intent without requiring direct citation of Kerth.

9.  **Learning and validation discipline.** The guide's emphasis that experiments require validation — checking whether the lesson was learned, not just whether the action was done — scales directly to Phase 0.5. What was the lesson the experiment was designed to surface? What changed because of it?

10.  **Psychological safety as the administrative firewall, scaled down.** The guide's administrative-firewall principle — without a signed commitment to no punitive actions, honest accounts will not surface — scales to retro as: without genuine psychological safety (however achieved), team members will not surface real patterns. The formal mechanism differs; the requirement is the same.

## Questions to Ask During Retrospective

### Phase 0: Setup

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Is the no-blame precondition set? | Has the retro opening established that this is a learning conversation — not an accountability exercise — such that team members can give honest accounts? |
| Is the counterfactual prohibition named? | If blame language or "if they had just…" framing is anticipated, state the rule at the opening: learn why people did what they actually did, not what hindsight suggests they should have done. |

### Phase 0.5: Experiment Review

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Was the experiment designed to surface a learning, not just to complete an action? | What was the lesson the experiment was supposed to validate? Was it validated? What changed in the team's beliefs, perceptions, or practices because of the experiment? |
| Was the experiment reviewed as a hypothesis, not as a success/failure verdict? | The guide's epistemic stance: learning is the precondition for improvement. Did the experiment produce a lesson, regardless of whether the outcome was as expected? |

### Phase 1: Priming

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Is the LFUO framing on variability relevant here? | Is the team treating mistakes as aberrations or as normal? "Errors, mistakes, and lapses are inherent in the human condition" (Part 1, "Learning"). Naming this shifts the energy before data gathering begins. |

### Phase 3: Insight / Cause Analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Has anyone used the phrase "root cause"? | Redirect: cause is constructed. What are the conditions — networked, not linear — that collectively shaped this recurring pattern? |
| Is counterfactual reasoning creeping in? | "If the build hadn't broken…" "If X had just reviewed the PR…" — redirect to why people did what they actually did, in context, at the time. |
| Are we applying the Five Hows to the recurring problem? | For each key action or non-action in the recurring pattern: what beliefs, perceptions, expectations, and paradigms led to it? What situational awareness did the team hold that made it reasonable? |
| Is the work-as-imagined / work-as-done gap visible? | Where is the gap between what the team's process documentation says should happen and what the team actually does? Illuminate the gap rather than resolving it immediately. |
| Are multiple conditions interacting to produce this pattern? | Have we anchored on one fix that hasn't worked? The LFUO principle: multiple improbable events are typically necessary. What are the interacting conditions the team has not yet named? |

### Phase 4: Experiment Design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Is the proposed experiment addressing the right conditions? | If the Five Hows analysis surfaced multiple interacting conditions, is the experiment targeting the one that, if addressed, would change the dynamics for the others? |
| Is the experiment designed as a hypothesis with a validation criterion? | "We believe X is true; if we do Y, we should see Z." Can the team name what evidence would confirm or disconfirm the belief the experiment is testing? |

### Phase 5: Close

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Has the team validated the named lessons against real observations? | Not just "we'll try X" but "we believe X because of Y observation, and the experiment will confirm or disconfirm that belief." |
| Is the gap between work-as-imagined and work-as-done named for follow-up? | If the insight phase surfaced a work-as-imagined / work-as-done gap that is above the team's authority to fix, has it been named for escalation? |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| Team uses the phrase "root cause" | Linear-cause framing will produce a single fix for a networked problem | Redirect: "What are the conditions — networked, not linear — that collectively shaped this pattern?" |
| "If they had just done X..." framing arrives | Counterfactual reasoning — learning what people should have done instead, not why they did what they did | State the prohibition aloud: the FLA learns why people did what they actually did |
| Recurring problem surfaces for the third retro | Multiple interacting conditions are keeping the problem in place; previous fixes addressed only one | Apply the Five Hows to each key decision or non-decision in the recurring pattern |
| Work-as-documented differs from work-as-done | Process documentation has drifted from actual practice | Illuminate the gap before resolving it; ask what makes the actual practice locally rational |
| Experiment was "done" but produced no learning | The experiment was designed as a box-tick, not as a hypothesis with a validation criterion | Redesign with an explicit belief-and-evidence structure: "We believe X; we will see Y if correct" |
| Blame language survives the data-gathering redirect | Deeper blame pattern persists; the container isn't holding it | Re-state the just-culture framing; consider applying the Five Hows directly to the blamed individual's decision context |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this distillation when:
- Blame language surfaces and the team needs a principled redirect grounded in something more substantive than "we don't do blame here."
- A recurring problem is resistant to previous experiments — the Five Hows and multiple-interacting-conditions framing are the structured alternative to one more round of the same fix.
- The work-as-imagined vs work-as-done gap is visible — the retro should illuminate the gap, not paper over it.
- An experiment is proposed without a learning criterion — the guide's validation discipline applies.
- Phase 0.5 (experiment review) needs a frame: was the experiment designed to surface learning, or to tick a completion box?

This source fires most sharply on Phase 0.5, Phase 3, and the background of Phase 0. Prefer the Approach Perfect Field Guide for the agenda structure and Liberating Structures for facilitation moves within phases.

## Worked Example

A team's retro Phase 3 is analysing why the same integration-testing gap has appeared for four Sprints. The first round of discussion produces: "the QA engineer keeps missing the edge cases." The lead names the counterfactual prohibition: the team is learning why the QA engineer's situation made the omission locally rational, not what they should have done instead.

The Five Hows applied to the QA engineer's decision point: How did they develop their situational awareness about the edge case? They relied on the acceptance criteria in the story. How were the acceptance criteria written? By the product manager without QA input. Why without QA input? Because the refinement meeting happens while QA is in another team's standup. Why that scheduling conflict? Because QA is split across two teams.

The team now has five networked conditions: story-writing process, refinement timing, QA availability, multi-team assignment, acceptance-criteria ownership. The previous experiment — "QA should flag edge cases earlier" — targeted one event (the flag), not the conditions that made early flagging impossible. The new experiment targets one condition: moving refinement ten minutes later so QA can attend.

Phase 4: the experiment is designed as a hypothesis with a validation criterion. "We believe the scheduling conflict is the primary barrier; if we shift refinement by ten minutes, QA will attend at least three of the next four sessions." Named owner, named date, named evidence criterion.

The cause-as-construction principle, counterfactual prohibition, Five Hows, local-rationality framing, and learning-validation discipline all trace to the LFUO guide (Appendix B, "A Deeper Dive into Causal Factors"; Part 9, "Avoid Counterfactual Arguments"; Part 7, "The Five Hows — A Sensemaking Tool"; Part 1, "Foundational Principles").

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Using "root cause" as a terminus — cause is constructed, and the construction shapes what gets fixed.
- Counterfactual reasoning: learning what people should have done instead is not learning; learning why they did what they did is.
- Treating a recurring problem as evidence that the team is lazy or negligent rather than as a signal of multiple interacting conditions.
- Designing experiments that tick a box ("we tried it") without a learning criterion for validation.
- Allowing blame language and "if they had just…" framing to pass unchallenged in Phase 3.
- Anchoring on a single fix for a recurring problem when LFUO's principle is that multiple improbable events are typically necessary.
- Skipping the work-as-imagined / work-as-done diagnostic: the gap between what process documentation says and what actually happens is a structural vulnerability, not an individual failing.
- Citing Dekker, Hollnagel, Weick, Klein, or Reason as if the demo holds them directly; the citations are borrowed-through this guide's Recommended Reading. Direct citation is not available in this open distribution.
