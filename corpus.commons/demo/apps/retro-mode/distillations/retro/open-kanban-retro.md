# Open Kanban, Retro Distillation

**Source:** Joseph Hurtado (with contributor Annita Yegorova Hurtado), AgileLion Institute. *Open Kanban*, Release 1.00 Rev A. CC BY 3.0 Unported. Scope: open. Note: Open Kanban is the corpus's redistributable Kanban-methodology slot, not a canonical-Kanban-authority source. The Goldratt and Deming lineage cited below is borrowed-through: both are cited by name in the source but are not in this corpus.

## Retro Relevance

Open Kanban's retro projection is strongest on three axes. The Holistic or Systemic Approach to Change value — citing Deming and Goldratt explicitly — is the demo corpus's open-licence carrier of the *find-the-one-constraint* discipline: "no single part of a system can ever bring overall improvement." [V] ("Open Kanban Values") This grounds the retro's Phase 4 experiment-design discipline in a systemic reading rather than a local fix. The Learn and Improve Continuously practice makes learning upstream of improvement structurally explicit — the retro must produce genuine understanding before the experiment can be trusted to change anything. The Reduce the Batch Size practice gives Phase 4 its action discipline: fewer concurrent experiments, not more; the constraint focus beats the long list every time. The Respect for People value (with its explicit *Muri* / overburden gloss) names the sustainable-pace discipline that Phase 4 commitments must honour.

## Key Concepts for Retro

1. **Holistic or Systemic Approach to Change — Deming + Goldratt borrowed-through.** "Deming's System of Profound Knowledge and Goldratt's Theory of Constraints reminds us that no single part of a system can ever bring overall improvement. We need to take a holistic view of the system and understand it. And the key part of the system is people, not just as resources, but also as full rounded individuals who make the system work." [V] ("Open Kanban Values") For Phase 3 insight and Phase 4 experiment design: the intervention must touch the constraint at the level it actually lives in, not a symptom in a local subsystem.

2. **Learning as upstream prerequisite.** "Learning is the key concept before continuous improvement can ever happen! Once learning is part of the culture, part of the workflow, then improving continuously becomes easy." [V] ("Open Kanban Practices") A retro that produces an experiment list without genuine shared understanding has not produced its benefit. The experiment is downstream of learning; if the team cannot articulate what they now understand that they didn't before, the experiment is a guess.

3. **Reduce the Batch Size — one-constraint focus for experiment design.** "Research in the way the mind works, and countless experiences from Lean, the Theory of Constraints and Kanban confirm that to deliver value faster, with better flow and good team morale we need to focus and limit the number of things we do. Multitasking does not work." [V] ("Open Kanban Practices") For Phase 4: pick the one constraint this Sprint revealed. One experiment owned and finished is worth five experiments started and carried over.

4. **WIP-limiting as consequence, not primary rule.** "Limiting WIP is a consequence of reducing the batch size of your efforts, and not the other way around... Open Kanban does not ask you to limit WIP, but it does request that you 'Reduce the Batch Size of your Efforts.'" [V] ("Open Kanban Practices") For retro experiment design: the discipline is batch-size reduction first; limiting the number of concurrent experiments is the downstream expression of that. The focus question is not "how many can we handle?" but "what is the smallest thing that would move the constraint?"

5. **Respect for People — sustainable pace and Muri.** "If you respect your team you will not work them to death, or subject any worker to intellectual or physical demands that make it nearly impossible to succeed. An exhausted developer, manager or team are the perfect recipe for disaster. Kanban cannot succeed this way." [V] ("Open Kanban Values") For Phase 4 experiment design: the experiment must fit within sustainable capacity. An experiment that requires the team to work beyond their steady pace is overburden (*Muri*) by design — it will either not happen or will damage the team.

6. **Courage — upward correction.** "When a manager, VP, or person in authority makes a mistake and someone with lower rank notices it, it takes courage for him to tell us about it." [V] ("Open Kanban Values") Attributed to Kent Beck (borrowed-through). For Phase 2 data gathering: the retro's written-first, individual-then-grouped pattern is the psychological infrastructure that makes courage less necessary. If the container is weak, courage fills the gap — but the container is the better investment.

7. **Learn and Improve Continuously — multiple implementations.** "There are many ways a Kanban team can implement this practice, you could have Retrospectives, Strategy Meetings or even Kaizen Groups." [V] ("Open Kanban Practices") The retro is one named implementation. Its value comes from regularity: a one-off retro after a crisis is crisis-management, not a learning culture.

8. **Visualize the workflow — making the invisible visible.** "When we are doing knowledge work, like programming a method, designing a user interface or writing a business report most of the work is invisible." [V] ("Open Kanban Practices") For Phase 2 data gathering: the team's process breakdowns during the Sprint are largely invisible until they are named and placed in a shared view. Written sticky notes, grouped by theme, are a form of workflow visualisation for the retrospective itself.

9. **Focus on Value — eliminate waste.** "Value is at the center of Lean and TPS, but frequently it is mentioned as the reverse side of the coin: eliminate waste or 'Muda' in Japanese. By eliminating waste, we optimize the creation of value." [V] ("Open Kanban Values") For Phase 3: frame the insight question as "where did waste appear in this Sprint?" Waste includes rework, waiting, over-processing, and work that was started and not finished.

10. **Communication and Collaboration — both required, neither sufficient alone.** "One value does not work without the other... To succeed we need to make ourselves heard (communicate) but also we need to be able to work with others to create value." [V] ("Open Kanban Values") For Phase 3 and Phase 4: if communication broke down, ask whether collaboration also broke down, or whether the information moved but nothing was done with it. The diagnosis changes the experiment.

11. **No single part of a system brings overall improvement — escalation discipline.** "An understanding of the whole is fundamental to arrive at steady, successful change." [V] ("Open Kanban Values") For Phase 0.5 experiment review: if the same experiment has been proposed three retros running without moving the needle, the constraint is not where the experiment is pointed. The intervention is in the wrong part of the system. Escalate or redesign.

## Questions to Ask During Retro

### Phase 0 — Setup

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Sustainable pace check | Is the team running at a sustainable pace, or has overburden (*Muri*) been normalised? If so, naming that is Phase 2 data, not background noise. |

### Phase 0.5 — Experiment review

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Same experiment recurring | Has this experiment been proposed two or three retros in a row without movement? If so, either the experiment is in the wrong part of the system, or a constraint above team level is blocking it. Escalation is the right move, not another iteration of the same fix. |
| Structural change territory | Has the experiment crossed into structural-change territory — something no single part of the team can deliver alone? "No single part of a system can ever bring overall improvement": escalate to leadership rather than retry at team level. |

### Phase 1 — Priming

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Constraint framing | Does the team understand what this retro is trying to find — not a list of problems, but the one constraint that, if moved, would most change the next Sprint? Frame the retro around constraint-finding, not complaint-collecting. |

### Phase 2 — Data gathering

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Waste as a named category | Where did waste appear in this Sprint — rework, waiting, over-processing, unfinished work? Naming waste directly surfaces the systemic reading faster than symptom-listing. |
| Communication vs collaboration | When a breakdown is named, is it a communication failure (information didn't move) or a collaboration failure (information moved but no one acted together on it)? The diagnosis changes the experiment. |

### Phase 3 — Insight / cause analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Genuine learning check | Can the team articulate what they now understand that they didn't before this retro? If not, the experiment will be a guess. |
| Systemic vs local | Is the insight pointing to a local symptom (one person, one tool, one process step) or to a systemic pattern (a feedback loop, an overburden structure, a missing agreement)? Local fixes of systemic problems don't hold. |

### Phase 4 — Experiment design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| One constraint | Of all the insights surfaced, which one — if addressed — would most move the system? That is the constraint. Act on that one. Leave the others on a visible backlog, not on a commitment list. |
| Smallest batch | What is the smallest version of this experiment that would still test the hypothesis? Smaller is faster to learn from; faster learning is the whole point. |
| Capacity discipline | Does this experiment fit within sustainable capacity? An experiment designed for a pace the team can't hold is overburden by design. |

### Phase 5 — Close

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Learning embedded | Has the learning from this retro been put somewhere the team will encounter it again — a Working Agreement update, a DoD change, a visible board entry — or is it only in the notes? |
| Improvement structure | Is there a structure (regular retro, Kaizen review, standing agenda item) that will make this experiment visible and accountable next cycle? One-off retros produce one-off learning. |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| Same experiment proposed for the third retro without movement | Either the constraint is mis-identified or it lives above team level | Ask: is the experiment in the wrong part of the system? Escalate rather than retry |
| Team proposes five or more experiments | Batch-size discipline hasn't fired; the long list will produce zero follow-through | Apply the one-constraint question: which single experiment, if done, would most move the system? |
| Experiment requires overtime or weekend work to execute | Muri is built into the design | Reshape the experiment to fit within sustainable capacity, or defer it |
| Team can't articulate what they now understand that they didn't before | Learning has not occurred; the experiment will be a guess | Pause Phase 4; return to Phase 3 and stay until the shared understanding is clear |
| A breakdown is named but the framing is "communication failed" | Communication vs collaboration distinction not yet applied | Ask: did the information move but no one acted together on it? If yes, the diagnosis is collaboration, not communication |
| Learning from prior retros lives only in notes | Not embedded in the team's workflow | Identify which structure (Working Agreement, DoD, board entry, standing agenda item) will make the learning visible and accountable |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this distillation when:
- The experiment-design phase needs the one-constraint discipline: batch-size reduction and systemic-locus thinking are this source's core contributions.
- The team is committing to overburden — the Muri naming is the specific vocabulary this source provides.
- A recurring experiment suggests the constraint is mis-identified or above team level.
- The retro needs a shared understanding that learning is upstream of improvement, not a side effect of the experiment.
- The retro needs to embed learning in a repeating structure, not just in the notes from one session.

This source does not supply the retro's agenda structure, facilitation moves, or experiment-quality criteria. Pair with the Field Guide for protocol and Liberating Structures or Open Practice Library for facilitation craft.

## Worked Example

A team reviews its Phase 0.5 experiment results. The prior retro committed to two experiments: a new code-review checklist and a pair-programming rotation. Both were done. Neither moved the metric the team was watching: deployment lead time. Phase 3 opens: why didn't these experiments hold?

The lead applies the systemic vs local question: did either experiment target the constraint at the level it actually lives in, or did they address local symptoms? The team realises the deployment lead time is driven primarily by the queue for the shared infrastructure team — something no checklist or pairing rotation touches. This is the systemic constraint.

Phase 4: the one-constraint question. Which single experiment would most move the system? Not another team-internal process change. The systemic move is: escalate the dependency with the infrastructure team, proposing a shared slack-capacity agreement. This is a structural escalation, not a retro experiment the team can run alone.

The lead applies Open Kanban's Holistic-Systemic value: "no single part of a system can ever bring overall improvement" [V] ("Open Kanban Values"). The retro's output is not a new experiment but a well-formed escalation: what the team needs from infrastructure, the evidence, and the impact on deployment lead time.

Before close: the shared understanding is named and embedded in the Working Agreement as a principle — recurring impediments from outside the team boundary will be escalated rather than retried.

The batch-size discipline, one-constraint focus, Muri naming, learning-upstream principle, and systemic-vs-local distinction all trace to Open Kanban ("Open Kanban Practices"; "Open Kanban Values").

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Designing a retro experiment that targets a local symptom when the constraint lives at a systemic level — the fix doesn't hold because the system absorbs it.
- Treating a long experiment list as diligence when batch-size discipline says one owned experiment beats five items on a list.
- Committing to experiments that require overburden to execute — Muri built into the design means the experiment will either not happen or will damage the team.
- Proposing the same experiment a third time without asking whether the constraint has been correctly identified.
- Treating the experiment as the learning output rather than recognising that learning is upstream: the experiment is downstream of understanding, not a substitute for it.
- Defaulting to communication fixes when the breakdown was a collaboration failure — information was available, but no one acted together on it.
- Closing the retro without embedding the learning in a structure the team will encounter again — learning that lives only in the notes is not part of the workflow.
- Running a single post-crisis retro and treating it as a learning culture — regularity is what makes the practice a system.
