# OpenStax Business Ethics, Retro Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2018). *Business Ethics*. Senior contributing authors Stephen M. Byars and Kurt Stanberry et al. Houston, TX: Rice University. Licence: CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. Scope: open-nc.

## Retro Relevance

Business Ethics is light-fire for the retro axis. Most retro phases have no load-bearing content here. The source earns its place at two moments only: when the retro surfaces an ethical exposure — a practice the team is following that harms someone — and when retro insights cross stakeholder boundaries and need to be framed for people outside the team. The Donaldson-Preston normative-stakeholder framing names why the affected party's interests matter regardless of whether they have power over the team. The social-contract argument grounds accountability frames when the team discovers a gap between what they did and what they owed. Whistleblower-protection awareness surfaces when the retro reveals a known-but-unreported problem. Phases with nothing are skipped.

## Key Concepts for Retro

1. **Three approaches to stakeholder theory** (Donaldson and Preston) — descriptive (map interests accurately), instrumental (stakeholder management serves financial outcomes), normative (stakeholders are ends in themselves) (Ch 3.2, "The Ethical Basis of Stakeholders' Claims"). When a retro insight crosses team boundaries and affects people outside the team, the normative approach names why those interests belong in the analysis even if those stakeholders have no direct power over the team's work.

2. **Ethical minimum and ethical maximum** — minimum is the least a company can do to claim ethical positioning; maximum is the strongest action available (Ch 3.1, "Ethical Responsibilities Often Extend Beyond Legal Requirements"). When a retro surfaces a gap between the team's practice and what it owed, this scale names where on the spectrum the proposed experiment lands.

3. **Social contract framing** — corporations enter a social contract; they owe a debt to the community that enables them to function (Ch 1.2, Ch 4.1). Scaled to team level: when a team's practice harms users, customers, or colleagues downstream, the social-contract argument names the obligation before the retro reaches action design.

4. **Whistleblower protections and safe reporting** — safe reporting channels are a precondition for ethical behaviour; absent safe channels, known problems go unreported not from bad character but from rational self-protection (Ch 7.5, "Whistleblowing: Risks and Rewards"). When a retro reveals that team members knew about a problem and did not surface it, the absence of safe channels — not character failure — is the more likely explanation.

5. **Tone at the top and ethical culture** — "employees are more likely to be unethical when they are dissatisfied with their jobs or see their superiors acting unethically" (Ch 11.2, "Relationships Matter"). When a retro experiment targets a behavioural norm the lead or manager models in the opposite direction, the experiment will not survive beyond the retro.

6. **Phronesis (practical wisdom)** — practical wisdom acquired from experience that enables prudent judgment in actual situations (Ch 1.1, "Aristotle and the Concept of Phronesis"). When a retro reveals that the team cut a corner that felt locally reasonable in the moment, phronesis names the gap: not character failure, but accumulated context that made the shortcut legible.

## Questions to Ask During Retro

### Phase 3: Insight / cause analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Name the ethical dimension of a recurring practice | Is the team's practice harming someone outside the team — a user, a colleague downstream, a customer? Naming it as an ethical gap (not just a process gap) changes the weight of the experiment: it is an obligation, not an option. |
| Check for safe-channel absence | Has this problem been known before? If yes — why wasn't it surfaced? Were reporting channels available and safe? If channels were absent or unsafe, the retro experiment should target channel design, not individual behaviour. |

### Phase 4: Experiment design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Locate the experiment on the ethical scale | Is the proposed change the minimum required, or is there a stronger response available? When the gap between minimum and maximum is large and the harm is real, name it and let the team decide where to land. |
| Check for tone-at-the-top alignment | Will the lead or manager model the behaviour the experiment asks for? If not, the experiment is designed to fail. The retro insight should include what the lead commits to change, not only what the team commits to. |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| A retro theme involves a practice that harms users, customers, or colleagues downstream | Ethical dimension is present but unnamed | Name it explicitly: is this an ethical gap (obligation) or a process gap (option)? The distinction changes the weight of the experiment |
| "We knew about this but didn't raise it" surfaces | Absence of safe reporting channels is the more likely explanation than character failure | Ask: were reporting channels available and safe? If not, the experiment targets channel design, not individual behaviour |
| An experiment asks the team to behave differently than their manager does | Tone-at-the-top will undo the experiment within a Sprint | The experiment must include what the lead or manager commits to change, not only what the team commits to |
| The team is heading toward the ethical minimum response | The minimum may not match the harm | Name the gap: where does the proposed experiment sit between minimum possible and maximum available? |
| "We didn't think about the people downstream" | Stakeholder interests were not in the analysis | Apply the normative stakeholder framing: downstream parties are ends in themselves, not just factors |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this distillation when:
- A retro theme has an ethical dimension: a practice that harms someone outside the team, a known problem that wasn't reported, an experiment that requires courage upward.
- The retro insight crosses team boundaries and needs framing for people outside the team — the normative-stakeholder language gives the team a principled argument.
- The team is designing an experiment and the lead or manager needs to be included in the commitment, not just the team.
- Safe reporting channels are absent and the team is treating silence as character failure.

This source fires on a subset of retro moments only: when the retro surface crosses an ethical threshold. For most retros, this source is background. The Field Guide, Liberating Structures, and Open Practice Library carry the retro's working structure.

## Worked Example

A team's Phase 3 surfaces: "We've been aware for three Sprints that the API is returning incorrect data to a downstream service that affects billing." No one raised it externally because the product manager tends to react defensively to problem reports and there's no formal issue-escalation channel.

The lead applies the whistleblower-protection framing: the silence is not a character failure. It is rational self-protection in the absence of safe channels (Ch 7.5, "Whistleblowing: Risks and Rewards"). The experiment must target channel design, not individual behaviour.

Phase 4: two experiments are proposed. First, a working-agreement entry that the team will report data-quality issues to the product manager in writing (creating a record) regardless of verbal reaction. Second, a request to the product manager to establish a non-punitive issue-log. The lead notes the tone-at-the-top principle: if the product manager does not model the non-punitive response, the team's commitment won't survive. The experiment must include a named commitment from the product manager, not just from the team.

The ethical minimum / ethical maximum framing: the minimum is reporting the issue to the product manager. The maximum is escalating outside the product group if the issue is not addressed. The team names where on the scale they are starting, and when they will escalate if the minimum produces no change.

The normative-stakeholder, whistleblower-protection, tone-at-the-top, and ethical-minimum/maximum frames all trace to Business Ethics (Ch 3.2, "The Ethical Basis of Stakeholders' Claims"; Ch 7.5; Ch 11.2; Ch 3.1, "Ethical Responsibilities Often Extend Beyond Legal Requirements").

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Treating a team practice that harms users or colleagues as a process problem only, bypassing the ethical dimension; naming it as an ethical gap raises the stakes appropriately and changes the quality of the experiment.
- Diagnosing silence about a known problem as character failure when the absence of safe reporting channels is the more likely explanation.
- Designing a retro experiment that requires the team to behave differently than their manager does; tone-at-the-top dynamics will undo the experiment within a Sprint.
- Framing accountability as individual blame when the social-contract argument points to a structural condition the team could not have resolved alone.
- Settling for the ethical minimum in experiment design when the harm warrants a stronger response; naming the gap between minimum and maximum gives the team and their lead an honest choice.
