# OpenStax Organizational Behavior, Retro Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2019). *Organizational Behavior*. Lead author J. Stewart Black (INSEAD). ISBN 978-1-947172-72-2 (digital). Licence: **CC BY-NC-SA 4.0**. Scope: open-nc.

## Retro Relevance

Organizational Behavior is the facilitator's and coach's diagnostic vocabulary for what is happening in the team dynamic during a retro — what is pulling participants toward defensiveness, conformity, or helplessness, and what is shaping the group's ability to produce honest data and own experiments. Its load-bearing contributions are: attribution biases that prevent the team from seeing systemic causes (fundamental attribution error, actor-observer bias, self-serving bias); group dynamics that suppress useful conflict (groupthink, Asch conformity); conflict-type distinction (process conflict is productive; relationship conflict is not); communication encoding-decoding-noise as the mechanism behind misaligned team accounts; and the motivation frameworks that diagnose a helpless team. The retro facilitator draws on OB not to clinically categorise participants but to name patterns in the room — this team is high-conformity, this team is in relationship conflict, this team has attribution bias about the last sprint — and to choose the facilitation move that addresses the pattern.

## Key Concepts for Retro

1.  **Fundamental attribution error.** "A tendency to underestimate the effects of internal or personal causes" of others' behaviour [V] (Ch 3.3, "Attributional Bias"). In retros: the team attributes sprint failures to individuals ("the PM kept changing requirements") rather than to system conditions (ambiguous ownership, no change-management process). The facilitator redirects to conditions before individuals.

2.  **Actor-observer bias and self-serving bias.** The team attributes its own difficulties to situational factors and other functions' difficulties to their character. Success goes to the team; failure goes to external actors (Ch 3.3). In retros: when "what slowed us down" maps cleanly onto the people outside the room, the facilitator asks what in the team's own process contributed.

3.  **Perception as functional.** The Dearborn and Simon study: different functional roles produce different readings of the same situation [BT] (Ch 3.1). In retros: the front-end engineer and the back-end engineer may have genuinely different accounts of the same sprint event. The facilitator treats divergence as information, not as a dispute.

4.  **Conformity.** The Asch conformity finding: subjects denied the evidence of their own senses when facing a unanimous group; the presence of a single dissenter collapsed conformity (Ch 9.2, "Conformity and Deviance"). In retros: written-first input before verbal discussion breaks the conformity dynamic. Once someone has written their delta, they are less likely to suppress it when the room converges on a different reading.

5.  **Groupthink.** Groups prioritise cohesion over critical thinking; symptoms include self-censorship, illusion of unanimity, and suppression of minority views (Ch 6.6, "Disadvantages of Group Decisions"). In retros: high-performing, cohesive teams are especially at risk. The team that "agrees too quickly" is a groupthink signal. Preventatives from OB: assign a devil's advocate, encourage everyone to speak, leaders withhold initial positions.

6.  **Process vs relationship conflict.** Process conflict — disagreement about how to do something — "can actually lead to improved performance" [V] (Ch 6.4, "Conflict"). Relationship conflict is generally harmful. In retros: the facilitator's job is to keep disagreement about what went wrong and what to try next firmly in process-conflict territory, and to interrupt personalisation before it becomes relationship conflict.

7.  **Group cohesiveness and its double edge.** High cohesiveness increases member satisfaction and group power but correlates with high performance only when the group accepts the organisation's goals [V, paraphrased] (Ch 9.2, "Consequences of Group Cohesiveness"). In retros: a tight, proud team may be resistant to surfacing real problems because doing so feels disloyal. The facilitator frames truth-telling as the team's strength, not as criticism.

8.  **Communication encoding-decoding-noise.** The basic model names the structural condition: the encoder's intended message and the decoder's received message differ because of noise (Ch 11.1). In retros: the lead's "I thought I was clear about the priority" and the team's "I didn't know that was the priority" are both accurate accounts of a communication system with noise in it. The retro's job is to name the noise, not adjudicate whose fault it was.

9.  **Six barriers to effective decision-making** as a diagnostic set (Ch 6.4): bounded rationality, escalation of commitment, time constraints, uncertainty, personal biases (including confirmation bias), conflict. In retros: the experiment that the team keeps proposing but never running may be blocked by escalation of commitment to an existing approach, not by lack of motivation.

10.  **Motivation frameworks for the helpless team.** Expectancy theory: motivation = E1 (effort-performance expectancy) × E2 (performance-outcome expectancy) × valence (Ch 7.3). A team that says "nothing we can do" may have low E1 (effort won't change performance), low E2 (even if we perform, the org won't change), or low valence (the outcomes on offer aren't worth it). The diagnosis matters for the response. SDT: intrinsic motivation decreases as extrinsic reward increases [V] (Ch 7.2, "Self-Determination Theory"). A team that is externally rewarded for velocity may have lost intrinsic motivation for quality — relevant when retros surface recurring quality issues.

11.  **Big Five personality traits as contextual diagnostics.** When relevant: high conscientiousness predicts engagement with experiment tracking; high agreeableness may suppress the willingness to name a problem; high neuroticism may amplify stress responses during a difficult retro (Ch 11.7). These are not categories to apply to individuals but patterns to notice in the aggregate and design the retro format around.

12.  **Reinforcement schedules and team norms.** Variable-ratio schedules produce the most extinction-resistant behaviour (Ch 4.2, Table 4.2). In retros: team norms that are reinforced unpredictably (sometimes the retrospective actions get done, sometimes they don't) are extremely hard to extinguish. The retro experiment-review phase is the scheduled reinforcement that makes action-completion norms durable.

13.  **Critical thinking and logical fallacies.** The OB text catalogues seven fallacies (Ch 6.5, Table 6.2): non sequitur, false cause, ad hominem, genetic fallacy, appeal to tradition, bandwagon, appeal to emotion. In retros: "we've always done it this way" (appeal to tradition) and "everyone agrees this is the problem" (bandwagon) are the two most common fallacies that prevent the insight phase from producing a genuine root-cause analysis.

## Questions to Ask During Retro

### Phase 0 — Setup

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Manager's presence may trigger conformity | (Coach to lead, before retro): "Remind the room that the manager is here to hear, not to evaluate, and that they'll speak last." |
| High-cohesion team resistant to surfacing problems | "The most cohesive teams are honest about what's not working — that's what makes them cohesive. Today that's our goal." |

### Phase 1 — Priming

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Team appears low-motivation / helpless | "Before we get into the data — on a scale of 1-5, how much do you believe this retro will change something? Drop it in chat. We'll work with whatever the number is." |
| Attribution bias expected (complaints will be about people outside the room) | "We're going to focus today on what's in our control and in our process. We can name external constraints — but let's not stop there." |

### Phase 2 — Data Gathering

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Written-first to break conformity | "Everyone writes their positives and deltas in chat before anyone speaks. We'll read the full set together first." |
| Groupthink — quick consensus | "We've converged fast. Before we lock this in, I want a devil's-advocate view. Who can give me the case against this reading?" |
| Attribution bias (external blame) | "That's the external factor. What did our own process contribute to that?" |

### Phase 3 — Insight / Cause Analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Process conflict becoming personal | "I want to keep this as a question about process — what the system was doing — rather than about people. Let's reframe: what was the condition that made this hard?" |
| Escalation of commitment blocking new experiment | "We've tried a version of this before. What would be different about trying it now? What was the condition last time that made it not stick?" |
| Logical fallacy — "we've always done it this way" | "Help me understand the argument: what's the reason this approach should continue to work even though it's producing this outcome?" |
| Helpless team ("nothing we can do") | "Where do you actually have freedom to act? Even a small percentage of the problem that's yours to change — what's in that zone?" |

### Phase 4 — Experiment Design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Experiment addressing symptom not system condition | "This experiment would change the symptom. Which of the conditions we named — the noise in the communication, the ambiguous ownership — does it actually address?" |
| Team motivation unclear about experiment value | "On a scale of 1-5: how confident is everyone that doing this experiment would actually improve the outcome? Drop it in chat." |

### Phase 5 — Close

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Conformity-produced consensus on experiment | "Before we commit — is there anyone who privately thinks this experiment is the wrong one? Now's the time." |
| Closing the communication loop | "What's the one thing we learned today about how our team communicates that we'd want to carry forward?" |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| Problems map cleanly to people outside the room | Fundamental attribution error or actor-observer bias | Ask what in the team's own process contributed before moving to Phase 3 |
| Team agrees very quickly in Phase 2 | Groupthink or conformity producing false unanimity | Ask for a devil's-advocate view; use written-first input before verbal round |
| Disagreement becomes personal | Process conflict sliding into relationship conflict | Redirect: "what was the condition in the system, not the action of the person?" |
| "Nothing we can do" | Helplessness — but which kind? Low E1 (effort won't change performance), low E2 (performance won't change outcomes), or low valence (outcomes not worth it)? | Name the expectancy component; address the right lever |
| High-cohesion team, fast consensus, no surface problems | Groupthink in a high-performing team | Invite a devil's-advocate view explicitly; high cohesion makes groupthink more likely, not less |
| Same experiment proposed but never completed | Reinforcement-schedule problem; variable-ratio schedule is maintaining the norm | Address the commitment mechanism in Phase 0.5; make follow-through scheduled, not random |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this distillation when:
- Attribution bias is shaping the data-gathering phase — complaints landing entirely on external actors or on individuals, without the team's own process in the frame.
- Conformity or groupthink is producing fast consensus that the facilitator suspects is false unanimity.
- Process conflict is becoming personal — the distinction between what went wrong (process) and who is to blame (relationship) needs naming.
- The helpless team needs a diagnosis: which expectancy component is low, and what is the right lever?
- A high-cohesion team is suspected of groupthink precisely because of its cohesion.

This source is the facilitator's and coach's diagnostic vocabulary for what is happening in the room. It does not supply the retro's protocol, facilitation moves, or experiment structure. Pair with the Field Guide for protocol and Liberating Structures for facilitation moves.

## Worked Example

A team's Phase 2 generates fourteen stickies. Thirteen refer to people outside the room: the PM, the infrastructure team, the design partner. The lead recognises the external-attribution pattern. Before Phase 3 opens, the lead asks: "We have a lot of data about what people outside the room did. What did our own process contribute?"

Two stickies appear: "We accepted ambiguous requirements without asking for clarification" and "We didn't raise the dependency until two days before the deadline." Both are internal. The field has shifted from external attribution to the team's own process.

Phase 3: the team is converging fast on "communication breakdown" as the cause. The lead recognises the pattern: consensus is arriving before the field has been fully examined. They ask: "Before we land on this — is there a devil's-advocate view? Who could make the case that this was not a communication breakdown?" One person notes: "The information was there. We just didn't act on it together." This is the process-conflict vs communication distinction from OB.

Phase 4: the proposed experiment is "more frequent syncs with the PM." The lead asks: which expectancy component does this address? If E2 is the problem (the team believes the PM won't change outcomes even if they perform), the sync frequency won't help. The team realises E2 is indeed low. The experiment needs to address the relationship with the PM, not the frequency of contact.

The attribution-bias, conformity, process-vs-relationship-conflict, and expectancy-theory framings all trace to Organizational Behavior (Ch 3.3, "Attributional Bias"; Ch 9.2, "Conformity and Deviance"; Ch 6.4, "Conflict"; Ch 7.3).

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Treating external attribution ("the PM, the infrastructure, the org") as complete data without probing for what the team's own process contributed.
- Accepting quick consensus as genuine agreement rather than recognising conformity or groupthink producing false unanimity.
- Letting retro disagreement slide from process conflict ("we disagree about what went wrong") into relationship conflict ("this is about who's at fault").
- Missing the distinction between a helpless team (low E1/E2, address the system) and a demotivated team (low valence, address the incentives).
- Diagnosing recurring failure as individual performance without checking whether a reinforcement-schedule problem — unpredictable action follow-through — is maintaining the norm.
- Letting "we've always done it this way" or "everyone thinks this is the problem" land as analysis rather than naming them as logical fallacies.
- Running data gathering verbally without written-first input, allowing the loudest or most senior voice to anchor the room before the team's data is on the table.
- Ignoring the high-cohesion team's resistance to surfacing real problems, accepting the surface consensus as the team's actual view.
