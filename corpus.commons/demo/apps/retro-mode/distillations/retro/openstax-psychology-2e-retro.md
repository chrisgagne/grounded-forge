# OpenStax Psychology 2e, Retro Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2020). *Psychology 2e*. Senior contributing author Rose M. Spielman. ISBN 978-1-951693-23-7 (digital). Licence: **CC BY-NC-SA 4.0**. Scope: open-nc.

## Retro Relevance

Psychology 2e is the facilitator's and coach's cognitive-science substrate for understanding what is happening in the team's heads during a retro: why their accounts of the last sprint are systematically biased (hindsight bias, availability heuristic, anchoring, confirmation bias), why conformity pressures produce false consensus (Asch), why situational factors shape behaviour more than participants assume (fundamental attribution error, Milgram, Zimbardo), and why motivation may be structurally depleted rather than personally lacking (overjustification effect, extrinsic reward crowding out intrinsic, Yerkes-Dodson and stress load). Memory is reconstructive; the team's shared story of the sprint is a co-constructed account shaped by who spoke first, who has authority, and what the group has collectively decided to believe. The retro process — written-first, phase-sequenced, one experiment at a time — is the structural response to these cognitive conditions. The facilitator who understands them can name the pattern without naming an individual.

## Key Concepts for Retro

1.  **Hindsight bias.** "Belief that the event just experienced was predictable" [V] (Ch 7.3, Table 7.3). In retros: once the sprint's outcome is known, what went wrong feels obvious. This bias makes the team harsh on past-sprint decisions that were made under genuine uncertainty and may make experiments seem trivially obvious ("we just needed to communicate better"). The facilitator's move: reconstruct what was actually known at the decision point, not what is known now.

2.  **Availability heuristic.** "Decision is based upon either an available precedent or an example that may be faulty" [V] (Ch 7.3, Table 7.3). In retros: the most vivid or recent incident dominates the team's theory of the sprint. The loud late-sprint failure crowds out the quieter mid-sprint wins and the structural conditions that produced both.

3.  **Anchoring bias.** "Tendency to focus on one particular piece of information when making decisions or problem-solving" [V] (Ch 7.3, Table 7.3). In retros: the first sticky posted, the first thing the lead says, the first pattern named — all anchor the room. Written-first input before discussion breaks the anchor. Leaders withholding their view until the team has produced its data prevents authority-anchoring.

4.  **Confirmation bias.** "Focuses on information that confirms existing beliefs" [V] (Ch 7.3, Table 7.3). In retros: the team that has already decided what the problem is will read the stickies to confirm that theory. Disconfirming data gets passed over. The facilitator's move: name the disconfirming sticky explicitly and ask the group to account for it.

5.  **Fundamental attribution error.** "People tend to overemphasize internal factors as explanations… for the behavior of other people" and "underestimate the power of the situation" [V] (Ch 12.1). In retros: the team attributes the sprint's difficulties to individuals (inside or outside the team) rather than to conditions. The coach's vocabulary is: situational pressure, structural constraint, information state — not character assessment.

6.  **Actor-observer bias and self-serving bias.** Actors attribute their own behaviour situationally; observers attribute others' dispositionally. Self-serving bias attributes success to the team's skill and failure to outside factors (Ch 12.1). In retros: the team's reading of "what we did well" and "what slowed us down" will follow this pattern by default. The facilitator surfaces the structural conditions behind both.

7.  **Memory as reconstructive.** Memory is subject to suggestibility, bias, and misinformation effects [AR] (Ch 8.1; Ch 8.3). Schacter's seven sins include "bias: current beliefs reshape memories" (Ch 8.3, Table 8.1). In retros: the team's shared story of the sprint is partly constructed in the retro room. The facilitator who names a problem first will find participants' memories reorganising around that framing. Written-first input captures accounts before the group's framing lands.

8.  **Conformity — Asch line studies.** Seventy-six per cent of participants conformed to group pressure on at least one trial; a single dissenter dropped conformity to near zero [BT] (Ch 12.4). In retros: the team that generates data verbally, starting with the most senior voice, will produce a conformity-shaped dataset. The fix is structural: written-first, individual input before discussion.

9.  **Normative vs informational social influence.** *Normative social influence* is conformity to fit in; *informational social influence* is conformity to learn the right answer (Ch 12.4). In retros: when a team member suppresses a delta because others seem fine, that is normative influence. When a team member defers to the lead's read because the lead must know better, that is informational influence. Both suppress real data; both require written-first structure to counteract.

10.  **Motivation — intrinsic vs extrinsic and the overjustification effect.** "As the level of extrinsic rewards increases, the amount of intrinsic motivation decreases" [V] (Ch 10.1). The overjustification effect: an activity once done for its own sake is reclassified as work when extrinsic reinforcement arrives (Ch 10.1). In retros: a team that has been velocity-tracked, OKR-graded, and management-reviewed for every metric may have had its intrinsic motivation for craft and quality crowded out. Recurring retro themes about quality and technical debt may be overjustification-effect presentations.

11.  **Reinforcement schedules and team norms.** Variable-ratio schedules produce the highest and most extinction-resistant response rates [V] (Ch 6.3, Table 6.3). In retros: the team norm of completing retrospective experiments is reinforced on a variable-ratio schedule — sometimes they get done, sometimes they don't, no predictable pattern. Variable-ratio schedules are extremely hard to extinguish. The Phase 0.5 experiment review is the scheduled reinforcement that makes follow-through norms durable: the schedule itself is the intervention.

12.  **Stress — Yerkes-Dodson and general adaptation syndrome.** Optimal arousal varies with task complexity: complex tasks favour lower arousal (Ch 10.1, "arousal theory"). Selye's GAS: alarm → resistance → exhaustion (Ch 14.1). In retros: a team that has been in sustained resistance or approaching exhaustion will have degraded problem-solving capacity during the retro itself. The facilitator adjusts the pace and depth of the insight phase to the team's stress state. A heavily loaded team may need a shorter, lighter retro with one experiment rather than a comprehensive root-cause analysis.

13.  **Groupthink.** Groups prioritise cohesion over critical thinking; symptoms include self-censorship, perceived invulnerability, and illusion of unanimity (Ch 12.4). In retros: high-performing teams under deadline pressure are especially susceptible — the cost of surfacing a real problem feels higher than the cost of staying silent. Preventatives: leaders withhold initial positions, private written accounts before discussion, explicit invitation of minority views.

## Questions to Ask During Retro

### Phase 0 — Setup

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Authority-anchoring risk (manager or lead likely to set the frame) | (Coach to lead): "Can you hold your read of the sprint until after the team has put their data on the table? Your view will carry more weight once they've had a chance to think independently." |
| Safety check returns 1-2 | "A low safety number tells me something in the container isn't safe enough yet. Before we go into sprint data, what would make it safer to speak openly?" |

### Phase 0.5 — Experiment Review

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Experiments from last retro weren't done | "Let's not punish the miss — let's learn from it. What made it hard to follow through? Was the experiment too big, was it unowned, or was there something in the system that worked against it?" |
| Variable-ratio reinforcement pattern (sometimes done, sometimes not) | "I notice we're inconsistent on follow-through. What would make this more predictable — what does the commitment mechanism need to look like?" |

### Phase 1 — Priming

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Team is exhausted / high stress | "Given how the sprint went, I want to check: are we in a place where a full insight-and-experiment retro is useful, or would a shorter, lighter retro give us more? The team decides." |
| Overjustification effect suspected | "One question before we start — on the work itself, apart from metrics and deadlines, what part of the sprint were you actually proud of? Drop it in chat." |

### Phase 2 — Data Gathering

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Anchoring from first sticky | "We've got one strong theme forming. Before it anchors the room — is there data that doesn't fit that pattern? What are we not seeing?" |
| Hindsight bias arriving | "It's clear now what went wrong. What was actually known at the time — not what we know looking back — that would have pointed to this?" |
| Conformity producing thin data | "Written-first: everyone posts their full set before we read anything aloud. We'll look at the whole room's data together." |

### Phase 3 — Insight / Cause Analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Fundamental attribution error (blame landing on a person) | "What were the situational conditions that person was operating under — information, authority, time, support? Let's start there." |
| Groupthink consensus forming too quickly | "We're converging fast. I want the dissenting view — if someone privately thinks this is wrong, now is the time." |
| Confirmation bias filtering data | "What's the sticky or the moment that doesn't fit the pattern we're building? Why are we not making it central?" |

### Phase 4 — Experiment Design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Intrinsic motivation depleted | "This experiment is about the metric. What experiment would the team actually want to run because it would make the work better — not just the number better?" |
| Experiment too big for stress-loaded team | "Is this experiment the right scale for where the team is right now? What's the smallest version that would still tell us something?" |

### Phase 5 — Close

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Self-serving summary arriving | "Let's check the closing summary: are we attributing everything that went well to us and everything that went wrong to the situation? What's the honest read on both?" |
| Memory reconstruction risk in the summary | "Before I summarise what we decided: what does each of you take as the one commitment from today? Drop it in chat. Let's make sure the summary matches what you actually heard." |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| All stickies about the last sprint converge on one cause within the first few minutes of discussion | Anchoring or authority-anchoring is collapsing the room's genuine spread of views | Pause discussion; run a second written-first round with the explicit prompt "what data doesn't fit the story forming on the board?" |
| The team's account of a sprint decision — made under uncertainty — is treated as an obvious failure | Hindsight bias is reshaping past uncertainty into present obviousness | Ask: what was actually known at the decision point? Reconstruct the information state at the time, not what is visible now (Ch 7.3, Table 7.3) |
| Team members stop contributing after one or two senior voices have spoken | Normative or informational social influence is suppressing genuine views | Switch to written-first; ask everyone to submit one more item privately before the next discussion turn (Ch 12.4) |
| A retro experiment produces a working agreement the team immediately commits to, but it looks suspiciously like what the lead wanted | Groupthink consensus — perceived unanimity without genuine individual endorsement | Before closing: each person states their real confidence level privately in writing; surface dissent explicitly (Ch 12.4) |
| A team's recurring theme is "we just need to communicate better" | Confirmation bias is filtering data toward the simplest available narrative | Name the narrative explicitly and ask for the sticky that doesn't fit it; rerun insight with the disconfirming data made central (Ch 7.3, Table 7.3) |
| Recurring retro experiments go undone and the team attributes this to laziness or low commitment | Variable-ratio reinforcement is maintaining inconsistent follow-through — not character | Name the reinforcement schedule; redesign Phase 0.5 as a structured commitment review, not a guilt check (Ch 6.3, Table 6.3) |
| The team's retro themes are almost entirely about metrics, velocity, and delivery targets | Overjustification effect: extrinsic metrics have crowded out intrinsic motivation for craft | Ask the craft question in Phase 1 priming: "what part of the sprint were you actually proud of — apart from any number?" (Ch 10.1) |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this distillation when:
- The team's data-gathering phase is producing thin, conformist, or anchored data and you need a structural explanation for why.
- A retro surfaces blame on a person and you need the situational-attribution vocabulary to redirect toward conditions (fundamental attribution error, actor-observer bias).
- The team is exhausted or stress-loaded and you need to calibrate the retro's scope to their cognitive state (Yerkes-Dodson, GAS).
- The team's recurring failure to do retro experiments is being treated as a motivation problem — the reinforcement-schedule framing is the more accurate diagnosis.
- A working agreement is forming too quickly and you suspect groupthink or normative social influence.

This source fires across every retro phase. Its primary contribution is not technique but cognitive-science grounding: the retro's structural choices (written-first, leader-withholds-view, one-experiment-at-a-time) are the correct structural responses to these documented patterns.

## Worked Example

A team is six Sprints into a major delivery push. Phase 0 safety check returns 3 out of 5. The lead says before written-first begins: "I think the main issue this sprint was our estimation discipline." The team proceeds to data gathering.

Phase 2 produces 26 stickies. Twenty-two are about estimation. Four — written by the two most junior team members — are about unclear acceptance criteria arriving late from the product side.

The facilitator names the anchoring effect: the lead's framing landed before written-first, and the data reflects it (Ch 7.3, Table 7.3). The facilitator asks the lead to hold any further framing and runs a second silent write for the prompt: "what slowed us down that we haven't captured yet?" Six new stickies surface, four of which cluster on late acceptance criteria.

Phase 3: the team begins attributing late criteria to the product manager's work style (fundamental attribution error). The facilitator asks: "what were the conditions — workload, access, information — that made late criteria the product manager's local rational move?" (Ch 12.1, "Fundamental Attribution Error"). The team identifies that the product manager has no visibility into when the team actually begins story-level work.

Phase 4: one experiment — a shared ready-signal protocol where the team flags when a story enters sprint-ready queue, giving the product manager a visible trigger for criteria finalisation. The lead commits to presenting this protocol to the product manager, not asking the team to manage it alone.

The safety check of 3 also flags a stress-load concern. The facilitator checks: is a full root-cause retro the right session, or is the team's cognitive load too high? The team confirms they can do the experiment design but cannot do a full causal-loop analysis this sprint. Facilitator accepts this and scopes accordingly (Ch 10.1, arousal theory; Ch 14.1, GAS).

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Accepting hindsight-bias-shaped accounts of sprint decisions as accurate evaluations of those decisions.
- Letting the first thing said (or the most senior person's view) anchor the room's reading of the sprint before the team's own data is on the table.
- Running data gathering verbally before written-first, systematically producing conformity-shaped data instead of the team's real view.
- Treating a team's recurring failure to complete retro experiments as a motivation problem rather than recognising the variable-ratio reinforcement schedule maintaining the norm.
- Missing the overjustification effect: a team whose craft motivation has been crowded out by external metrics will produce retro data about metrics, not about the work.
- Accepting a team under GAS-exhaustion through a full insight-and-experiment retro when a shorter, lighter session would serve them better and honour their state.
- Allowing groupthink to produce a consensus that no individual in the room privately endorses.
- Treating functional-group divergence in the team's account of the sprint as inconsistency or dishonesty rather than as partial perception shaped by different roles.
- Closing the retro with a summary that is itself the facilitator's interpretation rather than a check against what participants actually committed to.

The majority of the source's content — sensation and perception, developmental psychology, personality theory, clinical and abnormal psychology, industrial-organisational psychology, and health psychology — does not fire directly in a team retro context and is not reflected in this distillation. The source's CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 licence means this distillation inherits the non-commercial and ShareAlike obligations.
