# Krivitsky, Larman & Flemm, Org Topologies Primer — Retro Distillation

**Source:** Alexey Krivitsky, Craig Larman & Roland Flemm (2025). *Strategic Org Design: The Primer*. Org Topologies™ 2025 Edition. February 2025. Licence: CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://orgtopologies.com.

## Retro Relevance

The Primer is not a retrospective-facilitation text and does not address the team-internal iterative ceremony. Its applicability to the retro axis runs through a specific channel: **team-archetype-mismatch as a recurring retro topic**. When the same impediment surfaces three retros running — when the team keeps naming the same dependency, the same cross-silo handoff, the same scope-creep pattern, the same "we can't actually own this end-to-end" — the team is *retrospecting against a structural constraint, not a process problem*. The Primer is the corpus's reference for *naming the constraint in precise vocabulary* and for distinguishing *team-experimentable* moves from *structural moves requiring escalation*.

This is principally a Phase 3 (insight) and Phase 4 (experiment design) projection, with a Phase 0.5 (experiment review) hook for the recurring-experiment pattern. In Phase 3, when the team's "what" data clusters around topics that are downstream of the team's archetype rather than inside it, OT's named-archetype language ("we're TASKS-2 trying to deliver CAPS-3", "we're CAPS-2 inside a Resource-Topology surround") gives the team the insight vocabulary that lets them see *the constraint is above the team boundary*. In Phase 4, this matters for experiment design: 15% Solutions (Liberating Structures) and the small-experiment retro discipline both ask *what can we change inside our locus of control?* — and the Primer's strategic-design stance gives the team a clear way to distinguish what is genuinely 15%-experimentable (process moves within the existing archetype) from what is *structural and requires escalation* (moves that change the archetype itself, which the team cannot do unilaterally).

The Primer's stance that *single-team interventions inside an unchanged surround don't deliver* — "A single high-performing team can't deliver its full potential if surrounding structures and processes remain unchanged" — is the retro-relevant warning. A team that retrospects on the same structural impediment cycle after cycle, generating well-intentioned 15% experiments each time, is being asked to compensate for an archetype mismatch the team cannot fix from inside. The Primer gives the retro facilitator the framing language for escalating: *this isn't a retro experiment, it's a topology question — and Open Kanban's Holistic-Systemic value already says "no single part of a system can ever bring overall improvement"*.

The Primer's MAP discipline — "going to the hands-on workplace to understand how the units work" — also gives the retro a Phase 1 (priming) move: when the team is unsure whether the recurring topic is process or structure, *mapping the team's actual archetype against the work being demanded* is the priming activity that surfaces the answer.

The Primer is *not* a substitute for the retro methodology itself (Field Guide, Open Practice Library, Liberating Structures), the systems-thinking diagnostic (SSDL system archetypes), or the engagement-equity facilitation discipline (FLO). It is a *vocabulary* and a *constraint-classification* tool for the team-archetype-mismatch subset of recurring retro topics.

## Key Concepts for Retro

1.  **Team-archetype-mismatch as the named recurring-topic pattern.** When the same impediment surfaces multiple retros, the OT vocabulary lets the team name precisely what is happening: *"we are a CAPS-2 archetype (multi-skill but incomplete in capabilities) being asked to deliver CAPS-3 outcomes (end-to-end capability ownership); the cross-silo handoff that produces our recurring impediment is the structural mismatch, not a process problem."* The retro insight is: *the constraint is structural, not in our locus of control as a team alone.* (Source: Krivitsky, Larman & Flemm, *Org Topologies Primer* 2025, p. 5, "Language of Org Design"; pp. 6–7, axes)

2.  **The 15%-Solution vs structural-escalation distinction.** Liberating Structures' 15% Solutions ask "what can you do within your locus of control?" The OT vocabulary gives the team a way to distinguish *process moves within the existing archetype* (genuine 15% experiments) from *moves that change the archetype itself* (which require escalation outside the team). A team trying to use 15% Solutions to fix an archetype mismatch is being asked to compensate for a structural constraint they cannot change unilaterally. (p. 4, "The Hidden Complexities of Org Change" — single-team-interventions warning)

3.  **The single-team-island warning as retro-fatigue cause.** "A single high-performing team can't deliver its full potential if surrounding structures and processes remain unchanged." A team experiencing retro fatigue — generating experiments that don't move the needle — may be retrospecting inside an unchanged surround. The Primer's warning gives the team a name for the failure mode and a clear escalation criterion. (p. 4)

4.  **The Resource-framing detection.** "The Directing Archetypes... treat the Doing Archetypes as 'resources,' matching skill demand with skill supply." When a retro surfaces that the team is being framed externally as a resource pool (rotated through assignments, denied voice in design, denied end-to-end accountability), the Resource-framing detection is a Phase 3 insight worth naming. The retro experiment becomes: how do we signal upward that this framing is producing the impediments we keep retrospecting on? (p. 11)

5.  **The local-optimisation warning as retro-experiment design discipline.** "If each element (e.g. policies, structures) is optimized in isolation, the performance of the whole system may actually decline." When a retro proposes an experiment that changes one element (a process step, a ceremony format, a reward) without addressing systems-fit, the Primer's warning applies. Retro experiments should pass the systems-fit test: *will this change improve the system goal, or only the local element?* (p. 16)

6.  **The MAP step as a Phase 1 priming activity.** When a team is unsure whether the recurring retro topic is process or structure, OT's MAP step — "going to the hands-on workplace to understand how the units work" — can be inverted into a retro priming activity: *map our own team's archetype, then map the work being demanded*. The mismatch (if any) is the priming insight that frames the data-gathering phase. (p. 16)

7.  **Periodic re-mapping as a learning-loop-closure cadence.** "Periodic re-mapping with Org Topologies is an easy and fast way to complete a learning loop of feedback and adapting." The retro is already an iterative cadence; OT re-mapping every N retros (e.g. quarterly) is a Phase 5 (close) artefact that tests whether the team's archetype has shifted in response to the previous structural escalations. (p. 19)

8.  **The Driving-archetype car metaphor as a team-aspiration frame.** "Imagine a car with passengers, any one of whom can drive, who together decide where they want to go, drive and arrive, and then go on." When a team's retros surface a wish for more shared authority and distributed decision-making, the Driving-archetype frame gives them the language for what they're describing without immediately reaching for a framework (Holacracy, self-organisation). (p. 9, "Driving Archetypes")

9.  **Elevating-Kata-as-experiment as the retro-experiment shape.** The Primer's framing — "thoughtful experiments to change the organizational structure, processes, policies, and so forth" — is structurally identical to the retro experiment discipline (one or two owned experiments per retro, with hypotheses and check-points). The Primer endorses the retro shape from the org-design side: structural change happens through small experiments, not pronouncements. (p. 19)

10.  **The framework-fatigue retro pattern.** "Implementing an industry-standard management framework is key to success... is the pitch from consulting companies big and small." When a retro surfaces team frustration with a corporate framework rollout (SAFe, LeSS, Spotify model), the Primer's chain-of-fit gives the team a way to distinguish the framework's discipline (which they have to operate inside) from the topology question (which the framework is one tool for). This is escalation framing: *the framework isn't wrong, but it isn't a substitute for asking what topology the work actually needs.* (p. 22)

11.  **AI-tool adoption retros and the human-monitoring responsibility framing.** "We predict that monitoring will become an important responsibility for human workers." When a team's retros surface AI-tool-adoption discomfort, OT's strategic-AI stance gives the team a productive frame: the team is becoming the monitoring-and-elevation layer that the AI agents need, rather than being replaced by them. The retro experiment becomes: how do we make our monitoring role visible and valued, not invisible? (p. 20)

12.  **Co-creation as a retro-quality aspiration.** "A co-creating end-to-end team is not just about speed and flow." The Primer names co-creation as a property that increases with rightward and upward movement on the OT map. Retros where the team feels they are co-creating (vs being asked to validate decisions made elsewhere) are aspirationally Adaptive-Topology in shape; the Primer's vocabulary gives the team a name for that aspiration. (p. 27, "Closing note")

## Questions to Ask During Retro

### Phase 1: Priming (if a structural recurring topic is suspected)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Test whether the recurring topic is structural | Has the same topic surfaced two or more retros in a row, and have previous experiments not moved the needle? If yes, the recurring pattern may be structural, not process. |
| Surface the team's current archetype | Using the OT vocabulary, what is this team's archetype today (TASKS-2 multi-skill incomplete? CAPS-2 capability-focused multi-skill? CAPS-3 end-to-end capability ownership)? |
| Surface the work-demanded archetype | What archetype would the work being demanded require (e.g. CAPS-3 end-to-end, not CAPS-2 with cross-silo handoff)? |
| Use the MAP activity as a priming move | Spend 10 minutes mapping the team's current archetype and the work-demanded archetype on the OT axes. The mismatch (if any) is the priming insight. |

### Phase 2: Data gathering (when archetype mismatch is the frame)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Cluster data on the structural-vs-process axis | Of the data the team is generating, which items are inside our locus of control (process moves within the existing archetype), and which are outside it (moves that would change the archetype)? |
| Test for Resource-framing language | Is the team or its surround using "resources" language for people? Does work get assigned by skill-pool allocation rather than by team mandate? |
| Test for single-team-island patterns | Is the team a previously-fixed island in an unchanged surround? Are the impediments coming from the surround rather than from inside? |

### Phase 3: Insight (when the team is naming structural impediments)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Name the archetype mismatch precisely | "We are a {current archetype} being asked to deliver {work-demanded archetype} outcomes, and the structural mismatch is producing the recurring impediment." Precision beats vague "siloed" or "cross-functional issues" language. |
| Distinguish 15%-experimentable from structural-escalation | Which of the moves we could make are genuinely within our locus of control (15% Solutions), and which would require structural change above the team boundary (escalation)? |
| Detect the topology-mismatch pattern | Is this a Resource-Topology-drag pattern (utilisation pressure preventing us from owning end-to-end)? A Delivery-Topology-bloat pattern (we're delivering features that aren't validated against outcome)? An Adaptive-Topology-variance pattern (we're delivering high-variance results because the variance is structurally accommodated)? |
| Name the local-optimisation amplifier | If we propose multiple individually-defensible 15% experiments, will they combine to improve the system goal, or will they compete? |

### Phase 4: Experiment design (when separating team-level and structural moves)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Frame team-level experiments as topology-adjacent moves | Even within the existing archetype, what experiment moves the team rightward (broader skill mandate) or upward (broader work mandate) on the OT axes by a small amount? |
| Frame structural moves as escalation-with-evidence | If the team identifies a structural move needed, the experiment isn't a team experiment — it's an escalation. Design the escalation: who to, what evidence to attach (the OT mapping itself), what request (a structural experiment, not a permission). |
| Defend against framework-recommendation experiments | "We should adopt SAFe / LeSS / Team Topologies" as a team-level retro experiment is the cargo-cult failure mode. Reframe as: what topology do we need, and what concrete archetype change moves us toward it? |
| Use the Elevating-Kata-as-experiment framing | Structural escalations should propose *experiments*, not decisions: a defined small change with a hypothesis, a check-point, and a re-mapping plan. |
| Plan the re-mapping check-point | When will we re-MAP to see if the team's archetype has actually shifted in response to the experiment / escalation? Tie it to a calendar date. |

### Phase 0.5: Experiment review (when same experiment re-surfaces)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Detect the structural-repeat pattern | Is this the third or fourth retro proposing essentially the same experiment? That is the structural-mismatch detection signal. |
| Reframe from team-failure to structural-constraint | If the experiment isn't taking hold, is the team failing, or is the team retrospecting against a structural constraint it cannot change unilaterally? The Primer's single-team-island warning is the named pattern. |
| Escalate honestly | Open Kanban's Holistic-Systemic value already applies: "no single part of a system can ever bring overall improvement." If the experiment is structural, the retro must escalate, not retry. |

### Phase 5: Close (when retro-OT mapping is part of the cadence)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Schedule the next OT re-mapping | When do we re-MAP the team's archetype? Quarterly is a defensible cadence; tie it to a calendar date. |
| Test for drift toward Resource framing | Is the language of "resources" or "headcount allocation" creeping into how the team is talked about (internally or externally)? That is the named drift pattern. |
| Name emerging Elevating Katas | Which experiments worked here are worth naming as candidate Elevating Katas for future cycles? Naming them builds the team's structural-change vocabulary. |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| The same impediment has surfaced two or more retros running and previous experiments have not moved the needle | The team is retrospecting against a structural constraint, not a process problem | Apply the OT archetype vocabulary in Phase 1 priming: map the team's current archetype and the work-demanded archetype; name the mismatch (p. 5–7, axes) |
| The retro's experiment list looks the same as last retro's — better communication, earlier dependency scheduling, more working sessions | The team is generating 15%-Solutions for a problem that is above the 15% ceiling | Distinguish genuinely team-level moves from structural-escalation moves; reframe the experiment as an escalation if needed (p. 4, "The Hidden Complexities of Org Change") |
| The team frames their recurring problem as "siloed" or "cross-functional issues" without a more precise vocabulary | The team is naming the symptom but not the structural source | Offer the OT archetype vocabulary as a precision tool: what archetype are we, and what archetype does the work demand? |
| A team member uses "resource" language to describe how work is assigned — "we get allocated to tasks," "headcount availability" | Resource-framing by the surround is producing the impediments the team keeps retrospecting on | Name the framing explicitly in Phase 3; design the escalation as a topology request, not a complaint (p. 11) |
| Experiments from multiple retros are well-intentioned, individually defensible, but none has changed the metric the team cares about | Local optimisation: each experiment is tuning one element without addressing systems-fit | Apply the local-optimisation test: will this experiment improve the system goal, or only the local element? (p. 16) |
| The team expresses frustration with a corporate framework rollout without a clear articulation of what they need instead | Framework-fatigue: confusing the framework's discipline with the topology question | Reframe with the OT chain-of-fit: the framework is one tool; the topology question is what archetype does the work actually need? (p. 22) |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this distillation when:
- The same impediment has surfaced two or more retros in a row and previous team-level experiments have not moved the needle — this is the signal that the constraint is structural, not process.
- The retro is about to generate another round of 15%-Solutions and the facilitator needs vocabulary to distinguish team-experimentable moves from structural-escalation moves.
- A retro surfaces resource-framing, framework-fatigue, or AI-adoption discomfort and the team needs a precise vocabulary for the structural dimension of what they are experiencing.
- The team needs to frame an escalation as a precise topology request rather than a complaint.

The Primer does not carry retro methodology (ceremony structure, facilitation moves, experiment design discipline). The Field Guide, Open Practice Library, and Liberating Structures carry the methodology; the Primer is reached for when the retro topic is team-archetype mismatch.

## Worked Example

A team has retrospected on "we keep blocking on the back-end pool" for four sprints. Each retro generates experiments to communicate better with the back-end pool, to plan dependencies earlier, to schedule joint working sessions. None of them moves the needle. Phase 0.5 at Sprint 5's retro: three experiments from three prior retros all show the same result — the coordination improved, the blocking did not.

Phase 1 priming: the facilitator proposes a 10-minute MAP activity. The team maps their current archetype: TASKS-2 (multi-skill dev + test, incomplete). The work being demanded: CAPS-3 (end-to-end capability ownership across front-end and back-end). The mismatch is visible before Phase 2 begins.

Phase 3 insight: "The cross-silo handoff between our TASKS-2 team and the TASKS-1 back-end pool is structural. No 15% experiment inside our current archetype dissolves it." (p. 5–7, axes; p. 4, single-team-island warning). The team names the distinction: communication experiments are 15%-experimentable; the handoff itself is structural-escalation.

Phase 4: the experiment is not a team experiment. It is an escalation: the team prepares an OT vocabulary-attached escalation showing the archetype mismatch, the three experiments already tried, and a precise request — a structural experiment to pilot end-to-end CAPS-2 coverage for one capability area. The escalation is designed with a named recipient, an attached hypothesis, and a re-mapping check-point in 60 days (p. 19, "Periodic re-mapping").

Phase 5: the team schedules the re-MAP 60 days out. The local-optimisation test is applied to any remaining team-level experiment before it is committed: will this improve the system goal, or only one element? (p. 16).

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid (when projecting OT onto retro)

1. **Using OT vocabulary as a blame-deflection tool.** "It's the structure, not us" can become a way of avoiding genuinely-team-level moves the team could make. The Primer's strong systems-stance must not collapse the team's locus-of-control discipline (15% Solutions). Pair them: some moves are structural-escalation, others are 15%-experimentable; both can be in the same retro.

2. **Mapping-as-substitute for the retro itself.** OT mapping is a Phase 1 priming move and a Phase 5 close artefact — not a substitute for the data-gathering, insight, or experiment-design work. A retro that becomes an OT-mapping workshop has stopped being a retro.

3. **Single-element experiments without systems-fit.** Proposing an experiment that changes one process element (a ceremony format, a tool, a role) without addressing the surround. The Primer's local-optimisation warning applies: "performance of the whole system may actually decline."

4. **Framework-recommendation as the retro experiment.** "We should adopt Holacracy / self-organisation / a new framework" as a team-level retro experiment is the cargo-cult pattern projected into retro. Reframe as topology question: what archetype do we want, what small move gets us closer?

5. **Structural-escalation framed as complaint.** When the retro surfaces a structural constraint requiring escalation, framing the escalation as a complaint ("we need more authority") rather than as a precise topology request ("our work demands CAPS-3, our archetype is CAPS-2; here's the named mismatch") gets the escalation dismissed.

6. **Re-mapping without participant continuity.** If the OT re-mapping involves different participants each time, the comparative analysis (has the archetype shifted?) is degraded. The Primer's MAP step is about engagement-with-the-people; that engagement discipline propagates.

7. **Mistaking aspirational topology framing for current-state assessment.** A team that *wants* to be Driving-archetype Adaptive-Topology is not currently that. Use the vocabulary to name the aspiration and the current state distinctly; conflating them blocks the actual elevation work.
