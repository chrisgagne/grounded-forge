# US Army TC 25-20, Retrospective Distillation

**Source:** Headquarters, Department of the Army (1993). *A Leader's Guide to After-Action Reviews*. Training Circular 25-20, 30 September 1993. Licence: Public domain (17 USC §105 — US Government work product). Scope: open.

## Retro Relevance

TC 25-20 is Army AAR doctrine, not a retro methodology, but several of its epistemic commitments transfer directly to the iterative team-internal retro. The load-bearing contributions are: the discovery-over-critique frame (the team discovers what happened; it is not told), the open-ended question discipline (narrative invitation over yes/no), the train-to-weakness allocation rule, the closing-comments rule (summarise, end positively, link to next iteration, then leave so the team can talk privately), and the follow-up principle (the benefit lives in applying results, not in running the review).

TC 25-20 is *moderate fit* on the retro axis: it supplies a durable subset of facilitation moves the retro can borrow, but the canonical retro methodology lives in the Field Guide, Open Practice Library, and Liberating Structures. The TC's primary gift to the retro is epistemic clarity: the AAR is not a grading exercise, never asks yes/no questions, and places the benefit entirely in what happens after the review. Retros routinely violate all three. The TC also supplies the formal logic for the experiment-review phase: when last retro's experiments were not done, the principle is that the benefit lives in applying results, and the correct response is review-without-punishment, not blame.

## Key Concepts for Retro

1. **Discovery over critique.** "Soldiers discover for themselves what happened, why it happened, and how to sustain strengths and improve on weaknesses" (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, Ch 1, "Definition and Purpose"). A critique transmits one observer's view; the retro aggregates and tests views across all participants. This is the epistemic argument against the retro-as-report form.

2. **Climate as operational concern.** "The environment and climate surrounding an AAR must be one in which the soldiers and leaders openly and honestly discuss what actually transpired" (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, Preface). Participant engagement is directly related to the atmosphere created at the opening — climate is not a soft concern, it determines the quality of data that surfaces.

3. **The retro does not grade.** "An AAR does not grade success or failure. There are always weaknesses to improve and strengths to sustain" (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Introduction and Rules"). Retros that produce a performance verdict rather than a learning conversation have slipped the TC's rail.

4. **Open-ended question discipline.** "He should not ask yes or no questions, but encourage participation and guide discussion by using open-ended and leading questions. An open-ended question has no specific answer and allows the person answering to reply based on what was significant to him" (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Summary of Recent Events"). The worked contrast: "What happened when your Bradley crested the hill?" invites narrative; "Why didn't you engage the enemy tanks to your front?" invites self-defence.

5. **Train to weakness, not to strength.** "Train to weakness, not to strength" (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Tasks to Sustain/Improve"). When allocating retro experiment capacity — the team's finite resource — the default is known weakness. Known strengths warrant investment only with a reason.

6. **The benefit lives in follow-up.** "The real benefits of AARs come from taking the results and applying them to future training" (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, Ch 5, "Benefits"). A retro whose experiments never happen has not produced its benefit. The experiment-review phase is where this principle lands.

7. **Delayed follow-up must be visible and timed.** When retraining is deferred, "soldiers must understand that they did not perform the task to standard and that retraining will occur later" (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, Ch 5, "Benefits"). If last retro's experiment was not done, the gap must be named, not silently accepted as drift.

8. **Closing-comments rule.** "The AAR leader reviews and summarizes key points identified during the discussion. He should end the AAR on a positive note, linking conclusions to future training. He should then leave the immediate area to allow unit leaders and soldiers time to discuss the training in private" (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Closing Comments"). The lead summarises, ends positively, links to next Sprint, then leaves so the team can debrief without the lead present.

9. **SOP revision obligation.** "After-action reviews may reveal problems with unit SOPs. If so, unit leaders must revise the SOP and make sure units implement the changes during any future training" (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, Ch 5, "Revised Standing Operating Procedures"). If the retro surfaces a broken Working Agreement, Definition of Done, or process, revise it and confirm adoption before the next retro.

10. **Rank-consciousness seating rule.** "Higher echelon leaders and commanders should be seated to the flanks and rear of the horseshoe" so that junior voices are not pre-empted (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, App A, "Organization of the AAR Site"). In retro: any seating or facilitation pattern that positions authority-holders where junior voices can self-censor replicates the failure mode the TC is designed to prevent.

11. **Three discussion-organisation techniques.** Chronological (what happened in what order), BOS / systemic (which subsystem failed across the period), key events (which moments mattered most) (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Discussion of Key Issues"). Match the organising structure to the question being asked, not to convention.

12. **Trained-Practiced-Untrained (T-P-U) assessment.** After each training event, leaders conduct a T-P-U assessment and develop a future-training concept (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, Ch 5, "Benefits"). Scaled to retro: at the experiment-review phase, rate each prior experiment Trained (the team can now do this), Practiced (partial progress), or Untrained (the experiment did not move the needle).

## Questions to Ask During a Retro

### Phase 0.5: Experiment Review

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Were last retro's experiments done? | If an experiment was not done, is the gap named clearly — without blame — and is the next commitment timed and visible? Or has the gap silently become drift? |
| If the experiment was not done, who owned it? | If ownership was ambiguous, the experiment design failed, not the team. The TC's principle: if retraining is deferred, the deferral must be communicated, not silent. |
| Did completing the experiment produce the expected result? | T-P-U framing scaled down: Trained (the team can now do this), Practiced (partial progress), Untrained (the experiment did not move the needle). What is the honest assessment? |

### Phase 1: Priming

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Is the opening framing creating the right climate? | Have we established that this is a discovery conversation, not a grading exercise? Have we blocked rank-consciousness from suppressing contribution? |
| Are we opening with the right question? | Is the first question open-ended — inviting narrative — or closed — inviting defence? |

### Phase 2: Data Gathering

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Is one voice dominating? | The TC places higher-echelon leaders to "the flanks and rear of the horseshoe" to ensure junior voices are not pre-empted. Is equivalent seating logic in place (virtual or physical)? |
| Are we surfacing weaknesses, not just strengths? | The TC's direct instruction: train to weakness, not to strength. Is the retro surfacing uncomfortable patterns with the same energy as positive ones? |

### Phase 3: Insight / Cause Analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Are we choosing the right discussion-organisation technique for the pattern? | Chronological (what happened in what order); systemic (which subsystem failed across the Sprint); key events (which moments mattered most). Match the organising structure to the question being asked. |
| Are we asking open-ended questions at the insight phase? | Have any questions slipped into yes/no or blame-seeking form? Redirect by substituting an open-ended version: "What happened when X?" not "Why didn't you Y?" |

### Phase 4: Experiment Design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Are we allocating experiment capacity to weakness, not strength? | The default is known weakness. Is this experiment investing in an already-strong capability? If so, what is the reason for the deviation? |
| Is there a SOP / Working Agreement / Definition of Done that needs revision? | If the retro has surfaced a broken process agreement, is the revision committed and confirmed, not just noted? |

### Phase 5: Close

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Has the lead summarised key points, ended positively, and linked to next Sprint? | TC 25-20 closing-comments rule scaled down: summary → positive note → link to next iteration. |
| Has the lead left so the team can debrief privately? | The team processing without the lead is part of the retro, not a sign of failure. |
| Is every next-step named with an owner and a date? | A retro without a named-owner experiment commitment has not produced its benefit. What is the next action, who owns it, and when? |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| The retro opens with a question that has a yes/no answer — "Did we hit our sprint goal?" or "Was the deployment smooth?" | The closed-question form invites defence rather than narrative; the team will self-select toward safe answers | Replace with an open-ended version: "What happened in the last two sprints when we were near the deadline?" or "Walk me through the deployment — what did you notice?" (Source: Ch 4, "Summary of Recent Events") |
| The facilitator or a senior voice summarises "what went wrong" before the team has surfaced their own data | Critique frame has replaced the discovery frame; the team is receiving one observer's view rather than discovering collectively | Name the shift explicitly and invite the team to replay the event in their own words before any assessment is offered (Source: Ch 1, "Definition and Purpose") |
| The retro produces a performance verdict — "this was a bad sprint," "we failed on X" — rather than a learning conversation | The grading-exercise failure mode: the AAR "does not grade success or failure" | Redirect to the learning question: what do we sustain, and what do we improve? Both exist, always (Source: Ch 4, "Introduction and Rules") |
| Junior or quieter team members stop contributing once a senior voice has spoken | Rank-consciousness suppressing contribution — the failure mode the TC's seating rule is designed to prevent | Adjust facilitation to invite quieter voices explicitly before senior ones; in a virtual retro, use anonymous input collection before open discussion (Source: App A, "Organization of the AAR Site") |
| Experiment commitments from last retro were not done and the gap passed without comment | Silent drift — the deferral was never named and the commitment dissolved | Name the gap without blame, apply T-P-U, re-commit with a named owner and a specific date; if the gap is structural, treat it as a constraint to escalate, not a commitment to repeat (Source: Ch 5, "Benefits") |
| The retro keeps generating experiments in areas where the team is already strong — cleaner code, more thorough documentation — while known-weak areas go unaddressed | Training to strength, not weakness — "train to weakness, not to strength" | Redirect experiment selection: where are the known gaps? Invest experiment capacity there (Source: Ch 4, "Tasks to Sustain/Improve") |
| The retro surfaces a broken Working Agreement or Definition of Done and the team notes it but does not commit a revision | SOP-revision obligation unfulfilled | Commit the revision before the retro closes; assign a named owner to confirm adoption at the next retro's experiment-review phase (Source: Ch 5, "Revised Standing Operating Procedures") |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this distillation when:

- The retro's question design needs a filter: every proposed question can be tested against the TC's open-ended / no-yes/no rule before it goes to the team.
- The experiment-review phase needs a structured frame: T-P-U (Trained / Practiced / Untrained) gives the team a non-blaming vocabulary for rating whether last retro's experiment moved the needle.
- A senior voice or the facilitator is inadvertently sliding into critique mode: the TC's discovery-over-critique frame is the named alternative to put on the table.
- The retro's closing phase needs structure: the TC's closing-comments rule (summary, positive note, link to next iteration, lead leaves) is a reliable three-step the facilitator can run without preparation.
- The team has not done last retro's experiments: the TC's principle that the deferral must be communicated and timed — not silently absorbed — gives the facilitator a non-blaming frame for naming the gap.

TC 25-20 does not supply the full retro methodology (ceremony structure, facilitation moves, safety check, engagement-equity discipline). The Field Guide, Open Practice Library, and Liberating Structures carry the methodology. This reference is reached for when the retro needs one of the TC's specific contributions: question design, experiment-review framing, closing structure, or the epistemic case against grading.

## Worked Example

A seven-person product team is opening a Sprint 4 retro. The facilitator's draft opening question was: "Did we make progress on the reliability work we planned?" The question is closed — it invites yes or no, not narrative. Before the retro begins, the facilitator applies the TC's open-ended filter (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Summary of Recent Events") and rewrites: "Walk me through the reliability work from your vantage point — what happened?" The rewritten question invites the team to surface what was significant to each of them, not to ratify a binary verdict.

At Phase 0.5, the facilitator surfaces last retro's two experiments. One was completed: the on-call handoff checklist was drafted and used twice. The other was not: the team had committed to adding a latency budget to the Definition of Done, but it was not added. The facilitator names the gap without blame and invites the team to rate both experiments using the TC's T-P-U frame (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, Ch 5, "Benefits"): the checklist is Practiced — they ran it but have not yet confirmed it reduces handoff confusion; the latency budget is Untrained — the experiment did not happen. The team commits a revised owner and a specific date for the latency budget before moving to data gathering.

In Phase 2, a senior engineer speaks first and at length about the latency work. The facilitator notices two quieter engineers stop adding sticky notes. Applying the TC's seating logic to a virtual retro (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, App A, "Organization of the AAR Site"), the facilitator pauses the group input, switches to individual written input for three minutes, then re-opens the board. Four additional items surface that had not appeared while the senior voice was speaking.

In Phase 4, the team proposes two experiments: refining the checklist (already strong) and adding the latency budget (known weak). The facilitator surfaces the TC's allocation rule: train to weakness, not to strength (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Tasks to Sustain/Improve"). The team drops the checklist experiment this Sprint and commits the latency budget with a named owner and a date.

At Phase 5 close, the facilitator summarises the two commitments, names one thing the team did well this Sprint, and links both to the Sprint 5 goal. The facilitator then ends the call and creates a separate Slack thread for the team to debrief without the facilitator present — the TC's closing-comments rule, scaled to a remote context (Source: Dept. of the Army, *TC 25-20*, Ch 4, "Closing Comments").

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Using the retro as a graded performance verdict on the team or on individuals.
- Opening with yes/no or blame-seeking questions that cause participants to defend rather than describe.
- Investing retro experiment capacity in known-strong capabilities while known weaknesses go unaddressed.
- Closing the retro without every experiment commitment named with an owner and a date.
- Treating unfulfilled experiments from the prior retro as silent drift: the TC's rule is that the gap must be named and timed, not absorbed without comment.
- Leaving without giving the team space to debrief privately: the closing-comments rule is a structured gift, not an exit.
- Surfacing a broken Working Agreement or Definition of Done and filing it without committing a revision and a confirmation checkpoint.
- Allowing rank or authority gradient to suppress junior voices: the TC's seating logic (seniors to flanks and rear) exists to prevent this; the virtual equivalent is anonymous input before open discussion.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** TC 25-20's bibliography cites FM 25-100 (*Training the Force*, 1988) and FM 25-101 (*Battle Focused Training*, 1990) as the doctrine this TC supplements and expands [BT] (Preface; References). The TC's retraining-scheduling obligation references FM 25-100 and FM 25-101 directly but those manuals are not held as primary references in this corpus [BT] (Ch 5, "Immediate Retraining"). The Combined Arms Training Strategy (CATS), cited as the source of critical-gate-task definitions, is similarly cited by name only [BT] (Ch 5, "Immediate Retraining"). No other external authorities are invoked; the TC's claims are doctrine, not empirical research, and it cites no studies or datasets.

**Named limits of the source.** TC 25-20 addresses unit training in a military context; all worked examples are platoon-through-battalion scenarios. The retro distillation draws on the TC's epistemic and facilitation principles, not its resource-intensive planning apparatus (six-to-eight-week formal planning cycles, observer/controller selection, terrain models). The TC does not address psychological safety by name, safety checks, experiment hypothesis design, or engagement-equity facilitation. It does not address retrospective facilitation for co-located or distributed software teams. The transfer from military-unit-AAR to iterative-team-retro is a distillation judgment, not a claim the TC itself makes.

The closing-comments rule is `[V]` in the deep ref (Ch 4, "Closing Comments"); the distillation applies it as a Phase 5 three-step. The train-to-weakness directive is `[V]` in the deep ref (Ch 4, "Tasks to Sustain/Improve"); the distillation applies it as the experiment-selection allocation rule at Phase 4.
