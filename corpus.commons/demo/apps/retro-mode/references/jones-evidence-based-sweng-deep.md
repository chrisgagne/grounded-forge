## Author's thesis (paraphrased from source)

The book takes the position that, at the time of writing, "evidence-based software engineering is essentially a blank slate" [V] (Ch 1, "History of software engineering research"). Software development has progressed as a craft rather than an engineering discipline because building software has been a seller's market — customers have paid what it takes because the potential benefits have been so much greater than the costs, and "in a competitive market for development work and staff, paying people to learn from mistakes that have already been made by many others is an unaffordable luxury" [AP] (Ch 1, "Introduction"). The book's organising claim is that software engineering should be analysed as "an economically motivated cognitive activity occurring within one or more ecosystems" [V] (Ch 1, "Introduction"), with three load-bearing inputs — *human cognition* (the labour of the cognitariate is the means of production of intangible goods), *cognitive capitalism* (the economics of intangibles is fundamentally different from the economics of tangible goods because the cost of replicating software is effectively zero), and *ecosystems* (software systems are created and operated within ecosystems of intangibles whose evolution can reduce the functionality provided by existing software, i.e., "software wears out and breaks" through ecosystem change rather than through use) [AR] (Ch 1, "Introduction").

A second load-bearing claim is that the dearth of evidence-based research has left a vacuum that has been filled by folklore and old wives' tales. The text repeatedly singles out two artefacts of this folklore: the 28-to-1 productivity claim (the Grant-Sackman study) and Halstead's and McCabe's complexity metrics [AR] (Ch 1, "Folklore"; Ch 7.1.4, "Folklore metrics"). Jones writes: "Many beliefs relating to software engineering processes, commonly encountered in academic publications, are based on ideas formulated many years ago by researchers who were able to gain access to a relevant (often tiny) dataset" [V] (Ch 1, "History of software engineering research"). The text returns to this point repeatedly — that since around 1980 little published software engineering research has been evidence-based; the same systematic review of 5,453 papers (1993–2002) is cited in Ch 1 as 2% reporting experiments and in Ch 13.1 as 1.9% reporting controlled experiments, of which 72.6% used students only as subjects [V] (Ch 1, "History of software engineering research"; Ch 13.1, "Introduction").

A third load-bearing claim is that the contents of the book are dictated by the available public data, not by the importance of topics: "Adhering to this rule has led to a very patchy discussion, although it vividly highlights the almost non-existent evidence for current theories of software development" [V] (Read me 1st). Jones explicitly disclaims the rigour of his own analysis: "Most of these models were created by your author after seeing the data, what is sometimes known as HARKing (Hypothesizing After the Results are Known), or less technically they are just-so stories created from a fishing expedition. This is not how rigorous science is done." [V] (Ch 1, "What has been learned?").

A fourth load-bearing claim is that human cognitive characteristics — the operating limits of the brain that evolved in stone-age ecosystems — constrain software development more than the formal mathematical ideals that researchers have historically applied to it, and that effective practices have to be "fitted around the characteristics of the computing platform in our head" [AP] (Ch 2.1, "Introduction"). The text frames this through the metaphor that "cognition and the environment are like the two blades of a pair of scissors both blades have to mesh together to achieve the desired result" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Introduction"), and warns that the view of developers as omnipotent logicians is "driven by human self-image, a counter-productive mindset" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Introduction").

## Part I: Software engineering as evidence-blind craft (Ch 1)

### The dominant role of hardware cost reduction

Software ecosystems have been continually disrupted by improvements in their host, the electronic computer. The price of computer equipment averaged a 17.5% decline per year, "an economic firestorm that devoured existing practices; software systems are apex predators" [V] (Ch 1, "Introduction"). The cost of one million computing operations and the cost of storage per megabyte have both fallen by many orders of magnitude over the period covered by the book (Ch 1, Figures 1.1 and 1.2). The continual reduction in the cost of storage generated increasing economic incentives for using greater processing power. Power consumed per computation has halved every 1.6 years between 1946 and 2009 (Ch 1, "Software markets", Figure 1.9), which is what made mobile computing commercially viable.

### Three computing eras

The text frames the commercial history of computing as three eras: the IBM (mainframe) era, the Wintel era, and the Internet era [AP] (Ch 1, "Software markets"). In the IBM era, large organisations were paying most or all of the cost of bespoke software and so could see a problem they thought they should have control over; very large organisations are in a position to influence research agendas to target the problems they want solved (Ch 1, "Software markets"). The Wintel era reduced ownership costs dramatically and made it commercially profitable for companies to create and sell packaged software, but the direct cost of software maintenance became invisible to customers, who instead pay the costs of the consequences of faults they experience (Ch 1, "Software markets"). The Internet era has no single dominant vendor across the whole platform, but does have dominant vendors within large niches such as mobile phones (Ch 1, "Software markets"). The text observes: "It is not known whether the rate of change has been faster than in previous eras, or the volume of discussion about the changes has been higher because the changes have been visible to more people, or whether niche dominant vendors continue to drive change to suit their needs, or some other reason" [V] (Ch 1, "Software markets").

### The DOD and the early research agenda

Until the early 1980s most software systems were developed for large organisations, with over 50% of US government research funding for mathematics and computer science coming from the Department of Defense, "an organization that built large systems, with time-frames of many years. As customers of software systems, these organizations promoted a customer orientated research agenda, e.g., focusing on minimizing customer costs and risks, with no interest in vendor profitability and risk factors" [V] (Ch 1, "History of software engineering research"). The first major collection of evidence-based reports on factors involved in large software systems came out of the Rome Air Development Center (RADC) in the 1970s; the data, however, was not widely distributed and was used mainly by Air Force officers writing master's theses (Ch 1, "History of software engineering research"). The legacy of this first 30 years was "a research agenda oriented towards building large software systems" [V] (Ch 1, "History of software engineering research").

### The post-1980 evidence collapse

After approximately 1980, very little published software engineering research has been evidence-based; "during this period, not including a theoretical contribution in empirical research was considered grounds for rejecting a paper submitted for publication" [V] (Ch 1, "History of software engineering research"). A 1990s review found "an almost total lack of evidence-based analysis of its engineering characteristics" [AP] (Ch 1, "History of software engineering research"). When experiments were performed they suffered from small sample sizes, low statistical power, and many failed to report an effect size — only 29% of the 92 controlled experiments published between 1993 and 2002 did so (Ch 1, "History of software engineering research"). Jones offers a candid explanation: that those in computing departments are "more interested in algorithms and mathematics, rather than the human factors and economic issues that dominate commercial software development" [V] (Ch 1, "History of software engineering research"), and that researchers with a talent for software engineering tend to leave the field, "leaving the field to those with talents in the less employable areas of mathematical theory, literary criticism (of source code) or folklore" [V] (Ch 1, "History of software engineering research").

### Folklore: the 28-to-1 productivity claim

The well-known claim of a 28-to-1 productivity difference between best and worst developers, sometimes known as the Grant-Sackman study, is based on a casual reading of an easily misinterpreted table in a 1968 paper [AP] (Ch 1, "Folklore"). The study compared time-sharing and batch-processing for software development. The 28:1 ratio included programmer-system performance differences, and "if batch/time-sharing differences are separated out the maximum difference ratio is 14:1, the minimum 6:1" [V] (Ch 1, "Folklore"). A 1981 study by Dickey, which separated subject performance from other factors and adjusted for individual subject differences, found a performance difference ratio of 1:5; by then, however, the 28:1 ratio had become established as fact in widely read magazine articles and books (Ch 1, "Folklore"). A 1999 study by Prechelt explained what had happened, "for a new audience" (Ch 1, "Folklore").

### Research ecosystems and publication incentives

The text catalogues structural problems in the academic publishing of software engineering research, including: the dominance of peer review (which "first appeared in 1665, however, it only became widely used in the 1970s in response to concerns over public accountability of government funded research" [V] (Ch 1, "Research ecosystems")), the use of impact factor and paper count to measure academic performance (which generates "evolutionary pressure that selects for bad science" [V] (Ch 1, "Research ecosystems")), and the rise of predatory journals. One review found that the probability of a research dataset still being available fell by 17% per year (Ch 1, "Research ecosystems"). Jones reports his own experience: "Your author's experience of requesting data from researchers is that it often fails to survive beyond the lifetime of the computer on which it was originally held" [V] (Ch 1, "Research ecosystems").

The text takes a strong position on open access: "Your author's attitude is that academics are funded by taxpayers, and if they are unwilling to provide a freely downloadable copy on their web page, they should not receive any credit for the work" [V] (Ch 1, "Research ecosystems", footnote). Of Jones's own 574 data requests, the outcomes were: no reply 35%, received data 26%, pending 16%, confidential 11%, no longer have data 7%, best known address bounces 4% (Ch 1, "Research ecosystems", Table 1.1).

## Part II: Human cognition as the binding constraint (Ch 2)

### Stone-age cognition processing modern problems

The collection of cognitive characteristics in *Homo sapiens* is the end-result of evolutionary survival pressures over millions of years, "the last few hundred thousand years spent as hunter-gatherers roaming the African savannah, followed by 10,000 years or so having a lifestyle that involved farming crops and raising domesticated animals" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Introduction"). "Our skull houses a computing system that evolved to provide responses to problems that occurred in stone-age ecosystems" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Introduction"). The text adopts a *rational analysis* and *bounded cognition* framing: the structure of the natural environment places constraints on optimal performance, while cognitive, perception, and motor operations have their own sets of constraints targeting *good-enough* performance (Ch 2.1, "Introduction").

### WEIRD subjects and external validity

Models of human cognitive performance are based on results from subjects drawn almost entirely from Western, Educated, Industrialised, Rich and Democratic (WEIRD) societies [AP] (Ch 2.1, "Modeling human cognition"). The text argues that this is not a problem for software engineering because "those involved have usually had a WEIRD society education" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Modeling human cognition"). Cross-cultural differences in WEIRD vs non-WEIRD cognition include: WEIRD people will make inferences about abstract situations without direct experience while non-WEIRD subjects often refuse to; WEIRD people use a linear scale for mapping values between one and ten while non-WEIRD subjects often use a logarithmic scale (Ch 2.1, "Modeling human cognition"). Most studies sample from 18–21 year-olds studying psychology degrees, a subpopulation whose results "may well be good enough for dealing with software engineering issues" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Modeling human cognition"); the reasons why students are *not* appropriate subjects in software engineering experiments are deferred to Ch 13 (Ch 2.1, "Modeling human cognition").

### Embodied cognition, attention, and perfection

The text argues that many aspects of cognitive processing are "dependent on, or shaped by, sensory, motor, and emotional processes that are grounded in the features of their physical body" [V] (Ch 2.1.2, "Embodied cognition"). Tetris players prefer to physically rotate items on screen rather than mentally rotate them (Ch 2.1.2, "Embodied cognition"). The text takes the position that "perfection is not cost-effective" — evolution selects for *relative* performance, not flawless performance, and "a consistent flawless performance is not only unnecessary, but a waste of precious resources" [V] (Ch 2.1.3, "Perfection is not cost-effective"). NASA's astronaut error rates run between 1.9 × 10⁻³ and 1.05 × 10⁻⁴ per operation; touch typists make 4% errors per keystroke with no correction, 7.5% per word when typing nonsense (Ch 2.1.3, "Perfection is not cost-effective").

### Motivation and built-in behaviors

Anchoring and confirmation bias are catalogued as the two most commonly discussed cognitive biases relevant to software development. A study by Jørgensen and Sjøberg found that professionals' and students' estimates were strongly shifted by the customer's offered estimate [AP] (Ch 2.2.1, "Built-in behaviors"). The Wason 2-4-6 task is presented through Klayman and Ha's analysis showing that the *positive test strategy* people use is actually adaptive when the sought rule describes a minority case or is roughly the right size — that is, conventional Popperian disconfirmation is not the unique norm [AR] (Ch 2.2.1, "Built-in behaviors"). The chapter argues against treating well-replicated behavioural patterns as "biases"; researchers are starting to create models where "what were once thought to be cognitive biases, are actually ecologically rational uses of cognitive resources" [V] (Ch 2.2.1, "Built-in behaviors").

### Memory systems

Short-term memory (working memory) capacity is presented through the 7±2 rule with the modern correction that the actual capacity is approximately *two seconds of sound* — Miller did not propose 7±2 as the capacity of STM but drew attention to the fact that this range fitted several experiments [AP] (Ch 2.4.1, "Short term memory"). Welsh speakers can hold an average of 5.8 digits (the spoken sound of digits is longer); Chinese speakers 9.9 (the sound is shorter); English speakers 6.6 (Ch 2.4.1, "Short term memory"). The Baddeley model of working memory components — central executive, phonological loop, visuo-spatial sketch pad — is presented as the working framework (Ch 2.4.1, "Short term memory"). Sentence comprehension has a sharp degradation when embedding exceeds two levels (Ch 2.4.1, "Short term memory").

### Learning, expertise, and the 10,000-hours framing

The text presents the *power law of learning* — RT = a + bN⁻ᶜ — and the competing exponential model RT = a + be⁻ᶜᴺ as both reasonable fits to learning data, with the caveat that power laws can be a consequence of averaging data over multiple subjects rather than representing individual subject performance [AP] (Ch 2.5, "Learning and experience"). Expertise studies show that "the elapsed time between them starting out and carrying out their best work was at least 10 years, often with several hours of deliberate practice every day of the year" [V] (Ch 2.5.2, "Expertise"). A study of violinists found that by age 20 those at the top level had practiced for 10,000 hours, the next level down 7,500 hours, the lowest 5,000 hours (Ch 2.5.2, "Expertise"). The text emphasises that "Expertise within one domain does not confer any additional skills within another domain" [V] (Ch 2.5.2, "Expertise"), and that knowledge of a category of items (Ch 2.5.3) is what enables reuse of small amounts of information to infer useful conclusions.

### Reasoning, decision-making, and overconfidence

"The human mind is a story processor, not a logic processor" [V] (Ch 2.6, "Reasoning"). The Wason selection task is described as "to studies of reasoning like the fruit fly is to studies of genetics" [V] (Ch 2.6, "Reasoning"). The text argues that decision-making researchers, who study real-world problems, have made more progress than reasoning researchers, who tied themselves to using the norm of mathematical logic for decades and "created something of research ghetto" [V] (Ch 2.6, "Reasoning"). Bounded rationality is presented as the operational frame: "everyday human performance needs to be good enough, and an investment in finding a (near) optimal solution may not be cost effective" [AP] (Ch 2.8.3, "Decision-making"). Hyperbolic discounting (rather than exponential discounting) is presented as the empirical pattern for time-variable preferences (Ch 2.8.6, "Time discounting"). Overconfidence is shown to be *evolutionarily stable* — simulations have found that overconfidence has benefits in some situations, averaged over a population (Ch 2.8.5, "Overconfidence").

### Developer performance and the absence of measurement

"Although much talked about, there has been little research on individual developer productivity" [V] (Ch 2.8.7, "Developer performance"). The Grant-Sackman 28:1 claim is revisited and corrected to roughly 6-to-1 for batch vs online (Ch 2.8.7, "Developer performance"). One review found employee output variability had standard deviations from 19% to 48% as job complexity increased. Figure 2.69 shows almost an order of magnitude difference in the time taken by 593 PSP trainees to solve standard problems (Ch 2.8.7, "Developer performance"). The text observes that "Most organizations do not attempt to measure the mental performance of job applicants for developer roles" and speculates that this is from non-measurement culture, lack of reliable procedures, or fear of frightening off prospective employees [V] (Ch 2.8.7, "Developer performance").

## Part III: Cognitive capitalism (Ch 3)

### Software as intangible product

"Software systems are intangible goods that are products of cognitive capitalism; human cognition is the means of production" [V] (Ch 3.1, "Introduction"). The chapter analyses software production using the economic vocabulary of *tradable quantities such as money, time and pleasure* and notes that capitalist structures "are oriented towards the production of tangible goods, and are slowly being adapted to deal with the growing market share of intangible goods" [V] (Ch 3.1, "Introduction"). Software development costs run around 19% of revenue for software companies in SIC 7371/7372, with sales and marketing 22–40% and general & admin 11–22% — leaving the remainder as profit and taxes [AP] (Ch 3.1, "Introduction"; Figure 3.4).

### Investment decisions and ROI

Creating software is an *irreversible* investment — incomplete software has little or no resale value, and even completed software may not have any resale value (Ch 3.2, "Investment decisions"). The text presents the standard ROI formula and uncertainty propagation, NPV with discounting, and incremental investment-and-return analysis. The key applied result is the *survival-rate-adjusted break-even ratio* for development-time investments aimed at reducing future maintenance costs — incorporating system survival rate, the cost-effectiveness of any maintenance-cost-reducing investment must include the risk that the project is cancelled or the software has no future (Ch 3.2.3, "Incremental investments and returns"; Figure 3.7). Annual application survival rates are presented: 0.87 for mainframe applications (Tamai 1991 data) and 0.79 for Google SaaS (Ogden data); on this basis, software half-lives are approximately 5 years for the mainframe data and 2.9 years for the Google SaaS data (Ch 3.2.3, "Incremental investments and returns"; Ch 4.2.2, "Lifespan"). The standard option-pricing approaches (Black-Scholes, real options) are presented with explicit warnings about their inapplicability: "Implementing functionality using the minimum of investment, with the intent of investing more later, has the form of an American call option" [V] (Ch 3.2.5, "Real options"), but the term *technical debt* is explicitly rejected ("this is incorrect, there is no debt, and there may not be any need for more work in the future" [V] (Ch 3.2.5, "Real options", footnote)).

### Capturing cognitive output and intellectual property

Organisations whose income is derived from cognitive output are "social factories"; free meals and laundry are not perks but a means of bringing employees together and creating life-norms that limit alternative employers, i.e., reducing knowledge loss and spillover [AP] (Ch 3.3, "Capturing cognitive output"). Software patents in the US were not patentable until 1995; by 2014, 109,281 software patents were granted (36% of all utility patents) (Ch 3.3.1, "Intellectual property"). The text catalogues the proliferation of open source licences, the OSI vetting process, and the survival curve of OSI-approved licences (at the start of 2019, 81 licences were listed on the OSI website out of 107 that had been listed since 2000) (Ch 3.3.1, "Intellectual property"; Figure 3.16).

### Group dynamics and Brooks's Law

Surplus value is maximised when management can break tasks into simpler components, deskill jobs, and reduce dependence on individuals — "Scientific management is not a science of work, it is a science of the management of other people's work" [V] (Ch 3.4.1, "Maximizing generated surplus", footnote). The chapter presents the *team communication overhead* analysis: assuming each member spends t₀ fraction of their time communicating, peak effective team size before adding people starts reducing output is Dpeak = (1 + t₀)/(2t₀); for t₀ = 0.1 (10% of time per member), peak size is about 5 [AP] (Ch 5.5, "Development teams"). Brooks's Law is then expressed quantitatively as the condition for when adding a new person doesn't delay the project: Te < (Dr − Dt)(En − (1 − c)Ea1) (Ch 5.5.1, "New staff"). Information asymmetry and moral hazard between client and vendor are framed using agency theory (Ch 3.4.6–3.4.7).

### Maximising ROI through pricing

Pricing models include the *price dead zone* between manager sign-off ceilings and the minimum amount worth selling via sales reps (Ch 3.6.2, "Product/service pricing"); the *Bass diffusion model* of innovator + imitator product adoption, with peak sales at m(1/2 − p/2q) ln(q/p) when imitators dominate (Ch 3.6.3, "Predicting sales volume"); and the *customer lifetime value* (CLV) calculation under regular maintenance payments (Ch 3.6.4, "Managing customers as investments").

## Part IV: Ecosystems (Ch 4)

### Differences from biological ecosystems

Software ecosystem evolution is *Lamarckian* (acquired characteristics can be passed on through copying and modification) rather than Darwinian, and software can be perfectly replicated at effectively zero cost — evolutionary pressure comes from changes in the world the software runs in, not from copying errors [AR] (Ch 4.1, "Introduction"). Software has unlimited lifetime in theory; in practice it depends on the ecosystem in which it runs, e.g., third-party libraries and hardware functionality (Ch 4.1, "Introduction"). Resource interactions in software ecosystems are based on agreement; in biological ecosystems creatures go to great lengths to avoid common resource interactions like being eaten (Ch 4.1, "Introduction").

### Funding, hardware, and lifespan

Venture capital is a *hits business* — those who invested in VCs over the last 20 years would have received greater returns by investing in a basket of public-market stocks; VCs typically charge 2% management fee plus 20% carry (Ch 4.1.1, "Funding"). One Angel-investor study found 30% lost their money, 20% had ROI less than one, and only 14% had ROI greater than five (Ch 4.1.1, "Funding"). Hardware diversity historically drove software diversity; the rise of Wintel and later open source compilers eliminated most of it (Ch 4.1.2, "Hardware"; Ch 4.6.3, "Tools"). Software system lifespan data: Japanese mainframe software half-life ~5 years (Tamai 1991); Google SaaS half-life ~2.9 years (Ogden 2020); aircraft and physical infrastructure lifetimes are an order of magnitude longer (Ch 4.2.2, "Lifespan").

### Entering a market and population dynamics

Market entry barriers analysed include economies of scale (a supply-side effect that doesn't apply well to software, where replication is free), network effects (a demand-side effect that does apply), and switching costs [AP] (Ch 4.2.3, "Entering a market"). The Arthur lock-in analysis of two competing technologies with increasing returns is presented: technology A locks in when nA(t) − nB(t) exceeds (bS − aS)/s (Ch 4.3, "Population dynamics"). The Polya urn process is presented as the canonical example of *path-dependent population dynamics*: regardless of starting composition, the percentage of red and black balls always converges to some uniformly distributed random value (Ch 4.3.1, "Growth processes").

### Estimating population size: closed and open populations

For closed populations (no births or deaths), the *Chapman estimator* gives an unbiased mark-and-recapture estimate: N = (C1 + 1)(C2 + 1)/(C12 + 1) − 1. For open populations (additions and removals), the Cormack-Jolly-Seber and Jolly-Seber models are used, supported in the openCR and Rcapture packages (Ch 4.3.2.1, "Closed populations"; Ch 4.3.2.2, "Open populations"). This same estimator framework is reused in Ch 6 to estimate the number of remaining undiscovered fault experiences.

### Cultural and language ecosystems

There are over 33,830 religious denominations world-wide (Ch 4.2, "Evolution", footnote). Native English speakers map time onto a horizontal axis; native Chinese speakers use a vertical axis (Ch 4.4.2, "Culture"). The text observes the colonising effect of widely-used software systems (Unix, MS-DOS, Windows, X11, gcc, BSD) on language usage: English has become the lingua franca, with German-language comments in LibreOffice declining over time after a decision to make them all English (Ch 4.4.2, "Culture"; Figure 4.38). One language compiler list from 1959 catalogued around 25 languages, and a 1976 US DoD report estimated at least 450 general-purpose languages and dialects were currently in use (Ch 4.6.1, "Programming languages").

### Maintenance economics

The text takes the explicit position that mean total maintenance-to-development cost ratio is *less than one* when adjusted for system survival, contradicting the often-cited claim that maintenance dominates over development [AR] (Ch 4.5.2, "Pounding the treadmill"; Figure 4.47, citing Dunn). Snapshot-based analyses that report maintenance/development ratios greater than five suffer from survivorship bias: "the actual ratio is closer to 0.8" [V] (Ch 4.2, "Evolution"). The correlation between development and maintenance man-hours is 0.5 (95% CI 0.38–0.63) (Ch 4.5.2, "Pounding the treadmill").

## Part V: Projects (Ch 5)

### The bidding and contract environment

The Japanese SEC dataset of 2,992 projects shows most projects are completed in under a year, contain less than 40 KSLOC, and use significant external-contractor effort (Ch 5.1, "Introduction"; Figure 5.1). Project success is role-dependent: management considers being within budget an indicator of success, while team members and end-users are more interested in meeting the specification, with *user happiness* being the only success indicator common to all roles in one study (Ch 5.1, "Introduction", citing Milis). NASA originally estimated $10–12 billion for landing on the Moon; the head of NASA told Kennedy $20 billion; the final cost was $24 billion (Ch 5.1, "Introduction").

Bidding decisions are driven by factors that have "little or no connection with the technical aspects of software implementation, or its costs" [V] (Ch 5.2, "Pitching for projects"), including: keeping staff busy, bidding the maximum the client will pay, bidding low to win with contract clauses allowing later increases, and bidding low to recoup losses during the maintenance phase. The Libra project at the UK Magistrates' Courts went from an initial £146M bid through several renegotiations to a £232M revised contract over 8.5 years (Ch 5.2, "Pitching for projects"). Standard form contracts almost universally favour the software company that wrote them: "almost all had a net bias, relative to relevant default rules, in favor of the software company" [V] (Ch 5.2.1, "Contracts"). Estimation studies show 90% of transportation projects under-estimate, with average cost overrun 28% (Ch 5.3, "Resource estimation"); a US DoD study found a 60% average growth in total costs; Jones's commentary: "It should not be surprising that inaccurate resource estimates are endemic in many industries (and perhaps all human activities), software projects are just one instance" [V] (Ch 5.3, "Resource estimation").

### Estimation models

Multiple estimation models are catalogued and compared (Mohanty 1981 found that 12 different estimating models gave estimates varying widely for the same project; Ch 5.3.1, "Estimation models"; Figure 5.18). The Norden/Putnam model (work rate ∝ ate⁻ᵃᵗ²ᐟ²) and Parr model (work rate ∝ ¼sech²((αt+c)/2)) are derived from differential-equation analyses of project work as problem solving; both can be fitted to project effort data and have similar shape, though the Norden/Putnam model assumes linear team learning and no significant requirements changes (Ch 5.3.1, "Estimation models"; Figure 5.19). Function-point analysis methods are widely used but the function-point-to-cost mapping is highly variable across projects (Ch 5.3.1, "Estimation models"; Figure 5.20). For multiple implementations of the same specification, "the standard deviation is approximately one quarter of the mean" in lines-of-code, which means any LOC-driven estimate carries large intrinsic variation (Ch 5.3.1, "Estimation models"; Figure 5.23, across seven studies).

### Anchoring and rounding in estimates

Aranda's study shows large anchoring effects from middle-manager statements on estimate distributions (Ch 5.3, "Resource estimation"; Figure 5.15). Jones-and-Cullum agile data of 8,252 tasks shows distinct peaks at multiples of seven hours (the work day), and almost no estimates of six, eight, or nine hours — strong evidence that round-number preferences influence development estimates the same way they influence other numeric estimates (Ch 5.3, "Resource estimation"; Figure 5.17; Ch 2.7.1, "Numeric preferences"). The choice of measurement unit affects estimates: Jørgensen 2004 found that subjects asked to estimate in work-hours gave lower estimates than subjects asked in workdays for the same project (Ch 5.3, "Resource estimation").

### Waterfall vs iterative and the punctuated information arrival

Software development is "a punctuated information arrival process" [V] (Ch 5.4.1, "Development methodologies"). The Royce 1970 paper that "gave birth to this [waterfall] demon meme" contained a diagram and accompanying text claiming the approach was risky and invited failure; subsequent pages showed the *recommended* alternative; "the how not to do it approach, illustrated in the first diagram, was taken up, becoming known as the waterfall model, and included in the first version of an influential standard, DoD-Std-2167, as the recommended project management technique" [V] (Ch 5.4.1, "Development methodologies"). Iterative development has been "independently discovered many times" [V] (Ch 5.4.1, "Development methodologies"). The OSI seven-layer model is held up as the largest example of a waterfall failure — documented first, then later vendor-implemented — vs the IETF's rough-consensus-and-running-code iterative model (Ch 5.4.1, "Development methodologies"). The text dismisses Agile coverage because of lack of measurement data: "The Agile manifesto specified 'Individuals and interactions over processes and tools', but this has not stopped the creation and marketing of a wide variety of processes claiming to be the agile way of doing things. The rarity of measurement data for any of the agile processes means this evidence-based book has little to say about them" [V] (Ch 5.4.3, "The Agile approach").

### Stakeholders, requirements, and the "cone of uncertainty"

The widely cited *cone of uncertainty* curve is "at best useless" — the cone shape that appears in plots where the y-axis is the ratio of actual/estimated and the x-axis is percentage completed is a mathematical artefact created by the choice of axis [V] (Ch 5.4.4, "Managing progress"). Identifying stakeholders is presented through Lim's RALIC study at UCL; PageRank applied to the stakeholder network produced rankings that correlated strongly with the ground-truth ranking (Ch 5.4.5, "Discovering functionality needed for acceptance"). Stakeholder *salience* — defined as the degree to which managers give priority to competing stakeholder claims (Mitchell-Agle-Wood) — is used as the prioritisation metric. Requirements prioritisation by allocating a fixed budget across requirements (Regnell et al.) is presented as an empirical technique that exposes inter-stakeholder variance (Ch 5.4.5, "Discovering functionality needed for acceptance").

## Part VI: Reliability (Ch 6)

### What constitutes a fault

People are willing to keep using software containing faults provided it delivers a worthwhile benefit [AP] (Ch 6.1, "Introduction"). "Software systems containing likely fault experiences are shipped because it is not economically worthwhile fixing all the mistakes made during their implementation; also, finding and fixing mistakes, prior to release, is often constrained by available resources and marketing deadlines" [V] (Ch 6.1, "Introduction"). Two events are required for a running program to experience a software-related fault: (1) a mistake exists in the software, and (2) the program processes input values that cause it to execute the code containing the mistake in a way that results in a fault being experienced [AP] (Ch 6.1.2, "Why do fault experiences occur?"). The text repeatedly emphasises that almost all existing reliability research has focused on the source code side and treated input distributions as a convenience sample.

### Cost-benefit and risk asymmetry

With COTS software, the cost of fault experiences is asymmetric: "the customer bears the cost of the fault experience itself, while the vendor can choose whether to fix the mistake and ship an update" [V] (Ch 6.2, "Maximizing ROI"). Most listed CVE-style vulnerabilities are never actively exploited: one study found that only 2.6% of the vulnerabilities in the NVD have been used in viruses and network threat attacks (Ch 6.2, "Maximizing ROI"). Willingness-to-pay valuations of statistical life are catalogued — the UK Department for Transport's 2011 figures give average human-cost-saved per fatality at £1,039,530, slight injury £10,570 (Ch 6.1.4, "Cultural outlook", Table 6.1) — and provide the framework for cost/benefit analysis of reliability investments.

### Input profile and the bi-exponential pattern of duplicate fault reports

Repeated experiments on the same fault populations produce a striking pattern: the number of times the same fault is experienced (sorted in rank order) is well fitted by a *bi-exponential* equation a × e^(bx) + c × e^(dx). Jones offers a speculative explanation: "the two exponentials are driven by the two independent processes that need to interact to produce a fault experience: the distribution of input values, and the mistakes contained in the source code" [V] (Ch 6.3.1, "Input profile"). This pattern is seen in Microsoft Office fuzzing (Kaminsky et al., across 2003/2007/2010 releases; Figure 6.24), in GCC fault reports over 1999–2015 (Sun et al.; Figure 6.25), and in KDE reports over 18 years (Sadat et al.; Figure 6.26).

### Propagation of mistakes and silent benign mistakes

Danglot et al. showed that one-off perturbations in 10 short Java programs often had no observed effect — for instance, in `quicksort` the 41 perturbation points were each executed thousands of times per input, but a high fraction of perturbations had no impact on output (Ch 6.3.2, "Propagation of mistakes"). Studies of *soft errors* (radiation-induced bit-flips) have found that over 80% of bit-flips have no detectable impact on program behavior (Ch 6.3.2, "Propagation of mistakes"). This means many coding mistakes never produce a fault experience and the cost of finding and fixing them all is wasted.

### Where mistakes occur, and folklore metrics revisited

Brown and Altadmri found "very poor agreement between professor beliefs and the actual ranked frequency of student mistakes" [V] (Ch 6.4, "Where is the mistake?"). One Team Software Process study (Nichols et al.) found 36% of mistakes logged during development were made in phases *before* coding (Ch 6.4, "Where is the mistake?"). For source-code metrics, Israeli and Feitelson found that Halstead's volume and McCabe's cyclomatic complexity both scale with LOC for the 62,365 C functions in Linux 2.6.9 with at least 10 lines: Halstead_volume ∝ KLOC^1.1, McCabe_complexity ∝ KLOC^0.8 (Ch 7.1.4, "Folklore metrics"; Figure 7.10). The text explicitly attacks McCabe's metric as "easily manipulated by splitting functions with high values into two or more functions, each having lower metric values… The software equivalent of what is known as accounting fraud in accounting" [V] (Ch 6.4.2, "Source code"; see also Ch 7.1.4).

### Hardware as a source of unreliability

Single-bit memory errors from radiation are no longer science fiction. Autran et al.'s monitoring in the French Alps (130nm, 65nm, 40nm SRAM) showed soft-error rates that vary across process technology and altitude. Commodity DRAM at 1000 FIT/Mb gives an MTBF of about 33 hours for a 4 GB system; "Soft errors are a regular occurrence for installations containing hundreds of terabytes of memory" [AP] (Ch 6.5, "Non-software causes of unreliability"). The Cray Blue Waters system at the National Center for Supercomputing Applications experienced 28 uncorrected memory errors despite ECC and Chipkill correcting 722,526 single-bit and 309,359 two-bit errors — a 99.995% success rate (Ch 6.5, "Non-software causes of unreliability").

### Checking for intended behavior

The relative cost of correcting problems vs detecting them earlier is not, in general, evidence for early detection being more cost-effective; the relative-cost figures from NASA and DOD data reflect the very high cost of the hardware running those particular software systems, not a universal fact (Ch 6.6, "Checking for intended behavior"). Manual code review is presented through Nielsen and Landauer's diminishing-returns curve (N(1 − (1 − p)^S) for S reviewers, N issues, p discovery probability) and Lewis's empirical finding that 50% of usability issues are found by only one reviewer and 15% by two (Ch 6.6.1, "Code review"). For test generation, the Marcozzi et al. study showed that human-found compiler-fault patterns are not statistically distinguishable from those found by Csmith and EMI fuzzing tools when measured by how often the fault-trigger code path is reached in real-world Debian packages (Ch 6.6.2, "Testing"). MC/DC coverage has a guaranteed minimum 93.75% probability of detecting an incorrect condition (Ch 6.6.2.3, "Estimating test effectiveness").

## Part VII: Source code (Ch 7)

### Source code as one-sided communication

Source code is "a form of communication" but communicating with a computer is "a take-it or leave-it transaction; what the speaker said is treated as definitive by the listener, intent is not part of the process. There is no cooperative activity, and the only coordination involves the speaker modifying what has been said until the desired listener response is achieved" [V] (Ch 7.1, "Introduction"). Grice's maxims and the cooperative-principle assumptions that govern human communication don't apply between developer and compiler. The text takes a cognitive-linguistics rather than Chomskyan approach to source code.

### Quantity of source

Landman et al.'s measurements of 13,103 Java projects show that most methods are very short (Java has a very tight distribution of methods of 1–4 LOC), while in C the distribution is broader (4–10 LOC); 50% of Java source occurs in methods of 4 LOC or fewer, while 50% of C source occurs in functions of 114 LOC or fewer (Ch 7.2.2, "Narrative structures"; Figure 7.16, Figure 7.25). The mapping between Java source-system size characteristics is well-fitted by quantile regression as power laws with exponents close to 1, e.g., SLOC ≈ 13 × Methods^0.97 (Ch 7.1.1, "Quantity of source"; Table 7.2).

### Folklore metrics

Halstead's metric and McCabe's complexity persist despite the studies finding their predictive performance "comparable to that of lines of code" [V] (Ch 7.1.4, "Folklore metrics"). The Shen, Conte and Dunsmore 1977 report explained the problem at the time — Halstead's notion of *impurities* requiring *purification* (i.e., cleaning data to fit a theory) was an example of data tuning to fit theory, not theory tested against data [AP] (Ch 7.1.4, "Folklore metrics"). Jones notes: "Ptolomy's theory… was not discredited because it gave inaccurate answers, but because Copernicus's theory eventually made predictions that had a better fit to experimental measurements" [V] (Ch 7.1.4, "Folklore metrics") — implying that displacement of Halstead/McCabe will require empirically better-performing alternatives, not just criticism.

### Identifier names and the channel of meaning

Identifiers represent approximately 40% of all non-whitespace characters in C visible source; comments represent 31%. Identifier name conflicts and similarity contribute to mistakes (drug-name confusion errors are the human-factors analog, where 15–25% of reported medication errors involve drugs that look or sound alike) (Ch 7.2.8, "Identifier names"). A Nelson-McEvoy-Schreiber free-association study showed that across 5,018 stimulus words, the mean percentage of *unique* responses was 18% — so the probability that an identifier will trigger the same semantic association in two different readers is bounded (Ch 7.2.8, "Identifier names"). Across seven large software systems, the percentage of identifiers occurring in any two systems with the same spelling rarely exceeds 12% (Ch 7.2.8, Table 7.5).

### Patterns of use and consistency

Embedded C software has fewer function parameters than desktop/SPECint95 C software — Engblom found λ ≈ 0.8 for embedded vs λ ≈ 2 for the C book corpus (function-parameter Poisson fit) — because parameter-passing was costly on the resource-limited processors embedded software runs on (Ch 7.2.7, "Consistency"; Figure 7.30). Different programming languages produce significantly different distributions of statement-kind frequencies in C, C++, and Java (Zhu et al.; Figure 9.12), showing that language choice is correlated with what gets written.

### Software evolution

Software changes only when developers have an incentive to spend time making the changes — being paid, or satisfying a personal need [AP] (Ch 7.4, "Evolution of source code"). Growth patterns vary widely: FreeBSD and Linux grow at consistent rates for many years; glibc has effectively stopped growing; Groovy compiler grows sporadically (Ch 7.4, "Evolution of source code"). Most files are only ever edited by one person (Ch 3.2.3, "Incremental investments and returns"). Identifier renaming is a common activity but rarely follows clear grammatical patterns; one Eshkevari et al. study of Eclipse-JDT and Tomcat found half of renamings were on method names, a quarter on field names, and no correlation between renamings and release dates (Ch 5.4.8, "Refactoring"; Figure 5.49).

## Part VIII: Stories told by data (Ch 8)

The chapter is a methodology chapter about communicating numeric findings. Key positions: color is an essential component of numeric storytelling; logarithmic axes are appropriate when values span several orders of magnitude but can hide useful information by spreading data out; quantities plotted on each axis must be carefully analysed to avoid generating artificial relationships. The *defect-density-vs-LOC* U-shape, popularly used to argue for an optimal function length, is a mathematical artefact created by the choice of plotting F/LOC against LOC; "the idea suggested by the U-shaped pattern, that there might be an optimal function length, is the result of misinterpreting the mathematical behavior of a ratio quantity plotted against one of the values used in the ratio calculation" [V] (Ch 8.3.2.3, "Plot axis: what and how"; Figure 8.39).

The chapter recommends communicating fitted models by giving the equation and its coefficient uncertainties at the point they apply, rather than expecting readers to decode `summary()` output, since "as model complexity increases, readers have to invest more effort to correctly interpret equations" (Ch 8.3.4, "Communicating fitted models").

## Part IX: Statistics and regression as default tools (Ch 9–14)

### Probability, distributions, and the Negative Binomial

Many software-engineering measurements are well-fitted by Poisson, Exponential, or — when the variance exceeds the Poisson mean — Negative Binomial distributions (Ch 9.3, "Fitting a probability distribution to a sample"). 7Digital feature-implementation-time data fits a zero-truncated Negative Binomial; one interpretation is that "there are many distinct processes involved in the implementation of a feature, with the duration of each process being a distinct Poisson process; a sum of independent Poisson processes can produce a Negative Binomial distribution" [V] (Ch 9.3.1, "Zero-truncated and zero-inflated distributions"; Ch 5.4.6, "Implementation"). The Markov-chain framework is used to estimate the reliability of multi-subsystem programs (the gcc reliability example from Perugupalli, using the *composite* and *hierarchical* methods, Ch 9.4.1).

### Statistics fundamentals: sample, population, effect-size

"Probability makes inferences about individual events based on the characteristics of the population, while statistics makes inferences about the population based on the characteristics of a sample of the population" [V] (Ch 9.1, "Introduction"; Ch 10.1, "Introduction"). The Berry-Esseen theorem bound on the convergence of the distribution of sample means: error ∝ 1/√N (Ch 10.2.2, "Sampling error"). Statistical power is presented through the inverted relationship between Type I and Type II errors (Ch 10.2.3, "Statistical power"). The Syed et al. Firefox hardware-configuration study is used to show what statistical power *cannot* detect: with 10 runs per condition, only a >50% change in fault proportion would be detectable, so absence of evidence is not evidence of absence (Ch 10.2.3, "Statistical power"; Figure 10.13).

### Regression modeling as the universal hammer

"Regression modeling is the default hammer used in this book to create the output from data analysis of software engineering data" [V] (Ch 11.1, "Introduction"). The chapter prefers `glm()` over `lm()` because "the mathematics behind `glm` makes fewer assumptions about the sample characteristics, e.g., it does not require that the variance in the error be constant (which `lm` does)" [V] (Ch 11.2, "Linear regression", footnote). The book uses `glm()` for nearly every model, with appropriate link functions (logit, Poisson, gamma, binomial, beta), and uses the bootstrap (`boot` package) for confidence intervals when closed-form alternatives are not available. The frequentist approach is used throughout, with explicit acknowledgement that "the p-value is the fall-guy of the frequentist approach to statistical analysis" [V] (Ch 10.1.1, "Statistical inference"). Bayesian methods are mentioned but not deployed; the *prior* selection "opens the door to the bias of opinion and policy guidelines" [AP] (Ch 10.1.1, "Statistical inference").

### Multicollinearity, time series, and survival analysis

Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) is presented as a measure of the impact of multicollinearity. The cross-correlation between glibc lines-added and lines-deleted per week shows spikes at +2 and +8 weeks lag (with smaller spikes at negative lags); "Your author has no explanation for this correlation" [V] (Ch 11.10.6, "Relationships between time series"). Survival analysis using the Cox proportional-hazards model is recommended when there is doubt about the parametric hazard distribution (Ch 11.11.3.1, "Cox proportional-hazards model"). Businge's Eclipse third-party plug-in study showed that plug-ins using *internal* APIs survived (in the sense of having new releases) for more years than plug-ins using only *official* APIs — likely because changes to internal APIs forced updates to keep the plug-in working (Ch 11.11.2, "Survival curve"; Figures 11.76, 11.77).

### Bradley-Terry preferences and other techniques

Bradley-Terry statistics fit preference orderings; for binary-operator-precedence judgements by 12 professional developers, the fitted β coefficients give ordering with relative precedence consistent with C-language conventions (Ch 12.4.2, "Preferred item ordering"; Figure 12.7, using Jones's own 2008 study). The chapter (Ch 12) also covers cluster analysis, principal-component analysis, k-means, hierarchical clustering, and simulation through agent-based modelling, discrete-event simulation, and numerical solution of differential equations.

### Experiments and benchmarking

Randomisation is "the foundation on which any claims of causation, involving experimental results, are based" [V] (Ch 13.2, "Design of experiments"). Students vs professional developers: "students' commercial software skills and knowledge is likely to be very poor, in comparison to professional developers… typically, have very little experience of writing software, perhaps 50 to 150 hours…, while commercial software developers are likely to have between 1,000 to 10,000 hours of experience" [V] (Ch 13.2.1, "Subjects"). The text notes the publication-bias: "many academic studies use the phrase *expert* to describe subjects who are final-year undergraduates or graduate students, with the term *novice* used to describe first-year undergraduates" — whereas commercially a recent graduate is a novice and 5+ years of commercial experience is expert (Ch 13.2.1, "Subjects").

Benchmarking is presented through extensive examples of measurement variability: same SSD model across runs, performance differences from environment-variable size changes, performance differences from object-link order, performance differences across gcc releases, and especially the *EC2 cloud benchmark* study by Schad et al. that showed bimodal performance distributions on supposedly identical instances (Ch 13.3.3, "The cloud"; Figure 13.32). Goodhart's law is named as the warning against using measurements for evaluation: "Any observed statistical regularity will tend to collapse once pressure is placed on it for control purposes" [V] (Ch 13.1.1, "Measurement uncertainty").

### Data preparation

"Books and presentations on data analysis rarely mention that a large percentage of the time spent on data analysis often has to be invested in data preparation (perhaps 80%, or more, of analysis effort)" [V] (Ch 14.1, "Introduction"). Domain knowledge is essential — knowing 1.1 is not a sensible measurement value for lines of code, knowing developers don't work weekends, knowing system support staff used `pwd` to check Unix uptime (so jobs labelled `pwd` should be filtered from a job-time analysis) (Ch 14.1, "Introduction"). The widely-used NASA MDP dataset has many quality problems, including columns with all-same values, divide-by-zero in derived rows, and "nonsensical values, e.g., lines of code having fractional values" (Ch 14.1.1, "Documenting cleaning operations"). One survey by Hall et al. found 58 of 208 fault-prediction papers between 2000–2010 used MDP without remediating its known problems.

## Connections the author makes in the text

- Cognitive psychology lineage: the text draws heavily on Anderson, Newell, Baddeley (for memory and cognition), Gigerenzer (for fast-and-frugal heuristics), and Ericsson (for deliberate practice); Eysenck & Keane's *Cognitive Psychology: A Student's Handbook* is named as recommended further reading [BT] (Ch 1.6, "Further reading").
- Economics lineage: Tockey's *Return on Software*, Brealey & Myers's *Principles of Corporate Finance* — both named as recommended reading [BT] (Ch 1.6, "Further reading"). Boyd & Richerson on cultural evolution; Henrich on cultural intelligence and WEIRD societies; Christensen on disruptive innovation (Ch 4.2.3, "Entering a market", citing Christensen 1997 implicitly via the *moats* discussion).
- Probability and statistics: Feller's *An Introduction to Probability Theory and its Applications*, Grinstead and Snell's *Introduction to Probability*, Olive's *Applied Robust Statistics*, Crawley's *The R Book*, Kuhn and Johnson's *Applied Predictive Modeling*, Box, Hunter & Hunter's *Statistics for Experimenters* — all named as recommended further reading [BT] (Ch 1.6, "Further reading").
- Software engineering process lineage: Brooks's Law is presented quantitatively rather than as a maxim, with a derivation of the condition under which adding a person delays a project (Ch 5.5.1, "New staff", citing Brooks 1995). The Royce 1970 waterfall paper is read as having been *misread* by the practitioners who adopted it (Ch 5.4.1, "Development methodologies"). The Standish CHAOS report is described as "not representative because it explicitly focuses on failures" [V] (Ch 5.1.2, "Project lifespan"). Halstead's metrics and McCabe's cyclomatic complexity are presented as folklore that has resisted empirical falsification (Ch 7.1.4, "Folklore metrics").
- Main intellectual opponents: Jones's main targeted intellectual rivals are not management thinkers but the academic software-engineering researchers whose evidence-base he finds wanting; the book is structured as a corrective to the corpus of theory-first, evidence-thin software-engineering literature that accumulated from the 1970s onwards.

## Positions the author explicitly frames against

1. **Software engineering as established science.** Jones argues that, at the time of writing, evidence-based software engineering is "essentially a blank slate" — most existing theories are folklore unsupported by data [AR] (Ch 1, "History of software engineering research").
2. **Programmers should aspire to be omnipotent logicians.** The text argues this view is "driven by human self-image, a counter-productive mindset" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Introduction") and that techniques must instead be fitted to the cognitive characteristics actually present in the human brain.
3. **Halstead's and McCabe's complexity metrics are useful.** The text shows these metrics scale with LOC and are easily manipulated by refactoring without changing the underlying logic [AR] (Ch 7.1.4, "Folklore metrics"; Ch 6.4.2, "Source code").
4. **The 28-to-1 productivity range between developers.** The Grant-Sackman 1968 figure is shown to confound batch vs time-sharing system performance with individual subject performance; the corrected figure is roughly 6-to-1 or smaller [AR] (Ch 1, "Folklore"; Ch 2.8.7, "Developer performance").
5. **Cost-of-fixing-faults grows by orders of magnitude with development phase.** The widely-cited 1:10:100 figures come from NASA and DOD data where hardware costs were extreme; in commercial development cost ratios are much smaller (Ch 6.6, "Checking for intended behavior").
6. **The cone of uncertainty as a useful project-management metaphor.** The cone shape is a mathematical artefact of plotting Actual/Estimated against percentage-completed; the metaphor is "at best useless" [V] (Ch 5.4.4, "Managing progress").
7. **The waterfall model is what Royce 1970 recommended.** The text argues Royce 1970 explicitly *warned against* the waterfall approach in the same paper that introduced its diagram; DoD-Std-2167 then enshrined the warned-against approach as the recommended one [AR] (Ch 5.4.1, "Development methodologies").
8. **Maintenance-cost-to-development-cost is typically 5+ to 1.** Survivorship-adjusted analysis of available data gives a mean ratio below 1, with most software systems retired or replaced before substantial maintenance costs accumulate (Ch 4.5.2, "Pounding the treadmill").
9. **Strong typing improves developer reliability.** The few experimental studies finding language-typing effects have shown them to be much smaller than between-subject variation; the available evidence does not support strong claims for type systems improving reliability of code written by professionals [AR] (Ch 7.3.6, "Expressions"). Jones declares his personal belief is that strong typing has cost/benefit advantages when integrated into design — but explicitly marks this as personal belief rather than evidence-based claim.
10. **The CHAOS report's failure rates accurately measure industry project performance.** The CHAOS survey "is not representative because it explicitly focuses on failures, subjects were asked: '… to share failure stories'" [V] (Ch 5.1.2, "Project lifespan").
11. **Bayesian statistics is preferable to frequentist statistics for general use.** While noting that Bayesian methods have advantages when prior beliefs are well-justified, the prior-selection step "opens the door to the bias of opinion and policy guidelines" [AP] (Ch 10.1.1, "Statistical inference"). The book uses frequentist methods throughout for practical reasons.
12. **Static-analysis warnings reliably indicate true coding problems.** The text models how developers will *stop* using analysis tools as false-positive rates rise — using the Ballot Theorem and a hot-hand-style consecutive-false-positive model (Ch 9.1, "Introduction"; Figure 9.1) — suggesting tool design must trade detection against developer adoption.
