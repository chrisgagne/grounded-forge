
# US Forest Service, Learning From Unintended Outcomes (LFUO/FLA/LR) — 2024 Guide (light reference)

**Source:** US Forest Service, Risk Management Council & Office of Safety & Occupational Health (2024). *Learning From Unintended Outcomes and Learning Review Implementation Guide*. Revised April 2024. 98 pages. Licence: **Public domain** (17 USC §105 — US federal-employee work).
**Structure:** 13 Parts plus 8 Appendices (A–H) plus 5 Handouts (A–E) plus Glossary plus Recommended Reading. Part 1 names the three foundational principles (Just Culture, Systems Thinking, Learning); Part 2 distinguishes the four learning tools (AAR, RLS, FLA, Learning Review); Parts 3–9 walk the FLA process from initiation through completion; Parts 10–11 add the Learning Review and Learning Review Board. Lineage: Paul Chamberlain's original FLA → APA/FLA → FLA → combined FLA-LR (2020) → LFUO 2024.

## Author's thesis (condensed)

How an agency responds to an unintended outcome — accident, near-miss, or surprising non-accident — determines whether the organisation actually learns from it. The traditional response (find who broke the rule, blame them, return the system to safety) is "ineffective for improving safety related to a specific accident, but also damaging to the overall learning culture." The guide's organising claim is that learning requires three intertwined foundational principles: *Just Culture* (the work environment of trust, fairness, and dignity in which honest accounts can be given), *Systems Thinking* (examination of policies, procedures, processes, and culture rather than the individual at the sharp end), and *Learning* (the treatment of unintended outcomes as valued learning opportunities and a means of reducing future uncertainty).

A second load-bearing claim is that human performance variability is the rule, not the exception. Adaptation and innovation are continuous and almost always produce successful outcomes; errors, mistakes, and lapses are inherent in the human condition. A third claim is that recommendations and causal statements impede learning in complex systems and should be avoided in the FLA — the Learning Review is the place where vetted recommendations may be produced if a Delegating Official is willing to own them. *Networked causality* (the combined total of conditions that collectively influenced the event) replaces *root cause* as the causal vocabulary. A fourth claim is that the FLA's central learning artefact is "the story" — a factual, sensory, perspective-faithful narrative reconstruction of how the event made sense to participants at the time. Storytelling moves Lessons Learned into the vicarious experience of Lessons Lived.

## The three foundational principles

**Systems Thinking** is the alternative to the blame frame. When an accident happens, systems thinking examines how the organisation's policies, procedures, processes, and culture shaped the conditions around the event. The diagnostic question is framed in *work-as-imagined vs work-as-done* terms: how were employees actually completing the work versus how managers expected the work to be completed?

**Just Culture** is the intersection of trust, fairness, and dignity, with an open and safe reporting system in which everyone can speak. In contrast to criminal-justice accountability (punishment and correction), Just Culture accountability means creating space for each person involved to provide an honest account of what happened from their perspective. *Accountability* in this frame means "an account is something you tell and learn from." The slang antonym *Accountabilism* names the failure mode: claiming to learn from accounts but then punishing via judgmental language or training-as-punishment. Restorative justice (Dekker & Breakey 2016) is a fourth component aimed at restoring victims to wholeness after a serious accident. The Thirtymile, Cramer, and Esperanza fatalities are cited as the real-world example of what happens without a Just Culture — criminal prosecution of firefighters, then employee refusal to participate in the next investigation.

**Learning** treats human performance variability as "not only normal, it's the rule." Adaptations and innovations are continuous and almost always result in successful outcomes; errors and mistakes are inherent. Organisational learning operates at multiple scales — grass-roots story-sharing (Mann Gulch via Maclean's *Young Men and Fire* is the canonical example), leadership-level adaptation of equipment, policy, and training (the Ten Standard Firefighting Orders, the fire shelter, the Incident Command System), and complex FLA / Learning Review Lessons Learned Analyses.

## The four tools

- **AAR (After-Action Review)** — closed, personal, confidential; immediate, local learning that stays inside the work unit.
- **RLS (Rapid Lessons Sharing)** — a simple shareable learning product (one or two-page narrative) for a near-miss, accident, or new tool/technique/process. WFLLC hosts an anonymous submission tool.
- **FLA (Facilitated Learning Analysis)** — has a signed delegation of authority assuring no punitive actions. Scalable from a basic FLA (couple of people, one week) to a complex FLA (5–15 people, weeks to months). Output is a learning product, not recommendations. Suitable for accidents, near-misses, escaped prescribed fires, interagency events, rare events. The FLA/LR family is explicitly distinguished from the DOI Serious Accident Investigation (SAI) process: SAI uses root-cause analysis; FLA/LR use networked causality.
- **Learning Review** — extends the FLA when the Delegating Official wants vetted recommendations and is willing to own them. Adds focus groups (drawn from communities of practice), academic SMEs, a Network of Influences map, and a Learning Review Board that approves recommendations and produces a Safety Action Plan.

## Delegating Official decisions before the team forms

Choose the process, write the delegation of authority. The guide warns against using severity-of-outcome as the trigger for review-team size — the illustrative tree-cutting case (firefighter knocked unconscious by a glancing blow) is framed as "should a chance occurrence of two inches be the determining factor for organizational learning?" Decision-support questions include: how deep will you look? is there enough trust? are there conflicting stories? do people close to the accident believe it could have been much more severe? are recommendations going to be essential (necessitating a Learning Review)?

The tools are explicitly named as scope-extensible beyond accidents — to close calls, near-misses, and surprising non-negative events. Named worked examples include the Retardant Avoidance FLA, the Bear Meadows Stop Work FLA, the Burdette Fire Case Study, the Mud Lake Complex FLA, the COVID-19 Area Command FLA, and "How Do We Honor the Fallen?"

The administrative firewall is non-negotiable: "It is critical to maintain a solid firewall between the FLA and any potential administrative actions... Information is the lifeblood of safety." Administrative actions excluded include disciplinary actions, forced retraining, removal from an incident, and reduction in planned overtime.

## Forming the FLA team

Pre-team priorities: take care of employees (CISM, EAP, Family/Hospital Liaisons), assign trusted liaisons, manage concurrent investigations (claims, prescribed fire, law enforcement). Memory research is cited for note-taking guidance — capture thoughts as quickly as possible; personal notes remain the employee's property.

Minimum team-member attributes: basic understanding of Just Culture, basic understanding of the FLA process, solid reputation for confidentiality, preferably no members from the local unit, selection considers ability to extricate from bias.

Roles named: FLA Process Coach, Learning Review Process Coach, Team Leader (delegated and accountable), Lead Facilitator (FLA process expert, analogous to "chief investigator" on an SAI team), Storyteller/Writer-Editor, Subject-Matter Experts/Peers (current peer to the affected role — non-negotiable for credibility), Technical Specialists, NTDP equipment SMEs (mandatory for PPE/burnover/shelter-deployment cases), the National Chainsaw Program Manager (mandatory for tree-falling/chainsaw accidents), the Missoula Fire Lab (escape-route/safety-zone cases), Human Performance experts via IOL, FARO LIDAR operators, videographers and graphic designers, Union Representative (NFFE has been a strong supporter), Interagency Participation members, and Documentation Specialist.

## The "Recommendations vs Lessons Learned" choice

The guide's central design choice. The old paradigm — find the root cause, fix it, prevent the next accident — is problematic in complex systems because the analysis is always outcome-dependent. "FLAs must avoid causal statements." Recommendations as default output are critiqued on two grounds: managers may jump straight to recommendations, develop an action plan, and believe the problem is solved; and recommendations, when implemented, often add complexity to the workplace, which has the paradoxical effect of increasing risk. The position is not absolute — if the Delegating Official insists on recommendations, the Learning Review process must be initiated, recommendations must be vetted through communities of practice, and a Safety Action Plan must assign owners and dates.

## Engaging the process — site visits, interviews, group dialogue

The heart of the FLA is dialogue with participants. Three modalities: site visits, individual interviews, and the facilitated group dialogue.

**Participant control.** Give participants as much control as possible over the setting. The field where the incident occurred is the best location; the engine bay, saw shop, or picnic tables beat the available conference room. The aim is to prevent participants from feeling interrogated.

**Interviews.** The intent is to capture the person's story. Two-interviewer teams are recommended for key participants (one engages, one notes) with the caveat that two interviewers can be intimidating. The *deflection-question* protocol handles emotional recall: if a participant goes to a difficult point of recall, redirect with a question about something that went right or something they're comfortable talking about, to mentally "walk the person away" from vivid memories. The interview's task is to "describe how the people involved developed their understanding of the situation and made sense of their choices at the time and in context."

**Ten Principles and Agreements for the group dialogue.** No administrative actions; respectful discussion; learning matters more than past blame; willingness to be vulnerable; the FLA team is not here to fix a problem; we all make mistakes; almost all human actions are intuitive responses based on past experience (rarely careless); accidents are usually combinations of normal performance variability and unlikely event sequences; safety is never absolute; safety should be thought of as the reasonableness of risk — a feeling about which two competent professionals can disagree and both be right.

**Facilitator moves.** Location matters; "you are not your point of view"; invite uncertainty; exploit *points of departure* (moments of inconsistency between accounts that reveal different perspectives — not to validate one over the other but to let all see from another's perspective); encourage sensemaking, not adjudication; listen for the silent voice; question the "we just need more training" reflex. Sand tables, Google Earth, and ArcMap are named as reconstruction aids.

## The complex FLA: Lessons Learned Analysis and the Five Hows

The Lessons Learned Analysis is a confidential team dialogue in which the team aims to reach the same situational awareness, belief, and expectation that participants held *before* the accident. Consensus is not the goal; tension between team-member perspectives is valuable. The framing quote is Dekker & Pruchnicki (2013): "Actions that are interpreted as 'bad decisions' after an adverse event are, at the same time actions that seem reasonable — or people would not have taken them."

The *Five Hows* is the question framework for each key action or decision in the event. The guide explicitly instructs against cook-booking it — use the hows that are helpful, skip the redundant or irrelevant ones. The intent is to explore how and why decisions that seemed wrong in hindsight were valid or legitimate in context. The seven-step procedure: gather the team in private; post quotes and key information from interviews; discuss surprising or troubling statements; identify lessons learned and dialogue the beliefs/perceptions/expectations/paradigms held before the event; identify key decisions and actions; deliberate the Five Hows for each; write up the Lesson Learned Analysis.

## Storytelling — the FLA's primary learning artefact

The story is a factual description of what occurred, told to convey context, perspective, and sensory detail. Cognitive science is cited: humans make sense of and remember facts by attaching them to narratives; a listing of findings without a story will be distorted by the reader's mind because they will either create a story or attach the facts to an existing one. Multiple stories are acceptable and add resilience — diversity of narrative is "an enormous source of resilience in complex system not as weakness" (Dekker, Cilliers, Hofmeyr 2011).

Storytelling craft elements (drawing on Lisa Cron's *Wired for Story*): set up main characters without identifying information; set up the place with sensory detail; connect characters with the place; share the character's internal voice (goals, motivations, conflicts between personal and public goals); introduce challenges, obstacles, and goal conflicts (Hollnagel's ETTO is the underlying frame); show how decisions made sense at the time. Past-to-present tense shift during the unintended outcome is named as a craft move.

The *Readback* validates the story with participants before publication, in two phases — first to those directly involved, then to other participants, supervisors, and administrators. Hard copies are collected afterward to prevent contradictory drafts circulating.

## Final FLA considerations and termination

**Counterfactual arguments are forbidden.** "If this person had done X, then Y would have happened" reasoning is incompatible with the FLA's purpose, which is to learn why people did what they actually did.

**Display misalignments.** Contrast how participants made sense of the environment with how administrators thought employees would make sense of it. This is the work-as-imagined / work-as-done diagnostic.

**Termination criterion: reckless and willful disregard.** Conduct that is "intentional, unjustifiable, and occurred with the foreknowledge that the conduct was likely to result in serious harm, death, or injury." The FLA is terminated; the team leader writes a memo recommending administrative or law enforcement action; physical evidence is released to the Delegating Official; interview notes are sealed; team members do not discuss what they learned with anyone except external authorities outside agency control.

## The Learning Review

Frame: analysis (breaking systems into components to find the broken one — useful for machines) vs synthesis (understanding how emergent properties come to be — useful for social systems). The Learning Review incorporates both. For mechanical, ergonomic, and structural failures, engineering-based analysis is admitted; the output is often a *barrier* (tangible or intangible). Barriers are effective in simple and complicated systems but less effective in complex (social) ones.

Focus groups draw from communities of practice (line officers, engine crewmembers, hotshots, helitack, wildland fire modules, sawyers, trail crew, timber markers). External academic SMEs may participate. The output set: Network of Influences map, complex narrative, sensemaking document, proposed recommendations — vetted by a Learning Review Board chaired by the Delegating Official. Approved recommendations become a *Safety Action Plan* with owners and deadlines.

Two learning products: the **Organizational Learning Product** for policy-makers and senior leaders (focused on the gap between work as imagined and work as performed) and the **Field Learning Product** for district/field-office employees (designed for dialogue, simulation, or personal reflection — not limited to written reports).

## Bias and watch-outs

**Hindsight bias** is named explicitly. Human memory builds a coherent story after the outcome is known; sensemaking does not stop after the accident — interview language drifts from "what I wished I had done differently" to "what other people should have done" over the weeks following. The counter-move is to interview as soon as possible and keep participants focused on telling the story from their perspective. The Roese & Vohs 2012 hindsight-bias paper is in Recommended Reading.

**Fundamental attribution error** is named as the trap of blaming employees rather than examining systems. The wider HRO lens (Weick & Sutcliffe 2007) is offered as an alternative dialogue framing in Handout D, organised around the five HRO hallmarks: preoccupation with failure, reluctance to oversimplify, sensitivity to operations, commitment to resilience, deference to expertise.

## Risk and safety — Handout A consolidation

- Risk is inherent; 100% safety is impossible short of inaction.
- Employee perception, anticipation, interpretation, and reaction to risk are systematically connected to workplace design, systems, features, and culture.
- Risk-as-construct (Paul Slovic via Kahneman): "Risk does not exist 'out there'... Human beings have invented the concept of 'risk' to help them understand and cope with the dangers and the uncertainties of life... there is no such thing as a 'real risk' or 'objective risk.'"
- Safety = "the reasonableness of risk" — a feeling, contextual, personal, varying between people in identical situations.
- The agency does not exist to be safe; it exists to accomplish a taxpayer-funded mission as efficiently as possible, knowing the work is hazardous.
- Every safety precaution carries cost to production or compromise to efficiency; employees continuously and often subconsciously make these tradeoffs.
- Outcome-based safety programmes are shortsighted; risk management is about managing risks, not outcomes — and is counterintuitive.

## Signature contrarian positions

1. **Severity-of-outcome should not drive review-team size.** The two-inch tree-cutting case is the canonical illustration: chance occurrence should not determine whether an organisation learns.
2. **Recommendations often interfere with learning.** They add workplace complexity, which paradoxically increases risk; and they offer false reassurance ("if I follow the recommendations, I must be safe").
3. **Causal statements degrade understanding in complex systems.** Cause is created by the investigator's reconstruction, not discovered; networked causality replaces root cause.
4. **The machine metaphor is overused for social systems.** Analysis works for machines; synthesis (and sensemaking) is needed for emergent social systems.
5. **Counterfactuals do not belong in an accident narrative.** Why people did what they did, in context, is the only useful framing.
6. **Severity-blind accountability culture leads to underground learning.** Criminal prosecution of the Thirtymile/Cramer firefighters produced the Esperanza non-cooperation; the FLA/LR family is the response to that failure.
7. **The reckless-and-willful threshold is high.** Most events are good people doing what made sense to them at the time; reckless-and-willful disregard requires intent, unjustifiability, and foreknowledge of likely serious harm.
8. **Risk has no "real" or "objective" referent.** Risk is a human construct; safety is a feeling about the reasonableness of risk.
9. **"More training" is the wrong default response.** Sometimes that's the answer; sometimes the issue is that the work-system design makes the work impossible to do safely while following procedure.
10. **Peer participation is non-negotiable.** Don't try to save money by cutting peers from the team — peers carry the credibility and current-practice knowledge that nobody else can substitute for.
