## Authors' thesis (paraphrased from source)

The book frames accounting as "the process of organizing, analyzing, and communicating financial information that is used for decision-making" [V] (Ch 1.1, "Explain the Importance of Accounting and Distinguish between Financial and Managerial Accounting"). Two parallel branches structure the discipline: *financial accounting*, which "measures the financial performance of an organization using standard conventions to prepare and distribute financial reports" for stakeholders outside the organisation [AP] (Ch 1.1), and *managerial accounting*, which uses both financial and nonfinancial information to support decisions inside the organisation [AP] (Ch 1.1). Volume 1 covers financial accounting; Volume 2 covers managerial accounting. The book repeatedly emphasises that "accounting is the language of business" and extends the traditional adage to "accounting is the language of life" because "at some point, most people will make a decision that relies on accounting information" [V] (Ch 1.1).

A second load-bearing claim runs through the volume: financial reporting is a *multi-stakeholder information system*, and the standards governing it (US GAAP through FASB, with a parallel IFRS regime through IASB, all under SEC regulatory oversight for publicly traded companies) exist precisely because external stakeholders cannot otherwise verify management's representations. Stakeholder categories include stockholders, creditors and lenders, governmental and regulatory agencies, customers, and managers and other employees [AP] (Ch 1.4, "Explain Why Accounting Is Important to Business Stakeholders").

A third load-bearing claim is that the *accounting cycle* — identify and analyze transactions, journalize, post to ledger, prepare unadjusted trial balance, adjust, prepare adjusted trial balance, prepare financial statements, close, prepare post-closing trial balance — is the disciplined backbone that translates raw business events into the four financial statements (income statement, statement of owner's equity / retained earnings, balance sheet, statement of cash flows) [AR] (Chs 3-5). The cycle's coherence depends on accrual accounting under the revenue-recognition and expense-recognition (matching) principles.

A fourth load-bearing claim is that *internal controls* and *Sarbanes-Oxley* exist because financial reporting is a trust artefact between management and external stakeholders, and the structural separation of duties, segregation of cash from custody, executive sign-offs (SOX Section 302), and annual control attestations (SOX Section 404) are how that trust is operationalised in publicly traded companies [AR] (Ch 8). The text presents Enron, WorldCom, HealthSouth, Waste Management, and Bristol-Myers Squibb as cases where the absence or override of internal controls produced fraud at scale.

## Part I: The role of accounting in society (Ch 1)

### Accounting defined

**Accounting** is "the process of organizing, analyzing, and communicating financial information that is used for decision-making" [V] (Ch 1.1). The text situates accounting between financial and nonfinancial considerations: "managers and other decision makers often use nonfinancial, or managerial, information," and "sound organizational decisions are often (and should be) based on both financial and nonfinancial information" [V] (Ch 1.4, "Managers and Other Employees"). Financial accounting is "used to generate information for stakeholders outside of an organization, such as owners, stockholders, lenders, and governmental entities such as the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and the Internal Revenue Service (IRS)" [V] (Ch 1.1). Managerial accounting "uses both financial and nonfinancial information as a basis for making decisions within an organization with the purpose of equipping decision makers to set and evaluate business goals by determining what information they need to make a particular decision and how to analyze and communicate this information" [V] (Ch 1.1).

### Financial reporting institutions

The text catalogues the regulatory institutions that govern financial accounting: **GAAP** (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles), the **FASB** (Financial Accounting Standards Board) which "is an independent, nonprofit organization that sets financial accounting and reporting standards for both public and private sector businesses in the United States" [V] (Ch 1.1), the **SEC** (Securities and Exchange Commission), the **AICPA** (American Institute of Certified Public Accountants), and the **PCAOB** (Public Company Accounting Oversight Board), which "was created after several major cases of corporate fraud, leading to the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002, known as SOX" [AP] (Ch 1.1). Governmental accounting follows separate **GASB** (Governmental Accounting Standards Board) standards. International accounting under **IFRS** (International Financial Reporting Standards) is set by the **IASB**, with FASB and IASB working since 2002 toward convergence; over 120 countries have adopted IFRS or permit its use [AP] (Ch 1.4, IFRS Connection).

### Three organisation types and their stakeholders

Organisations sort into for-profit (manufacturing, retail/merchandising, service), governmental, and not-for-profit. For-profit businesses are further sorted by stock availability: *publicly traded companies* whose stock trades on exchanges (NYSE, NASDAQ) versus *privately held companies* whose stock is not available to the general public [AP] (Ch 1.4, "Stockholders"). The text notes: "while accounting for the largest number of businesses and employment in the United States, [privately held companies] are often smaller (based on value) than publicly traded companies" [V] (Ch 1.4).

### Stakeholders explicitly named

Ch 1.4 ("Explain Why Accounting Is Important to Business Stakeholders") is the spine of the book's stakeholder framing. A **stakeholder** "refers to a person or group who relies on financial information to make decisions, since they often have an interest in the economic viability of an organization or business" [V] (Ch 1.4). Six stakeholder categories are named [AP]:

- **Stockholders.** Owners of stock; in publicly traded companies they "predominantly focus on profitability, expected increase in stock value, and corporate stability" (Ch 1.4, "Stockholders"). Stock as ownership claim is presented in detail in Ch 14.
- **Creditors and Lenders.** Creditors grant extended payment terms (typically 30-45 days); lenders are banks and institutions that lend with longer time frames and interest. Both "use financial information to make decisions. The ultimate decision that both creditors and lenders have to make is whether or not the funds will be repaid by the borrower. The reason this is important is because lending money involves risk" [V] (Ch 1.4, "Creditors and Lenders").
- **Governmental and Regulatory Agencies.** SEC enforcement: "Each year the SEC brings hundreds of civil enforcement actions against individuals and companies for violation of the securities laws. Typical infractions include insider trading, accounting fraud, and providing false or misleading information about securities and the companies that issue them" [V] (Ch 1.4, citing SEC website).
- **Customers.** End-user customers and business customers both have stakes: "customers benefit when the business is financially successful" because "profitable businesses will continue to sell the products the customers want, maintain and improve the business facilities, provide employment for community members, and undertake many other activities that contribute to a vibrant and thriving community" [V] (Ch 1.4, "Customers").
- **Managers and Other Employees.** Employees have employment-security and compensation interests; managers have day-to-day and strategic decision responsibilities.
- (Implicit) communities, suppliers, vendors — surfaced in later chapters.

### Three sources of capital

Businesses have "three ways to raise capital: profitable operations is the first option; selling ownership—stock—which is also called equity financing, is the second option; and borrowing from lenders (called debt financing) is the final option" [V] (Ch 1.4, "Sources of Funding"). Each carries different stakeholder implications, expanded in Ch 13 (long-term debt) and Ch 14 (equity).

### Career paths and the auditor's role

The chapter catalogues accounting career paths (auditing, taxation, financial accounting, consulting, accounting information systems, cost and managerial accounting, financial planning, entrepreneurship). The auditor's role is presented as central to the integrity of external financial reporting: external auditors "typically work for a public accounting firm" and "are independent of the company and provide an unbiased opinion" [V] (Ch 1.5, "Auditing"). The chapter introduces the major certifications: CPA, CMA, CIA, CFE, CFA, CFP. Codes of ethics are highlighted: each certification body has its own; "all require that a professional accountant perform his or her work with integrity" [V] (Ch 1.5, Ethical Considerations).

## Part II: The four financial statements (Ch 2)

### Three legal structures

Three common business structures: sole proprietorship (single owner, ease of formation, unlimited personal liability, single taxation, limited life), partnership (two or more co-owners, ease of formation, unlimited personal liability though LP/LLP variants exist, single taxation, limited life), corporation (one or more legally distinct owners, harder to form, easier to raise capital, limited liability, double taxation, perpetual life) [AP] (Ch 2.1, Table 2.1).

### Stakeholders and the utilitarian framing of accounting decisions

Ch 2.1 contains an Ethical Considerations box that grounds the multi-stakeholder framing in moral theory: "Utilitarianism suggests that an ethical action is one whose consequence achieves the greatest good for the greatest number of people. So, if we want to make an ethical decision, we should ask ourselves who is helped and who is harmed by it... the utilitarian view is an ethical theory that the best action of a company is the one that maximizes utility of all stakeholders to the decision" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Utilitarian View of Accounting Decisions and Stakeholder Well-Being"). The text continues: "If a company focuses on modifying operations and financial reporting to maximize short-term shareholder value, this could indicate the prioritization of certain stakeholder interests above others. When a company pursues only short-term profit for shareholders, it neglects the well-being of other stakeholders. Professional accountants should be aware of the interdependent relationship between all stakeholders and consider whether the results of their decisions are good for the majority of stakeholder interests" [V] (Ch 2.1).

### The four statements and their interrelationships

Four statements prepared in order: (1) **Income Statement** (revenues – expenses ± gains – losses = net income or net loss; for a period of time); (2) **Statement of Owner's Equity** (or Retained Earnings for a corporation; shows changes for a period); (3) **Balance Sheet** (assets = liabilities + equity; on a specific date); (4) **Statement of Cash Flows** (cash inflows and outflows for a period) [AP] (Ch 2.1, "The Four Financial Statements"). The statements are interrelated: net income carries from the income statement to the statement of retained earnings; ending retained earnings carries to the balance sheet.

### The ten elements

Ten elements of the financial statements per FASB SFAC No. 6: revenue, expenses, gains, losses, assets, liabilities, equity, investments by owners, distributions to owners, comprehensive income [V, FASB definition] (Ch 2.3, "Elements of the Financial Statements"). The text quotes FASB on key definitions: assets are "probable future economic benefits obtained or controlled by a particular entity as a result of past transactions or events" [V] (Ch 2.1, footnote citing SFAC No. 6, p. 12); liabilities are "probable future sacrifices of economic benefits arising from present obligations of a particular entity to transfer assets or provide services to other entities in the future as a result of past transactions or events" [V] (Ch 2.1, footnote citing SFAC No. 6, p. 13); revenues are "inflows or other enhancements of assets of an entity or settlements of its liabilities (or a combination of both) from delivering or producing goods, rendering services, or other activities that constitute the entity's ongoing major or central operations" [V] (Ch 2.1, footnote citing SFAC No. 6, p. 23); expenses are "outflows or other using up of assets or incurrences of liabilities (or a combination of both) from delivering or producing goods, rendering services, or carrying out other activities that constitute the entity's ongoing major or central operations" [V] (Ch 2.1, footnote citing SFAC No. 6, p. 23).

### Cash basis versus accrual basis

Cash basis accounting records transactions when cash is exchanged. Accrual basis accounting records transactions when they occur, regardless of cash timing. "The accrual method is theoretically preferable to the cash basis of accounting" because "accrual accounting is advantageous because it distinguishes between the timing of the transactions (when goods and services are provided) and when the cash involved in the transactions is exchanged" [AP] (Ch 2.1, "Statement of Cash Flows"). The accrual method underlies the rest of the volume.

### Liquidity ratios introduced

**Working capital** = current assets – current liabilities. **Current ratio** = current assets / current liabilities. A current ratio greater than 1 indicates the firm can meet short-term obligations [AP] (Ch 2.3, "Liquidity Ratios"). Liquidity is "the business's ability to convert assets into cash in order to meet short-term cash needs" [V] (Ch 2.3).

## Part III: Analyzing and recording transactions (Ch 3)

### Eight GAAP principles, assumptions, and concepts

Ch 3.1 catalogues the conceptual framework that guides US GAAP [AP]:

- **Revenue recognition principle:** revenue is recognised in the period earned, not when cash is received.
- **Expense recognition (matching) principle:** expenses are matched with the revenues they helped generate.
- **Cost principle:** assets are recorded at value at acquisition date, with limited exceptions for financial instruments under fair-value accounting.
- **Full disclosure principle:** any business activities that could affect the financial statements must be reported (in footnotes or addenda).
- **Separate entity concept:** "a business may only report activities on financial statements that are specifically related to company operations, not those activities that affect the owner personally" [V] (Ch 3.1, "Separate Entity Concept").
- **Conservatism:** "if there is uncertainty in a potential financial estimate, a company should err on the side of caution and report the most conservative amount. This would mean that any uncertain or estimated expenses/losses should be recorded, but uncertain or estimated revenues/gains should not" [V] (Ch 3.1, "Conservatism").
- **Monetary measurement concept:** transactions must be measured in a monetary unit (US dollar in the US).
- **Going concern assumption:** "a business will continue to operate in the foreseeable future" absent contrary evidence [V] (Ch 3.1, "Going Concern Assumption").
- **Time period assumption:** information is broken into shorter periods (years, quarters, months) for timeliness.

### Auditor independence

"Auditors' independence from company management is essential for a successful audit because it enables them to approach the audit with the necessary professional skepticism" [V] (Ch 3.1, citing the Center for Audit Quality). The audit committee, not management, oversees the external auditor's work to "monitor disagreements between management and the auditor about financial reporting" [AP] (Ch 3.1). Internal auditors are not independent of the company; only external auditors provide GAAP-conformity opinions.

### The accounting equation, double-entry, debits and credits, T-accounts

The fundamental accounting equation: **Assets = Liabilities + Owners' Equity** (or Stockholders' Equity for corporations). The expanded equation breaks equity into common stock, dividends, revenues, and expenses. **Double-entry bookkeeping** requires that every transaction record at least two accounts and that total debits equal total credits. Each account has a normal balance (debit for assets, expenses, dividends; credit for liabilities, common stock, revenues) [AP] (Ch 3.1, "Normal Balance of an Account", Table 3.1).

### The accounting cycle: nine numbered steps, four addressed in Ch 3

The full cycle: identify and analyse transactions, record to journal, post to general ledger, prepare unadjusted trial balance (Ch 3); record adjusting entries, prepare adjusted trial balance, prepare financial statements (Ch 4); close, prepare post-closing trial balance (Ch 5). Reversing entries, sometimes presented as a tenth step, are not covered in this textbook [AP] (Ch 3.3, Figure 3.5).

The text grounds the cycle in journal entries, T-accounts, and trial balances using a worked example (Printing Plus, Lynn Sanders' fictional printing business). The trial balance verifies that debits equal credits but does not catch all errors (omissions, transpositions in some forms, and compensating errors are not caught).

## Part IV: The adjustment process (Ch 4)

### Adjusting entries: deferrals and accruals

**Adjusting entries** "update accounting records at the end of a period for any transactions that have not yet been recorded" [V] (Ch 4.2, "Discuss the Adjustment Process and Illustrate Common Types of Adjusting Entries"). They are required because of the time period assumption, the revenue recognition principle, and the expense recognition principle.

Two types of adjusting entries [AP] (Ch 4.2):

- **Deferrals:** prepaid expenses (Supplies, Prepaid Insurance, Prepaid Rent) and unearned revenues. Recognition is deferred until the asset is consumed or the obligation is fulfilled.
- **Accruals:** accrued revenues (interest earned but not received, services performed but not yet billed) and accrued expenses (interest, salaries, taxes incurred but not yet paid).

The text takes a clear teaching position: "Cash will never be in an adjusting entry" [V] (Ch 4.3, opening). Each adjusting entry has at least one income statement account and one balance sheet account.

### Depreciation, accumulated depreciation, and book value

Depreciation is "the systematic method to record the allocation of cost over a given period of certain assets" [V] (Ch 4.2, "Prepaid Expenses"). The **useful life** is "the time period over which an asset cost is allocated." A **contra account** (here, Accumulated Depreciation) "is paired with another account type, has an opposite normal balance to the paired account, and reduces the balance in the paired account at the end of a period" [V] (Ch 4.2). **Book value** is the asset's value (cost) minus accumulated depreciation. (Detailed treatment in Ch 11.)

### Earnings management and "cookie jar accounting"

Ch 4 contains an Ethical Considerations box on Bristol-Myers Squibb's "cookie jar accounting" (2000-2001), which resulted in $150 million in SEC fines: "Bristol-Myers inflated its results primarily by (1) stuffing its distribution channels with excess inventory near the end of every quarter in amounts sufficient to meet its targets by making pharmaceutical sales to its wholesalers ahead of demand; and (2) improperly recognizing $1.5 billion in revenue from such pharmaceutical sales to its two biggest wholesalers" [V] (Ch 4.1, "Illegal Cookie Jar Accounting Used to Manage Earnings"). The case anchors the text's argument that adjusting entries can be manipulated, and that boundary between legitimate estimation and earnings manipulation requires ethical discipline.

### The 10-column worksheet and the four financial statements from the adjusted trial balance

The adjusted trial balance feeds the financial statements: revenue and expense accounts go to the income statement; net income flows to the statement of retained earnings; assets, liabilities, and ending retained earnings go to the balance sheet. The 10-column worksheet (trial balance, adjustments, adjusted trial balance, income statement, balance sheet) is presented as an optional aid [AP] (Ch 4.5).

## Part V: Completing the accounting cycle (Ch 5)

### Closing entries: temporary versus permanent accounts

**Closing entries** "prepare a company for the next accounting period by clearing any outstanding balances in certain accounts that should not transfer over to the next period" [V] (Ch 5.1, "Introduction to the Closing Entries"). **Permanent (real) accounts** — assets, liabilities, equity — carry balances forward. **Temporary (nominal) accounts** — revenues, expenses, dividends, income summary — are closed to retained earnings each period.

Four closing entries [AP] (Ch 5.1, "Journalizing and Posting Closing Entries"):

1. Close revenue accounts to Income Summary (debit revenues, credit Income Summary).
2. Close expense accounts to Income Summary (debit Income Summary, credit expenses).
3. Close Income Summary to Retained Earnings (the resulting net income or loss flows to equity).
4. Close Dividends to Retained Earnings.

### The classified balance sheet and operating cycle

A **classified balance sheet** sub-divides assets (current; property, plant, and equipment; long-term investments; intangibles) and liabilities (current; long-term). The **operating cycle** is "the amount of time it takes a company to use its cash to provide a product or service and collect payment from the customer" [AP] (Ch 5.3, "The Classified Balance Sheet"). For most businesses, one year is the dividing line between current and non-current.

## Part VI: Merchandising transactions (Ch 6)

### Service versus merchandising

A **service company** "provides intangible services to customers and does not have inventory" [V] (Ch 6.1, "Comparison of Merchandising Transactions versus Service Transactions"). A **merchandising company** "resells finished goods (inventory) produced by a manufacturer (supplier) to customers" [V]. Merchandising companies have an additional concern (cost of goods sold, gross margin) that service companies do not.

### Purchase and sales transactions, gross/net, discounts, returns, allowances

**Gross purchases** are "the original amount of the purchase without considering reductions" and **net purchases** subtract purchase discounts, returns, and allowances [V] (Ch 6.1). **Gross sales** is the original sale amount; **net sales** subtracts sales discounts, returns, and allowances [V].

Credit terms like "2/10, n/30" mean: 2% discount if paid in 10 days, otherwise net due in 30 days. **Cash discount** is a discount on the final price for prompt payment; **trade discount** is a price reduction negotiated before purchase and not separately recorded [AP] (Ch 6.1).

### Perpetual versus periodic inventory systems

A **perpetual inventory system** "automatically updates and records the inventory account every time a sale, or purchase of inventory, occurs" [V] (Ch 6.2, "Characteristics of the Perpetual and Periodic Inventory Systems"). A **periodic inventory system** "updates and records the inventory account at certain, scheduled times at the end of an operating cycle." Most modern systems use perpetual; periodic remains in use where computerised systems are absent.

### FOB shipping point versus FOB destination

**FOB shipping point** transfers ownership at the seller's location; the buyer pays freight and bears in-transit risk. **FOB destination point** transfers ownership at the buyer's location; the seller pays freight and bears in-transit risk [AP] (Ch 6.5). The distinction determines whether shipping costs are *freight-in* (added to inventory cost) or *freight-out* (recorded as a selling expense by the seller).

### Multi-step versus simple income statement; gross profit margin

A **multi-step income statement** separates cost of goods sold from operating expenses to produce **gross margin**, then deducts operating expenses for **income from operations**, then adjusts for other revenue and expense to produce net income. A **simple income statement** combines all revenues and all expenses [AP] (Ch 6.6). The **gross profit margin ratio** = gross profit / net sales — "the larger the margin, the more availability the company has to reinvest in their business, pay down debt, and return dividends to shareholders" [AP] (Ch 6.6, "Final Analysis of the Two Income Statement Options").

### Internal controls over returns

Ch 6.1 surfaces internal-control discipline early through an Ethical Considerations box (citing COSO Internal Control–Integrated Framework, 2013): "Merchandise return controls require that there be a separation of duties between the employee approving the return and the person recording the return of merchandise in the accounting records. Basically, the person performing the return should not be the person recording the event in the accounting records. This is called separation of duties and is just one example of an internal control that should be used when merchandise is returned" [V] (Ch 6.1, "Internal Controls over Merchandise Returns"). Full treatment in Ch 8.

## Part VII: Accounting information systems (Ch 7)

### Three stages: input, processing, output

An **accounting information system (AIS)** is "a system or set of processes for collecting data about accounting transactions; recording, organizing, and summarizing the data; and culminating with the preparation of financial statements and other reports for internal and external users" [V] (Ch 7.1, "Manual and Computerized Accounting Information Systems"). All AISs have the same components: input data, store data, process data, aggregate data, present data [AP].

The chapter contrasts manual and computerised systems. Examples include QuickBooks (for small organisations), SAP and Oracle (for large or multinational organisations), and ERP (enterprise resource planning) systems for company-wide integration.

### Source documents and the audit trail

A **source document** is "the original document that provides evidence that a transaction occurred" [V] (Ch 7.1, "Inputting/Entering Data"). The **audit trail** is "a trail of evidence documenting the history of a specific transaction starting from its inception/source document and showing all the steps it went through until its final disposition" [V]. The audit trail enables internal investigation of errors, vendor disputes, and customer complaints — and is the structural device that allows external auditors to test transactions.

### Special journals and subsidiary ledgers

The general journal records all transactions in chronological order, but for high-volume transaction types (cash receipts, cash disbursements, sales, purchases) **special journals** improve searchability and division of labour. A **subsidiary ledger** (e.g., accounts receivable subsidiary ledger by customer, accounts payable subsidiary ledger by vendor) supports the corresponding general-ledger control account [AP] (Ch 7.2-7.4).

### IFRS and global AIS design

The IFRS Connection in Ch 7.1 notes that companies with international subsidiaries often need accounting systems that handle both US GAAP and IFRS — "We have basically become a two GAAP world—IFRS and US GAAP—and many companies will find it necessary to have accounting information systems that can handle both sets of rules due to the global nature of business and the global nature of raising money through borrowing and issuing stock" [V] (Ch 7.1).

## Part VIII: Fraud, internal controls, cash, and Sarbanes-Oxley (Ch 8)

### Fraud defined and the fraud triangle

**Fraud** is "the act of intentionally deceiving a person or organization or misrepresenting a relationship in order to secure some type of benefit, either financial or nonfinancial" [V] (Ch 8.1, "Analyze Fraud in the Accounting Workplace"). The **fraud triangle** (Donald Cressey, criminologist and sociologist) has three elements: *perceived opportunity*, *rationalization*, and *incentive (or pressure)* [BT] (Ch 8.1). Cressey developed the framework "to help explain why law-abiding citizens sometimes commit serious workplace related crimes" — they came into "a 'non-sharable financial problem'" they felt could be alleviated by violating a position of trust.

The text concentrates on the *opportunity* axis because that is where internal controls bite: "Companies usually focus on the opportunity aspect of mitigating fraud because, they can develop internal controls to manage the risk. The rationalization and pressure to commit fraud are harder to understand and identify" [AP] (Ch 8.1).

### COSO and the five components of internal control

Internal controls are "the systems used by an organization to manage risk and diminish the occurrence of fraud" [V] (Ch 8.2). The **Committee of Sponsoring Organizations (COSO)** issued the Internal Control–Integrated Framework, identifying five components of an effective internal control system: control environment, risk assessment, control activities, information and communication, and monitoring [BT] (Ch 8.2, Figure 8.3).

A properly designed system: "ensure assets are properly used; ensure that the accounting system is functioning properly; monitor operations of the organization to ensure maximum efficiency; ensure that assets are kept secure; ensure that employees are in compliance with corporate policies" [V] (Ch 8.2).

### Six elements of a strong internal control system

[AP] (Ch 8.3, "Elements of Internal Control"):

- Establishment of clear responsibilities.
- Proper documentation (with off-site backups).
- Adequate insurance.
- Separation of assets from custody.
- Separation of duties.
- Use of technology (cameras, password protection, biometric devices).

The AICPA on segregation of duties: "based on shared responsibilities of a key process that disperses the critical functions of that process to more than one person or department. Without this separation in key processes, fraud and error risks are far less manageable" [V] (Ch 8.2, "Bank Fraud in Enid, Oklahoma", citing AICPA).

### Enron, WorldCom, and the genesis of Sarbanes-Oxley

Enron's failure: "When this practice was uncovered, the owners of Enron stock lost $40 billion as the stock price dropped from $91 per share to less than $1 per share" [V] (Ch 8.2, citing Linder). Arthur Andersen, Enron's auditor, "did not maintain an adequate degree of independence" — providing both auditing and consulting services — "which prevented them from approaching the audit of Enron with a proper degree of independence" [V] (Ch 8.2). The 2002 **Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX)** was passed in response.

### Major accounting components of Sarbanes-Oxley

[AP] (Ch 8.2, "Major Accounting Components of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act"):

- Auditor must issue an internal control report following evaluation of internal controls.
- Limit non-audit services (such as consulting) provided to the same client.
- Rotate audit lead: "the person in charge of the audit can serve for a period of no longer than seven years without a break of two years" [V].
- The PCAOB oversees the auditors of public companies, "established in 2002... to ensure independent, accurate, and informative audit reports, monitoring the audits of securities brokers and dealers, and maintaining oversight of the accountants and accounting firms that audit publicly traded companies" [AP].
- Section 404: management must perform annual audits to assess and document internal control effectiveness, and selected executives must sign attesting that the audit fairly represents the company's financial records and conditions.
- Section 302: CEO and CFO personally certify that they have reviewed the internal control report, that it does not contain inaccurate information, and that they believe all financial information fairly states the financial conditions, income, and cash flows. "The sign-off under Section 302 makes the CEO and CFO personally responsible for financial reporting as well as internal control structure" [AP] (Ch 8.5, "Discuss Management Responsibilities for Maintaining Internal Controls within an Organization").
- Penalties: up to $5 million in fines and 20-25 years in prison; "The penalty is more severe for securities fraud (25 years) than for mail or wire fraud (20 years)" [V] (Ch 8.2).
- All business records, electronic records, and electronic messages must be stored for at least five years (Ch 8.7).

### Tone at the top

"Even if a company is not public and not governed by the SOX, it is important to note that the tone is set at the managerial level, called the tone at the top. If management respects the internal control system and emphasizes the importance of maintaining proper internal controls, the rest of the staff will follow and create a cohesive environment. A proper tone at the top demonstrates management's commitment toward openness, honesty, integrity, and ethical behavior" [V] (Ch 8.5).

### Petty cash and bank reconciliation

A **petty cash fund** is "a predetermined amount of cash held on hand to be used to make payments for small day-to-day purchases" [V] (Ch 8.4); it is an **imprest account** with a fixed balance replaced as it is spent.

A **bank reconciliation** is "the internal financial report that explains and documents any differences that may exist between the balance of a checking account as reflected by the bank's records (bank balance) for a company and the company's accounting records (company balance)" [V] (Ch 8.6, "Define the Purpose of a Bank Reconciliation"). Common reconciling items: outstanding checks, deposits in transit, bank service fees, NSF (nonsufficient funds) checks, errors, and interest or fee additions.

### Financial statement fraud: cooking the books

**Financial statement fraud** "occurs when the financial statements are used to conceal the actual financial condition of a company or to hide specific transactions that may be illegal" [V] (Ch 8.7, "Describe Fraud in Financial Statements and Sarbanes-Oxley Act Requirements"). HealthSouth (1996-2002) used "a combination of false revenue accounts and misstated accounts receivable in a direct manipulation of the revenue accounts to commit a multibillion-dollar fraud" [AP] (Ch 8.7, citing Dickinson and McCann).

Inventory manipulation methods catalogued [AP] (Ch 8.7):

- Channel stuffing: encouraging customers to buy more than they need under generous return terms.
- Sham sales: recording sales without actual customers.
- Bill-and-hold sales: recognising income before title transfers, with inventory still in seller's warehouse.
- Improper cutoff: recording sales in the wrong period (early revenue recognition).
- Round-tripping: selling with a contemporaneous repurchase agreement.

Enron used **special purpose entities** (SPEs) — "separate, often complicated legal entities that are often used to absorb risk for a corporation" — to hide losses off the balance sheet [AP] (Ch 8.7).

### Cybersecurity as a SOX-era control

**Cybersecurity** is "the practice of protecting software, hardware, and data from digital attacks" [V] (Ch 8.7, "Sarbanes-Oxley Act Compliance Today"). Modern SOX compliance has expanded beyond financial reporting to information technology activities, with significant growth in cybersecurity disclosures [AP].

## Part IX: Accounting for receivables (Ch 9)

### The five-step revenue recognition model (FASB ASU 2014-09)

[AP] (Ch 9.1, "Accounting Principles and Assumptions Regulating Revenue Recognition"):

1. Identify the contract with the customer.
2. Identify the separate performance obligations in the contract.
3. Determine the transaction price.
4. Allocate the transaction price to the separate performance obligations.
5. Recognise revenue when each performance obligation is satisfied.

The model "replaces U.S. GAAP's current industry-specific revenue recognition practices with a principle-based approach, potentially affecting both day-to-day business accounting and the execution of business contracts with customers" [AP, citing AICPA] (Ch 9.1, Ethical Considerations).

### Bad debts: direct write-off versus the three allowance methods

The **direct write-off method** delays recognition until a specific account is identified as uncollectible. This method "is not an acceptable method of recording bad debts [under GAAP], because it violates the matching principle" [V] (Ch 9.2, "Fundamentals of Bad Debt Expenses"). However, the IRS requires the direct write-off method for tax purposes (creating a routine GAAP-tax difference).

The **allowance method** estimates bad debt during the period (matching principle). Three sub-variants [AP] (Ch 9.2):

- **Income statement method (percentage of sales):** estimates bad debt as a percentage of credit sales; simple but does not consider age of receivables.
- **Balance sheet method (percentage of accounts receivable):** estimates bad debt as a percentage of the AR balance; also does not age receivables.
- **Balance sheet aging of receivables method:** organises AR into past-due categories with different uncollectible percentages assigned to each. "The longer the time passes with a receivable unpaid, the lower the probability that it will get collected" [V] (Ch 9.2). This method is more complicated but more accurate.

The **net realizable value** of accounts receivable = AR – allowance for doubtful accounts (a contra asset account).

### Receivables ratios

[AP] (Ch 9.3):

- **Accounts receivable turnover** = net credit sales / average AR. Higher = faster collection.
- **Number of days' sales in receivables** = 365 / AR turnover. Lower = faster collection.

### Earnings management versus earnings manipulation

This section is the book's most explicit treatment of an ethics-edge area. The text distinguishes the two: "**Earnings management** works within GAAP constraints to improve stakeholders' views of the company's financial position. **Earnings manipulation** is noticeably different in that it typically ignores GAAP rules to alter earnings significantly. Carried to an extreme, manipulation can lead to fraudulent behavior by a company" [V] (Ch 9.4, "Discuss the Role of Accounting for Receivables in Earnings Management"). The text identifies several legitimate-but-pliable estimation choices in receivables: changing the bad-debt percentage, changing aging-category boundaries, changing the estimation method (income-statement vs balance-sheet vs aging), each within GAAP — but each affecting reported income and AR balances. "These changes would need to be considered acceptable by the company's outside auditors during the annual independent audit" [V].

The text takes a position on what motivates earnings management: showing healthier income, meeting market expectations, securing management bonuses, attracting investors, securing borrowing, increasing valuation in M&A contexts, deferring tax obligations [AP] (Ch 9.4). Ratio analysis and trend analysis are presented as the analyst's tools to spot earnings management.

### Long-term project revenue recognition

Two methods for long-term construction-type contracts [AP] (Ch 9.5):

- **Percentage of completion method:** revenue recognised based on portion of work completed each period. Default under most circumstances.
- **Completed contract method:** revenue and expenses delayed until contract complete. Used when cost and earnings cannot be reasonably estimated.

For real-estate **installment sales**, the installment method defers gross profit recognition over the life of the receivable based on a gross-profit-percentage application to each cash collection (Ch 9.5).

## Part X: Inventory (Ch 10)

### Goods available for sale and the inventory equation

**Goods available for sale** = beginning inventory + purchases. These are either sold (Cost of Goods Sold on the income statement) or held (Merchandise Inventory on the balance sheet) [AP] (Ch 10.1, "Fundamentals of Inventory").

### Four cost-flow assumptions

[AP] (Ch 10.1, "Data for Demonstration of the Four Basic Inventory Valuation Methods"):

- **Specific identification:** track each unit individually. Used for high-value, distinguishable items (cars, diamonds).
- **First-in, first-out (FIFO):** assumes earliest-purchased units are sold first. Ending inventory reflects most recent costs.
- **Last-in, first-out (LIFO):** assumes most recently purchased units are sold first. Ending inventory reflects oldest costs.
- **Weighted-average cost:** weighted average of all units' costs.

The text emphasises that the cost-flow assumption is a *choice about how to allocate costs*, not a description of physical flow [AP] (Ch 10.1).

### IFRS and LIFO

The text takes a clear position on the international divergence: "IFRS does not permit the use of LIFO. This is a major difference between US GAAP and IFRS. The AICPA estimates that roughly 35-40% of all US companies use LIFO, and in some industries, such as oil and gas, the use of LIFO is more prevalent. Because LIFO generates lower taxable income during times of rising prices, it is estimated that eliminating LIFO would generate an estimated $102 billion in tax revenues in the US for the period 2017-2026. In creating IFRS, the IASB chose to eliminate LIFO, arguing that FIFO more closely matches the flow of goods. In the US, FASB believes the choice between LIFO and FIFO is a business model decision that should be left up to each company" [V] (Ch 10.1, IFRS Connection).

### Inflation and deflation effects

In inflation, FIFO produces higher ending inventory and lower COGS (higher net income); LIFO produces lower ending inventory and higher COGS (lower net income, lower taxes). In deflation, the effects reverse [AP] (Ch 10.1, "Inflationary Versus Deflationary Cycles").

### Lower of cost or market (LCM) and the conservatism principle

**Lower-of-cost-or-market (LCM)** "requires that the balance in the inventory account should be decreased to the more conservative replacement value rather than be overstated on the balance sheet" [V] (Ch 10.1, "Lower-of-Cost-or-Market"). This is conservatism applied to inventory: write down when below cost, do not write up.

### Inventory ratios

[AP] (Ch 10.5):

- **Inventory turnover** = COGS / average inventory. Higher = faster sales rotation.
- **Number of days' sales in inventory** = average inventory / average daily COGS, or 365 / inventory turnover. Lower = faster sales.

### Auditor inventory fraud testing

Citing Statement on Auditing Standards (SAS) No. 99 and AU Section 316: auditors have a duty to perform "procedures at locations on a surprise or unannounced basis, for example, observing inventory on unexpected dates or at unexpected locations or counting cash on a surprise basis" [V] (Ch 10.1, "Auditors Look for Inventory Fraud", citing AICPA).

## Part XI: Long-term assets (Ch 11)

### Tangible and intangible distinguished

**Tangible assets** have physical substance (land, buildings, equipment, machinery, vehicles); **intangible assets** "have value but often lack a physical existence" (patents, copyrights, trademarks, franchises, licenses, goodwill, sometimes software) [V] (Ch 11.1).

### Capitalisation versus expensing

To **capitalise** is "the process by which a long-term asset is recorded on the balance sheet and its allocated costs are expensed on the income statement over the asset's economic life" [V] (Ch 11.2). The capitalised cost includes "all of the costs necessary to place the asset into service" — purchase price, sales taxes, shipping, assembly, legal fees [AP] (Ch 11.3). Routine repairs and maintenance are expensed; improvements that extend useful life or increase capacity are capitalised.

### Three depreciation methods

[AP] (Ch 11.3, Table 11.2):

- **Straight-line:** (cost – salvage value) / useful life. Equal annual expense.
- **Units-of-production:** (cost – salvage value) × (units produced this period / total units estimated). Tied to usage.
- **Double-declining-balance:** book value × straight-line rate × 2. Accelerated; salvage value is not subtracted in the first year's calculation but the asset cannot be depreciated below salvage value.

The text takes a position on the ethics of estimating useful life and salvage value: "management teams typically fail to invest either time or attention into making or periodically revisiting and revising reasonably supportable estimates of asset lives or salvage values, or the selection of depreciation methods, as prescribed by GAAP" [V, citing The CPA Journal] (Ch 11.3, "Depreciation Analysis Requires Careful Evaluation"). Waste Management was disciplined by the SEC "for fraudulently altering its estimates to reduce depreciation expense and overstate net income by $1.7 billion" [AP] (Ch 11.3, citing SEC).

### IFRS component depreciation and fair-value option

Two significant US GAAP / IFRS differences: (1) IFRS requires "component depreciation" where feasible — separate depreciation schedules for components of an asset with different useful lives (e.g., aircraft interior, engines, fuselage). (2) IFRS allows companies to revalue long-lived assets to fair value as of the balance sheet date by class of asset. US GAAP carries assets at depreciated historical cost regardless [AP] (Ch 11.3, IFRS Connection).

### Depletion of natural resources; amortization of intangibles

**Natural resources** (lumber, mineral deposits, oil/gas fields) are depleted as extracted, with **accumulated depletion** as a contra account; depletion is calculated like units-of-production [AP] (Ch 11.3). **Amortization** of intangibles is similar to depreciation but typically uses straight-line method, no salvage value, and writes down the asset directly without a separate accumulated-amortization contra account [AP] (Ch 11.3).

### Goodwill: testing for impairment

"Goodwill does not have an expected life span and therefore is not amortized. However, a company is required to compare the book value of goodwill to its market value at least annually to determine if it needs to be adjusted. This comparison process is called testing for impairment. If the market value of goodwill is found to be lower than the book value, then goodwill needs to be reduced to its market value. If goodwill is impaired, it is reduced with a credit, and an impairment loss is debited. Goodwill is never increased beyond its original cost" [V] (Ch 11.4, "Goodwill").

### WorldCom ethics case

Ch 11.2 contains a striking case: "WorldCom used a number of accounting gimmicks to defraud investors, mainly including capitalizing costs that should have been expensed... WorldCom controlled a large percentage of backbone routes, a major component of the hardware supporting the internet... If WorldCom's bankruptcy due to accounting malfeasance shut the company down, then the internet would no longer be functional" [AP] (Ch 11.2, "How WorldCom's Improper Capitalization of Costs Almost Shut Down the Internet").

## Part XII: Current liabilities (Ch 12)

### Definition and the burn rate framing

A **current liability** is "a debt or obligation due within a company's standard operating period, typically a year" [V] (Ch 12.1). The text frames current-liability classification as load-bearing for stakeholder analysis: "investors and creditors look to the current liabilities to assist in calculating a company's annual burn rate. The burn rate is the metric defining the monthly and annual cash needs of a company. It is used to help calculate how long the company can maintain operations before becoming insolvent" [V] (Ch 12.1, "Proper Current Liabilities Reporting and Calculating Burn Rate").

### Common current liabilities

[AP] (Ch 12.1):

- **Accounts payable:** financial obligations to suppliers after credit purchases.
- **Unearned revenue (deferred revenue):** customer's advance payment for product or service not yet provided.
- **Current portion of a note payable:** the portion due within the current year.
- **Taxes payable:** sales taxes collected on behalf of governments, income taxes payable, etc.
- **Interest payable:** accrued interest not yet paid.
- **Salaries / wages payable:** earned but not yet paid.

### Notes payable and interest

The interest formula presented: Interest = Principal × Annual Interest Rate × Part of Year. The text uses a 360-day year for some calculations but notes that "with today's technology, it is more common to see the interest calculation performed using a 365-day year" [V] (Ch 12.1).

### Contingent liabilities: FASB's two requirements

A **contingency** is "a current situation that has an outcome that is unknown or uncertain and will not be resolved until a future point in time" [V] (Ch 12.3). For a contingent liability to be recognised, two FASB requirements: (1) **likelihood of occurrence** (probable, reasonably possible, or remote) and (2) **measurement** (estimable or inestimable) [AP] (Ch 12.3, "Two Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) Requirements for Recognition of a Contingent Liability").

### Four treatments of contingent liabilities

[V, treatment table] (Ch 12.3, Table 12.2):

| Likelihood | Measurement | Journalize? | Note disclosure? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Probable | Estimable | Yes | Yes |
| Probable | Inestimable | No | Yes |
| Reasonably possible | (Either) | No | Yes |
| Remote | (Either) | No | No |

US GAAP probable threshold is often interpreted at ~80% likelihood; IFRS uses "more likely than not" (~50%). When estimating loss contingencies in a range, US GAAP records the low end of the range; IFRS records the discounted midpoint [AP] (Ch 12.3, IFRS Connection).

### Payroll: involuntary and voluntary deductions

**Involuntary deductions** [AP] (Ch 12.5, "Employee Compensation and Deductions"):

- Federal, state, and local income tax withholdings.
- FICA Social Security tax (6.2% rate, capped at $127,200 in 2017, $128,400 in 2018).
- FICA Medicare tax (1.45% rate, no cap).
- Additional Medicare Tax (0.9% under the ACA above income threshold).
- Garnishments, levies, child support, bankruptcy judgments.

**Voluntary deductions:** health insurance, life insurance, retirement contributions (defined contribution plans like 401(k) or 403(b)), charitable contributions, pension funds, union dues.

The employer matches FICA and pays additional payroll taxes [AP] (Ch 12.5):

- Federal Unemployment Tax Act (FUTA): 6%, can be reduced to 0.6% with state credit.
- State Unemployment Tax Act (SUTA): rates vary by state.

## Part XIII: Long-term liabilities (Ch 13)

### Notes payable versus bonds

Both are debt-based financing. Differences [AP] (Ch 13.1):

- A note is held by one entity (often the original lender); a bond is sold to many investors.
- Note issuance is simpler; bond issuance involves underwriters, regulatory approval, and many months of preparation.
- Notes typically have one owner over the loan's life; bonds may be owned by several investors over their life via the secondary market.
- Notes can usually be paid off early; most bonds must be held to maturity.

### Bond features

[AP] (Ch 13.1, "Fundamentals of Bonds"):

- **Bond indenture** (or bond certificate): the legal contract between issuer and lender.
- **Principal (face value, maturity value):** typically $1,000 per bond; the amount repaid at maturity.
- **Maturity date:** the date principal is repaid.
- **Stated interest rate (coupon rate):** the rate printed on the bond, used to calculate periodic interest payments.
- **Market interest rate (effective rate, bond yield):** the rate determined by market conditions at issuance.

When stated rate equals market rate, the bond sells at *par*. When stated > market, the bond sells at a *premium*. When stated < market, the bond sells at a *discount* [AP].

### Bond types

[AP] (Ch 13.1):

- **Debenture:** unsecured bond.
- **Secured bond:** pledged asset as collateral.
- **Term bond:** single-payment maturity.
- **Serial bond:** maturity in a series of payments.
- **Callable bond (redeemable bond):** issuer can repurchase before maturity.
- **Putable bond:** holder can sell back early.
- **Convertible bond:** can be converted to common stock.
- **Junk bond (speculative, high-yield):** higher interest, higher risk; market created in the 1970s-1980s, attributed to Michael Milken who was eventually indicted on 98 counts and pleaded guilty to six [AP] (Ch 13.1, "Junk Bonds").

### Credit ratings

The chapter includes a rating-agency comparison (Ch 13.2, Table 13.1) covering Moody's, Standard & Poor's, and Fitch from "Highest Quality" (Aaa/AAA/AAA) through "Investment Grade" (down to Baa3/BBB-/BBB-) into "Not Investment Grade" / Speculative grades (BB through CCC and below) and "In Default" (D/DDD/DD/D). The credit rating determines borrowing cost.

### Effective-interest method versus straight-line amortization

GAAP requires the **effective-interest method** for amortising bond discounts and premiums "unless there is no significant difference between the effective-interest method and the straight-line method" [V] (Ch 13.2). IFRS requires effective-interest with no exceptions.

### Interest types

**Simple interest** = principal × rate × time. **Compound interest** "means that the interest earned also earns interest" [V] (Ch 13.1). Typical loan amortization tables apply effective interest to the carrying value, with principal repayment increasing each period and interest expense decreasing.

## Part XIV: Corporation accounting (Ch 14)

### Five advantages of corporate form

[AP] (Ch 14.1, "Advantages of the Corporate Form"):

- Separate legal entity (can sue and be sued, contract, hold property).
- Limited liability: "A corporation usually limits the liability of an investor to the amount of his or her investment in the corporation" [V].
- Transferable ownership.
- Continuing existence (going concern in the legal sense).
- Ease of raising capital.

### Three disadvantages

[AP]: organisation costs, regulation, double taxation. C corporations pay corporate tax and stockholders pay personal tax on dividends and gains. S corporations (closely held, fewer than 100 stockholders) and LLCs avoid double taxation by passing income through to owners [AP].

### Debt versus equity financing — six considerations

[AP] (Ch 14.1, "Financing Options: Debt versus Equity"): dilution of ownership, repayment obligation, cash obligation, budgeting (variable-rate vs fixed-rate considerations), administrative costs, creditor risk assessment (debt-to-equity covenant compliance).

### Common and preferred stock

**Common stockholders' four basic rights** [AP] (Ch 14.1, "Common Stock"):

1. Vote on corporate matters (one share = one vote, typically).
2. Share in corporate net income through dividends.
3. Share in distribution of assets at liquidation, after creditors and preferred stockholders.
4. Preemptive right (in some jurisdictions): maintain ownership percentage when new shares are issued.

**Preferred stock** has fixed dividend rates (assuming sufficient declarations), priority over common in dividend distribution and at liquidation, but typically no voting rights. Preferred stock's accounting classification "is often a controversial area in accounting as some researchers believe preferred stock has characteristics closer to that of a stock/bond hybrid security" [AP] (Ch 14.1).

### Stock value vocabulary

[AP] (Ch 14.1, "Stock Values"):

- **Authorized shares:** maximum the corporate charter permits.
- **Issued shares:** sold to investors at some point.
- **Outstanding shares:** issued and currently held by investors (issued less treasury).
- **Treasury stock:** repurchased by the corporation; reduces outstanding but not issued.
- **Par value:** "value assigned to stock in the company's charter and is typically set at a very small arbitrary amount; serves as legal capital" [V].
- **Stated value:** for no-par stock, the board-assigned value functioning as legal capital.
- **Market value:** trading price on the stock market; not in the books.

### The Dodge v. Ford "business judgment rule"

Ch 14.1 surfaces a stakeholder governance frame via case law: "Courts have ruled that, 'A business corporation is organized and carried on primarily for the profit of the stockholders' as initially ruled in the early case Dodge v. Ford Motor Co., 204 Mich. 459, 170 N.W. 668 (Mich. 1919). This early case outlined the 'business judgment rule.' It allows for a corporation to use its judgment in how to run the company in the best interests of the shareholders, but also allows the corporation the ability to make decisions for the benefit of the company's stakeholders... A shareholder is also a stakeholder in any decision. A stakeholder is anyone with an interest in the outcome in the corporation's decision, even if the person owns no financial interest in the corporation. Corporations need to take a proactive step in managing stakeholder concerns and issues" [V/AP] (Ch 14.1, "Shareholders, Stakeholders, and the Business Judgment Rule").

### Three dividend types and three dividend dates

[AP] (Ch 14.3): cash dividends, property dividends (assets other than cash), stock dividends (additional shares).

Three dates: **date of declaration** (board votes; dividend becomes a legal liability), **date of record** (list of eligible shareholders prepared; no journal entry), **date of payment**. Stock between record and payment trades **ex dividend** (buyer not entitled to the dividend).

### Stock dividends and stock splits

A **small stock dividend** (less than 25% of outstanding) reduces retained earnings by market value of new shares. A **large stock dividend** (over 25%) reduces retained earnings by par value. A **stock split** issues new shares in place of old at a different par value (e.g., 2-for-1 doubles shares and halves par value); a **reverse stock split** is the opposite [AP] (Ch 14.3).

### Earnings per share

**Earnings per share (EPS)** = (net income – preferred dividends) / weighted average common shares outstanding [V] (Ch 14.5). The text observes that "many financial analysts believe that EPS is the single most important tool in assessing a stock's market price" [AP] (Ch 14.5). EPS is the only ratio GAAP requires to be reported on the face of the income statement; public companies report basic and diluted EPS.

### Stock buybacks and the ethics of EPS engineering

The text takes a position on stock buybacks: "S&P 500 companies will spend about $780 billion on share buybacks in 2017, marking a 30% rise from 2016" [V, citing Goldman Sachs via MarketWatch] (Ch 14.5, "Stock Buybacks Drive Up Earnings per Share: Ethical?"). Forbes is cited noting that buying back shares was legalized in 1982 but "for the majority of the twentieth century, corporate buybacks of shares was considered illegal because 'they were thought to be a form of stock market manipulation'... Buying back company stock can inflate a company's share price and boost its earnings per share—metrics that often guide lucrative executive bonuses" [V, citing Forbes]. The text leaves the ethical question open: "Is a corporation buying back its shares an ethical way in which to raise or maintain the price of a company's shares?"

## Part XV: Partnership accounting (Ch 15)

### Seven characteristics of a partnership

[AP] (Ch 15.1, "Characteristics of a Partnership"):

- Formation by agreement (Uniform Partnership Act / RUPA governs in absence of written agreement).
- Defined or limited life.
- **Mutual agency:** "give[s] each partner the ability to act as an agent for the partnership in dealing with outside entities such as vendors and lenders" [V].
- Unlimited liability (general partners; limited partners are exceptions in LP and LLP forms).
- Non-taxable income at the partnership level (income flows through to partners' Tax Form K-1).
- Co-ownership of property.
- Limited capital investment (compared to corporations, partnerships have a smaller capital-raising market).

### Three types of partnership

[AP] (Ch 15.1, Table 15.2):

- **General partnership:** all partners personally liable.
- **Limited partnership (LP):** at least one general partner; others are limited partners with liability capped at their investment.
- **Limited liability partnership (LLP):** all partners protected from other partners' obligations; common in professional services (lawyers, accountants).

### Fiduciary duty

"Whether there is a written partnership agreement or not, partners have an ethical duty to act in the best interests of the partnership and of each of their partners. This is generally called a fiduciary duty. A fiduciary is someone who has a legal and/or ethical obligation to act in the best interest of others in order to maintain a relationship of trust and confidence. What this means in practice is that partners are to avoid actual and potential conflicts of interests, and there is to be no self-dealing. Partners are expected to put the partnership's interest ahead of their own" [V] (Ch 15.2, "Ethical Obligations to Partners").

### Income and loss allocation methods

[AP] (Ch 15.3): fixed ratio, ratio based on capital balances (beginning, ending, or average), guaranteed salaries plus fixed-ratio split of remainder, capital-account proportion, or any combination.

### Partnership formation, admission, withdrawal, and dissolution

Initial contributions are recorded at fair value, not book value, to avoid distorting capital accounts (Ch 15.2). New partner admission can occur via direct purchase from an existing partner (no change to partnership assets) or via investment in the partnership (creates new capital account; can produce a *bonus* between value and cash payment) (Ch 15.4). **Dissolution** "occurs when a partner withdraws (due to illness or any other reason), a partner dies, a new partner is admitted, or the business declares bankruptcy. Whenever there is a change in partners for any reason, the partnership must be dissolved and a new agreement must be reached" [V] (Ch 15.1, "Dissolution of a Partnership").

### IFRS for SMEs as an alternative

IFRS for Small and Medium Sized Entities (SMEs) is a simpler standard set (~300 pages versus ~2,500 for full IFRS and ~25,000 for US GAAP) acceptable for non-public-fiduciary partnerships in some US states. "Why might a partnership want to use IFRS for SMEs? First, IFRS for SMEs contains fewer and simpler standards... Second, IFRS for SMEs is modified only every three years, whereas U.S. GAAP and IFRS are modified more frequently. This means entities using IFRS for SMEs don't have to adjust their accounting systems and reporting to new standards as frequently" [V] (Ch 15.1, IFRS Connection).

## Part XVI: Statement of cash flows (Ch 16)

### Three sections of the statement

[AP] (Ch 16.2):

- **Operating activities:** cash flows from activities producing net income (sales receipts, supplier and salary payments, interest and dividend revenue, interest expense, income tax).
- **Investing activities:** cash flows from long-term asset transactions (purchase and sale of property, plant, equipment, investments).
- **Financing activities:** cash flows from long-term debt and equity (note and bond issuance and repayment, stock issuance and treasury repurchase, dividend distributions).

### Indirect versus direct method

The **indirect method** "reconciles net income to cash flows by subtracting noncash expenses and adjusting for changes in current assets and liabilities" [V] (Ch 16.1). The **direct method** "lists net cash flows from revenue and expenses" [V]. "Because the vast majority of financial statements are presented using the indirect method," the chapter emphasises indirect [AP].

### Indirect-method reconciliation logic

Operating cash flow under the indirect method [AP] (Ch 16.3):

1. Begin with net income.
2. Add back non-cash expenses (depreciation, amortization, depletion).
3. Reverse gains and losses from investing activities.
4. Adjust for changes in current assets and liabilities (increases in non-cash current assets reduce cash; decreases increase cash; increases in current operating liabilities increase cash; decreases reduce cash).

### Cash-flow ratios

[AP] (Ch 16.5):

- **Free cash flow** = operating cash – capital expenditures – cash dividends.
- **Free cash flow to sales** = free cash flow / sales.
- **Free cash flow to assets** = free cash flow / total assets.

### "Cash is king" framing

The chapter opens with the framing: "Some investors say that 'cash is king,' meaning that they think a company's cash flow is more important than its net income in determining investment opportunities. Companies go bankrupt because they run out of cash" [V] (Ch 16, Why It Matters). The Mark A. Siegel quote in the CPA Journal anchors the discussion: "as Wall Street analysts have lost faith in earnings-based metrics in the wake of Enron, WorldCom, and others, many have gravitated toward the cash flow statement" [V] (Ch 16.5, "Cash Flow Analysis").

### Negative cash flow can be positive

The text takes a measured position on negative cash flow: "Investors do not always take a negative cash flow as a negative" [V] (Ch 16.2). Amazon's 2018 net loss of $124 billion accompanied a $254 billion financing inflow, including $50 billion paid for fixed-asset and acquisition spending — interpreted as growth-oriented investment rather than failure. The interpretation requires looking at all three sections together [AP].

## Appendix A: Financial statement analysis

### Three analysis tools

[AP] (Appendix A):

- **Horizontal analysis (trend analysis):** compares line items over multiple periods. Dollar change = year of analysis – base year. Percentage change = dollar change / base year × 100.
- **Vertical analysis (common-size analysis):** within a statement, expresses each item as a percentage of a base (net sales for income statement, total assets or total liabilities + equity for balance sheet).
- **Ratio analysis:** four categories — liquidity, solvency, efficiency, profitability.

### Eleven core ratios

[AP] (Appendix A):

**Liquidity (short-term):**
- Working capital = current assets – current liabilities.
- Current ratio = current assets / current liabilities.
- Quick ratio (acid-test) = (current assets – inventory – prepaid) / current liabilities.

**Solvency (long-term):**
- Debt-to-equity ratio = total debt / total equity.
- Times interest earned = earnings before interest and taxes / interest expense.

**Efficiency:**
- Accounts receivable turnover = net credit sales / average AR.
- Total asset turnover = net sales / average total assets.
- Inventory turnover = COGS / average inventory.
- Number of days' sales in inventory = average inventory / (COGS / 365), or 365 / inventory turnover.

**Profitability:**
- Profit margin = net income / net sales.
- Return on total assets = net income / average total assets.
- Return on equity = (net income – preferred dividends) / average stockholders' equity.

### Ratio interpretation caveat

"A stakeholder needs to keep in mind that past performance does not always dictate future performance. Attention must be given to possible economic influences that could skew the numbers being analyzed, such as inflation or a recession. Additionally, the way a company reports information within accounts may change over time" [V] (Appendix A, "Advantages and Disadvantages of Financial Statement Analysis"). Industry-relative comparison and trend analysis across multiple periods are emphasised.

- **Dodge v. Ford Motor Co. (1919):** business judgment rule and stakeholder management (Ch 14.1).
- **Donald Cressey's fraud triangle:** opportunity, rationalization, incentive (Ch 8.1).
- **AICPA Code of Professional Conduct, IFAC Code of Ethics for Professional Accountants:** professional ethics standards (Chs 1.5, 9.1).
- **PCAOB auditing standards:** post-SOX audit oversight regime (Chs 1.4, 8.2).
- **GASB (Governmental Accounting Standards Board):** governmental accounting (Ch 1.4).
- **FASB ASU 2014-09 / Topic 606 (Revenue from Contracts with Customers):** five-step revenue recognition (Ch 9.1).
- **Statement on Auditing Standards (SAS) No. 99 / AU Section 316:** auditor's duty to consider fraud (Ch 10.1).
- **IFRS / IASB convergence:** US GAAP vs IFRS differences across many chapters (Chs 1.4, 4.5, 7.1, 10.1, 11.3, 12.3, 13.3, 15.1).
- **Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) 3.10:** government-contractor code of business ethics requirements (Ch 8.3).

## Positions the authors explicitly frame against

- **The direct write-off method of bad debt under GAAP for financial reporting (though required for tax):** the text argues against on the matching principle [AR] (Ch 9.2). The text accepts both: GAAP for financial statements, IRS for tax returns.
- **Pure shareholder-primacy in stakeholder analysis:** the utilitarian Ethical Considerations box in Ch 2.1 critiques narrow short-term-shareholder focus and explicitly endorses a multi-stakeholder analysis.
- **Cookie jar accounting and earnings manipulation:** the text frames manipulation as fraudulent (against the law and against GAAP) and distinguishes it sharply from legitimate earnings management within GAAP [AR] (Ch 4.1, Ch 9.4).
- **Capitalising costs that should be expensed:** the WorldCom case grounds the position [AR] (Ch 11.2).
- **Treating stakeholders as separate from shareholders:** the Dodge-Ford-derived business-judgment-rule treatment positions stakeholders and shareholders together; corporations are encouraged to manage stakeholder concerns proactively [AR] (Ch 14.1).
- **Pure-rationality defence of stock buybacks for EPS engineering:** the text leaves the question open and notes that buybacks were illegal for most of the 20th century [AR] (Ch 14.5).
- **Hidden override of internal controls (e.g., HealthSouth, Enron):** management override is presented as the most dangerous internal-control failure mode [AR] (Ch 8.7).
- **LIFO under IFRS:** "the IASB chose to eliminate LIFO, arguing that FIFO more closely matches the flow of goods" [V] (Ch 10.1) — the text presents both sides, and notes US companies' resistance to losing LIFO due to the tax-revenue implications.
- **Pure mark-to-market accounting for long-lived assets:** "It is difficult to determine an accurate fair value for long-lived assets. This is one reason US GAAP has not permitted the fair valuing of long-lived assets" [V] (Ch 11.3, IFRS Connection) — the text accepts the IFRS option but does not endorse it.
- **Treating the US dollar as inflation-adjusted in financial statements:** the monetary measurement concept "ignores any change in the purchasing power of the dollar due to inflation" [V] (Ch 3.1) — the text accepts this convention while noting its limitation.
- **Pay or compensation manipulation through accounting choices:** the Bristol-Myers Squibb (Ch 4.1), Waste Management (Ch 11.3), HealthSouth (Ch 8.7), and stock-option-vesting-related earnings management (Ch 12.2) examples are presented as cases the discipline must guard against.
