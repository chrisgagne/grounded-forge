## Authors' thesis (condensed)

The book frames accounting as the process of organising, analysing, and communicating financial information used for decision-making, with two parallel branches: financial accounting (this volume) for stakeholders outside the organisation, and managerial accounting (Volume 2) for decisions inside it. Volume 1's spine is multi-stakeholder external reporting under US GAAP through FASB, with parallel IFRS coverage where the regimes differ. The accounting cycle, accrual accounting, the matching principle, and conservatism structure the discipline; internal controls, Sarbanes-Oxley, and the audit function structure the trust regime that lets external stakeholders rely on management's representations. Earnings management within GAAP is distinguished from earnings manipulation, with cases (Bristol-Myers Squibb, Enron, WorldCom, HealthSouth, Waste Management) anchoring the difference.

## Role of accounting and stakeholders (Ch 1)

Accounting is "the process of organizing, analyzing, and communicating financial information that is used for decision-making". Six stakeholder categories receive financial information: stockholders, creditors and lenders, governmental and regulatory agencies, customers, managers and other employees, and (implicitly) communities and suppliers. Stakeholders are people or groups who rely on financial information to make decisions because they have an interest in the economic viability of an organisation. Three sources of capital structure financing decisions: profitable operations, equity (selling stock), and debt (borrowing from lenders). The regulatory institutions (FASB for US GAAP, SEC for public-company oversight, AICPA, PCAOB for SOX-era audit oversight, GASB for governmental, IASB for IFRS) are the structural backbone that makes external financial reporting trustworthy.

## Financial statements (Ch 2)

Four interrelated financial statements: income statement (revenues less expenses, plus and minus gains and losses; for a period); statement of owner's equity / retained earnings (changes in equity for a period); balance sheet (assets equal liabilities plus equity; on a date); and statement of cash flows (operating, investing, financing for a period). Ten elements of the financial statements per FASB SFAC No. 6: revenue, expenses, gains, losses, assets, liabilities, equity, investments by owners, distributions to owners, comprehensive income. Three legal structures: sole proprietorship, partnership, corporation, with different liability and tax profiles. Accrual accounting (revenue and expenses recorded when earned/incurred, not when cash moves) is preferred over cash-basis accounting because it matches revenues with their associated expenses in the proper period. Liquidity is introduced through working capital and the current ratio.

## Analyzing and recording transactions (Ch 3)

Eight GAAP principles, assumptions, and concepts govern recording: revenue recognition, expense recognition (matching), cost (historical), full disclosure, separate entity, conservatism, monetary measurement, going concern, time period assumption. Auditor independence is essential to the audit's value: external auditors report to the audit committee, not management. Double-entry bookkeeping records every transaction in at least two accounts with debits equal to credits. The accounting cycle has nine numbered steps; the first four (identify and analyse transactions, journalize, post, prepare unadjusted trial balance) are introduced in this chapter, with the rest covered in Chs 4 and 5. T-accounts and trial balances are introduced as the working tools.

## The adjustment process (Ch 4)

Adjusting entries update accounts at period end for transactions not yet recorded, in conformity with the time period assumption, revenue recognition, and matching. Two categories: deferrals (prepaid expenses and unearned revenues) and accruals (accrued revenues and accrued expenses). Cash never appears in an adjusting entry. Depreciation is allocated cost over an asset's useful life, with accumulated depreciation as a contra account. The Bristol-Myers Squibb cookie-jar-accounting case ($150 million SEC fine for inflating profits 2000-2001) anchors the ethics-of-estimation discussion: legitimate earnings management within GAAP is distinguished from manipulation outside GAAP. The 10-column worksheet aids the transition from adjusted trial balance to financial statements.

## Completing the accounting cycle (Ch 5)

Closing entries clear temporary accounts (revenue, expense, dividends, income summary) to retained earnings each period. Permanent accounts (assets, liabilities, equity) carry forward. Four closing entries: revenues to income summary, expenses to income summary, income summary to retained earnings, dividends to retained earnings. The classified balance sheet sub-divides assets and liabilities for stakeholder usefulness; the operating cycle is the time from cash to product or service to cash collection. Ratio analysis (working capital and current ratio detailed; broader ratio toolkit deferred to Appendix A) supports stakeholder decisions about liquidity.

## Merchandising transactions (Ch 6)

Service companies sell intangible services with no inventory; merchandising companies resell finished goods. Gross purchases minus discounts, returns, and allowances equals net purchases; the same applies to sales. Credit terms (e.g., 2/10, n/30) signal cash-discount opportunities; trade discounts are negotiated before purchase and not separately recorded. Perpetual inventory systems update inventory at every transaction (real-time); periodic systems update only at period end. FOB shipping point versus FOB destination determines who pays shipping (and bears in-transit risk): freight-in (added to inventory cost) versus freight-out (selling expense). Multi-step income statements separate cost of goods sold from operating expenses to surface gross margin and income from operations; simple income statements do not. Internal controls over returns (separation of duties between approval and recording) are introduced via COSO.

## Accounting information systems (Ch 7)

An AIS is the system or set of processes for collecting, recording, organising, and summarising accounting data, culminating in financial statements and reports for internal and external users. Three stages: input, processing, output. Source documents and the audit trail are central — the audit trail is the documented history of a transaction from source through final disposition that allows internal investigation and external audit. General journals chronicle all transactions; special journals (sales, purchases, cash receipts, cash disbursements) and subsidiary ledgers improve searchability and division of labour. Modern AISs are computerised, with QuickBooks, SAP, Oracle, and ERP systems as common examples. Cloud computing and cybersecurity are recent developments that change AIS design and risk.

## Fraud, internal controls, cash, and SOX (Ch 8)

Fraud is intentional deception for financial or non-financial benefit. The fraud triangle (Cressey) identifies three necessary elements: opportunity, rationalization, and incentive (pressure). Internal controls bite primarily on opportunity. COSO's Internal Control–Integrated Framework names five components: control environment, risk assessment, control activities, information and communication, monitoring. Six elements of strong internal control: clear responsibilities, proper documentation, adequate insurance, separation of assets from custody, separation of duties, use of technology. The Enron and Arthur Andersen failures led to the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002. SOX requires PCAOB oversight, audit-rotation rules, internal control reports (Section 404), and personal CEO/CFO certification of financial statements (Section 302). The "tone at the top" framing extends the spirit of internal controls beyond public companies. Petty cash funds and bank reconciliations are introduced as routine cash controls. Financial statement fraud — "cooking the books" — is illustrated through HealthSouth, Enron's special-purpose entities, and inventory-manipulation methods (channel stuffing, sham sales, bill-and-hold, improper cutoff, round-tripping). Cybersecurity has become a SOX-era concern.

## Receivables and revenue recognition (Ch 9)

The five-step revenue recognition model under FASB ASU 2014-09 / Topic 606: identify the contract, identify the performance obligations, determine the transaction price, allocate the price to obligations, recognise revenue when each obligation is satisfied. The matching principle requires associated expenses to be recorded in the same period. Bad debts: the direct write-off method violates matching and is unacceptable for GAAP financial reporting (but required by IRS for tax) — the allowance method is GAAP's solution. Three sub-variants of the allowance method: income statement (percentage of credit sales), balance sheet (percentage of accounts receivable), and balance sheet aging of receivables (more accurate; longer overdue periods get higher uncollectible percentages). Net realizable value of AR equals AR minus allowance for doubtful accounts. Receivables ratios: AR turnover (net credit sales over average AR) and days' sales in receivables (365 over turnover). Earnings management is distinguished from earnings manipulation: management works within GAAP to improve stakeholders' views; manipulation ignores GAAP and is fraudulent. Long-term project accounting uses percentage-of-completion or completed-contract methods; real-estate installment sales use the installment method to defer gross profit.

## Inventory (Ch 10)

Goods available for sale equals beginning inventory plus purchases. Four cost-flow assumptions: specific identification, FIFO, LIFO, weighted-average cost. Lower-of-cost-or-market (LCM) requires inventory to be written down to replacement value when below cost (conservatism). IFRS prohibits LIFO; US GAAP allows it. Inventory ratios: inventory turnover (COGS over average inventory) and days' sales in inventory (365 over turnover). In inflation, FIFO produces higher ending inventory and lower COGS than LIFO; in deflation the effects reverse. Auditors test inventory existence with surprise observations under SAS No. 99. Consigned goods belong to the consignor, not the consignee, regardless of physical location.

## Long-term assets (Ch 11)

Tangible assets (property, plant, equipment) versus intangible assets (patents, copyrights, trademarks, goodwill, sometimes software). Capitalisation records an asset on the balance sheet at total cost to make it ready for use; routine repairs are expensed; improvements that extend useful life are capitalised. Three depreciation methods: straight-line (equal annual expense), units-of-production (tied to usage), double-declining-balance (accelerated). IFRS adds component depreciation (separate schedules for components with different lives) and an option to revalue long-lived assets to fair value; US GAAP carries assets at depreciated historical cost. Natural resources are depleted (units-of-production analogue). Intangibles are amortized over their useful life, typically straight-line, with no salvage value, written down directly without an accumulated-amortization contra. Goodwill is not amortized but tested annually for impairment. The WorldCom case anchors the consequences of capitalising costs that should be expensed: $11 billion in fraud, the largest US bankruptcy at the time.

## Current liabilities (Ch 12)

Current liabilities are debts due within one year (or one operating cycle if longer): accounts payable, unearned revenue, current portion of notes payable, taxes payable, interest payable, salaries payable. The burn rate framing makes current-liability classification load-bearing for stakeholder analysis of solvency. FASB requires two conditions for contingent-liability recognition: likelihood of occurrence (probable, reasonably possible, or remote) and measurement (estimable or inestimable). Four treatments: probable-and-estimable (journalise and disclose), probable-and-inestimable (disclose only), reasonably possible (disclose only), remote (no disclosure required). US GAAP probable threshold is roughly 80%; IFRS uses "more likely than not" at roughly 50%. Payroll involves involuntary deductions (federal, state, local income tax withholding; FICA Social Security at 6.2% capped at $127,200/$128,400; FICA Medicare at 1.45% uncapped; Additional Medicare at 0.9% above income threshold) and voluntary deductions (health, retirement contributions to 401(k) or 403(b), charitable). Employers match FICA and pay FUTA (6%, reducible to 0.6%) and SUTA (state-specific).

## Long-term liabilities (Ch 13)

Long-term debt comes through notes payable (typically one-lender) or bonds (sold to multiple investors via underwriters). Bond features: principal (face value), maturity date, stated (coupon) interest rate, and market interest rate. Bonds sell at par when stated equals market, at a premium when stated exceeds market, at a discount when stated is below market. Bond types: debenture (unsecured), secured, term, serial, callable (issuer can repurchase), putable (holder can sell back), convertible (to common stock), junk (high yield, high risk). Credit ratings from Moody's, S&P, and Fitch (Aaa/AAA through D) determine borrowing cost. The effective-interest method amortises bond discounts and premiums based on carrying value times market rate (GAAP requires this unless straight-line is not materially different; IFRS requires effective-interest with no exceptions). Simple interest applies to principal only; compound interest applies to principal plus accumulated interest.

## Corporation accounting (Ch 14)

Corporate advantages: separate legal entity, limited liability, transferable ownership, continuing existence, ease of raising capital. Disadvantages: organisation costs, regulation, double taxation (avoided via S corporation or LLC pass-through). Six considerations in choosing debt versus equity: dilution of ownership, repayment obligation, cash obligation, budgeting, administrative cost, and creditor risk assessment (debt-to-equity covenant). Common stockholders have four basic rights: vote, share in dividends, share in liquidation distributions, preemptive right (in some jurisdictions). Preferred stockholders typically have no vote but receive dividends with priority over common; preferred stock is sometimes classified as a debt-equity hybrid. Stock vocabulary: authorized, issued, outstanding, treasury, par value, stated value, market value. The Dodge v. Ford "business judgment rule" frames stakeholder management as compatible with shareholder primacy: a corporation can act for the benefit of stakeholders, who include shareholders. Three dividend types (cash, property, stock) and three dividend dates (declaration, record, payment). Stock dividends and stock splits are presented; small stock dividends are recorded at market value, large stock dividends at par value. Earnings per share (EPS) is the only ratio GAAP requires on the face of the income statement; companies report basic and diluted. Stock buybacks can engineer EPS upward without changing earnings — the text leaves open the ethical question of whether this practice is appropriate.

## Partnership accounting (Ch 15)

Partnerships are formed by agreement (Uniform Partnership Act / RUPA in absence of writing) with seven characteristics: formation by agreement, defined or limited life, mutual agency, unlimited liability, non-taxable income at the partnership level (income flows through to partners' K-1), co-ownership of property, limited capital investment. Three types: general (all liable), limited (LP, with general and limited partners), limited liability (LLP, professional services). Fiduciary duty: each partner must act in the best interests of the partnership and other partners; no self-dealing. Initial contributions are recorded at fair value, not book value. Income and loss are allocated by the partnership agreement: fixed ratio, capital-balance ratio, guaranteed salary plus split, capital-account proportion, or some combination. New partner admission can occur via direct purchase (no change to partnership assets) or new investment (creates capital account, possibly with bonus). Dissolution occurs whenever a partner is added, leaves, dies, or the business declares bankruptcy. IFRS for SMEs is shorter and simpler than full IFRS or US GAAP and may be acceptable for some non-public partnerships.

## Statement of cash flows (Ch 16)

Three sections: operating (cash flows from net-income-producing activities), investing (long-term asset transactions), financing (long-term debt and equity transactions). The indirect method reconciles net income to operating cash flow by adding back non-cash expenses (depreciation, amortization, depletion), reversing investing-activity gains and losses, and adjusting for changes in current assets and liabilities. The direct method lists net cash flows from each revenue and expense line. The vast majority of statements use indirect. Cash-flow ratios: free cash flow (operating cash less capital expenditures less cash dividends), free cash flow to sales, free cash flow to assets. The "cash is king" framing reflects investor focus on cash generation in the post-Enron era. Negative cash flow from investing can be a positive sign of growth investment; the three sections must be read together.

## Financial statement analysis (Appendix A)

Three analysis tools: horizontal analysis (year-over-year trend in dollar and percentage terms), vertical analysis (common-size; each item as percentage of a base — net sales for income statement, total assets or total liabilities-plus-equity for balance sheet), and ratio analysis (eleven core ratios in four categories). Liquidity ratios: working capital, current ratio, quick ratio. Solvency ratios: debt-to-equity, times interest earned. Efficiency ratios: AR turnover, total asset turnover, inventory turnover, days' sales in inventory. Profitability ratios: profit margin, return on total assets, return on equity. Industry comparison and trend analysis across multiple periods are emphasised; past performance does not always predict future performance, and changes in reporting policies can confound trend analysis.

## Signature contrarian positions

1. **Direct write-off violates matching for GAAP financial reporting (despite IRS tax requirement).** The allowance method is the GAAP-correct approach; IRS uses direct write-off to prevent taxpayer manipulation of when bad debt is recognised.
2. **Earnings management within GAAP is distinct from earnings manipulation.** Management of percentages, aging brackets, and estimation methods is legitimate within GAAP and audit oversight; manipulation outside GAAP is fraudulent.
3. **The matching principle is foundational; cookie-jar accounting violates it.** Bristol-Myers Squibb's deferral of expenses and acceleration of revenues to meet quarterly earnings targets was illegal; the case anchors a principles-based critique of similar practices.
4. **Capitalising costs that should be expensed is fraud.** WorldCom's accounting gimmick led to the largest US bankruptcy at the time and showed the systemic stakeholder consequences (the internet itself was at risk).
5. **Stakeholders are broader than shareholders, and shareholders are stakeholders too.** The Dodge v. Ford business judgment rule treatment endorses stakeholder management as compatible with shareholder primacy. The Ch 2 utilitarian framing critiques short-term-shareholder-only focus.
6. **Tone at the top matters even outside SOX-regulated companies.** Internal control discipline begins with management's commitment to integrity; without that, technical controls are easily circumvented.
7. **Auditor independence is essential to the audit's value.** The Arthur Andersen / Enron case shows what happens when consulting and auditing are mixed at one firm. SOX's auditor-rotation rules (seven-year cap with two-year break) and limits on non-audit services for the same client codify the fix.
8. **Stock buybacks engineer EPS without changing earnings.** Buybacks were illegal for most of the 20th century as stock-price manipulation; they were legalised in 1982. The text leaves the ethical question open but presents the structural critique.
9. **Cash is king; net income alone is not enough.** A company can be profitable on accrual basis and bankrupt on cash basis. The statement of cash flows reveals the difference.
10. **IFRS prohibits LIFO; US GAAP allows it.** The IASB argues FIFO better matches physical flow; US companies and FASB resist due to the substantial tax-revenue implications of forced LIFO repeal.
11. **Conservatism (LCM, allowance for doubtful accounts, contingent-liability recognition asymmetry) systematically prefers under-statement over over-statement.** Uncertain losses are recognised; uncertain gains are not. This protects external stakeholders against management optimism.
12. **Fiduciary duty in partnerships is unwritten but enforceable.** Partners owe each other an ethical and legal obligation to act in the partnership's best interest, regardless of whether the partnership agreement is in writing.
13. **The audit trail makes external auditing possible.** Source documents, journals, ledgers, and trial balances are not pedagogical scaffolding; they are the structural device that lets external auditors verify management's representations to stakeholders.
