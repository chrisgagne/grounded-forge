## Author's thesis (paraphrased from source)

Managerial accounting is "the process that allows decision makers to set and evaluate business goals by determining what information they need to make a particular decision and how to analyze and communicate this information" [V] (Ch 1.1, "Define Managerial Accounting and Identify the Three Primary Responsibilities of Management"). Where financial accounting reports historical results to external users under GAAP, managerial accounting produces detailed, timely, often forward-looking information for internal users — managers and other employees responsible for planning, controlling, and evaluating business operations [AP] (Ch 1.2, "Distinguish between Financial and Managerial Accounting"). The volume's organising frame is the planning-controlling-evaluating cycle: management sets goals, deploys controls to monitor progress, and compares actual to planned outcomes to inform the next cycle [AR] (Ch 1.1, "Planning"; "Controlling"; "Evaluating").

A second load-bearing claim runs through the volume: that *cost classification is decision-relative*. Costs are not intrinsically fixed or variable, direct or indirect, product or period, relevant or irrelevant; they earn those labels relative to the decision under consideration. The same rent expense can be a fixed cost for one company, a committed cost for another, and a period cost for a third, depending on how the cost will be used [AP] (Ch 2.2, "Identify and Apply Basic Cost Behavior Patterns"). The text argues that "in practice, the classification of costs changes as the use of the cost data changes" [V] (Ch 2.2, opening). This decision-relative stance carries through the volume's treatment of relevant cost analysis (Ch 10) and capital budgeting (Ch 11).

A third load-bearing claim is that *short-term and long-term decisions warrant different analytical frames*. Short-term operational decisions — special orders, make-or-buy, keep-or-discontinue, sell-or-process, constraint management — turn on relevant costs and revenues that differ between alternatives, and ignore the time value of money (Ch 10.1, "Identify Relevant Information for Decision-Making"). Long-term capital decisions turn on the time value of money, with payback period and accounting rate of return as non-time-value screens, and net present value and internal rate of return as the substantive evaluation tools (Ch 11). Mixing the frames produces poor decisions in both directions.

A fourth load-bearing claim is that *financial measures alone are insufficient for evaluating performance*, and that responsibility-accounting frameworks must align manager incentives with corporate strategy. The volume develops three quantitative tools (return on investment, residual income, economic value added) and one integrating framework (the balanced scorecard) to address this alignment problem (Ch 9, Ch 12). The balanced scorecard's claim is explicit: financial measures emphasise past results and can be gamed; non-financial measures across customer, internal-process, and learning-and-growth perspectives provide forward-looking information that triangulates manager performance against corporate strategy (Ch 12.4, "Describe the Balanced Scorecard and Explain How It Is Used").

A fifth load-bearing claim, developed in Ch 13, is that *sustainability reporting is a now-mainstream extension of corporate accountability* that responds to a wider stakeholder set than financial accounting addresses. The text states that "in 2017, a KPMG report noted that 93% of the world's 250 largest companies by revenue produced corporate responsibility reports" [V] (Ch 13.3, "Discuss Examples of Major Sustainability Initiatives"), and frames the triple bottom line — people, planet, profit — as a useful organising vocabulary for the field (Ch 13.1, "Initial Sustainability Reports").

## Part I: Managerial accounting and the manager's three responsibilities (Ch 1)

### What managerial accounting is and is not

Managerial accounting is "the process that allows decision makers to set and evaluate business goals by determining what information they need to make a particular decision and how to analyze and communicate this information" [V] (Ch 1.1). The text contrasts managerial with financial accounting along seven dimensions: users (internal vs external), governance (no GAAP rules vs GAAP), reports (custom vs financial statements), frequency (as-needed vs quarterly/annual), purpose (planning/controlling/evaluating vs external decision-making), focus (segments and components vs whole company), and nature (monetary plus non-monetary vs monetary only) [AP] (Ch 1.2, "Financial and Managerial Accounting Comparative"). The Institute of Management Accountants (IMA) defines management accounting as "a profession that involves partnering in management decision making, devising planning and performance management systems, and providing expertise in financial reporting and control to assist management in the formulation and implementation of an organization's strategy" [V] (Ch 1.3, "Explain the Primary Roles and Skills Required of Managerial Accountants", citing the IMA).

### The three primary responsibilities of management

Most managerial responsibilities sort into one of three categories: planning, controlling, or evaluating [AP] (Ch 1.1, "Distinguish between Financial and Managerial Accounting" — actually the framing of Ch 1.1's Three Primary Responsibilities). *Planning* begins with a mission statement, then sets goals (longer-term aspirations) and objectives (shorter-term targets), with strategic planning involving "setting priorities and determining how to allocate corporate resources to help an organization accomplish both short-term and long-term goals" [AP] (Ch 1.1, "Planning"). *Controlling* is the monitoring activity that "involves the monitoring of the planning objectives that were put into place" through control mechanisms ranging from financial ratios (inventory turnover) to physical controls (fingerprint identification, password protection) [V] (Ch 1.1, "Controlling"). *Evaluating*, "also called assessing or analyzing, involves comparing actual results against expected results, and it can occur at the product, department, division, and company levels" [V] (Ch 1.1, "Evaluating").

### Skills and roles of the managerial accountant

The text catalogues seven skills required of effective managerial accountants: solid foundation in accounting, commercial awareness ("knowing how a business is run and how it is influenced by the external environment"), collaboration, effective communication, strong technology skills, extensive analytical skills, and ethics and values [V] (Ch 1.3, "Explain the Primary Roles and Skills Required of Managerial Accountants"). The IMA reports that "nearly 75 percent of financial professionals work in business as management accountants in positions such as financial analysts, accounting managers, controllers, and chief financial officers" [V] (Ch 1.3, citing IMA).

The accounting career structure is organised around the chief financial officer (CFO), who oversees the controller (accounting records, financial statements, tax returns, internal reports), the treasurer (cash position, corporation funds), and often internal audit (Ch 1.3, "Organizational Structure"). Career roles within the function include financial analyst, budget analyst, internal auditor, cash-management accountant, cost accountant, and government / nonprofit specialists. Multiple certifications structure the profession: CPA (top-tier, AICPA-administered, four-part exam, less than 50 per cent pass rate as of 2017), CMA (corporate accounting management, IMA-administered, two-part exam, 50 per cent global pass rate), CFA (finance and investment, less than 45 per cent pass rate), EA (taxation, IRS), CIA (internal audit, IIA, accepted worldwide), CFE (fraud examination, ACFE), and CGAP (government auditing, IIA) [AP] (Ch 1.3, "Certifications").

### Ethics, the IMA Statement, and ethics legislation

The IMA's Statement of Ethical Professional Practice rests on four standards: competence, confidentiality, integrity, and credibility. Credibility is "based on an accountant communicating information with fairness and objectivity, disclosing all information that is relevant to the intended users understanding, and disclosing 'delays or deficiencies in information, timeliness, processing, or internal controls in conformance with organization policy and/or applicable law'" [V] (Ch 1.4, "Business Ethics", citing IMA). The text frames ethics as "more than simply obeying laws; it involves doing the right thing as well as the legal thing" [V] (Ch 1.4, "Business Ethics").

The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (SOX) and the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) anchor ethics legislation. SOX's most significant compliance issues are: Section 302 (CEO and CFO must review and sign all financial reports), Section 404 (annual financial-report audit accompanied by internal-control audit), Section 806 (whistleblower protections), and Section 906 (criminal penalties up to $5 million in fines and 25 years in prison for fraudulent reports) [AP] (Ch 1.4, "Ethics Legislation"). The FCPA's antibribery section "specifically prohibits payments to foreign government officials to aid in attaining or retaining business" and "applies to all US persons and foreign firms acting within the United States"; its accounting section requires good internal controls so that a slush fund cannot be created and maintained [AP] (Ch 1.4, "Ethics Legislation").

The text proposes an eight-step ethics-issue framework: recognise the ethical issue and those involved; establish the facts; recognise competing values; determine alternative courses of action; evaluate each course against the values; recognise consequences for those involved; make a decision and act; evaluate the decision (Ch 1.4, "Ethics Legislation").

### Trends in the business environment

Five trends are catalogued in Ch 1.5 as forces shaping contemporary managerial accounting:

**Technology.** Enterprise resource planning (ERP) systems "help companies streamline their operations and help management respond quickly to change" by integrating siloed business systems [V] (Ch 1.5, "Technology"). Over the last forty years, "tangible assets (buildings, machinery, and vehicles) have declined from 80 percent of a company's value to 15 percent, while intangible assets (trademarks, patents, and competencies) are now at an average of 85 percent of a company's value" [V] (Ch 1.5, "Technology"). Automation, outsourcing, and radio-frequency identification (RFID) reshape cost structures.

**Lean practices.** A lean business model "strives to eliminate waste in its products, services, and processes, while still fulfilling the company's mission" [V] (Ch 1.5, "Lean Practices"). The text catalogues four interrelated lean philosophies: total quality management (TQM, focused on continuous quality improvement), just-in-time manufacturing (JIT, originated at Toyota, requires accurate forecasting and creates supply-chain disruption risk), Lean Six Sigma (LSS, originated at Motorola in 1986, combines lean's waste-elimination focus with Six Sigma's defect-reduction focus), and Kaizen (Japanese for "change for the better", small day-to-day improvements adopted post-WWII as part of Japanese industrial rebuilding). Goldratt's Theory of Constraints (TOC) is presented as a separate lean practice: "recognizing and removing bottlenecks within the value chain that may be limiting an organization's profitability" [V] (Ch 1.5, "Lean Practices"). TOC's five-step continuous improvement cycle: identify the system constraint; decide how best to exploit it; subordinate everything else; elevate the constraint; repeat (Ch 1.5).

**Balanced Scorecard.** The BSC "uses both financial and nonfinancial measures in evaluating all attributes of the organization's procedures" across four perspectives: financial, customer, internal process, and learning and growth/capacity (Ch 1.5, "Balanced Scorecard"). Full development is in Ch 12.

**Globalisation.** "The development of business through international influence or extending social and cultural aspects around the world" expands competitive borders and gives customers more alternatives; managers must consider where to source, sell, manufacture, and what laws and customs apply [V] (Ch 1.5, "Globalization").

**Social Responsibility and Sustainability.** Sustainability is defined here using the United Nations formulation: "the ability to meet the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs" [V] (Ch 1.5, "Social Responsibility and Sustainability"). Three components: economic, social, environmental. Corporate social responsibility (CSR) is "an organization's programs that evaluate and take responsibility for the organization's effects on environmental and social welfare" [V] (Ch 1.5). Full development is in Ch 13.

> [V] (Ch 1.4, "Business Ethics")

## Part II: Cost behaviour and the building blocks of managerial accounting (Ch 2)

### Three organisational types

Most businesses fall into one or more of three categories: manufacturing, merchandising, or service. *Manufacturing organisations* use parts, components, or raw materials to produce finished goods; their cost categories include direct materials, direct labour, and manufacturing overhead. *Merchandising firms* purchase finished products and resell them; their core cost is cost of goods sold derived from the purchase cost plus mark-up. *Service organisations* "earn revenue by providing intangible products" with "no physical substance" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Service Organizations") and have larger personnel and administrative cost categories. The service industry provides "65% of the U.S. private-sector gross domestic product and more than 79% of U.S. private-sector jobs" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Service Organizations", citing Ward 2010, ITA Newsletter).

### Cost classification — the load-bearing decision-relative claim

Most costs fall into one of three behavioural categories: fixed, variable, or mixed (with hybrid costs as a residual category) [AP] (Ch 2.2, "Major Cost Behavior Patterns"). A *fixed cost* "is an unavoidable operating expense that does not change in total over the short term, even if a business experiences variation in its level of activity" [V] (Ch 2.2, "Fixed versus Variable Costs"). A *variable cost* "is one that varies in direct proportion to the level of activity within the business" [V] (Ch 2.2). A *cost driver* is "any activity that causes the organization to incur a variable cost" [V] (Ch 2.2). On a per-unit basis the relationship inverts: fixed costs are constant in total but change per unit as volume changes, while variable costs are constant per unit but change in total as volume changes (Ch 2.2, "Fixed versus Variable Costs"; Table 2.6 "Summary of Fixed and Variable Cost Behaviors").

The *relevant range* is "a specific activity level that is bounded by a minimum and maximum amount" within which a stepped cost remains constant [V] (Ch 2.2, "Mixed Costs and Stepped Costs"). Stepped costs "remain constant at a fixed amount over a range of activity"; once the range boundary is crossed, the cost steps to a higher (or lower) plateau (Ch 2.2). Mixed costs combine a fixed and variable component: total cost Y = a + bx, where a is the fixed cost, b the variable cost per unit, and x the level of activity (Ch 2.2, "Mixed Costs and Stepped Costs").

The volume's signature framing of cost classification is decision-relative: "in practice, the classification of costs changes as the use of the cost data changes. In fact, a single cost, such as rent, may be classified by one company as a fixed cost, by another company as a committed cost, and by even another company as a period cost" [V] (Ch 2.2, opening).

### Manufacturing cost components

For manufacturing firms, three categories of product cost recur:

- **Direct materials**: materials that "can be directly traced to the manufacturing of the product" [V] (Ch 2.2, "Product versus Period Costs"; expanded Ch 4.2).
- **Direct labour**: "production labor costs that can be identified on a per item-produced basis" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Manufacturing Organizations").
- **Manufacturing overhead** (sometimes "factory overhead"): "those costs incurred in the production process that are not economically feasible to measure as direct material or direct labor costs" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Manufacturing Organizations"). Manufacturing overhead has three sub-components: indirect materials, indirect labour, and other overhead.

Two derived categories combine these: *prime costs* are direct materials plus direct labour; *conversion costs* are direct labour plus manufacturing overhead. Direct labour appears in both because it is the bridge between materials and finished product.

*Period costs* are "all of the expenses that are not product costs, such as all selling and administrative expenses" [V] (Ch 2.2, "Product versus Period Costs"). Product costs are inventoried until the product is sold; period costs are expensed in the period incurred.

### Cost estimation methods

Three estimation methods are presented for separating mixed costs into fixed and variable components: scatter graph, high-low method, and least-squares regression (Ch 2.3, "Estimate a Variable and Fixed Cost Equation and Predict Future Costs"). The *high-low method* uses the highest and lowest activity-level periods to estimate variable cost per unit: variable cost per unit = (Y2 - Y1) / (X2 - X1), where Y is total cost and X is activity level [AP] (Ch 2.3, "Demonstration of the High-Low Method"). The fixed-cost component is then computed by subtracting the estimated variable component from total cost at either the high or low data point. The text notes that the high-low method "is not the most accurate approach to predicting future costs because it is based on only two pieces of cost data" and recommends least-squares regression for more accurate estimation (Ch 2.3, "Demonstration of the High-Low Method"). *Scatter graphs* are a diagnostic tool used "to verify [the] assumption" that the cost-activity relationship is linear before applying high-low or regression analysis (Ch 2.3, "Demonstration of the Scatter Graph Method").

## Part III: Cost-volume-profit analysis (Ch 3)

### Contribution margin

*Contribution margin* is "the amount by which a product's selling price exceeds its total variable cost per unit" [V] (Ch 3.1, "Explain Contribution Margin"). The unit contribution margin is calculated as selling price per unit minus variable cost per unit; the contribution margin ratio expresses the same relationship as a percentage of the selling price; total contribution margin multiplies unit contribution margin by sales volume. Contribution margin is the per-unit contribution toward covering fixed costs: "this $5 contribution margin is assumed to first cover fixed costs first and then realized as profit" [V] (Ch 3.1, "Unit Contribution Margin").

The contribution margin income statement is "a specialized income statement [...] used to determine how changes in sales volume impact the bottom line" (Ch 3.1, "Total Contribution Margin"). Notably, "the contribution margin income statement is used for internal purposes and is not shared with external stakeholders" [AP] (Ch 3.2). The text observes that as labour-intensive operations are converted to technology-intensive operations, fixed costs rise and variable costs fall, changing the cost structure and therefore the CVP analysis (Ch 3.1, "The Evolution of Cost-Volume-Profit Relationships").

### The break-even point and target profit

The *break-even point* is "the dollar amount (total sales dollars) or production level (total units produced) at which the company has recovered all variable and fixed costs", where Total Cost = Total Revenue [V] (Ch 3.2, "Basics of the Break-Even Point"). Break-even in units = fixed costs / contribution margin per unit; break-even in dollars = fixed costs / contribution margin ratio. Adding a target profit treats the desired profit as if it were an additional fixed cost: target sales = (fixed costs + target profit) / contribution margin per unit [AP] (Ch 3.2, "Examples of the Effects of Variable and Fixed Costs in Determining the Break-Even Point").

After-tax target profit requires conversion to pre-tax profit before applying the formula: pre-tax profit = after-tax profit / (1 - tax rate). For example, with a 40 per cent tax rate, an after-tax target of $24,000 corresponds to a pre-tax target of $40,000, which is then added to fixed costs in the break-even formula (Ch 3.2).

### CVP assumptions, sensitivity analysis, and the multi-product case

CVP analysis rests on four assumptions: costs are linear and clearly fixed or variable within the relevant range; selling price per unit is constant across volume; inventory does not change (all units produced are sold); and in multi-product environments, the sales mix remains constant (Ch 3.2, "Basics of the Break-Even Point"). *Sensitivity analysis* shows what happens to the break-even point and net operating income when sales price, units sold, variable cost per unit, or fixed costs change (Ch 3.3, "The Effects on Break-Even under Changing Business Conditions"). *Target pricing* is "a process in which a company uses market analysis and production information to determine the maximum price customers are willing to pay" combined with a markup percentage; if production cost permits the target price plus desired margin, the company proceeds [V] (Ch 3.3, "Change in Sales Price").

In a *multi-product environment*, the sales mix is treated as a *composite unit* — "a selection of discrete products associated together in proportion to the sales mix" [V] (Ch 3.4, "Calculating Break-Even Analysis in a Multi-Product Environment"). The composite contribution margin is computed across the bundle, then the break-even-units formula is applied; once composite break-even units are known, the sales mix ratio is applied to compute units of each product. The text notes that "if the sales mix changes from its current ratio, then the break-even point will change" (Ch 3.4).

### Margin of safety and operating leverage

*Margin of safety* is "the difference between [a company's] current sales and its break-even sales" [V] (Ch 3.5, "Margin of Safety"). Expressed as a percentage of total budgeted or actual sales, it tells management how much sales can decline before operations move below break-even. *Operating leverage* is "a measurement of how sensitive net operating income is to a percentage change in sales dollars" [V] (Ch 3.5, "Operating Leverage"). Higher fixed-cost structures produce higher operating leverage: "as sales volumes increase, the payoff is typically greater with higher fixed costs than with higher variable costs. In other words, the higher the risk the greater the payoff" [V] (Ch 3.5, "Operating Leverage"). The degree of operating leverage equals the contribution margin divided by net operating income; multiplying the degree of operating leverage by a sales-percentage change yields the corresponding net-income percentage change [AP] (Ch 3.5).

## Part IV: Job order costing (Ch 4)

*Job order costing* "is an accounting system that traces the individual costs directly to a final job or service, instead of to the production department" and is used "when goods are made to order or when individual costs are easy to trace to individual jobs" [V] (Ch 4.1, "Job Order Costing versus Process Costing"). It contrasts with process costing, which is used when manufacturing is continuous and unit-level cost tracing is impractical (Ch 4.1, Table 4.1).

Job order costing flows costs through three inventory accounts: raw materials inventory (materials available for production), work in process (WIP) inventory (materials, direct labour, and applied overhead in unfinished jobs), and finished goods inventory (completed but unsold jobs). When the product is sold, costs move from finished goods to cost of goods sold (Ch 4.3, "Use the Job Order Costing Method to Trace the Flow of Product Costs"). The *cost of goods manufactured* (COGM) is "the costs of all of the units that a company completed and transferred to the finished goods inventory during an accounting period" [V] (Ch 4.3, "Work in Process Inventory"). Cost of goods sold is calculated as beginning finished goods inventory plus cost of goods manufactured minus ending finished goods inventory.

A *materials requisition form* documents the transfer of raw materials into WIP, including quantity, item, and cost; *time tickets* document labour assignment to specific jobs; the *job cost sheet* aggregates direct materials, direct labour, and applied overhead per job (Ch 4.2). Indirect labour and indirect materials feed into the manufacturing overhead account rather than directly to a job; for example, "a furniture factory classifies the cost of glue, stain, and nails as indirect materials" because the cost per unit is too small to trace economically [AP] (Ch 4.2, "Indirect Materials"). The *expense recognition principle* — that costs should be recognised in the period they help generate revenue — anchors the inventoried treatment of product costs and the immediate expensing of period costs (Ch 4.1, "Recording Costs in Job Order Costing versus Process Costing").

### Predetermined overhead rates

Because overhead cannot be traced to individual jobs in real time and overhead costs are not uniform across the year, manufacturers compute a *predetermined overhead rate* at the start of the fiscal year: estimated total manufacturing overhead divided by estimated total cost driver activity [AP] (Ch 4.4, "Compute a Predetermined Overhead Rate and Apply Overhead to Production"). The *cost driver* is "a production factor that causes a company to incur costs" [V] (Ch 4.2, "Accounting for Manufacturing Overhead"); historically, direct labour hours, direct labour dollars, or machine hours have been most common. The predetermined rate is applied to actual cost-driver activity to compute applied overhead per job. Differences between actual overhead incurred and overhead applied at year-end are *underapplied* (actual exceeds applied) or *overapplied* (applied exceeds actual) and are typically closed to cost of goods sold (Ch 4.6).

A *loss leader* is "a product that is sold at a price that is often less than the cost of producing it in order to entice you to buy accessories that are necessary for its use" [V] (Ch 4.2). Even loss-leader pricing requires accurate cost analysis to know how much money is being lost.

## Part V: Process costing (Ch 5)

*Process costing* is "the optimal costing system when a standardized process is used to manufacture identical products and the direct material, direct labor, and manufacturing overhead cannot be easily or economically traced to a specific unit" [V] (Ch 5.1, "Compare and Contrast Job Order Costing and Process Costing"). It is used "most often when manufacturing a product in batches"; examples include cereal, soft drinks, paint, petroleum, and basic chemicals (Ch 5.1).

The central concept is *equivalent units*: "the number of units that would have been produced if the units were produced sequentially and in their entirety in a particular time period" [V] (Ch 5.1, "Equivalent Units"). Forty units that are 25 per cent complete equal ten equivalent units. The text uses the analogy of "five large pizzas that each contained eight slices" with sixteen leftover slices equating to two equivalent whole pizzas (Ch 5.1, "Equivalent Units"). Equivalent units must be calculated separately for direct materials and conversion costs because materials are typically added at discrete points (often the start of a process) while conversion costs (direct labour plus manufacturing overhead) are incurred evenly throughout production.

The four-step process-costing procedure: determine the units to which costs will be assigned; compute equivalent units of production for direct materials and for conversion costs; determine cost per equivalent unit for materials and for conversion; allocate costs to units transferred out and to ending WIP (Ch 5.3, "Step One" through "Step Four"). Two valuation methods are mentioned: weighted-average (combines current-period and prior-period costs) and FIFO (first-in, first-out, which separates the two). The text notes that "the weighted-average method [...] is often favored, because in the process cost production method there often is little product left at the end of the period and most has been transferred out" [AP] (Ch 5.1, "Major Characteristics of Process Costing").

A *production cost report* is "the report that shows the costs used in the preparation of a product, including the cost per unit for materials and conversion costs, and the amount of work in process and finished goods inventory" [V] (Ch 5.3, "Step Four"). Costs flow from one department's WIP to the next as units progress; the cost transferred in becomes the cost basis for the next stage's processing (Ch 5.4).

## Part VI: Activity-based, variable, and absorption costing (Ch 6)

### Activity-based costing

*Activity-based costing* (ABC) is "the process that assigns overhead to products based on the various activities that drive overhead costs" [V] (Ch 6.3, "Calculate Activity-Based Product Costs"). It contrasts with *traditional allocation*, which uses a single cost driver (typically direct labour hours, dollars, or machine hours) to allocate manufacturing overhead. ABC's value increases as overhead's share of total product cost rises and as direct labour's share falls — a trend driven by automation. Chrysler is cited as a case where ABC "estimates that the benefits of cost savings, product improvement, and elimination of inefficiencies have been ten to twenty times greater than the investment in the program at some sites" [V] (Ch 6.1, "Calculate Predetermined Overhead and Total Cost under the Traditional Allocation Method", citing Ness and Cucuzza 1995, HBR).

ABC's five-stage process: (1) identify activities performed in the organisation; (2) determine activity cost pools; (3) calculate activity rates for each cost pool; (4) allocate activity rates to products or services; (5) calculate unit product costs (Ch 6.3, "Establishing an Activity-Based Costing System"). Cost pools group costs incurred when related activities are performed (e.g., production, purchasing material, inspection, assembly). Costs can be gathered at four levels: unit-level (incurred per unit produced), batch-level (per batch), product-level (per product line), or factory-level (per facility) (Ch 6.3, "Historical Perspective").

The text's worked example contrasts traditional and ABC allocation for Musicality Manufacturing's three products (Solo, Band, Orchestra). Under traditional allocation, the Solo product appears profitable; under ABC, "the Solo product was generating a loss for every unit sold" [AP] (Ch 6.3, "The Calculation of Product Costs Using the Activity-Based Costing Allocation Method"). This is the volume's central illustration of the cost-information distortion that ABC corrects.

ABC's advantages: multiple overhead cost pools each with its own activity measure; allocation bases that capture diverse cost drivers; activity rates that may use capacity rather than budgeted activity; assignment of both manufacturing and non-manufacturing costs based on cost-product relationship. Disadvantages: some manufacturing costs may be excluded from product costs; the system is more expensive to operate; and "an ABC system takes much more to implement and operate, as information on cost drivers must be collected in an objective manner" (Ch 6.4, "Advantages and Disadvantages of Creating an Activity-Based Costing System for Allocating Overhead"). ABC information is supplemental and not GAAP-compliant by itself; companies still compute traditional cost for financial reporting.

### Variable versus absorption costing

*Variable costing* (also direct costing or marginal costing) "is a method in which all variable costs (direct material, direct labor, and variable overhead) are assigned to a product and fixed overhead costs are expensed in the period incurred" [V] (Ch 6.5, "Compare and Contrast Variable and Absorption Costing"). *Absorption costing* (also full costing) "applies all direct costs, fixed overhead, and variable manufacturing overhead to the cost of the product" [V] (Ch 6.5). Absorption costing is GAAP-compliant; variable costing is not.

The text takes a positional stance that variable costing produces better information for internal decision-making, while acknowledging absorption costing's reporting requirement. Variable costing's advantages include direct usefulness for CVP analysis, income that does not vary with production-versus-sales differences, easier understandability, more visible fixed costs, less margin distortion, easier control through flexible budgets, and simpler incremental analysis (Ch 6.5, "Advantages and Disadvantages of the Variable Costing Method"). Absorption costing's advantages are GAAP and tax compliance, with the disadvantage that it can be used to manipulate income via overproduction: "Increasing production at year-end results in a higher net income than if the additional goods had not been produced, since increasing the number of units decreases the fixed cost per unit. Under absorption costing, these fixed costs follow the units produced and do not become a part of cost of goods sold until they are sold" [AP] (Ch 6.5, "Comparing the Operating Income Statements for Both Methods").

The Big Three U.S. auto companies are cited as a cautionary example: decisions made on absorption costing led to "the manufacturing of more vehicles than the market demanded" because allocating fixed overhead to inventory lowered apparent unit cost (Ch 6.5, "Concepts in Practice: Absorbing Costs through Overproduction").

## Part VII: Budgeting (Ch 7)

### The master budget

A *budget* is a "quantitative plan estimating when and how much cash or other resources will be received and when and how the cash or other resources will be used" [V] (Ch 7.1, "The Basics of Budgeting"). The master budget is composed of two interrelated halves: the *operating budget* (sales, production, materials, labour, overhead, selling and administrative, and a budgeted income statement) and the *financial budget* (capital asset budget, cash budget, budgeted balance sheet) (Ch 7.1, "The Role of the Master Budget").

Operating budgets cascade from the sales budget. Sales drive production volume (with desired ending inventory layered on); production drives direct materials, direct labour, and manufacturing overhead; the selling and administrative expense budget runs parallel; all feed into the budgeted income statement (Ch 7.2). Financial budgets begin with the capital asset budget, which feeds the cash budget along with the cash collections schedule (from sales) and cash payments schedule (from purchases); the cash budget and supporting budgets then feed the budgeted balance sheet (Ch 7.3, Figure 7.15).

### Budgeting approaches and styles

The text catalogues four budgeting approaches:

- **Top-down**: "begins with senior management" who set goals, assumptions, and predicted revenue/expenses, with middle and lower management allocating within constraints. Advantage: alignment with strategic plan; disadvantage: budget preparers lack operational nuance (Ch 7.1, "The Basics of Budgeting").
- **Bottom-up** (also "self-imposed" or "participative"): "begins at the lowest level of the company" with departments planning and predicting based on goals communicated downward. Advantage: managers feel valued and produce realistic numbers; disadvantage: managers may misunderstand the strategic plan (Ch 7.1).
- **Combination**: "guidelines and targets are set at the top while the managers work to develop a budget within the targeted parameters" (Ch 7.1).
- **Zero-based**: "begins with zero dollars and then adds to the budget only revenues and expenses that can be supported or justified". Advantage: eliminates unnecessary expenses; disadvantage: time-consuming because every line must be justified (Ch 7.1).

A *static budget* "is one that is prepared based on a single level of output for a given period" [V] (Ch 7.4, "Static versus Flexible Budgets"). A *flexible budget* "is one based on different volumes of sales" and "flexes the static budget for each anticipated level of production" (Ch 7.4, "Flexible Budgets"). A *rolling budget* is "continuously updated" by adding a new period as each old one expires (Ch 7.1, "The Basics of Budgeting").

### Variances and the use of budgets to evaluate goals

A *favorable variance* occurs when revenue exceeds budget or expenses fall short of budget; an *unfavorable variance* is the inverse (Ch 7.5, "Explain How Budgets Are Used to Evaluate Goals", Table 7.2). The text emphasises that favourable does not always mean good (a labour-rate favourable variance may mean lower-quality employees); unfavourable does not always mean bad. Comparing actual results to a static budget can mislead because the comparison combines volume effects with cost-control effects; flexible-budget comparison isolates cost control by recomputing budgeted costs at the actual production volume (Ch 7.5).

## Part VIII: Standard costs and variances (Ch 8)

### Standards as planning and control tools

A *standard* is an expectation for a component used in production, and a *standard cost* is "an expected cost that a company usually establishes at the beginning of a fiscal year for prices paid and amounts used" [V] (Ch 8.1, "Explain How and Why a Standard Cost Is Developed"). Standards rest on a balance: an *ideal standard* assumes everything goes perfectly and is a poor motivator because employees see it as unattainable; an overly easy standard provides no incentive to improve. The text recommends an *attainable standard* — one "that employees can reach with reasonable effort" — as the productive middle (Ch 8.1, "Fundamentals of Standard Costs").

The standard cost card lists material standard cost (price per unit and amount per unit), labour standard cost (rate per minute or hour and time per unit), and overhead standards (variable and fixed). Variances are differences between actual results and standards, classified as favourable (less spent or used than the standard) or unfavourable (more) (Ch 8.1, Figure 8.2).

### Materials variances

Two materials variances:

- **Direct materials price variance** = (actual price per unit - standard price per unit) × actual quantity purchased. Favourable when actual price is below standard; unfavourable when above (Ch 8.2, "Direct Materials Price Variance").
- **Direct materials quantity variance** = (actual quantity used - standard quantity at actual production output) × standard price per unit. Favourable when less material is used than expected for the actual production level; unfavourable when more (Ch 8.2, "Direct Materials Quantity Variance").

The text emphasises a key timing decision: "the material price variance is computed at the time of purchase and not when the material is used in production" [V] (Ch 8.2, "Direct Materials Price Variance") because earlier signal allows earlier corrective action.

The *total direct materials cost variance* is the combination: actual cost - standard cost (where standard cost = standard price × standard quantity at actual production) (Ch 8.2, "Total Direct Materials Cost Variance"). A favourable price variance with an unfavourable quantity variance can signal that lower-grade material was purchased — an early example of variance interaction (Ch 8.1, "Manufacturing Cost Variances").

### Labour variances

Two labour variances parallel the materials variances:

- **Direct labour rate variance** = (actual rate per hour - standard rate per hour) × actual hours worked.
- **Direct labour time variance** = (actual hours worked - standard hours at actual production) × standard rate per hour.

A favourable rate variance with an unfavourable time variance can signal lower-experience workers paid less but taking longer (Ch 8.3).

### Overhead variances

The volume catalogues four overhead variances grouped into variable and fixed:

- **Variable overhead rate variance** (also "spending variance") = actual variable overhead - (actual hours × standard variable overhead rate). Favourable means less spent than expected at the actual activity level (Ch 8.4, "Determination of Variable Overhead Rate Variance").
- **Variable overhead efficiency variance** (also "controllable variance") = (actual hours - standard hours at actual production) × standard variable overhead rate (Ch 8.4, "Determination of Variable Overhead Efficiency Variance").
- **Fixed overhead spending and volume variances** distinguish too-much-or-too-little spending from production above or below the expected level (Ch 8.4, "Fundamentals of Fixed Factory Overhead Variances").

### How variance analysis is used

The text distinguishes companies that explain only unfavourable variances from those that explain both: "requiring managers to explain favorable variances allows them to assess whether the favorable variance is sustainable" (Ch 8.5, "Describe How Companies Use Variance Analysis"). It warns of two failure modes: managing "to the variances" by short-term decisions that meet variance thresholds at the cost of long-term goals; and *budget slack*, where managers overestimate material price or labour hours so that actual results comfortably beat standards (Ch 8.5).

## Part IX: Responsibility accounting and decentralisation (Ch 9)

### Centralisation vs decentralisation

*Centralisation* is "a business structure in which one individual makes the important decisions (such as resource allocation) and provides the primary strategic direction for the company" [V] (Ch 9.1, "Centralized Organizations"). Apple under Tim Cook is cited as a centralised organisation. Advantages: clarity in decision-making, streamlined implementation, control over strategic direction. Disadvantages: limited employee feedback opportunities; inflexibility risk (Ch 9.1, "Centralized Organizations").

*Decentralisation* is "a business structure in which the decision-making is made at various levels of the organization" [V] (Ch 9.1, "Decentralized Organizations"). Advantages: quick decisions, expansion capacity, specialised management, employee morale, link between compensation and responsibility, and broader use of lower and middle management. Disadvantages: coordination problems, duplication of efforts, operational incongruity, departmental fiefdoms, and reliance on divisional managers (Ch 9.1).

The text notes that organisations are rarely purely centralised or decentralised; most fall somewhere on the spectrum. Decision authority for *daily decisions* (operational, immediate) and *strategic decisions* (infrequent, long-term) may be allocated differently (Ch 9.1, "Daily and Strategic Decision-Making"). Most companies move from centralised toward decentralised structures as they grow.

### Responsibility accounting and the five responsibility centres

*Responsibility accounting* is "a basic component of accounting systems for many companies as their performance measurement process becomes more complex" — segments are structured as responsibility centres in which "designated supervisors or managers will have both the responsibility for the performance of the center and the authority to make decisions that affect the center" [V] (Ch 9.3, "Describe the Types of Responsibility Centers"). Five responsibility-centre types are catalogued:

- **Cost centre**: "an organizational segment in which a manager is held responsible only for costs", with a direct cost-product link (e.g., custodial department) (Ch 9.3).
- **Discretionary cost centre**: similar to cost centre but without a well-defined cost-output link (e.g., human resources, accounting).
- **Revenue centre**: "an organizational segment in which a manager is held accountable only for revenues" (e.g., the reservations group of an airline).
- **Profit centre**: "an organizational segment in which a manager is responsible for both revenues and costs" (e.g., a Starbucks store).
- **Investment centre**: "an organizational segment [...] in which a manager is accountable for profits (revenues minus expenses) and the invested capital used by the segment" (e.g., a Best Buy regional unit).

For each centre type, the text develops a worked example using Apparel World (a department store): a custodial cost centre and a children's clothing profit centre, with reports comparing actual to budgeted figures and highlighting both dollar and percentage differences (Ch 9.3, Figures 9.4-9.11). The text emphasises a methodological point: dollar comparisons can mislead across differently-sized segments, while percentage comparisons standardise the analysis (Ch 9.3, "Profit Centers").

### ROI, residual income, and EVA — initial development

For investment centres, the text introduces three evaluation methods that are developed more fully in Ch 12:

- **Profit margin percentage** = net profit / net sales (Ch 9.3, "Investment Centers").
- **Return on investment (ROI)** = profit (or loss) / investment base; a measure of how much profit a segment realises per dollar invested. The text cautions that "investment base" is variously defined and that the choice affects the calculation (Ch 9.3, "Investment Centers").
- **Residual income (RI)** = segment income - (investment value × cost of capital percentage). Establishes a minimum investment threshold across all segments and reduces the goal-incongruence problem of pure ROI evaluation (Ch 9.3, "Investment Centers"). The volume's argument: under ROI, a manager whose segment currently earns 25.9 per cent ROI may reject a project earning 18 per cent because it would lower the segment's average, even when the project benefits the company; under RI, the same manager would accept any project with a positive RI (Ch 9.3).

### Controllable, uncontrollable, allocated costs and transfer pricing

*Controllable costs* are "costs that a company or manager can influence" (Ch 9.4, "Describe the Effects of Various Decisions on Performance Evaluation of Responsibility Centers"); *uncontrollable costs* are those "that the organization or manager has little or no ability to influence" in the short term. *Allocated costs* are uncontrollable costs charged to segments based on a formula. The text's prescription: "evaluate managers only on the decisions over which they have control" (Ch 9.4).

*Transfer pricing* is "the pricing process put into place when one segment of a business 'sells' goods to another segment of the same business" (Ch 9.4, "Application of Transfer Pricing"). Transfer pricing affects *goal congruence* — alignment between segment, manager, and corporate goals. With excess capacity, the selling division would benefit the company by selling internally at variable cost, but a manager evaluated on segment profit would prefer to sell internally at a price above variable cost. The text develops the trade-offs but does not prescribe a single solution (Ch 9.4).

## Part X: Short-term decision-making (Ch 10)

### Relevant information for short-term decisions

The volume frames short-term decisions as "operational in nature" — make-versus-buy, scarce-resource allocation, sell-as-is or process further — distinct from long-term capital decisions (Ch 10.1, "Identify Relevant Information for Decision-Making"). The decision-making process: identify the goal; identify alternative courses of action; perform a comprehensive analysis; decide; review and evaluate the results (Ch 10.1).

In short-term analysis, costs and revenues are *relevant* if they "differ between alternatives" (Ch 10.1, "Avoidable versus Unavoidable Costs"). Several cost categories recur:

- **Avoidable cost**: "one that can be eliminated (in whole or in part) by choosing one alternative over another" — variable costs are usually avoidable; some fixed costs are.
- **Unavoidable cost**: one that does not change between alternatives in the short run — e.g., a long-term lease.
- **Sunk cost**: "one that cannot be avoided because it has already occurred" and "will not change regardless of the alternative that management chooses; therefore, sunk costs have no bearing on future events and are not relevant in decision-making" [V] (Ch 10.1, "Sunk Costs"). The text notes that sunk costs are difficult to ignore "due to human nature" and are sometimes incorrectly included.
- **Future costs that do not differ**: irrelevant.
- **Opportunity cost**: "the costs associated with not choosing the other alternative" (Ch 10.1, "Opportunity Costs").

### Special-order decisions

For special orders, three factors recur: capacity (whether normal capacity allows fulfilment), price (whether the offered price covers differential costs), and qualitative factors (effect on existing customers, morale, reputation, Robinson-Patman compliance). When excess capacity exists, fixed costs are typically irrelevant; only variable costs matter. When the company is at capacity, accepting the order may require new fixed costs (overtime, additional equipment) that must be covered (Ch 10.2). The text's example: Franco Inc. produces dental chairs at $2,800 retail with $1,650 variable cost; a special order at $1,800 per chair is profitable when fixed costs are already covered and capacity is available, but unprofitable when accepting requires $90,000 in additional supervisor cost and $70,000 in additional utilities — at which point the floor price rises to $1,850 (Ch 10.2, "Calculations Using Sample Data").

### Make-or-buy decisions

The make-or-buy analysis compares the avoidable cost of producing internally to the cost of purchasing externally. Variable costs (direct materials, direct labour, variable overhead) are avoidable; fixed costs may be either (Ch 10.3). The text's worked example: Thermal Mugs's T6 lid costs $2.19 to produce ($0.51 of which is fixed); Plato Plastics offers it at $1.75. If all fixed costs are unavoidable, internal production is more cost-effective ($1.68 avoidable internal cost vs $1.75 buy cost); if $0.12 of fixed costs are avoidable, external purchase is more cost-effective. The example is the volume's central illustration of why proper cost identification is "critical" in outsourcing analysis (Ch 10.3, "Calculations Using Sample Data").

Outsourcing trade-offs: advantages include external expertise, freed capacity and capital, focus on core competencies, and risk transfer; disadvantages include loss of control over quality and timing, capacity constraints, hidden costs, reduced innovation, and the difficulty of bringing production back in-house (Ch 10.3, Table 10.2).

### Keep-or-discontinue decisions

Two analytical approaches: contribution-margin comparison (with and without the segment) and net-income comparison. The text's central illustration: SnowBucks's snow-boot product line appears unprofitable when allocated common costs are charged equally to three product lines, but contributes positively to fixed costs when the contribution margin and avoidable fixed costs are isolated. The volume's argument: "the reason the snow boot product line was showing an operating loss was due to the allocation of common costs" (Ch 10.4, "Final Analysis of the Decision"). The contribution-margin approach avoids the misleading allocation effects and the volume recommends it as the primary tool for keep-or-discontinue decisions.

### Sell-or-process-further decisions

When a *split-off point* exists — where a single raw material can be sold or processed into multiple end products — managers compare incremental revenue from further processing with incremental cost. *Joint costs* (costs shared by products up to split-off) are sunk for the further-processing decision and are irrelevant; only future costs matter (Ch 10.5). The text observes that joint cost allocation has methodological complications (physical measurement, relative sales value at split-off, net realisable value after further processing) but emphasises that for the further-processing *decision*, allocation choice is irrelevant since joint costs are sunk.

### Constrained-resource decisions

When a constraint limits production, the text prescribes ranking products by *unit contribution margin per unit of the constraining resource*, not by unit contribution margin alone. The Wood World worked example: with 80,000 machine hours available and orders requiring 94,000 hours, ranking by contribution margin per machine-hour (bookcases > desks > chairs) reveals that the highest-contribution-per-unit product may not be the highest-contribution-per-hour product (Ch 10.6, "Calculations Using Sample Data"). A *bottleneck* is a constraint that slows production; relaxing or *elevating* a constraint is a strategic option (overtime, additional staff, outsourcing, process redesign, defect reduction) (Ch 10.6, "Fundamentals of How to Make Decisions When Resources are Constrained").

The Ford Pinto case is presented as an ethical caution: "Ford was aware of the defect" but the cost-benefit analysis "indicated it was less expensive to build Pintos without the rubber bladder, even when including expected reimbursement costs for anyone injured or killed", and litigation costs "were higher than the original cost of including the rubber bladder component" (Ch 10.6, "Ethical Considerations: When to Include a Lifesaving Option").

## Part XI: Capital budgeting decisions (Ch 11)

### Capital investment versus operating expense

*Capital investment* (also capital budgeting) is "a company's contribution of funds toward the acquisition of long-lived (long-term or capital) assets for further growth" [V] (Ch 11.1, "Describe Capital Investment Decisions and How They Are Applied"). It contrasts with *operating expense* — "a regularly-occurring expense used to maintain the current operations of the company". Capital decisions are characterised by large outlay, long horizon, and significant risk if wrong (Ch 11.1).

The capital decision process: determine capital needs; identify and establish resource limitations; establish baseline criteria for alternatives; evaluate alternatives via *screening decisions* (filtering out alternatives that fail minimum requirements) and *preference decisions* (ranking alternatives that pass screening); make the decision (Ch 11.1, "Fundamentals of Capital Investment Decisions").

### Non-time-value methods

Two non-time-value-of-money methods are used as screening tools:

- **Payback method (PM)**: "computes the length of time it takes a company to recover their initial investment" — initial investment / annual net cash flow when cash flows are even; partial-year computation when cash flows are uneven (Ch 11.2, "Fundamentals of the Payback Method"). Limitation: ignores the time value of money and ignores cash flows after the payback point.
- **Accounting rate of return (ARR)**: "computes the return on investment considering changes to net income" — incremental revenues less incremental expenses (including depreciation) divided by the initial investment (less salvage value) (Ch 11.2, "Fundamentals of the Accounting Rate of Return Method"). Limitation: ignores time value of money and uneven income growth.

A *hurdle rate* is "the minimum required rate of return on an investment to consider an alternative for further evaluation" [V] (Ch 11.2, "Demonstration of the Accounting Rate of Return Method").

### Time value of money

The *time value of money* asserts that "the value of a dollar today is worth more than the value of a dollar in the future" because today's dollar can earn a return and because future inflation reduces purchasing power (Ch 11.3, "Time Value of Money Fundamentals"). The text illustrates with the Ford Mustang's 1964 to 2019 price rise from $2,368 to $25,680 — about 4.5 per cent annual inflation — and post-WWII home price appreciation of about 5 per cent annually.

Two cash-flow types: *lump sums* (single payments) and *annuities* (equal payments at equal intervals). *Compounding* is "the process of earning interest on previous interest earned, along with the interest earned on the original investment" (Ch 11.3, "Compounding"). *Discounting* is the reverse — finding the present value of a future amount.

The text presents four time-value tables: Future Value of $1, Future Value of an Ordinary Annuity, Present Value of $1, Present Value of an Ordinary Annuity. *Ordinary annuities* pay at the end of each period; *annuities due* pay at the beginning of each period (Ch 11.3, "Annuity Table").

### Discounted cash flow methods: NPV and IRR

*Net present value (NPV)* "discounts future cash flows to their present value at the expected rate of return and compares that to the initial investment" (Ch 11.4, "Time Value-Based Methods"). Decision rule: positive NPV is a candidate for investment; negative NPV is rejected. The volume develops examples for both equal cash flows (using the Present Value of an Ordinary Annuity table) and unequal cash flows (using the Present Value of $1 table per year).

*Internal rate of return (IRR)* "shows the profitability or growth potential of an investment at the point where NPV equals zero" — the discount rate that equates the present value of cash inflows with the initial investment (Ch 11.4, "Basic Characteristics of the Internal Rate of Return Model"). Decision rule: accept if IRR exceeds the required rate of return.

When projects are *mutually exclusive* (only one can be selected), the *profitability index* — present value of net cash flows divided by initial investment — facilitates comparison across different-sized projects (Ch 11.4, "Basic Characteristics of the Net Present Value Model"). The text notes that NPV expresses results in dollars while IRR expresses results in interest rates; both are useful and they sometimes lead to different rankings.

## Part XII: Balanced scorecard and other performance measures (Ch 12)

### Performance measurement and goal congruence

Performance measurement is used "to motivate managers to make decisions that benefit the corporation and themselves" (Ch 12.1, "Fundamentals of Performance Measurement"). The key is *goal congruence* — "aligning of goals between a corporation's strategy and a manager's personal goals" (Ch 12.1). Performance measures should be based on *controllable factors* over which the manager has authority; evaluating managers on *uncontrollable factors* "will be demotivating and does not promote goal congruence" (Ch 12.1).

Characteristics of a good performance measurement system: based on activities the manager controls or influences; measurable; timely; consistently applied; comparable to budgeted results, standards, or past performance; not biased toward the manager over the company (Ch 12.2, "Identify the Characteristics of an Effective Performance Measure").

The text catalogues limitations of traditional accounting-based measurement: results can be affected by manager actions (LIFO inventory timing example), by economic conditions outside manager control (currency-exchange effects), by multi-period decisions (capital budgeting), and by manager short-term focus when bonuses are tied to current-period results. The recommended remedy: combine accounting-based measures with non-accounting measures, short-term with long-term indicators, quantitative with qualitative components (Ch 12.1, "Potential Limitations of Traditional Performance Measurement").

### ROI, RI, EVA — fuller development

The *DuPont Model* decomposes ROI into two components: sales margin × asset turnover. *Sales margin* = net income / sales (how much profit per sales dollar). *Asset turnover* = sales / average operating assets (how many sales dollars per asset dollar). When multiplied, sales components cancel and the result is income / average operating assets, the standard ROI formula. The DuPont decomposition reveals different paths to high ROI: a jewellery store's high margin / low turnover versus a grocery store's low margin / high turnover (Ch 12.3, "Calculation and Interpretation of the Return on Investment").

ROI's drawback is the goal-congruence problem identified in Ch 9: a high-ROI segment may reject a project below its current ROI even when the project benefits the company. Residual income, computed as income - (invested capital × minimum required rate of return), addresses this by rewarding any positive contribution above the firm-wide threshold (Ch 12.3, "Calculation and Interpretation of the Residual Income").

*Economic value added (EVA)* is "similar to RI but is a measure of shareholder wealth that is being created by a project, segment, or division" (Ch 12.3, "Calculation and Interpretation of Economic Value Added"). EVA = after-tax income - (invested capital × weighted average cost of capital). The *weighted average cost of capital (WACC)* is "the cost that the company expects to pay on average to finance assets and growth using either debt or equity", weighted by the proportion of debt and equity in the capital structure. The text develops a worked example: with 45 per cent debt at 8 per cent and 55 per cent equity at 9.8 per cent, WACC = 9 per cent. EVA differs from RI in two ways: WACC reflects actual financing cost rather than a chosen minimum return; EVA uses after-tax income while RI typically uses pre-tax income (Ch 12.3).

The text discusses methodological complications: invested capital may be defined as fixed assets, productive assets (fixed plus inventory), or operating assets (productive plus intangibles plus current); valuation may be net book value, gross book value, or fair (market) value. Income may be operating income, EBIT, EBITDA, net income, or return on funds employed (ROFE). Each definition produces different numbers; the volume recommends consistency and alignment with manager controllability (Ch 12.3, "Considerations in Using the Three Evaluative Tools").

### The balanced scorecard

The balanced scorecard (BSC) was first proposed by Art Schneiderman of Analog Devices in 1987 and developed into its current form by Robert Kaplan and David Norton (Harvard) [BT] (Ch 12.4, "History and Function of the Balanced Scorecard"). Its claim: financial measures alone evaluate the past and can be gamed; *quantitative factors* (financial measures) plus *qualitative factors* (non-financial measures) across four perspectives produce more durable evaluation (Ch 12.4, "Four Components of a Balanced Scorecard").

The four perspectives:

1. **Financial perspective**: ROI, RI, EVA, EPS, revenue growth, sales growth, inventory turnover, or domain-specific measures (e.g., seat revenue for an airline).
2. **Internal business perspective**: defects, machine downtime, transaction efficiency, products completed per employee per day, or domain-specific measures (e.g., percentage of time planes are on the ground).
3. **Customer perspective**: customer satisfaction, repeat customers, new customers, customer referrals, market share.
4. **Learning and growth**: employee suggestions adopted, turnover rate, training hours, scope of process improvements, new products.

The BSC enables a *stakeholder perspective* rather than a stockholder-only perspective, with stakeholders defined as "people who are affected by the decisions made by a company, such as investors, creditors, managers, regulators, employees, customers, suppliers, and even laypeople who are concerned about whether or not the company is a good world citizen" (Ch 12.4, "Four Components of a Balanced Scorecard").

The volume's worked example (Scrumptious Sweets bakery) demonstrates BSC at three levels: corporate, divisional (brownie division), and individual (storefront employees) — each preserving the same four categories but increasing in goal specificity (Ch 12.4, "Combining the Four Components of a Balanced Scorecard"; Figures 12.6-12.8). The text emphasises that BSC design works best collaboratively, with employee input on goals and metrics, "rather than being told what goals are to be met" (Ch 12.4, "Combining the Four Components of a Balanced Scorecard").

> "The combination of these different types of quantitative and qualitative measures—project-specific measures, employee-level measures, divisional measures, and corporate measures—enables an organization to more adequately assess how it is progressing toward meeting short- and long-term goals."
> [V] (Ch 12.4, "Final Summary of Quantitative and Quantitative Performance Measurement Tools")

## Part XIII: Sustainability reporting (Ch 13)

### Sustainability and CSR

*Sustainability* is defined here as "meeting the needs of the present generation without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs" [V] (Ch 13.1, "Describe Sustainability and the Way It Creates Business Value", citing the Brundtland Commission Report 1987). *Corporate social responsibility* (CSR) is "the set of actions that firms take to assume responsibility for their impact on the environment and social well-being" (Ch 13.1).

The Paris Climate Agreement of December 2015 — "a historic plan to work together to limit the increase of global temperatures to 1.5 °C" adopted by 196 nations — anchors the contemporary international framing (Ch 13.1, "Describe Sustainability and the Way It Creates Business Value"). The text notes the United States's 2017 announced withdrawal and a coalition of 20 U.S. states and 50 cities (led by California governor Jerry Brown and former NYC mayor Michael Bloomberg) that pledged to continue supporting the Agreement (Ch 13.1).

Companies adopting sustainable practices may benefit through saving money on water and energy, lowering insurance costs, attracting investors, reducing social risks, improving customer loyalty, reducing regulation risk through proactive measures, attracting and retaining employees with shared values, strengthening community relationships, and improving environmental sustainability (Ch 13.1, "Sustainability Reporting").

### Three foundational corporate cases

The text develops three cases as historical drivers of contemporary sustainability reporting:

- **Union Carbide / Bhopal (1984)**: methyl isocyanate leak at the Bhopal pesticide plant exposed more than 600,000 people, with an official toll of 3,598 dead and 42,000 injured (some accounts: 16,000-20,000 deaths). The case "highlighted the power disparity between corporations and poor individuals and became a stark emblem of corporate disregard for the human toll of the quest for profit" (Ch 13.1, "Historical Drivers of Contemporary Sustainability Reporting"). Union Carbide ultimately paid $470 million in compensation; was bought by Dow Chemical in 2001.
- **Nestlé / baby formula**: a long-running consumer boycott originating in the mid-1970s when aggressive marketing of formula in developing countries led to mothers using contaminated water for preparation and having their breast milk dry up. UNICEF estimated formula-fed infants were "14 times more likely [...] to die of diarrhea and four times more likely to die of pneumonia than breast-fed children" [V] (Ch 13.1, "Nestlé"). The boycott prompted Nestlé to support WHO breastfeeding guidelines and adopt stricter internal policies.
- **Johnson & Johnson / Tylenol (1982)**: cyanide-laced Tylenol capsules killed seven people. CEO James Burke formed a strategy team asking "How do we protect the people?" and "How do we save the product?". Within two months, Tylenol was relaunched with three-way tamper-proof packaging (Ch 13.1, "Johnson & Johnson"). The recall cost more than $100 million but the share price returned to its previous high within six weeks; the response is "lauded in business case studies and has formed the basis of crisis communications strategies developed by researchers" (Ch 13.1).

### The triple bottom line and information users

The *triple bottom line* (TBL or 3BL) was "first proposed in 1997 by John Elkington to expand the traditional financial reporting framework so as to capture a firm's social and environmental performance" [V] (Ch 13.1, "Initial Sustainability Reports"). Elkington's "People, Planet, Profit" formulation accompanies the framing. *Life-cycle* or *full-cost accounting* takes a "cradle to grave" approach including disposal costs and externalities.

The triple bottom line "expanded the role of reporting beyond shareholders and investors to a broader range of stakeholders – that is, anyone directly or indirectly affected by the organization, including employees, customers, government entities, regulators, creditors, and the local community" [V] (Ch 13.2, "Identify User Needs for Information"). Primary information users are shareholders and lenders; secondary users are customers, suppliers, governments and regulators, the community, and employees.

User-by-user analysis (Ch 13.2):

- **Shareholders**: focus on future viability and value retention; use ratios like P/E, EPS, ROI; ethical-investment movement is growing — "in 2016, ethical investments topped $8.7 trillion, up 33% from 2014, and they now account for 20% of all investment under professional management" [V] (Ch 13.2, "Shareholders"). The Forum for Sustainable and Responsible Investment defines sustainable investing as one that "considers environmental, social and corporate governance criteria to generate long-term competitive financial returns and positive societal impact" (Ch 13.2, "Ethical Considerations: Millennials Are Demanding Sustainable Investments"). Studies suggest "socially responsible companies post higher profits and stock performance than those that were not focused on social responsibility", with a Deutsche Bank analysis of more than 2,000 studies dating to the 1970s, "90% of which suggested that socially responsible investing gives better returns than passive investing" [V] (Ch 13.2).
- **Lenders**: want to know going-concern risk; geographic concentration risk; human-rights-violation exposure.
- **Employees**: increasingly want to work for value-aligned companies. ### Three reporting frameworks

Three sustainability reporting frameworks are catalogued:

- **Global Reporting Initiative (GRI)** (formed 1997). According to GRI, "92% of the Global 250 produced sustainability reports in 2016" (Ch 13.3, "Global Reporting Initiative (GRI)"). GRI's 2016 standards include three universal standards (foundation, general disclosures, management approach) plus topic-specific standards selected based on materiality. GRI defines materiality as: "The report should cover Aspects that: Reflect the organization's significant economic, environmental and social impacts; or substantively influence the assessments and decisions of stakeholders" [V] (Ch 13.3, citing GRI).
- **Sustainability Accounting Standards Board (SASB)** (established 2011). Targeted at investor-focused reporting; available for 79 industries across 10 sectors. SASB adopted the U.S. Supreme Court's view of materiality (TSC Indus. v. Northway): "a substantial likelihood that the disclosure of the omitted fact would have been viewed by the reasonable investor as having significantly altered the 'total mix' of information made available" [V] (Ch 13.3, "Sustainability Accounting Standards Board (SASB)").
- **Integrated Reporting** (International Integrated Reporting Council, formed 2010). Brings together "financial, social, environmental and governance information in a clear, concise, consistent and comparable format" (Ch 13.3, "Integrated Reporting"). The framework identifies six categories of capital: financial, manufactured, intellectual, human, social and relationship, natural. Mandatory in South Africa and Brazil; adopted by Clorox, Entergy, GE, Jones Lang LaSalle, PepsiCo, Prudential Financial, Southwest Airlines.

### Future issues

Sustainability reporting "is still relatively new and its use is not yet mandatory" but is converging on materiality-based disclosure. The text projects future issues in cybersecurity, globalisation under shifting political winds, and artificial intelligence's impact on white-collar work — citing Bill Gates's call for a robot tax to finance a Universal Basic Income, and Yanis Varoufakis's concept of a Universal Basic Dividend funded by a portion of corporate IPOs (Ch 13.4, "Future Issues in Sustainability"). The chapter closes with the framing that sustainability reporting "moves us a long way from merely reporting the financial results of businesses [...] and helps bind us all in a way that says we are all truly part of a single, global environment that is determined by the actions of both businesses and citizens" [V] (Ch 13.4).

> "In short, sustainability reporting has evolved to describe both how the company's practices contribute to the social good and how they add value to the company, which ultimately provides better returns to its investors."

## Appendix A: Financial statement analysis

Financial statement analysis "reviews financial information found on financial statements to make informed decisions about the business" (App A, "Financial Statement Analysis"). Three primary tools: horizontal analysis (trend analysis across periods), vertical analysis (line-item proportions within a period), and financial ratios.

### Horizontal and vertical analysis

*Horizontal analysis* compares one period (typically the prior year as base) to a current year via dollar change and percentage change. Multiple periods are needed to identify a trend (App A, "Horizontal Analysis"). *Vertical analysis* expresses each line item as a percentage of a base — net sales for income statements, total assets and total liabilities-plus-equity for balance sheets — producing common-size statements that facilitate cross-company comparison (App A, "Vertical Analysis").

### Four ratio categories

Ratios fall into four categories (App A, "Overview of Financial Ratios"):

- **Liquidity ratios**: working capital (current assets - current liabilities), current ratio (current assets / current liabilities), quick ratio (current assets less inventory and prepaid / current liabilities). Show ability to pay short-term obligations.
- **Solvency ratios**: debt-to-equity (total liabilities / total equity), times interest earned (EBIT / interest expense). Show ability to meet long-term obligations.
- **Efficiency ratios**: accounts receivable turnover (net credit sales / average accounts receivable), total asset turnover (net sales / average total assets), inventory turnover (cost of goods sold / average inventory), days' sales in inventory (365 / inventory turnover). Show how well assets are managed.
- **Profitability ratios**: profit margin (net income / net sales), return on total assets (net income / average total assets), return on equity (net income - preferred dividends / average stockholders' equity). Show returns on operations.

### Limits of the analysis

The text cautions that "past performance does not always dictate future performance"; that economic influences (inflation, recession) can skew numbers; and that companies' reporting practices may shift over time, sometimes invisibly within published statements (App A, "Advantages and Disadvantages of Financial Statement Analysis").

## Positions the author explicitly frames against

- *Pure absorption costing for managerial decisions.* The text frames absorption costing as required by GAAP but inferior for internal decision-making because it can mask the impact of fixed costs and create incentives to overproduce: "the Big Three auto companies made decisions based on absorption costing, and the result was the manufacturing of more vehicles than the market demanded" (Ch 6.5, "Absorbing Costs through Overproduction").
- *Equal allocation of common costs in segment evaluation.* The SnowBucks worked example is the volume's central illustration that equal common-cost allocation can produce false signals about segment unprofitability; the contribution-margin approach is recommended (Ch 10.4).
- *Sole reliance on ROI as a segment evaluation measure.* The text argues that ROI alone produces goal-incongruence problems: a high-ROI segment manager will reject a project that benefits the company because it lowers the segment's average ROI. Residual income and EVA are positioned as remedies (Ch 9.3, Ch 12.3).
- *Pure financial-measure performance evaluation.* The balanced scorecard's case rests on the claim that financial measures alone are gameable, lagging, and short-term-biased; non-financial measures across four perspectives produce more durable evaluation (Ch 12.1, "Potential Limitations of Traditional Performance Measurement"; Ch 12.4).
- *Stockholder-only accountability.* Sustainability reporting is framed as a corrective to financial-only reporting that ignores wider stakeholder interests; the public benefit corporation model is presented as a legal expression of the same correction (Ch 13.1, Ch 13.2).
- *Pure cost-minimisation in ethical decisions.* The Ford Pinto case is the volume's central illustration that "more than cost and revenue should be considered when making an ethical business decision"; quantitative cost-benefit analysis that excludes ethical and reputational consequences produces decisions that look profitable in the short term and disastrous in the long term (Ch 10.6, "Ethical Considerations: When to Include a Lifesaving Option").
- *Budget slack and managing-to-the-variance.* The text repeatedly warns that variance-based evaluation can be gamed via inflated standards (slack) or via short-term decisions that meet variance targets at the cost of long-term goals (Ch 7.5, Ch 8.5).
- *Ideal standards as motivators.* Ideal standards "may see this level as unattainable" and are not the best motivator; attainable standards are recommended (Ch 8.1, "Fundamentals of Standard Costs").
