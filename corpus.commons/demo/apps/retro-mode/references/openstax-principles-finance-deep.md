## Author's thesis (paraphrased from source)

*Principles of Finance* presents finance as the study of the management, movement, and raising of money, organised around three primary domestic areas: business finance (how managers apply financial principles to maximise firm value in a risky environment), investments (products and processes used to create and grow wealth), and financial markets and institutions (the firms and regulatory agencies that oversee the financial system) [V] (Ch 1.1, "Definition of Finance"; Ch 1.1, "Basic Areas in Finance"). The book opens with a load-bearing claim: "business finance is about developing and understanding the tools that help people make consistently good and repeatable decisions" [V] (Ch 1, "Why It Matters"). Finance is positioned as a decision-making toolkit, not a body of doctrine.

A second load-bearing claim is that the **risk-return tradeoff** is finance's organising principle: "an increase in risk results in an increase in expected return" [AP] (Ch 1.1, "Risk and Return in Finance"). Investors are risk-averse, so they must be compensated through higher expected returns to bear greater uncertainty. The text returns to this principle repeatedly across bond pricing, stock valuation, the CAPM, capital structure, and risk management.

A third load-bearing claim is that **the time value of money** is the most important concept in the study of finance [AR] (Ch 7, "Why It Matters"). A dollar received today is worth more than a dollar received in the future, for three reasons stated by the text: money received now can be invested to earn interest, future payments carry default risk, and human nature prefers present consumption [AP] (Ch 7, "Why It Matters"). Compounding (carrying value forward) and discounting (bringing value back) are inverses of the same operation, applied to single sums (Ch 7), equal payment streams or annuities (Ch 8), and unequal mixed streams (Ch 9).

A fourth load-bearing claim is that **firm value depends on cash flows, not accounting earnings**: "managers focus on three broad areas" of business finance — working capital management, capital budgeting, and capital structure — each tied to the balance sheet [V] (Ch 1.1, "Business Finance"). The text repeatedly distinguishes accounting earnings (book values, recorded under accrual rules) from cash flows (the actual liquid resources that determine viability and valuation), and argues that the latter govern business survival and asset pricing [AR] (Ch 4-5; Ch 11.3, "Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Model").

A fifth load-bearing claim is that **the goal of management is to maximise shareholder wealth** subject to ethical and stakeholder constraints. "Stockholders are not just an abstract group . . . they are individuals who have chosen to invest their hard-earned cash in a company" [V] (Ch 2, "Why It Matters"). Yet "a firm has important nonfinancial goals" [V] (Ch 2, "Why It Matters") and a stakeholder is "any person or group that has an interest in the outcomes of an organization's actions . . . employees, customers, shareholders, suppliers, communities, and governments" [V] (Ch 2.2, "Stakeholders"). Agency theory (principal-agent problems) supplies the analytical apparatus for the conflicts of interest that arise when managers (agents) hold goals different from those of shareholders (principals) (Ch 2.4, "Agency Issues: Shareholders and Corporate Boards").

## Part I: What finance is, and the role of finance in an organisation (Ch 1)

### The three areas of finance and the three focal areas of business finance

Finance is divided into three primary areas in the domestic market: business finance, investments, and financial markets and institutions [V] (Ch 1.1, "Basic Areas in Finance"). Within business finance, managers focus on three broad areas: working capital management (short-term assets and liabilities), capital budgeting (long-term or fixed assets), and capital structure (long-term debt and equity financing) [AP] (Ch 1.1, "Business Finance"). Capital budgeting decisions "add the greatest value to a firm. As such, capital budgeting is thought to be one of the most important financial functions within a firm" [V] (Ch 1.1, "Business Finance"). The opening narrative case (Bacon Signs, 1978-onwards) frames the three areas concretely: a regional sign company that grew by combining quality production with a financing plan that anticipated capital requirements (Ch 1, "Why It Matters").

### The five components of investment risk

The overall uncertainty of returns has several components, each named with a definition (Ch 1.1, "Risk and Return in Finance"):

- **Default risk**: the chance that the issuer will fail to make the required payment [V].
- **Inflation risk**: reduced purchasing power from realised cash flows due to rising prices [V].
- **Diversifiable risk** (unsystematic risk): risk that can be eliminated by holding a more well-rounded portfolio [V].
- **Non-diversifiable risk** (systematic risk): what remains after portfolio diversification has eliminated unnecessary diversifiable risk; measured with beta [V].
- **Political risk**: risk associated with macroeconomic issues beyond the firm's control — local, state, or national governments "changing the rules" through zoning, product liability, taxation, or nationalisation [V] (Ch 1.1).

### Roles within the finance function

The text names a hierarchy of finance roles. The **comptroller** (controller) handles financial reporting and oversight of accounting activities (payroll, AR, AP, taxes, inventory control); the **treasurer** monitors cash flow and is the contact for bankers, underwriters, and outside financing sources; the **vice president of finance (VP-F)** oversees both; the **chief financial officer (CFO)** is in a "big picture" position, setting policy for working capital management, determining optimal capital structure, making the final decision in matters of capital budgeting, and engaged in strategic financial planning [AP] (Ch 1.2, "The Finance Function"). "Where a controller may face inward toward the organization, the treasurer often faces outward as a representative to the public" [V] (Ch 1.2).

### Markets and participants

The **primary market** is the market for new securities; the **secondary market** is the market for used securities [V] (Ch 1.5, "Primary and Secondary Markets"). Most equity-market activity is secondary; new issues come through SEOs (seasoned equity offerings) by established firms and IPOs (initial public offerings) for firms going public for the first time. Treasury auctions of new securities are a substantial primary market.

Three classes of intermediaries operate in secondary markets. **Dealers** own the securities they buy or sell; they "make markets," willing and able to buy and sell at current bid and ask prices, earning the bid-ask spread [AP] (Ch 1.5, "Dealers"). **Brokers** facilitate trades between buyers and sellers and earn commissions; they hold no inventory of their own (Ch 1.5, "Brokers"). **Financial intermediaries** (commercial banks, mutual fund investment companies) accept funds from one set of parties and create different securities for another — for instance, a bank takes deposits and creates loans (Ch 1.5, "Financial Intermediaries"). Disintermediation, when borrowers and lenders bypass intermediaries, can cause significant turmoil; the 1970s loss of savings to direct Treasury investments effectively eliminated the savings and loan industry and led to deregulation of US commercial and investment banking (Ch 1.5).

### Money market and capital market instruments

The **money market** is for short-term (less than one year), low-risk, highly liquid, homogeneous securities. Instruments include Treasury bills (T-bills, short-term federal debt, default-risk-free, exempt from state and local income taxes), commercial paper (CP, short-term unsecured corporate or financial-institution debt, 1-270 days, taxable, weak secondary market), negotiable certificates of deposit (NCDs, jumbo CDs in increments of $100,000+ with active secondary market, principal not insured), and federal funds (overnight interbank lending; the Fed targets the equilibrium rate as its primary monetary policy tool) [AP] (Ch 1.7, "Money Markets and Instruments").

The **capital market** is for longer-term financial instruments — Treasury notes (2-10 year maturities, semiannual coupon), Treasury bonds (20 or 30 year maturities, semiannual coupon), municipal bonds ("munis," issued by state and local governments, with interest exempt from federal taxes and sometimes from state and local taxes), corporate bonds (specified by an indenture contract), and stocks (Ch 1.7, "Capital Markets and Instruments"). Stock cash flows are more uncertain than bond cash flows because dividends and resale prices are not contractually guaranteed.

### Time value, present value, and economic value

The chapter opens the time-value theme: the choice between consumption today and consumption in the future hinges on the rate of return earned by delaying consumption [AP] (Ch 1.8, "Impact of Time on Saving and Spending"). Long-term investments enjoy compounded returns and the time to recover from temporary poor performance; short-term investments preserve principal but earn less. The text closes Ch 1 by distinguishing **market value** ("calculated using expected future cash flows discounted to today" or "the prices that consumers pay for a product") from **economic value** ("what we believe consumers are actually willing to pay for a product, service, or experience"); economic value is at least as great as market value [V] (Ch 1.8, "Definition of Economic Value").

## Part II: Corporate structure, governance, agency, and stakeholders (Ch 2)

### The four most common forms of business organisation

The four most common forms are sole proprietorships, partnerships, corporations, and hybrids (LLCs and LLPs) [AP] (Ch 2.1, "The Most Common Types of Business Organization"). The vast majority of US businesses are proprietorships by count, but more than 80 per cent of all US business by total dollar value of combined sales is conducted by corporations [V] (Ch 2.1).

Each form trades simplicity against liability and capital-raising capacity:

- **Sole proprietorships**: simple, inexpensive, light regulation, no separate taxation; but unlimited personal liability, life limited to the owner's life, difficulty raising large capital, and dependence on the owner's expertise (Ch 2.1, "Sole Proprietorships").
- **Partnerships**: similarly easy to form; multiple partners may bring complementary expertise and capital; income flows through to partners' personal taxes (no corporate-level tax); but unlimited liability for all partners, where one partner's actions can ruin the others (Ch 2.1, "Partnerships").
- **Corporations**: separate legal entity, limiting stockholders' losses to the amount originally invested; unlimited corporate life; transferable ownership; ease of raising capital — but subject to double taxation (corporate earnings are taxed, then dividends are taxed again as personal income). The S corporation form (no more than 100 stockholders) is taxed as a partnership and exempt from corporate income tax; the C corporation form is more flexible for raising capital and prevalent among larger firms (Ch 2.1, "Corporations").
- **Hybrids (LLC, LLP)**: liability protection with partnership-like taxation; LLPs typical of professional services firms (accounting, architecture, law); investors' votes proportional to ownership share; limited partners may have unlimited liability for designated general partners only (Ch 2.1, "Hybrids: Limited Liability Corporations and Partnerships").

The corporate form's value advantage rests on three drivers: limited ownership liability reduces investor risk, growth opportunities allow corporations to pursue investments more easily than other forms, and stock liquidity makes corporate ownership transferable (Ch 2.1).

### Stakeholders versus shareholders

A **stakeholder** is "any person or group that has an interest in the outcomes of an organization's actions. Stakeholders include employees, customers, shareholders, suppliers, communities, and governments" [V] (Ch 2.2, "Stakeholders"). A **shareholder** is "an owner of a company because of the shares of stock they own"; a stakeholder may not own part of the company but is "in many ways equally dependent on the performance of the company. However, their concerns may not be financial" [V] (Ch 2.2, "The Differences between a Shareholder and a Stakeholder").

The text observes that "a firm has important nonfinancial goals," giving examples: expanding sales to existing customers, increasing customer loyalty for weaker brands, developing new products, becoming international, and improving customer satisfaction [V] (Ch 2, "Why It Matters"). Even so, the dominant frame is that "the main financial goal is to create value for investors" [V] (Ch 2, "Why It Matters") and stock-price maximisation requires a long-term view because "managerial actions that affect a company's value may not immediately be reflected in the price of a company's stock but rather will become evident in the long-term prospects of the organization" [V] (Ch 2, "Why It Matters").

There are two types of shareholders: **common shareholders** (with voting rights and the right to bring charges if management harms the organisation) and **preferred shareholders** (no voting rights or management involvement, but a fixed amount of annual dividends apportioned before common shareholders are paid). Common shareholders experience higher capital gains and losses (Ch 2.2, "Types of Shareholders").

### The board of directors and corporate governance

A **board of directors (BOD)** is "a group of people who are elected to represent shareholders, and these directors are then ultimately responsible for running the company" [V] (Ch 2.3, "Functions of a Board of Directors"). The board acts as a fiduciary for shareholders and is also tasked with setting company goals, creating dividend and stock-option policies, hiring and firing CEOs, and ensuring resources sufficient for performance (Ch 2.3). Public companies are legally required to install a board; nonprofits and private companies often do so voluntarily.

The structure typically includes both internal directors (involved in daily operations) and external directors (representing outside interests); the CEO often serves as chair (Ch 2.3, "Basic Structure of a Board of Directors"). International structures vary: in Asia and the European Union, executive boards (company insiders elected by employees and shareholders) are commonly paired with supervisory boards (Ch 2.3, "International Structure of a Board of Directors"). **Corporate governance** is "the system of rules, practices, and processes by which a firm is directed and controlled" — focusing on how a company conducts its business and the controls implemented to ensure proper procedures and ethical behaviour (Ch 2.3, "Oversight: Corporate Governance"; Key Terms).

The text frames a regulatory history. After the 1929 crash, the US government created the Securities Act of 1933, the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, and the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) to oversee them. After the early-2000s scandals, the **Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX)** of 2002 required CEO and CFO certification of financial-reporting accuracy, an effective structure of internal controls over reporting, and confirmation by both the company and an external auditor; it also created the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (Ch 2.3, "Oversight: Corporate Governance").

A strong independent **audit committee (AC)** is a separately chartered subcommittee of the board that confirms the accuracy of financial reporting, verifies systems of internal control and risk management, ensures compliance with legal and regulatory requirements, verifies the qualifications and independence of the external auditor, and coordinates the activities of the internal audit function [AP] (Ch 2.3, "The Role of the Audit Committee"). With SOX, AC importance has grown; some firms split duties to a compensation committee, a disclosure committee, and a governance committee.

Independent boards — directors with no material interest in the company beyond directorship — are a corporate governance best practice; they bring expertise and objectivity that helps assure ownership the company is being run legally, ethically, and in shareholders' interests; allows for objectivity regarding senior managers; and prevents hidden agendas (Ch 2.3, "The Importance of Independence in Boards of Directors"). Diversity in boards has become a high priority for businesses worldwide, though "many business observers believe that corporate governance practices have a long way to go in this respect" [AP] (Ch 2.3, "The Importance of Diversity in Boards of Directors").

### Agency problems and ESG

**Agency problems** arise when an **agent** (manager) entrusted with following the interests of a **principal** (shareholder or owner) abuses their position to further personal goals [AP] (Ch 2.4, "Agency Problems and Issues"). Three primary types are catalogued (Ch 2.4):

1. **Stockholders versus management**: external managers may make self-serving decisions or misuse company funds, leading to declining performance and conflicts of interest. The WorldCom case (2001 CEO using company assets to underwrite personal loans) is given as an example [AE] (Ch 2.4, "Stockholders versus Management").
2. **Investors versus creditors**: if a company engages in risky investments to drive profitability, the increased risk threatens the company's ability to service debts; bondholders may suffer if riskier projects fail (Ch 2.4, "Investors versus Creditors").
3. **Stockholders versus other stakeholders**: shareholders' interests may conflict with employees, customers, communities, or other stakeholders. The Harry & David leveraged-buyout case (2011 bankruptcy) is given as an example of stockholders putting their interests ahead of other stakeholders' [AE] (Ch 2.4, "Stockholders versus Other Stakeholders").

Mitigations include incentives for strong performance and ethical behaviour, stock-based compensation packages encouraging long-term thinking, and penalties for poor performance, shortsightedness, or unethical behaviour (Ch 2.4, "How to Resolve Agency Problems"). Hostile takeovers, even the threat of one, "tends to unify and discipline a management or agent group, thus fostering a union of agent and shareholder interests" [V] (Ch 2.4).

The Enron and Madoff cases are presented at length as cautionary illustrations: Enron as an example of a multibillion-dollar company concealing losses through inappropriate accounting, with bond ratings reduced to junk status only weeks before the December 2001 bankruptcy; Madoff's Ponzi scheme as an example of how lack of oversight enables agency theory's pathological case (Ch 2.4, "Infamous Agency Problems").

**ESG** ratings (environmental, social, and governance) have become increasingly used by the investment community. ESG ratings were originally developed to assess the general risk of ESG factors but have grown into "unique scores used by investors to gauge the potential attractiveness of investment in the subject company" [AP] (Ch 2.4, "Impact of ESG Ratings"). Firms with high ESG metrics are believed to represent superior investments. ESG is a starting point, not the entire story; investment decisions should include additional data.

### Investor relations and quarterly reporting

**Investor relations (IR)** combines communication, marketing, and finance to control the flow of information between a public corporation and its investors and stakeholders (Ch 2.5, "Investor Relations"). IR responsibilities include coordinating shareholder meetings and press conferences, disseminating financial information, briefing financial analysts, publishing the quarterly and annual reports, and addressing financial-disclosure issues. IR has heavier regulatory obligations than standard public relations because of SEC and IFRS requirements, and SOX significantly increased the volume and rigour of public-company financial reporting.

Publicly traded companies are required to file annual Form 10-K, quarterly Form 10-Qs (within 45 days of quarter-end), and Form 8-Ks for major events; quarterly **press releases** and **conference calls** with the investment community are best practice (not legal requirements) and add context and commentary to reported financials (Ch 2.5, "Quarterly Earnings Conference Calls"). Annual meetings, investor roadshows, and investment conferences are critical for relationships with shareholders and the investment community; "key messages supporting any ownership or potential investment case should be clear and consistent" and "embedded within the company's materials" [V] (Ch 2.5, "Investment Meetings and Conferences").

### Domestic versus international versus global firms

**Domestic firms** operate completely or for the most part within US borders, governed by US accounting and securities laws (SEC) and reporting under **Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP)**. **International firms** are US-based with significant international investments and operations; parent-company accounting follows GAAP, but foreign subsidiaries may be regulated by host-country laws. **Global firms** have substantial operations and investments in different countries with no single centre; corporate-governance regulations are determined by the country in which the parent is established. Outside the US, accounting is generally governed by **International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS)** (Ch 2.6, "Important Differences among Domestic, International, and Global Organizations"). GAAP and IFRS differ on inventory accounting, consolidation of subsidiaries, and accounting for minority interests (Ch 2.6, "Difference in Financial Reporting: GAAP versus IFRS"). The **Electronic Data Gathering, Analysis, and Retrieval system (EDGAR)** is the SEC's primary system for collecting and indexing public-company filings (Ch 2.6, "SEC Reporting and EDGAR").

## Part III: Microeconomic and macroeconomic foundations (Ch 3)

### Demand, supply, and market equilibrium

**Demand** is the quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to purchase at various prices during a given time period, **ceteris paribus** ("all other things being equal") [V] (Ch 3.1, "Demand"). The **law of demand** is the inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded, generated by diminishing marginal utility and limited consumer income (Ch 3.1).

**Supply** is the quantity of a good or service that firms are willing to sell at various prices during a given time period, ceteris paribus. The supply curve typically slopes upward (Ch 3.1, "Supply"). The **equilibrium** is where supply and demand intersect; at the **equilibrium price**, quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. Above-equilibrium prices generate surpluses that drive prices down; below-equilibrium prices generate shortages that drive prices up (Ch 3.1, "Equilibrium Price"). Demand and supply curves shift in response to changes in income, population, tastes, prices of other goods (for demand) or input costs (for supply).

### Inflation, unemployment, and GDP

**Inflation** is "a general increase in price levels" [V] (Ch 3.2, "Inflation"). It is measured by:

- The **consumer price index (CPI)**, the BLS's measure of the cost of buying a fixed basket of goods and services across eight major categories: food and beverages, housing, apparel, transportation, medical care, recreation, education and communication, and other goods and services [V] (Ch 3.2, "How Is Inflation Measured").
- The **core inflation index**, calculated by excluding volatile food and energy prices from the CPI (Ch 3.2).
- The **producer price index (PPI)**, based on prices producers pay for supplies and raw materials; rising PPI can foreshadow rising CPI (Ch 3.2).
- The **GDP deflator**, calculated by the BEA using all components of gross domestic product rather than a fixed basket (Ch 3.2).

Historical CPI for 1947-2020 shows inflation as the norm, with the 1970s and early 1980s as the only US experience of sustained double-digit inflation [V] (Ch 3.2, "Historical Trends in the Inflation Rate").

To be considered **unemployed**, a person must be "(1) jobless, (2) actively seeking work, and (3) able to take a job" [V] (Ch 3.2, "Unemployment"). The unemployment rate is the percentage of the labour force unemployed, not of the total population.

**Gross domestic product (GDP)** is "the dollar value of all of the final goods and service produced within that country during a year" [V] (Ch 3.2, "Gross Domestic Product"). GDP is measured by adding consumption (about two-thirds of US GDP), investment (15-18 per cent), government spending (nearly 20 per cent), and net exports (Ch 3.2, "How Is GDP Measured?"). Nominal GDP is multiplied by current prices; real GDP adjusts for inflation by valuing output at base-year prices.

### The business cycle

The **business cycle** is alternating expansion and contraction; the **peak** marks the turn from expansion to contraction, the **trough** the reverse [AP] (Ch 3.3, "Stages of the Business Cycle"). The contraction is referred to as a **recession**; the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) defines a recession as "a significant decline in economic activity that is spread across the economy and lasts for at least a few months" [V] (Ch 3.3). Historically, contractions have averaged about 17 months and expansions about 41 months; the 2009-2020 expansion was the longest in US history at 128 months (Ch 3.3, "Historical Trends"). The April-2020 COVID-19 trough was the shortest US recession on record.

### Interest rates: the loanable-funds market

The market for loanable funds determines the equilibrium interest rate; lenders supply funds (upward-sloping supply curve in interest rate vs quantity), borrowers demand funds (downward-sloping demand) [AP] (Ch 3.4, "Market for Loanable Funds"). The **nominal interest rate** is the stated, quoted rate; the **real interest rate** adjusts the nominal rate for inflation and is "the true determinant of the cost of borrowing and the reward for lending" [V] (Ch 3.4, "Real Interest Rates"). The chapter previews the **Fisher effect** formula formally introduced in Ch 7.

Risk premiums reflect credit risk: the US Treasury rate is the proxy for the risk-free rate; the prime rate is what banks charge their best customers; credit-card rates are higher because individuals are riskier, default rates are higher, and many credit-card debts are unsecured (Ch 3.4, "Risk Premiums"). All else equal, longer maturities and riskier borrowers carry higher rates.

### Foreign exchange and exchange rate risk

The **spot exchange rate** is the rate at which currencies trade right now [V] (Ch 3.5, "Spot Exchange Rate"). Currency **appreciation** occurs when a unit of one currency buys more of a foreign currency than before; **depreciation** is the inverse. By definition, if one currency depreciates, the other appreciates. Three forms of currency exchange-rate risk are distinguished (Ch 3.5, "Exchange Rate Risk"; revisited at length in Ch 20.3):

- **Transaction exposure**: risk that the value of expected receipts or expenses changes as exchange rates change.
- **Translation exposure**: accounting risk that the reported value of foreign-currency-denominated items on financial statements changes as rates change.
- **Economic exposure**: risk that exchange-rate changes affect a business's number of customers and sales — even for firms with no direct international operations.

### Sources of economic data and indexes

**FRED (Federal Reserve Economic Data)**, maintained by the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis, contains more than 765,000 economic time series and is a primary source for empirical work in finance (Ch 3.6, "FRED: Federal Reserve Economic Data"). Indexes (such as the CPI) are constructed by selecting a base year (set to index 100) and tracking changes from there; this allows comparisons of magnitudes across countries and time even when absolute levels differ (Ch 3.6, "Indexes"). Data can be presented in levels or as percentage changes; the latter highlights growth rates and turning points (Ch 3.6, "Levels versus Percentage Changes").

## Part IV: Accrual accounting (Ch 4)

### Cash versus accrual accounting

**Cash-basis accounting** records transactions only when cash flows; it is most often used by small businesses dealing primarily in cash transactions [AP] (Ch 4.1, "Cash-Basis Accounting"). **Accrual-basis accounting** records transactions when they occur, using the **matching principle** to pair revenues with the expenses that generated them; it is required by GAAP for public companies (Ch 4.1, "Accrual-Basis Accounting"). Companies with inventory must use accrual-based accounting for income tax purposes. The cash basis is simpler, but the accrual basis "typically provides a better sense of the financial well-being of a company" [V] (Ch 4.1).

### The accounting equation and double-entry system

The **accounting equation** is `Assets = Liabilities + Equity`, framed in the text as "sources and claims": every asset has a claim against it by creditors and/or owners (Ch 4.2, "The Accounting Equation"). The **double-entry accounting system** requires each transaction to record a change in at least two different accounts, with at least one **debit** (left side) and one **credit** (right side), and with debits and credits equal in sum (Ch 4.2, "Double-Entry Accounting"). On the expanded equation: assets, dividends, and expenses increase on the debit side; liabilities, common stock, and revenues increase on the credit side.

### Revenue and expense recognition

**Revenue recognition** records sales or fees earned within the period earned [V] (Ch 4.3, "Revenue Recognition"). A cash sale is recorded under both cash and accrual basis; a credit sale is recorded under cash basis only when paid, but under accrual basis at the time of the sale. The same logic applies symmetrically to purchases. Accounting Standards Update No. 2014-09 replaced industry-specific revenue-recognition rules with a principle-based approach (Ch 4.3, "Ethics in Revenue Recognition"). Gift-card revenue recognition is treated as an explicit example of estimation in revenue recognition (Ch 4.3).

### Capitalisation and depreciation of long-term assets

**Long-term assets** are used by the business for more than one year, are not intended for resale, and are anticipated to help generate revenue in the future. **Tangible long-term assets** (property, plant, and equipment, or PP&E) are physically substantive; **intangible long-term assets** lack physical substance (patents, copyrights). Companies **capitalise** a fixed asset at material cost rather than expensing it immediately (Ch 4.4, "What Is a Fixed Asset?").

**Depreciation** is "the process of allocating the cost of a tangible asset over its useful life, or the period of time that the business believes it will use the asset to help generate revenue" [V] (Ch 4.4, "What Is Depreciation?"). Three depreciation methods are catalogued (Ch 4.4):

- **Straight-line depreciation** divides the depreciable base (cost less salvage value) evenly over the asset's useful life.
- **Units-of-production depreciation** allocates cost based on actual usage; appropriate when life is a function of usage rather than time.
- **Double-declining-balance depreciation** doubles the straight-line percentage and applies it to declining book value; takes more expense in early years; never depreciates below salvage.

The **expense recognition principle** (matching) requires that "expenses should be recorded in the same period with the revenues that the expense helped create" [V] (Ch 4.4). Total depreciation over the asset's life is the same across methods; only the timing differs. Estimating useful life and salvage value involves uncertainty; the SEC's 2002 action against Waste Management is given as an example of fraudulent estimation manipulation (Ch 4.4, "Estimating Useful Life and Salvage Value").

### Profit, loss, and the cash-flow distinction

Net income equals revenues minus expenses, plus gains, minus losses. Gains and losses involve items ancillary to the primary business purpose (Ch 4.5, "Calculating Profit and Loss"). The text emphasises that profit and cash flow are distinct: "stakeholders need to know the financial performance . . . and financial position . . . of the business . . . However, since these financial statements are prepared using accrual accounting, stakeholders do not have a clear picture of the business's cash activities. The statement of cash flows solves this inadequacy by specifically focusing on the cash inflows and cash outflows" [V] (Ch 4.5, "Profit versus Cash Flow"). The text takes a position that "high-quality earnings are based on sustainable earnings, also called permanent earnings, while relying less on infrequent earnings, also called temporary earnings" [V] (Ch 4.5).

## Part V: The four financial statements (Ch 5)

### The income statement

The **income statement** "shows a firm's performance over a specific period of time" [V] (Ch 5.1, "Functionality of the Income Statement"). Standard structure: gross sales less sales returns and allowances yields net sales; net sales less cost of goods sold (COGS) yields gross profit; gross profit less operating expenses yields operating income (income from operations); operating income less interest, gains/losses, and taxes yields net income.

**EBITDA** (earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization) "removes the effects of noncash items from the net income equation" and is "considered a useful measure in assessing the cash flows provided by operating activities" [V] (Ch 5.1, "EBITDA"). Amortization is "the spreading of the cost of an intangible asset over the course of its useful life" (Ch 5.1).

### The balance sheet

The classified balance sheet shows financial state at a specific point in time, in three sections: assets, liabilities, and equity (Ch 5.2, "The Accounting Equation and the Classified Balance Sheet"). Both assets and liabilities are split into current (one year or less) and noncurrent (longer than one year). The text catalogues four limitations of the balance sheet (Ch 5.2, "Limitations of the Balance Sheet"):

1. Assets are recorded at historical cost; market value of land and buildings is not reflected.
2. Estimates (allowance for doubtful accounts, useful lives) are inherently inexact.
3. The balance sheet reflects balances at one point in time; account values can change radically the day before or after.
4. Internally generated assets and human capital are not on the balance sheet.

### The relationship between income statement and balance sheet

Net income flows into retained earnings on the balance sheet; the income statement is prepared first because net income is a required figure on the balance sheet (Ch 5.3, "Net Income and Retained Earnings"). Retained earnings reflects all income earned since inception less all dividends paid out. Expenses and accounts payable are distinct: expenses are income statement events; payables are balance sheet liabilities; the two are connected through double-entry accounting but not equivalent (Ch 5.3, "Expenses versus Payables").

### The statement of owner's equity

The **statement of owner's equity** breaks down equity into contributed capital (paid-in by owners — common stock, preferred stock, additional paid-in capital, treasury stock) and earned capital (retained earnings) (Ch 5.4, "What Is Equity?"). Distributions to owners (dividends) can be cash dividends (requires cash on hand and sufficient retained earnings), stock dividends (additional shares to existing shareholders), or property dividends (other assets). Regardless of type, dividends always reduce retained earnings. Treasury shares are "not outstanding, so no dividends are declared or distributed for these shares" [V] (Ch 5.4, "Distributions to Owners").

### The statement of cash flows

The statement of cash flows reconciles net income to actual cash flows. Two methods of preparation: the **direct method** (lists cash flows directly from revenues and expenses, favoured by FASB) and the **indirect method** (reconciles net income to cash flow, the most common method in practice) [AP] (Ch 5.5, "Importance of the Statement of Cash Flows"). The statement is broken into three sections (Ch 5.5):

- **Operating activities**: cash from day-to-day operations; under the indirect method, starts with net income and adds back noncash expenses (depreciation) and adjusts for changes in working-capital accounts.
- **Investing activities**: investments in other firms or in the firm itself (machinery, land, fixed assets).
- **Financing activities**: funds used to run the business (loans, interest, dividends).

External users compare earnings to cash flow to assess earnings quality; "a firm reporting a strong profit but very little cash flow might raise some questions as to what was recorded to drive profits that isn't also driving cash flows" [V] (Ch 5.5).

### Operating cash flow and free cash flow

**Free cash flow (FCF)** is operating cash flow less capital expenditures; it represents cash available to repay debts, pay dividends, or fund growth (Ch 5.6, "Free Cash Flow"). The text takes a managerial position: cash management is shaped by customer payment terms, collection policies, and vendor payment terms — and changing terms changes timing of cash flows even if it does not change profit (Ch 5.6, "Managing Cash Flow").

### Common-size statements

Common-size income statements express each line as a percentage of net sales; common-size balance sheets express each line as a percentage of total assets [AP] (Ch 5.7, "Common-Size Income Statements"; "Common-Size Balance Sheet"). This allows comparison across firms of different sizes and within a firm across time. The chapter compares Clear Lake Sporting Goods (a small retailer) with Charlie's Camping World (a much larger competitor) on common-size measures, finding differences in COGS percentage, rent percentage, and salaries percentage that hint at different operating models (Ch 5.7, "Industry Comparison").

### Reporting financial activity

GAAP requires companies to provide quarterly financial statements; most firms prepare statements monthly even if not required. The **time period principle** holds that financial information must be timely to be useful (Ch 5.8, "Defining Accounting Periods"). Firms can use a calendar year (January 1 to December 31) or a fiscal year aligned with their business cycle. Public companies must file annual 10-K, quarterly 10-Q, and event-driven 8-K filings with the SEC (Ch 5.8, "SEC Reporting Requirements").

## Part VI: Financial-health ratios (Ch 6)

### Four families of ratios

Four families: liquidity, solvency, efficiency, and profitability (Ch 6.1, "Importance of Ratios and Analysis"). Ratios are most useful in three comparisons: against industry/peer averages, against the firm's history (trend analysis), and across firms of different sizes.

### Operating efficiency ratios

The **operating cycle** is the time between purchase/production of inventory and sale; the **cash conversion cycle** extends this to the receipt of cash from customers [AP] (Ch 6.2, "Operating Cycle"; "Cash Conversion Cycle").

Four efficiency ratios are catalogued (Ch 6.2):

- **Accounts receivable turnover**: net credit sales divided by average accounts receivable. Higher turnover indicates faster collection but may signal credit terms too tight; lower turnover may signal lenient terms or bad debt.
- **Total asset turnover**: net sales divided by average total assets. Higher is better — more sales per dollar of assets.
- **Inventory turnover**: cost of goods sold divided by average inventory. Higher is generally preferable; extremely high may indicate stock-out risk.
- **Days' sales in inventory**: number of days to convert inventory to sales (365 divided by inventory turnover).

### Liquidity ratios

**Liquidity** is "the business's ability to manage current assets or convert assets into cash in order to meet short-term cash needs" [V] (Ch 6.3). Three liquidity ratios in increasing conservatism (Ch 6.3):

- **Current ratio**: current assets / current liabilities. A ratio of greater than 1 indicates the firm can meet short-term obligations.
- **Quick ratio (acid-test ratio)**: like the current ratio but excludes inventory and prepaid expenses, both of which take time to convert to cash.
- **Cash ratio**: cash and cash equivalents / current liabilities. The most conservative; a cash ratio of 1.0 means the firm could pay all current liabilities immediately if needed.

### Solvency ratios

**Solvency** is the ability to meet long-term obligations (Ch 6.4). Two solvency ratios:

- **Debt-to-assets**: total liabilities / total assets. A ratio greater than 1 means negative equity.
- **Debt-to-equity**: total liabilities / total equity.
- **Times interest earned (TIE)**: EBIT / interest expense. Indicates how many times over the firm can cover its interest expense from operating income.

### Market-value ratios

**Earnings per share (EPS)** is "the portion of a corporation's profit allocated to each outstanding share of common stock" — calculated as `(Net Income - Preferred Dividends) / Weighted Average Common Shares Outstanding` [AP] (Ch 6.5, "Earnings per Share"). Public companies are required by GAAP to report both basic and diluted EPS on the income statement; "this is the only ratio that requires such prominent reporting" [V] (Ch 6.5, "Measuring Performance with Earnings per Share").

The **price/earnings (P/E) ratio** is market price per share divided by EPS; investors use it to compare valuations across firms or to industry averages, and to express how much they pay for each dollar of earnings (Ch 6.5, "Price/Earnings Ratio"). **Book value per share** is `(Total Equity - Preferred Equity) / Common Shares Outstanding` and represents the per-share liquidation value if the firm were wound up at book values; comparison to market value indicates whether the stock is over- or undervalued (Ch 6.5, "Book Value per Share").

### Profitability ratios and the DuPont method

Three profitability ratios (Ch 6.6):

- **Profit margin**: net income / net sales. How much of each sales dollar returns as profit.
- **Return on total assets (ROA)**: net income / average total assets.
- **Return on equity (ROE)**: (net income - preferred dividends) / average stockholders' equity.

The **DuPont method**, developed at DuPont in 1919 for internal measurement, decomposes ROE into three components: profit margin (operating efficiency) × total asset turnover (asset usage) × equity multiplier (leverage) [BT] (Ch 6.6, "The DuPont Method"). The decomposition lets analysts attribute changes in ROE to operations, asset use, or financing structure. Industry comparisons (Table 6.1) show ROE varying from -47.03 per cent (air transport, 2020) to 70.64 per cent (hospitals/healthcare facilities, 2020).

## Part VII: Time value of money I — single payments (Ch 7)

### TVM rationale

The text gives three reasons a dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow: "(1) money received now can be saved or invested now and earn interest or a return, resulting in more money in the future; (2) any promise of future payments of cash will always carry the risk of default; and (3) it is simple human nature for people to prefer making their purchases of goods and services in the present rather than waiting to make them at some future time" [V] (Ch 7, "Why It Matters").

### Future value, present value, and the four solution methods

The future-value formula for a single sum is `FV = PV × (1 + r)^n`, where `(1 + r)^n` is the future-value interest factor (FVIF) [AP] (Ch 7.2, "The Impact of Compounding"). Present value reverses this: `PV = FV / (1 + r)^n` (Ch 7.4, "Single-Period Scenario"). The text frames four solution methods: mathematical equations, financial calculators, spreadsheets, and FVIF tables; tables are characterised as obsolete given calculators and spreadsheets (Ch 7.3).

For financial calculators, five keys cover most TVM problems: N (periods), I/Y (interest rate per period), PV (present value), FV (future value), PMT (payment) — only the first four for single sums [AP] (Ch 7.3, "Using a Financial Calculator to Solve TVM Problems"). The **cash flow sign convention** treats inflows as positive and outflows as negative; both PV and FV cannot be the same sign or the calculator returns an error (Ch 7.3, "Important Notes for Using a Calculator").

In Excel, the four core TVM functions are `FV(rate, nper, pmt, [pv], [type])`, `PV(rate, nper, pmt, [fv], [type])`, `NPER(rate, pmt, pv, [fv], [type])`, and `RATE(nper, pmt, pv, [fv], [type], [guess])` (Ch 7.3, "Using Excel to Solve TVM Problems"). Cell references are preferable to hard-coded numbers because they preserve flexibility for what-if analysis.

### Inflation, real rates, and the Fisher effect

The **Fisher effect**, named for Irving Fisher, expresses the relationship between nominal and real interest rates: `(1 + i) = (1 + R)(1 + h)`, where `i` is the nominal rate, `R` is the real rate, and `h` is the expected inflation rate [BT] (Ch 7.4, "Nominal versus Real Interest Rates"). For positive inflation, the nominal rate exceeds the real rate. Negative real interest rates (as occur when nominal rates are very low and inflation is positive) cause holders of cash to lose purchasing power.

### How compounding frequency affects TVM

"The more frequent the compounding period, the greater the future value of a savings amount, a bond, or any other financial instrument" [V] (Ch 7.4, "Compounding Interest"), all else equal. Sub-annual compounding is handled by dividing the nominal annual rate by the number of compounding periods per year and multiplying the number of years by the same number of periods — the standard adjustment threaded through Ch 8 amortization and Ch 10 bond pricing.

## Part VIII: Time value of money II — equal multiple payments (Ch 8)

### Perpetuities

A **perpetuity** is "a series of payments or receipts that continues forever, or perpetually" [V] (Ch 8.1, "What Is a Perpetuity?"). Examples in the text: college chair endowments funding perpetual professorships; preferred stock with fixed dividends. The constant perpetuity formula is `PV = C / Rs`, where `C` is the constant cash flow and `Rs` is the required rate of return (Ch 8.1, "How to Value a Perpetuity"). The growing perpetuity formula adds a growth term: `PV = C / (Rs - G)`. Future value of a perpetuity is undefined; only present value is computable because of the discounting effect of distant cash flows.

### Annuities

An **annuity** is "a stream of fixed periodic payments to be paid or received in the future" [V] (Ch 8.2, "Calculating the Present Value of an Annuity"). An **ordinary annuity** has the first payment one period after time zero; an **annuity due** has the first payment immediately. The present value of an ordinary annuity formula is `PVa = PYMT × [(1 - (1+r)^-n) / r]`. The future value of an ordinary annuity formula is `FVa = PYMT × [((1+r)^n - 1) / r]`.

The text takes a position: whether calculating present or future value, "the result of an annuity due must always be larger than that of an ordinary annuity, all other facts remaining constant" [AP] (Ch 8.2, "Calculating the Present Value of an Annuity Due"). The annuity-due adjustment is to multiply the ordinary-annuity result by `(1 + r)`.

A **lump sum** in this chapter refers to a one-off cash flow that contrasts with an annuity. Lottery winnings are a recurring example: comparing a one-time lump sum to a stream of annual payments requires discounting the future stream to present value before comparison (Ch 8.2). Structured legal settlements use the same methodology (Ch 8.2, "Calculating Annuities Used in Structured Settlements").

Retirement planning uses the present-value annuity formula in reverse to solve for the payment that a fund of given size, return, and duration can support (Ch 8.2, "How Annuities Are Used for Retirement Planning").

### Loan amortisation

**Loan amortization** is "a schedule of how and when a debt will be repaid with interest" [V] (Ch 8.3, "Calculating Loan Payments Using Simple Amortization"). For a fixed-rate loan, the present-value-of-an-annuity formula is solved for PYMT given PV (loan amount), r (period rate), and n (number of periods). The amortisation table allocates each fixed payment between interest (the period rate times the remaining balance) and principal (the remainder); over the loan's life, interest's share of each payment falls and principal's share rises. The text catalogues common business and personal loans: term loans (matched to asset useful life, often collateralised), revolvers (no fixed schedule, secured by AR/inventory or unsecured), personal lines of credit, secured and unsecured personal loans, mortgages (secured by the home), and student loans.

### Stated versus effective rates

The **effective interest rate** "reflects compounding within a one-year period" [V] (Ch 8.4, "The Difference between Stated and Effective Rates") and exceeds the stated rate when sub-annual compounding occurs. The formula is `Effective = (1 + stated/m)^m - 1`, where `m` is the number of compounding periods per year. The text's example: 1.5 per cent monthly compounding yields a stated annual rate of 18 per cent but an effective rate of 19.56 per cent.

Payday advance loans (PALs) and refund anticipation loans (RALs) are presented as cautionary cases of usurious effective rates that look modest expressed as fees: a $14 fee on a $200 one-week loan is 7 per cent of the principal but annualises to 364 per cent (Ch 8.4, "How Tempting Is That Refund Anticipation?").

## Part IX: Time value of money III — unequal mixed streams (Ch 9)

A **mixed stream** is a series of cash flows that varies in amount from one period to the next (Ch 9.1, "Multiple Payments or Receipts of Unequal Value"). When payments are not constant, the annuity shortcut cannot be used; each cash flow must be discounted (or compounded) individually and the results summed. The TI BA II Plus Professional and Excel both provide cash-flow-stream functions: Excel's NPV is the standard tool for present value of a mixed stream.

A subtlety the text emphasises: Excel's NPV function assumes the first cash flow occurs one period in the future, so the time-zero outflow must be subtracted separately from the function's result to compute the conventional NPV. This becomes load-bearing in capital budgeting (Ch 16.6).

The chapter includes an interview with Irina Simmons (former chief risk officer and treasurer of EMC Corporation) emphasising cash-flow management over profitability: "while profitability is very useful for analysis by investors to measure performance, an organization's cash flow provides superior measurement. Cash flow is easy to understand, provides a transparent way of assessing a firm's health, and is not subject to any qualifications" [V] (Ch 9.1, "Thoughts on Cash Flow from Irina Simmons"). The author and Simmons agree that "cash flow is the axis upon which the world of business spins."

## Part X: Bonds and bond valuation (Ch 10)

### Bond basics

When an investor purchases a bond, they make a loan to the bond issuer; the issuer pays back the original face value at maturity and pays periodic coupon interest in the meantime. Five basic terms anchor bond mathematics (Ch 10.1, "Basic Terminology"):

- **Par value (face value, face amount)**: the principal repaid at maturity; typically $1,000 for corporate bonds.
- **Coupon rate**: the annual interest rate; expressed in annual terms even if coupons are paid semiannually or quarterly.
- **Coupon payment**: par value × coupon rate; if coupons are paid semiannually, the per-period payment is the annual amount divided by 2.
- **Maturity date**: the bond's expiration; final coupon and face value are paid.
- **Yield to maturity (YTM)**: "the discount rate used to bring the future cash flows of a bond into present value terms . . . the return that the investor will receive if the bond is held to maturity" [V] (Ch 10.1).

Three primary categories of bonds (Ch 10.1, "Types of Bonds"):

- **Government bonds**: T-bills (short-term, default-risk-free), savings bonds (consumer-targeted), Treasury notes (2-10 years), Treasury bonds (20-30 years), TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities, with principal indexed to CPI).
- **Municipal bonds**: state and local government debt; interest is exempt from federal taxes and sometimes state and local; rated like corporate bonds.
- **Corporate bonds**: issued by companies; carry credit risk. **Junk bonds** are non-investment-grade corporate bonds; **zero-coupon bonds** make no periodic interest payments and are issued at deep discounts; **convertible bonds** can be exchanged for a predetermined number of common shares.

### Bond price-yield inverse relationship

"Bond price and interest rate have an inverse relationship. When interest rates fall, bond prices rise, and vice versa" [V] (Ch 10.1, "The Relationship between Bond Prices and Interest Rates"). **Floating-rate bonds** (FRNs) are an exception: their coupons reset to a benchmark rate (US Treasury note rate, federal funds rate, LIBOR, prime rate) and hold price approximately constant. **Deep discount bonds** have very low coupon rates and large discounts; the smaller the coupon rate, the greater the price change when rates move.

### Pricing a bond

A coupon bond's price is the sum of two present values: the present value of the annuity of coupon payments (using the present-value-of-annuity formula) plus the present value of the face value paid at maturity (using the present-value-of-single-sum formula) [AP] (Ch 10.2, "Pricing a Bond in Steps"). Both are discounted at the YTM. For semiannual coupons, the YTM is divided by 2 and the number of periods is doubled — the standard sub-annual adjustment.

If the coupon rate exceeds the YTM, the bond sells at a **premium** (above par); if below, at a **discount**; if equal, at par (Ch 10.2). Bonds quote prices as percentages of par; a price of 105.12 means $1,051.20 per $1,000 face value.

### The yield curve and term structure

The **yield curve** plots yield against maturity for bonds of equal credit quality; the curve for government bonds is the **risk-free yield curve** [AP] (Ch 10.3, "Term Structure of Interest Rates"). A **normal yield curve** slopes upward (longer-term yields exceed shorter); economic expansion typically produces normal curves and signals investor expectation of higher future inflation. An **inverted yield curve** slopes downward and has historically preceded recessions; the August 2018-2020 inversion preceded the COVID recession (Ch 10.3, "Different Shapes of the Yield Curve"). A **flat yield curve** indicates transition between normal and inverted, or low absolute rates.

### Bond risks and ratings

The text catalogues seven bond risks (Ch 10.4, "Bond Risks"):

- **Interest rate risk** (market risk): rising rates reduce existing bond prices.
- **Default risk**: issuer fails to pay; quantified by **bond ratings**.
- **Credit risk**: investors believe issuer is less likely to meet payment commitments and demand higher yield.
- **Liquidity risk**: difficulty selling the bond.
- **Duration risk**: sensitivity of price to a 1 per cent change in interest rates.
- **Call risk**: issuer redeems the bond before maturity, often when rates fall (forcing investors to reinvest at lower rates).
- **Reinvestment risk**: investors face unfavourable alternatives for reinvesting cash flows received.
- **Term risk**: lending at fixed rates over long horizons exposes investors to rate rises; the term premium compensates.

Three rating agencies dominate: Fitch, Moody's, and Standard & Poor's. Investment-grade bonds are BBB/Baa or higher; below BBB are speculative ("junk") bonds (Ch 10.4, "Bond Ratings and Rating Providers"; Table 10.11). Many institutional investors (pension funds, commercial banks) are constrained to investment-grade. Bond ratings are judgments, not guarantees: Enron's bonds were investment grade only two months before the December 2001 bankruptcy (Ch 10.4, "The Collapse of Enron"). The **bond ladder** strategy holds bonds with staggered maturities to balance return and liquidity while diversifying duration risk (Ch 10.4, "Bond Laddering as an Investment Strategy").

## Part XI: Stocks and stock valuation (Ch 11)

### Why stocks differ from bonds

"With common stock, there is no specific promise of how much cash investors will receive or when they will receive it. This differs from bond investments, which are valued entirely on the basis of their guaranteed timing of future cash flows to bondholders" [V] (Ch 11, "Why It Matters"). Stocks have no maturity dates, face values, or coupon-payment guarantees; some pay dividends, but dividends are declared at the discretion of the board and are not contractual. Three reasons investors purchase common stock (Ch 11):

- To use stocks as instruments for maintaining value.
- To accumulate wealth over the term of the stock investment.
- To earn income through capital gains and dividend payments.

### Valuation by ratio multiples

The **price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio** is `Market Price per Share / EPS`; it is "extremely useful to analysts in that it shows the expectations of the market" [V] (Ch 11.1, "The Price-to-Earnings Ratio"). The **price-to-book (P/B) ratio** is `Market Price per Share / Book Value per Share`; the **price-to-sales (P/S) ratio**, **price-to-cash-flow (P/CF) ratio**, and **dividend yield** are also covered (Ch 11.1).

### Dividend discount models (DDMs)

The **dividend discount model** values a stock as the present value of all future dividends [AP] (Ch 11.2, "DDMs"). Four variants are presented:

- **Zero growth DDM**: dividend amount is constant forever; price = D / r. Mathematically the same as the perpetuity formula.
- **Gordon (constant) growth model**: dividend grows at a fixed rate g forever; price = D₁ / (r - g), where D₁ is next year's dividend [BT] (Ch 11.2, "The Gordon Growth Model"). Requires r > g.
- **Variable (nonconstant) growth DDM**: extends the constant-growth formula to multiple growth phases, calculating present value separately for each.
- **Two-stage DDM**: assumes initial higher growth followed by sustainable lower growth; applicable to mature companies with established dividend records (Ch 11.2, "Two-Stage Dividend Discount Model").

The text takes a position that DDMs have flaws: they cannot be used for non-dividend-paying companies (Amazon, Google, Biogen as examples); the Gordon model is highly sensitive to assumptions about g and r; results may not match operational reality if companies maintain dividends through borrowing rather than from earnings (Ch 11.2, "Limitations of DDMs"). Warren Buffett's view is given: "the payment of dividends to shareholders is 'almost a last resort for corporate management'" [V] (Ch 11.2).

### Discounted cash flow (DCF) model

The **DCF model** values a firm based on the present value of its future cash flows rather than dividends; it is used for non-dividend-paying firms and "is the evaluation tool of choice for experienced financial analysts when evaluating companies and their common stock" [AP] (Ch 11.3, "Understanding How the DCF Model Differs from DDMs"). The DCF formula sums the discounted cash flows over the explicit forecast horizon plus a terminal value (often a multiple of the final period's discounted cash flow). Per-share value follows from dividing total firm value by shares outstanding. The text takes a position: "many analysts prefer DCF methods of valuation because these are based on a company's cash flows, which are far less easily manipulated through accounting treatments than revenues or bottom-line earnings" [V] (Ch 11.3).

### Preferred stock valuation

Preferred stock has a fixed dividend, no maturity, and no voting rights; it stands ahead of common stock in claims but behind bondholders. Preferred stock "may be considered a form of perpetuity" and valued with the constant-dividend perpetuity formula: `Price = D / r` (Ch 11.4, "Determining the Intrinsic Value of Preferred Stock"). Preferred dividends are after-tax payments by the corporation (unlike bond interest, which is tax-deductible), making preferred stock a more expensive financing option than debt for issuing firms.

### Efficient markets

**Efficient markets** are markets in which costs are minimal and prices are current and fair to all traders [AP] (Ch 11.5, "Efficient Markets"). Two forms of efficiency:

- **Operational efficiency**: speed and accuracy of order processing at the best available price.
- **Informational efficiency**: how quickly prices reflect comprehensive information.

Three forms of market efficiency from the **efficient market hypothesis (EMH)** (Ch 11.5, "Forms of Market Efficiency"):

- **Weak form**: current prices reflect price history and trading volume; technical analysis cannot consistently beat the market.
- **Semi-strong form**: prices reflect all publicly available information; fundamental analysis cannot consistently beat the market.
- **Strong form**: prices reflect all information including non-public; even insider information cannot beat the market.

## Part XII: Historical performance of US markets (Ch 12)

### Money market and bond market structure

Ch 12.1 catalogues money market instruments by maturity, minimum amount, and issuer (T-bills, CP, NCDs, MMMFs, federal funds), then bond markets (Treasury notes, T-bonds, TIPS, munis, corporate bonds), then equity markets [AP]. The **Federal Reserve funds rate** has changed character since 2008: the Fed now pays interest on reserves, reducing banks' need to enter the federal funds market.

### Equity-market structure and the IPO process

US firms had a combined market capitalisation of $50.8 trillion at the end of 2020, about 66 per cent of the world total estimated at $90 trillion (Ch 12.1, "Equity Markets"). Stock exchanges have centralised trading locations, broker-mediated trades, and exchange-guaranteed liquidity; OTC markets are electronic with direct buyer-seller transactions and less liquidity guarantee.

An **initial public offering (IPO)** is a firm's first public stock sale, undertaken with an investment banker who advises on price, share count, and underwriter syndication. IPOs cost 5-8 per cent of the offering value; smaller investors rarely participate at issue. Burton Malkiel's view that IPOs underperform the market by 4 per cent per year on average is given as a contrarian counter [BT] (Ch 12.1, "Process of Offering Equities"). A **seasoned equity offering (SEO)** is an additional issue by an already-public firm.

**Special purpose acquisition companies (SPACs)** are "blank check" companies: an IPO without an operating business, raising capital first, then seeking a target. SPACs shift IPO risk and expense; the 2015-2020 study cited reports SPAC mergers averaged a -18.8 per cent return versus a +37.2 per cent average for traditional IPOs (Ch 12.1, "Alternative Methods of Raising Capital"). The text suggests SPACs benefit private firm owners (better negotiated price) but harm public-market investors who hold the SPAC.

### Inflation, bond returns, and stock returns

Inflation redistributes wealth: lenders making fixed-rate loans receive less-valuable dollars; borrowers repay with the same; consumers on fixed income suffer. Modest inflation can benefit a consumer-driven economy by motivating spending; high inflation harms it (Ch 12.2, "Inflation Impacts").

Average annual returns by asset class for 1981-2020 (from Aswath Damodaran data):

| Asset class | Nominal average annual return | Standard deviation of returns |
|---|---|---|
| Large company stocks | 12.64% | 16.06% |
| Baa bonds | 10.34% | 7.67% |
| 10-year T-bonds | 8.21% | 9.92% |
| US T-bills | 3.94% | 3.39% |
| Inflation | 2.93% | 1.76% |

(Ch 12.4, Table 12.4)

A $100 invested in 1928 in large-company stocks grew to over 50 times the value of the same $100 invested in long-term US government bonds (Ch 12.4). This supports asset allocation as more important than security selection. Small-cap stocks historically outperformed large-cap, but the differential has narrowed since 1980 (12.13 per cent vs 11.82 per cent compounded annual return for Wilshire small-cap vs large-cap, 1980-2020).

### Profiles cited

Three profile sidebars are presented in the chapter: Janet Yellen (career path through Federal Reserve, first woman Fed chair, first woman Treasury Secretary), Bill Gross (founder of Pimco, "the bond king," focus on total returns rather than yield), and Warren Buffett (career, investment philosophy, philanthropy). All three are framed as illustrating long-term investing discipline (Ch 12.2, 12.3, 12.4).

## Part XIII: Statistical analysis in finance (Ch 13)

### Measures of centre

**Arithmetic mean** is the most common measure of average; sum of values divided by count [AP] (Ch 13.1, "Arithmetic Mean"). The **median** is the middle value of an ordered data set; preferred when outliers are present because it is insensitive to extreme values (Ch 13.1, "Median"). The **mode** is the most frequent value (Ch 13.1, "Mode"). The **geometric mean** "redistributes not the sum of the values but their product"; it is the nth root of the product of n values; used for compound growth rates such as multi-period returns [AP] (Ch 13.1, "Geometric Mean"). The **weighted mean** assigns different weights to different values; common application is computing the average purchase price of a stock acquired at different prices in different quantities (Ch 13.1, "Weighted Mean").

### Measures of spread

The **standard deviation** measures how far data values are from the mean; finance uses it as a primary measure of volatility and therefore risk [AP] (Ch 13.2, "Standard Deviation"). Sample and population formulas differ: the sample uses n-1 in the denominator. **Variance** is the square of the standard deviation; the standard deviation is in the same units as the underlying data, making it more interpretable.

### Measures of position

A **z-score (z-value)** is `(x - μ) / σ`; it expresses the number of standard deviations a value is from the mean (Ch 13.3, "z-Scores"). **Quartiles** divide ordered data into quarters; the first, second (median), and third quartiles correspond to the 25th, 50th, and 75th percentiles. The **interquartile range (IQR)** is Q3 - Q1; the IQR is used to flag potential outliers — values below Q1 - 1.5×IQR or above Q3 + 1.5×IQR are typically flagged (Ch 13.3, "Outlier Detection").

### Distributions and expected value

The **normal distribution** is the foundational continuous distribution in finance; it is symmetric about the mean, with mean = median = mode. Its parameters are the mean μ and the standard deviation σ. Excel's `NORM.DIST(x, μ, σ, TRUE)` returns the area under the curve to the left of x — i.e., the probability that a value falls below x (Ch 13.4, "Normal Distribution"). The **exponential distribution** is one with fewer large values and more small values; common in modelling time-to-event problems such as time-to-default, customer-spending distribution, or product-reliability lifetimes (Ch 13.4, "Exponential Distribution").

A **probability distribution** assigns probabilities to outcomes; **expected value** of a probability distribution is the weighted mean where probabilities are the weights: `E[R] = Σ Pᵢ × Rᵢ` [AP] (Ch 13.5, "Calculate and Interpret Expected Values"). In portfolio analysis, weights by asset category (stocks, bonds, cash equivalents) are determined by dividing each category's value by total portfolio value; portfolio rebalancing brings actual weights to target weights (Ch 13.5, "Calculate Portfolio Weights").

## Part XIV: Regression analysis in finance (Ch 14)

### Correlation

**Correlation** is the measure of association between two numeric variables (Ch 14.1, "Calculate a Correlation Coefficient"). The **correlation coefficient (r)**, developed by Karl Pearson, is the strength and direction of linear correlation. Its value lies between -1 and +1; +1 is perfect positive correlation, -1 is perfect negative correlation, 0 is no linear correlation (Ch 14.1, "Interpret a Correlation Coefficient"). The text takes a position: "correlation does not imply causation" [V] (Ch 14.1).

The text gives a quick rule for testing significance: if r exceeds `2/√n`, the correlation between the two variables is statistically significant at approximately the 0.05 level; with 10 observations the threshold is 0.6325, with 30 observations it is 0.3651, with 100 observations it is 0.2000 (Ch 14.1, "Test a Correlation Coefficient for Significance").

### Linear regression

**Linear regression** uses the **method of least squares** to find a best-fit line that minimises the sum of squared residuals (the differences between observed y-values and predicted ŷ-values) [AP] (Ch 14.2, "Method of Least Squares and Residuals"). Five assumptions underlie the test of significance for linear regression (Ch 14.2, "Assumptions for Linear Regression"):

1. There is a linear relationship in the population.
2. The y-values for any particular x-value are normally distributed about the line.
3. The standard deviations of population y-values are equal across x-values.
4. The residual errors are mutually independent.
5. The data are produced from a well-designed random sample or randomised experiment.

### Applications in finance

Regression analysis underpins the calculation of a stock's **beta**, regressing stock returns on market returns. Beta is the slope of the regression line and measures systematic risk. Regression also produces **prediction intervals** (Ch 14.5) — interval estimates of where a future observation is likely to fall. Excel's SLOPE and INTERCEPT functions, or the chart trendline tool, perform the regression directly. The **R statistical software** is named as an open alternative beyond Excel (Ch 14.6).

## Part XV: How to think about investing — risk, return, CAPM (Ch 15)

### Realised return and average return

The **realised return** of an investment over a holding period is `(Final Value + Dividends - Initial Value) / Initial Value` [AP] (Ch 15.1, "Individual Investment Realized Return"). The **dividend yield** is dividends received divided by the initial price; the **capital gain yield** is the change in price divided by the initial price; total percent return is their sum. The **effective annual rate (EAR)** annualises a holding-period return: `EAR = (1 + holding-period return)^(periods per year) - 1`.

The **arithmetic average return** is the mean of single-period returns; the **geometric average return** is the compounded annual return: `((1+R₁)(1+R₂)...(1+Rₙ))^(1/n) - 1`. The geometric average is always less than or equal to the arithmetic average; the two are equal only if all returns are identical [AP] (Ch 15.1, "Average Annual Returns"). The text takes a position: the geometric average tells you what you actually earned per year on average compounded; the arithmetic average tells you what you earned in a typical year. "When we are looking at the historical description of the distribution of returns and want to predict what to expect in a particular year, the arithmetic average is the relevant calculation" [V] (Ch 15.1).

### Volatility, firm-specific risk, and diversification

Volatility of returns is measured by standard deviation; in finance, this is risk [AP] (Ch 15.1, "Volatility of Returns"). Risk has two components (Ch 15.2):

- **Firm-specific risk (unsystematic, diversifiable risk)**: events affecting one company's revenue or costs (a product liability lawsuit, a new product, accounting irregularities). Diversification eliminates this risk.
- **Systematic risk (market risk)**: events affecting the overall market (Federal Reserve actions, geopolitical events, pandemics). Diversification does not eliminate this risk.

A diversified portfolio's standard deviation falls quickly with the first few stocks added but plateaus; per Meir Statman's research, a reasonably diversified portfolio can be created with about a dozen stocks [BT] (Ch 15.2, "Portfolio Size and Risk"). The benefit of diversification is greatest when stocks have low correlation; the 2011-2020 example shows DAL (Delta) and LUV (Southwest) at 0.87 correlation — both airlines, much diversification benefit lost — versus DAL and XOM (ExxonMobil) at 0.35.

### Risk-free rate, risk premium, and beta

The **risk-free rate** is the return on a default-risk-free investment, typically proxied by short-term US Treasury securities; the average three-month T-bill return for 1928-2020 was 3.36 per cent [V] (Ch 15.3, "Risk-Free Rate"). The **risk premium** is the extra return earned for taking on risk; the average S&P 500 return for 1928-2020 was 11.64 per cent, implying a market risk premium of 8.28 per cent [V] (Ch 15.3, "Risk Premium").

**Beta** is the measure of how much a stock moves with the market — its systematic risk [V] (Ch 15.3, "Beta"). A beta of 1 means the stock moves in step with the market; greater than 1 means more pronounced moves than the market; less than 1 means smaller moves. Beta is calculated as the slope of the regression line of stock returns on market returns.

### The CAPM equation

The **capital asset pricing model (CAPM)** is the asset-pricing model based on the idea that investors are rewarded for systematic risk only; firm-specific risk can be diversified away [AR] (Ch 15.3, "The Capital Asset Pricing Model"). The CAPM equation is:

`Re = Rf + β × (Rm - Rf)`

where Re is the expected return of the asset, Rf is the risk-free rate, β is the asset's beta, and (Rm - Rf) is the market risk premium.

### Performance measurement

Three measures of investment performance are catalogued (Ch 15.4):

- **Sharpe ratio**: `(Rp - Rf) / σp`; portfolio risk premium divided by portfolio standard deviation. A reward-to-risk measure of total risk.
- **Treynor ratio**: `(Rp - Rf) / βp`; portfolio risk premium divided by portfolio beta. A reward-to-risk measure of systematic risk.
- **Jensen's alpha**: portfolio's actual return minus the CAPM-predicted return. A positive alpha indicates abnormal positive performance.

Sharpe and Treynor are relative measures; alpha is an absolute measure of abnormal return.

## Part XVI: How companies think about investing — capital budgeting (Ch 16)

### Payback period

The **payback period** is the time it takes for a company to make enough free cash flow from a project to recover the initial cost [AP] (Ch 16.1, "Payback Period Calculation"). Its principal advantage is simplicity. Its disadvantages: it ignores the time value of money, has no theoretically grounded acceptance criterion (the chosen target is arbitrary), ignores cash flows after the breakeven point, and makes no risk adjustment.

### Net present value (NPV)

**Net present value (NPV)** is "the difference between the present value of the cash inflows and the present value of cash outflows" [V] (Ch 16.2, "NPV Calculation"). The decision criterion is grounded in financial theory: accept projects with positive NPV (which add value to the firm) and reject those with negative NPV. NPV uses the firm's cost of capital as the discount rate; the discount rate can be increased to adjust for project-specific risk.

The **NPV profile** plots NPV against discount rate. Where the profile crosses the horizontal axis (NPV = 0), the discount rate equals the project's IRR; this also bounds the firm's allowable cost of capital (Ch 16.2, "NPV Profile").

The text takes positions: NPV is "the criterion for whether or not a project is a good project" but "does not always provide a good solution when a company must make a choice between several acceptable projects because funds are not available to pursue them all" [V] (Ch 16.2, "Disadvantages").

### Internal rate of return (IRR)

The **internal rate of return (IRR)** is the discount rate at which NPV equals zero [V] (Ch 16.3, "IRR Calculation"). The decision rule: accept projects whose IRR exceeds the cost of capital; reject those whose IRR is below. The text catalogues three disadvantages (Ch 16.3, "Disadvantages"):

- **No single mathematical solution** when negative cash flows occur in more than one period — multiple IRRs may exist.
- **Reinvestment rate assumption**: IRR assumes intermediate cash flows are reinvested at the IRR itself, which may not be feasible if the IRR is high.
- **Overlooking differences in scale**: IRR is a percentage and ignores absolute magnitudes; it cannot rank projects of different sizes.

### Alternative methods

The **profitability index (PI)** is the present value of inflows divided by the present value of outflows [AP] (Ch 16.4, "Profitability Index"). PI uses the same inputs as NPV but expresses them as a ratio; it is useful for ranking projects under capital constraints. PI > 1 means accept; PI < 1 means reject (consistent with NPV).

The **discounted payback period** discounts the cash flows before computing the payback period; addresses the time-value flaw of the simple payback period but retains its other limitations (Ch 16.4, "Discounted Payback Period").

The **modified internal rate of return (MIRR)** finds the discount rate that equates the present value of outflows to the future value (terminal value) of inflows compounded at the firm's cost of capital. MIRR resolves IRR's reinvestment-rate assumption and multiple-solution problems but cannot rank projects across different scales (Ch 16.4, "Modified Internal Rate of Return").

### Choosing between projects

For **mutually exclusive projects** (projects that compete so that choosing one excludes the others), the rule is to take the project with the highest NPV (not the highest IRR or the shortest payback) [AR] (Ch 16.5, "Choosing between Mutually Exclusive Projects").

For projects with **different lives**, two methods correct for unequal horizons (Ch 16.5, "Choosing between Projects with Different Lives"):

- **Replacement chain approach**: assume the short project is repeated as many times as needed to match the long project's life, then compare NPVs.
- **Equal annuity approach**: convert each project's NPV into an equivalent annuity payment (a payment that, if received continuously, would have the same NPV); the project with the higher annuity is preferred.

Both methods assume projects can be replicated; this is not always realistic and errors compound over longer horizons.

For **resource-constrained capital budgeting** (more good projects than capital), rank projects by profitability index and select highest PI first until the budget is exhausted. Ranking by NPV alone produces inferior outcomes when project sizes differ (Ch 16.5, "Choosing Projects When Resources Are Limited").

The chapter closes with an honesty note: NPV calculations are based on projected cash flows that are estimates. "A project that has a positive NPV and is accepted when a company is planning how to allocate its capital toward investments may end up being a bad project that the company wishes it had avoided if the future is much different from what it projected" [V] (Ch 16.5, "Capital Budgeting Challenges"). The 2020 oil-price collapse during the COVID-19 pandemic is given as an example of how cash flows can deviate dramatically from projections.

## Part XVII: Capital structure and the cost of capital (Ch 17)

### Capital structure and weights

A firm's **capital structure** is "the relative proportions of debt and equity that a firm uses in financing its assets" [AP] (Ch 17.1, "The Basic Balance Sheet"). Debt is referred to as **financial leverage**. Capital has an opportunity cost: "providing a return equal to what potential investors could expect to earn elsewhere for a similar risk is the cost a company bears in exchange for obtaining funds from investors" [V] (Ch 17.1, "Attracting Capital"). The **weighted average cost of capital (WACC)** is the firm's overall cost of capital, weighted by the market-value proportions of each financing source.

Weights are derived from current market values, not book values, on a market-value-based balance sheet (Ch 17.1, "Weights in the Capital Structure").

### Cost of debt capital

A firm's cost of debt is the YTM on its existing debt — the interest rate it would have to pay to refinance. The **after-tax cost of debt** is `Rd × (1 - T)`, where Rd is the YTM and T is the corporate tax rate [V] (Ch 17.2, "After-Tax Cost of Debt"). Interest is tax-deductible, so the effective cost is lower than the rate paid to lenders. The text catalogues the example of Bluebonnet Industries: 6 per cent coupon bonds selling at 97 per cent of face value with 15 years to maturity yield 6.312 per cent before tax; at a 21 per cent corporate tax rate, the after-tax cost is 4.99 per cent.

### Cost of equity capital

Two methods estimate the cost of equity capital (Ch 17.2, "Cost of Equity Capital"):

- **CAPM**: Re = Rf + β(Rm - Rf). Requires assumptions about the risk-free rate, the market risk premium (typically 5-8 per cent), and the firm's beta. The text catalogues a range of beta estimates from MarketWatch and Yahoo! Finance; for some firms, the two sources yield estimates of equity cost of capital that differ by more than a factor of two (Ch 17.3, "Estimating the Equity Cost of Capital", Table 17.3).
- **Constant dividend growth model**: Re = D₁/P₀ + g. Requires assumptions about next year's dividend and the dividend growth rate. Cannot be used for non-dividend-paying companies.

### WACC

The full WACC formula:

`WACC = D% × Rd × (1 - T) + P% × Rp + E% × Re`

where D%, P%, E% are the weights of debt, preferred stock, and common equity; Rd is the before-tax cost of debt; Rp is the cost of preferred stock; and Re is the cost of common equity (Ch 17.3, "WACC Equation"). For firms without preferred stock, P% = 0.

**Net debt** is total debt minus cash and cash equivalents; many practitioners use net debt rather than total debt when calculating WACC weights, especially for firms holding substantial cash reserves (Ch 17.3, "Net Debt").

### Capital structure choices: MM Proposition II and the impact of taxes

In a perfect-capital-markets benchmark with no taxes, the choice of capital structure does not affect firm value. As leverage increases, the equity beta and equity cost of capital rise proportionally to offset the lower cost of debt, leaving WACC constant. This result is **MM Proposition II**: `Re = Ru + (D/E)(Ru - Rd)`, where Ru is the cost of equity for an unlevered firm [BT] (Ch 17.4, "Leverage and the WACC").

In the real world, taxes break the irrelevance result. Interest is tax-deductible, generating an **interest tax shield**: `Tax shield = Rd × D × T`. The WACC with taxes is:

`WACC_after_tax = D% × Rd × (1 - T) + E% × Re`

This is lower than the pretax WACC, encouraging leverage use (Ch 17.4, "The Impact of Taxes").

### Optimal capital structure: trade-off theory

The interest tax shield grows with leverage, but **financial distress** also grows. Direct costs of financial distress include legal, consulting, appraisal, and auctioneer fees; indirect costs include loss of customers and suppliers (Ch 17.5, "Debt and Financial Distress"). **Trade-off theory** weighs the interest tax shield against the costs of financial distress; the optimal debt level is where the marginal benefit of additional tax shield equals the marginal cost of financial distress [AP] (Ch 17.5, "Trade-Off Theory"). Firms with higher costs of financial distress have lower optimal leverage.

The chapter table (Ch 17.5, Table 17.7) shows substantial industry variation in capital structure. Online retailers average less than 7 per cent debt; rubber and tires firms average 63.62 per cent debt. These reflect industry-specific risks and the trade-off between tax shields and distress costs.

### Alternative sources of funds

**Preferred stock** has a fixed dividend, no maturity, no voting rights, and stands ahead of common stock in liquidation; its cost is `Rp = D / P` (Ch 17.6, "Preferred Shares"). Preferred dividends are after-tax payments by the issuer.

**Convertible bonds** give the bondholder the option to convert the bond into a fixed number of common shares; the **conversion ratio** is the number of shares per bond. Convertible debt carries lower interest rates because of the option's value but dilutes existing shareholders if exercised; the lower interest expense compensates for the possibility of conversion (Ch 17.6, "Convertible Debt").

**Issuing new common stock** is the most expensive form of capital because of investor risk premium and **flotation costs** (filing fees, investment banker fees). Net proceeds from issuance are price minus flotation cost; the cost of new equity capital is `Re_new = D₁ / (P - F) + g`, where F is the flotation cost per share (Ch 17.6, "Issuing New Common Stock").

## Part XVIII: Financial forecasting (Ch 18)

### Why forecast

Forecasts use historical data, analytical tools, and other contextual information to make educated guesses about future financial performance [AP] (Ch 18.1, "Financial Statement Foundations"). A budget is a static forecast; a rolling forecast adjusts as time passes and new information arrives. The text emphasises ethics in forecasting: "there can be tremendous pressure on management to perform, to deliver certain levels of profit, and to meet shareholder expectations . . . Forecasting . . . is not an exact science. There is a great deal of subjectivity that can come into play when forecasting sales and expenses. Ethical behavior is crucial in this area" [V] (Ch 18.1).

### Forecasting sales

Sales forecasts are the foundation; many other forecasts are derived from them [AP] (Ch 18.2, "Sales as the Driver"). Three steps:

1. Look at past data — typically the past 12 months of monthly data to identify seasonality and trends.
2. Use common-size and trend analyses on historical sales to project future totals.
3. Make qualitative adjustments based on what is known about the business, competitors, the economy, and customer demographics.

The text emphasises that "the past is not always a reliable predictor of the future" [V] (Ch 18.2, "Changes for the Future"). Sales-forecasting accuracy is improved by considering external factors (new competition, regulatory change, demographic shifts) and internal factors (new product lines, marketing campaigns, leadership changes).

### Pro forma financials

**Pro forma** financial statements are forward-looking; they use sales forecasts, historical data, and account relationships to project future statements [AP] (Ch 18.3, "Purpose of a Forecast"). One-year forecasts broken into monthly increments are typical for cash-flow planning; multi-year forecasts (typically quarterly or annual) are typical for strategic planning. **Variable costs** vary with sales (direct labour, direct materials); **fixed costs** do not change with sales within a relevant range (rent, salaries). Forecasting variable costs as a percentage of sales is appropriate; fixed costs are forecast as dollar amounts.

### Generating the complete forecast

The **forecasted income statement** uses the sales forecast and historical relationships to project each line. Where future events are known (new compensation programs, lease changes, planned capital purchases), they override historical relationships (Ch 18.4, "Forecast the Income Statement"). The **forecasted balance sheet** is generated by linking accounts to the forecasted income statement: accounts receivable depend on sales and credit terms; inventory depends on cost of goods sold and inventory policy; accounts payable depend on cost of goods sold and payment terms; retained earnings depend on net income and dividend policy. Cash is typically the "plug" that balances the balance sheet (Ch 18.4, "Forecast the Balance Sheet").

### Cash flow forecast

A **cash forecast** begins with the beginning cash balance, adds anticipated cash inflows, and deducts anticipated cash outflows; this identifies surpluses and deficits month by month [AP] (Ch 18.5, "Create a Cash Flow Forecast"). Companies often have a target minimum cash balance (Clear Lake's example uses $35,000); deficits below this trigger short-term financing decisions.

### Scenarios and sensitivity analysis

**Scenario analysis** explores how multiple variable changes affect the financial outlook (a recession scenario versus a normal-economy scenario versus an expansion scenario) [AP] (Ch 18.5, "Scenarios in Forecasting"). **Sensitivity analysis** examines how output changes with one input at a time — useful for identifying which forecast inputs are most consequential (Ch 18.5, "Sensitivity Analysis in Forecasting"). Both are facilitated by spreadsheet software with linked formulas.

## Part XIX: Trade credit and working capital (Ch 19)

### Working capital basics

**Working capital** describes the resources needed to meet daily, weekly, and monthly operating cash flow needs [AP] (Ch 19.1, "What Is Working Capital?"). **Gross working capital** is current assets; **net working capital (NWC)** is current assets minus current liabilities. The goal of working capital management is to maintain adequate working capital to meet operational needs and current liabilities while not holding excessive (idle) assets that generate no return.

The **operating cycle** is the time from inventory acquisition to sale; the **cash conversion cycle** extends this to receipt of cash from customers minus the time to pay suppliers (Ch 19.1, "The Cash Cycle"):

`Cash conversion cycle = Inventory conversion period + Receivables collection period − Payables deferral period`

Each component is calculated from the corresponding turnover ratio. Shortening the cycle frees up cash; lengthening it strains liquidity. The text takes a position on payables: "the payables deferral period is the one element that probably cannot be optimized without violating credit terms" — paying suppliers on time preserves the relationship and credit rating (Ch 19.1).

Working capital needs vary by industry: home construction has very long cycles; restaurants can have very short or even negative cycles. Walmart's current ratio of around 0.9 is enabled by strong supply-chain management (Ch 19.1, "Working Capital Needs by Industry").

### Trade credit

**Trade credit** (accounts payable) is a vendor's extension of credit to a buyer; it is "spontaneous financing" that grows with sales [AP] (Ch 19.2, "How Trade Credit Works"). Trade credit is normally interest-free; **net terms** specify the credit period, and **cash discounts** offer incentives for early payment.

The annual cost of forgoing a quick-payment discount is given by the formula in the chapter (Ch 19.2, "Cost of Trade Credit"). The text's example: terms of 2/10 net 30 imply that not taking the discount costs 36.73 per cent on an annualised basis — far more than most firms pay for short-term financing.

### Cash management

Three motives for holding cash (Ch 19.3, "Motivations for Holding Cash"):

- **Transactional motive**: paying employees, vendors, creditors, dividends.
- **Precautionary motive**: emergencies and unexpected events.
- **Speculative motive**: ready availability for unexpected opportunities.

**Compensating balances** are minimum balances required by a bank in exchange for other corporate services. Excess cash is invested in **cash equivalents (cash alternatives)** — short-term, low-risk, highly liquid investments such as T-bills, federal agency securities, certificates of deposit, and commercial paper.

### Receivables and inventory management

Receivables management begins before the sale: can the customer be approved? what credit terms? what credit limit? Creditworthiness is judged by financial statements, financial ratios, and credit reports/scores (Ch 19.4). The **accounts receivable aging schedule** classifies outstanding receivables by age (how long past due); older receivables have higher uncollectibility percentages. Aging is used to estimate **bad debt expense** and the **allowance for doubtful accounts** (a contra-asset).

Inventory management balances four cost components (Ch 19.5, "Inventory Cost"):

- **Purchasing costs**: the invoice amount.
- **Carrying costs**: storage, insurance, obsolescence, spoilage, opportunity cost of capital tied up.
- **Ordering costs**: the cost of placing orders.
- **Stockout costs**: lost sales when inventory is unavailable.

**Just-in-time (JIT)** inventory practices and **economic order quantity (EOQ)** are tools for minimising total inventory cost while meeting demand. **Lower of cost or market** is the GAAP requirement to mark inventory down when its market value falls below cost.

## Part XX: Risk management for the financial manager (Ch 20)

### Three areas of focus for the financial manager

Three focal areas for the financial manager (Ch 20.1, "What Is Risk?"): increase cash inflows and decrease cash outflows; speed up cash inflows and slow down cash outflows; decrease the riskiness of both inflows and outflows. Risk in finance is uncertainty — "risk occurs because you cannot predict the future" [V] (Ch 20.1, "What Is Risk?"). Hedging takes actions to reduce exposure to risk; speculating bets on future outcomes for profit. The text takes a position: "in finance, hedging is a risk management tool. . . Speculating . . . involves directional bets" [V] (Ch 20.1, "Risk and Firm Value").

### Commodity price risk

Three hedges for commodity price risk (Ch 20.2):

- **Long-term contracts** with suppliers lock in prices but expose both parties to default risk and reduce flexibility.
- **Vertical integration** merges a company with its supplier; the buyer's costs and the supplier's revenues are now internalised.
- **Futures contracts** are standardised, exchange-traded agreements to trade an asset on a future date at a price locked in today. Margin requirements and **marking to market** (daily exchange of cash flows based on price changes) reduce counterparty risk.

### Exchange rate risk

Three exchange-rate risks were introduced in Ch 3.5 and revisited (Ch 20.3, "Exchange Rate Risks"):

- **Transaction risk**: change in the value of expected receipts or expenses.
- **Translation risk**: change in the reported (book) value of foreign assets when consolidated into the home currency.
- **Economic risk**: change in customer base or sales because of relative purchasing-power changes — affects firms with no direct international transactions.

Hedges (Ch 20.3):

- **Futures contracts** for currencies (standardised, exchange-traded).
- **Forward contracts**, contractual agreements between two parties (often a firm and its bank) to exchange currencies on a future date at a forward rate. Forward contracts are flexible (any size, any date) but carry counterparty risk.
- **Natural hedges** offset risk through internal operations — a British travel agency selling Italian tours to British customers can offset that risk by selling London tours to euro-zone customers.

### Options

A financial **option** "gives the owner the right, but not the obligation, to purchase or sell an asset for a specified price at some future date" [V] (Ch 20.3, "Options"). Options are **derivatives** — their value derives from another asset.

Terminology: the **strike price (exercise price)** is the price the holder pays/receives if exercising; the **premium** is the price paid for the option; the **expiration date** is when the option expires. **American options** can be exercised at any time up to expiration; **European options** only on expiration (the labels are unrelated to where they are traded).

Two main types (Ch 20.3):

- **Call option**: right to buy. Holder profits when the underlying price rises above strike + premium; option writer's loss is mirror image.
- **Put option**: right to sell. Holder profits when the underlying price falls below strike - premium; option writer's loss is mirror image.

The text takes a position: option contracts are zero-sum games. The holder's gain equals the writer's loss and vice versa.

### Interest rate risk and swaps

When interest rates rise, the present value of future cash flows falls, including the price of fixed-coupon bonds. **Duration** is the standard measure of bond price sensitivity to interest rate changes; the chapter notes (without computing) that duration rises with longer maturity, lower coupon rate, and lower current yield [AP] (Ch 20.4, "Measuring Interest Rate Risk").

A **swap** involves two parties agreeing to exchange specified payment streams. The classic example is an interest rate swap: a high-rated firm (say, AAA) borrows at a fixed rate, while a lower-rated firm (BBB) borrows at a floating rate; they then swap, with the higher-rated firm paying floating to the lower-rated firm and the lower-rated firm paying fixed. Both firms benefit because their respective comparative advantages in fixed and floating markets are exploited (Ch 20.4, "Swap-Based Hedging").

## Positions the author explicitly frames against

- **Pure profit-as-goal thinking**. The text repeatedly distinguishes profit from cash flow and argues cash flow is the more reliable measure of business health. "Cash flow is the axis upon which the world of business spins" (Irina Simmons interview, Ch 9.1).
- **Use of payback period as a primary capital-budgeting criterion**. The text catalogues four disadvantages (ignores TVM, no theoretically grounded acceptance threshold, ignores cash flows after breakeven, no risk adjustment) and recommends NPV as the theoretically correct method (Ch 16.1).
- **IRR-only ranking of mutually exclusive projects**. The text takes the position that the highest-NPV project should be chosen, not the highest-IRR project, because IRR ignores scale and assumes intermediate cash flows reinvested at the IRR rate (Ch 16.5).
- **Treating SPACs as superior alternatives to IPOs from the public investor's perspective**. The Renaissance Capital study cited shows SPAC mergers averaging -18.8 per cent return vs +37.2 per cent for traditional IPOs (Ch 12.1).
- **Speculating on payday advance loans, refund anticipation loans, and similar short-term credit products**. The text explicitly highlights effective annual rates that can exceed 300 per cent on what appear to be modest fees (Ch 8.4).
- **Investing in non-investment-grade junk bonds without understanding their cyclical default behaviour**. The text notes 11 per cent of US junk bonds defaulted in 2001 and that the market had a brief resurgence in the late 1990s (Ch 10.4).
- **Trade credit as truly free**. Although trade credit is interest-free, the opportunity cost of forgoing cash discounts can be 36+ per cent annualised — the text frames not taking discounts as a substantial hidden expense (Ch 19.2).
- **Treating Excel's NPV function as computing the conventional NPV**. The text emphasises Excel's NPV starts with year-1 cash flows; the time-zero cash flow must be subtracted separately to get the conventional NPV (Ch 16.6).
