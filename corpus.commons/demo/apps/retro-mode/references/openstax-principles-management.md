## Author's thesis (condensed)

Principles of Management is presented as a traditional introductory management text organised around the leading, planning, organising, and controlling functions, with two stated themes: the variables that affect how, when, where, and why managers perform their jobs, and the theories and techniques successful managers use across organisational levels. Management is defined as the process of planning, organising, directing, and controlling employee activities in combination with other resources to accomplish organisational goals.

Five load-bearing claims structure the book. First, managerial work is fragmented, conversational, and interactive — managers spend most of their time in brief, unplanned verbal exchanges rather than executing the textbook list of activities. Second, managerial decision-making is a dual-system cognitive process, and effective managers learn when to engage which system. Third, organisations are open systems whose internal structure, culture, and processes must align with external environmental demands. Fourth, strategic success requires systematic external and internal analysis feeding into deliberate strategic positioning. Fifth, motivation, performance, and reward must be linked through expectancy and equity mechanisms, with no single motivation theory universal.

## The nature of managerial work

Mintzberg's CEO observations found managers averaged 36 written and 16 verbal contacts per day, "almost every one of them dealing with a distinct or different issue", with most activities lasting less than nine minutes. Kotter's general managers spent only 25 per cent of their time alone. Stewart's 160 British managers spent 47 per cent of their time with peers, 41 per cent with their unit, and only 12 per cent with superiors. Mintzberg's three role clusters — interpersonal (figurehead, leader, liaison), informational (monitor, disseminator, spokesperson), decisional (entrepreneur, disturbance handler, resource allocator, negotiator) — frame managerial work. Three management levels (executive, middle, first-line) require three skill types (technical, human relations, conceptual); as one rises, conceptual demand increases and technical demand decreases, while human-relations skill is required at every level.

## Managerial decision-making

The brain processes decisions through one of two routes: a reflective system (logical, analytical, deliberate) and a reactive system (quick, impulsive, intuitive); only one operates at a time. Decisions are programmed (rule-amenable, supports heuristics) or non-programmed (novel, ambiguous, requires the systematic six-step process). Six barriers recur: bounded rationality, escalation of commitment, time constraints, uncertainty, personal biases (similar-to-me, confirmation bias), and conflict. Process conflict is productive; relationship conflict is harmful. Evidence-based decision-making collects the best available evidence systematically. Critical thinking catalogues seven logical fallacies (non sequitur, false cause, ad hominem, genetic, appeal to tradition, bandwagon, appeal to emotion). Rest's four-component ethical model (sensitivity, judgment, motivation, action) anchors ethical decision-making. Group decisions improve quality with diverse membership and devil's advocate roles; groupthink and suppression of dissent are the failure modes.

## History of management thought

Management origins trace through Sumer, Hammurabi's Code, Egypt (division of labour, specialisation, span of control), the Han dynasty (meritocratic Confucian-trained bureaucracy), and Greece and Rome. The Italian Renaissance contributed double-entry bookkeeping and the corporation; Adam Smith contributed specialisation and division of labour. Taylor's scientific management rested on four principles, time studies, piecework, and first-class work, but treated workers as machine tools. Fayol's 14 principles of management and five functions (planning, organising, staffing, controlling, directing) addressed top managers; Weber's bureaucracy addressed middle managers. The Hawthorne studies sparked the human relations movement: Mayo's social-factor interpretation, Barnard's zone of indifference, Follett's three conflict-resolution paths (dominance, compromise, integration). The contingency school (Woodward) replaced one-best-way with "it depends".

## External and internal organisational environments and corporate culture

Five macro-environmental forces shape organisations: sociocultural, technological, economic, government and political, natural disasters and human-induced problems. The Duncan-Daft 2x2 of environmental complexity x change yields four cells with corresponding structures. Mechanistic structures fit stable, simple environments; organic structures fit unstable, complex environments. Six structural types span the continuum: functional, divisional, geographic, matrix, networked team, virtual. Open systems theory (Katz and Kahn) frames organisations as resource-exchanging with feedback loops. The McKinsey 7-S model captures internal alignment around shared values. The Cameron-Quinn Competing Values Framework names four cultural types — Adhocracy, Clan, Hierarchy, Market — across external/internal x flexibility/control axes. Drucker's "culture eats strategy for breakfast" anchors the cultural-fit argument.

## Ethics, corporate responsibility, and sustainability

Ethics concerns how we act, live, and treat others; business ethics is applied ethics for real-world transactions. Goodpaster-Nash distinguishes individual, organisational, and societal/global levels. Terminal values (desired end states) and instrumental values (preferred means) shape behaviour. Seven ethical principles — utilitarianism, universalism, rights, justice, virtue ethics, common good, ethical relativism — frame moral reasoning. Servant leadership (Greenleaf) and stewardship anchor ethical-leadership styles. Schein defines culture as shared tacit assumptions for external adaptation and internal integration. CSR rests on the triple bottom line (profit, people, planet); stakeholder theory (Freeman) holds that corporations should treat all constituencies fairly. The Foreign Corrupt Practices Act and UN Global Compact frame international ethics.

## International management

Three globalisation factors: trade-barrier reductions (WTO with 164 members), foreign direct investment ($1.52 trillion 2017), and the Internet (3.8 billion users). Hofstede's IBM-employee survey (88,000 employees, 72 countries) yielded four dimensions: power distance, individualism vs collectivism, uncertainty avoidance, masculinity. The GLOBE project (17,000 managers, 62 countries) added performance orientation and humane orientation. Universally admired traits across cultures include trustworthy, dependable, intelligent, just, honest, decisive. Universally disliked: loner, egocentric, antisocial, ruthless, dictatorial. Cultural intelligence works through cognitive, meta-cognitive, motivational, and behavioural dimensions. Three internationalisation strategies (global, regional, local) and six entry modes (exporting, licensing, franchising, alliances, FDI, born globals).

## Entrepreneurship

Entrepreneurs have vision, drive, and creativity, willing to take risk for profit; small-business owners stay small by choice. Three types: classic (with micropreneurs and growth-oriented sub-types), multipreneurs, intrapreneurs. Nine entrepreneurial traits: ambitious, independent, self-confident, risk-taker (moderate, controlled risk), visionary, creative, energetic, passionate, committed. US small businesses (under 500 employees) employ ~48 per cent of the private workforce; women-owned firms grew 114 per cent 2007-2017 vs 44 per cent overall. The SBA, SCORE, and SBICs provide financing and consulting support.

## Strategic analysis

Strategic analysis studies the layers of a firm's competitive environment. SWOT identifies internal strengths and weaknesses, external opportunities and threats. PESTEL examines the macro environment across political, economic, sociocultural, technological, environmental, and legal forces. Porter's Five Forces evaluate the micro environment: industry rivalry, threat of new entrants, threat of substitutes, supplier power, buyer power. The value chain decomposes primary and support activities. VRIO evaluates resources by value, rarity, imitability, and organisation; all four yields sustained competitive advantage. Porter's three generic strategies — cost leadership, differentiation, focus — should not be combined ("stuck in the middle"). Strategic groups are firms following similar generic strategies; rivalry is fiercest within groups. Strategic positioning seeks unique, hard-to-imitate position.

## The strategic management process

The strategic management cycle integrates vision and mission, strategic analysis, strategic objectives, strategy formulation, implementation, and measurement — running simultaneously rather than strictly sequentially. A vision statement expresses what founders want the business to accomplish; a mission statement describes how. Strategic objectives are big-picture goals fulfilling the mission. Three strategy levels: business-level (single product/unit), corporate (portfolio via BCG matrix), international. Three grand strategies: growth, stability, defensive. SMART goals (specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound). Three planning levels (strategic, tactical, operational). Performance measurement and benchmarking close the cycle.

## Organisational structure and change

Formal organisation is the officially defined relationship set; informal organisation is the invisible interpersonal network. Weber's bureaucratic dimensions: specialisation, command-and-control, span of control, centralisation, formalisation. Three change types: structural, technological, cultural (most difficult). Four organisational life-cycle phases: entrepreneurship, survival, sustained success, renewal/decline. Three change dimensions: scope (incremental vs transformational), level (individual/group/organisation), intentionality (planned vs emergent). Four change models: Lewin's three-stage (stable contexts), Kotter's eight-step (mechanistic, top-down, deficit), Cooperrider's Appreciative Inquiry 5-D cycle (abundance, bottom-up, narrative-shifting), Olson-Eoyang Complex Adaptive Systems (emergent, self-organising). Choice depends on deficit vs abundance, top-down vs bottom-up mechanism, and conventional vs appreciative mindset.

## Human resource management

Ulrich's four waves of HR: administrative, practice design, business-strategy integration, external-stakeholder responsiveness. Major US laws: ADEA (40+), ADA (disability), FLSA (wages/hours), FMLA (12 weeks unpaid leave), NLRA (organising rights), WARN (60-day layoff notice). Performance management traces from WWI military merit ratings through Welch-era forced ranking (which GE later abandoned because it undermined collaboration) to current real-time models (Deloitte's four-question redesign). Pay-for-performance is grounded in WorldatWork's six total-rewards elements: compensation, benefits, work-life effectiveness, recognition, performance management, talent development. Nohria-Groysberg-Lee four-drives model maps drives (acquire, bond, comprehend, defend) to organisational levers. Fernandez-Araoz/Groysberg/Nohria seven-step recruiting framework. The 9-box talent review (performance x potential) and succession planning are paired.

## Diversity in organisations

Three diversity types: surface-level (visible), deep-level (non-observable), hidden (invisible social identities at the individual's discretion). Workforce 2014-2024 projections: white non-Hispanic share -3 per cent, Hispanic/Latino +28 per cent, Asian +23.2 per cent, Black +10.1 per cent. The glass ceiling, model-minority myth, and LGBTQ pass-or-reveal choice frame contemporary diversity challenges. Cox-Blake's six competitive advantages: cost, resource acquisition, marketing, system flexibility, creativity, problem-solving. The resource-based view treats diverse workforces as VRIO assets. Five diversity theories: cognitive diversity hypothesis, similarity-attraction paradigm, social cognitive theory, social identity theory, schema theory, justification-suppression model. The Ely-Thomas paradigm names three orientations: integration-and-learning (strongest fit), access-and-legitimacy, discrimination-and-fairness. Highly structured interviews with 15 specific characteristics reduce similarity bias.

## Leadership

Leadership is a social influence relationship between two or more people who depend on each other for goal attainment. The leadership process has five components: leader, follower, context, process, consequences; the follower is "the most critical factor". Leader-Member Exchange (LMX) frames leadership as dyadic relationships of varying quality. French and Raven's five power bases — reward, coercive, referent, expert, legitimate — produce different responses; rationality is the most effective influence tactic for follower commitment. McGregor's Theory X versus Theory Y. Locke's trait list: drive, leadership motivation, honesty and integrity, self-confidence, cognitive ability, business knowledge. Ohio State studies identified consideration and initiating structure dimensions; Blake-Mouton Grid maps concern-for-results x concern-for-people. Fiedler's contingency theory matches LPC-based styles with situation favourability. House-Evans path-goal theory has four leader behaviours (supportive, directive, participative, achievement-oriented). Transformational leaders inspire through values, vision, passion; charismatic leaders carry both opportunity and risk.

## Work motivation for performance

Performance is a function of ability, motivation, role perceptions, and the performance environment; deficiencies in one cannot be compensated. Five content theories: manifest needs (Murray), learned needs (McClelland: nAch, nAff, nPow — high social-power need most predicts managerial success), Maslow's hierarchy (text notes research does not support strict prepotency), Alderfer's ERG (with frustration regression), Herzberg's motivator-hygiene (separate scales). Self-determination theory's signature claim: as extrinsic rewards rise, intrinsic motivation falls. Four process theories: operant conditioning (continuous reinforcement for shaping; variable-ratio schedules superior thereafter); equity theory (Adams: outcome-input ratio comparison; people overrate own performance and others' outcomes); goal theory (Locke: difficult, specific, accepted, committed-to goals); expectancy theory (Vroom: E1 x E2 x valence; outcomes must be contingent on performance). Self-efficacy (Bandura) drives E1.

## Managing teams

Katzenbach-Smith team has five elements: common commitment, performance goals, complementary skills, work-process commitment, mutual accountability. Teams differ from working groups (shared vs focused leadership; mutual vs individual accountability). Eight effective-team practices including urgency, skill-based selection, ground rules, immediate goals. Tuckman's stages: forming, storming, norming, performing, adjourning. Linda Hill frames team management as paradox: individual vs collective, support vs confrontation, performance vs learning, authority vs discretion. Lencioni's five dysfunctions begin with absence of trust, leading to fear of conflict, lack of commitment, avoidance of accountability, inattention to results. Mining and real-time permission surface conflict productively. McKinsey 2015: top quartile ethnic diversity 35 per cent more likely above-mean returns; top quartile gender diversity 15 per cent. Multicultural teams face four conflict sources (direct/indirect communication, accent and fluency, hierarchy attitudes, decision-making norms) and four interventions (adaptation, structural intervention, managerial intervention, exit). Cultural intelligence develops through head, body, heart.

## Managerial communication

The basic model — encoding, decoding, noise — frames communication. Three feedback types: informational, corrective, reinforcing. Three communication types (oral most prevalent; written as documented record; nonverbal). Mintzberg's role clusters anchor managerial communication context. Drucker is quoted approvingly on language: managers must learn to know language, understand what words are and what they mean. Eccles and Nohria reframe: "the essence of what management is all about [is] the effective use of language to get things done". Borden's research: managers spend ~75 per cent of time in verbal interaction. Writing is "a career sifter" and the documented record of analysis and commitment. The chapter's signature claim is that communication is invention — managers create meaning through discourse rather than merely transmitting it; "the numbers" never speak for themselves. Larkin and Larkin: in ten years of consulting, no manager has ever said they were a poor communicator.

## Organisational planning and controlling

Planning establishes goals and specifies how they are attained, with outcome statements and action statements. Five reasons to plan: offset uncertainty, focus activity, provide road map, increase efficiency, facilitate control. The Koontz-O'Donnell five-step planning process: awareness of present state, outcome statements, premising, course of action, supportive plans. Goal planning vs domain/directional planning vs hybrid planning; goal planning produces higher performance. The Deming/Shewhart cycle (Plan-Do-Check-Act) integrates organisational learning. Five plan categories: hierarchical (strategic/administrative/operating), frequency (standing/single-use), time-frame, organisational scope, contingency. Drucker's eight goal areas: market standing, innovation, productivity, profitability, resources, manager performance, employee performance, public responsibility. Strategic-planning median ROI: 17.1 per cent (vs 5.9 per cent non-planners). Goal specificity and difficulty enhance motivating potential (Weyerhaeuser's $250,000 truck-loading example). MBO is the participatory goal-setting technique. Control-oriented vs involvement-oriented approaches contrast top-down monitoring against participatory goal-setting.

## Management of technology and innovation

Technology is the processes converting inputs into outputs plus evaluation and control mechanisms. Management of technology is planning, implementation, evaluation, and control of resources to create value and competitive advantage. Innovation includes newness in products and processes; management of innovation requires casting a wide net, creating newness with existing products, opening culture, communicating knowledge, and changing with courage to avoid strategic inertia (Kodak, IBM cautionary cases). Three creation methods: external (M&A, joint ventures, alliances), internal (R&D), new business/entrepreneurship. Five common external-process types: M&A, joint ventures, franchise agreements, licensing agreements, formal/informal contracts. The lean start-up suits low-cost, fast-iteration contexts (mobile applications). Two knowledge types: explicit (codified) and tacit (experiential). Followship is the mirror image of leadership: "the success of organizations is more the result of good followship than of great leadership".

## Signature contrarian positions

1. **Maximising shareholder wealth as primary decision criterion is rejected.** It is short-sighted and "not an acceptable reason for causing harm to others".
2. **Pure rationality without emotion is an inadequate decision model.** Effective decision-making relies on both logic and emotions; the dual-system reflective/reactive frame replaces uniform-rational models.
3. **Punishment is a poor primary reinforcer.** It tells people what not to do but not what they should do; positive reinforcement should dominate.
4. **Strict Maslow hierarchical sequencing is not supported by research.** Lower-order needs are typically satisfied before higher-order, but the rigid five-step prepotency claim does not hold.
5. **Equality is not equity.** Equality-focused organisations produce weak E2s and demotivate top performers; proportionality is what employees expect.
6. **Forced ranking damages collaboration over time.** GE backed away after Welch retired because it fostered internal competition and undermined teamwork.
7. **Process conflict is productive; relationship conflict is harmful.** The blanket "conflict is bad" framing misses the distinction.
8. **Top-down change is not universally appropriate.** The more complex the potential change, the greater the need for employee involvement.
9. **Culture eats strategy for breakfast (Drucker, endorsed).** Culture is more influential than strategy because it is rooted in values.
10. **Cultural stereotyping fails empirically.** 80 per cent of cultural variation is within countries, not between them.
11. **Cost leadership and differentiation should not be combined.** Trying to do both leaves a firm "stuck in the middle".
12. **Communication is invention, not transmission.** Managers create meaning through discourse; "the numbers" never speak for themselves.
13. **Conflict avoidance damages teams.** Unaddressed conflicts resurface insidiously and through back channels.
14. **Hiring family and friends as default for startups is hazardous.** Long-term hazards outweigh benefits because such teams think too similarly and miss outside perspectives and connections.
