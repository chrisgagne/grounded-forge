## Author's thesis (paraphrased from source)

Psychology is presented as "the scientific study of the mind and behavior" [V] (Ch 1.1, "What Is Psychology?"), and the introductory text frames the discipline as both a natural and a social science whose methods are "empirical" — "grounded in objective, tangible evidence that can be observed time and time again, regardless of who is observing" [V] (Ch 2.1, "Use of Research Information"). Across 16 chapters the book argues that human behaviour and inner experience are jointly shaped by biological, cognitive, social, developmental, and cultural processes, and that no single perspective is sufficient. The historical narrative in Chapter 1 traces six schools — structuralism, functionalism, psychoanalytic theory, Gestalt, behaviorism, humanism — and the cognitive revolution, presenting the modern field as integrative rather than allegiant to any single school [AP] (Ch 1.2, "History of Psychology").

A second load-bearing claim runs through the volume: that *cognition is constructive*. Perception is "the process by which one screens, selects, organizes, and interprets stimuli to give them meaning" and produces top-down as well as bottom-up effects (Ch 5.1, "Perception"); thinking is organised by *concepts*, *prototypes*, and *schemata*, and judgement under uncertainty is shaped by *heuristics* and systematic *biases* (anchoring, confirmation, hindsight, representative, availability) [AP] (Ch 7.1, "Cognition"; Ch 7.3, "Pitfalls to Problem Solving"). Memory is reconstructive rather than reproductive — encoded in stages, subject to *misinformation effects*, *suggestibility*, and Schacter's *seven sins* (transience, absentmindedness, blocking, misattribution, suggestibility, bias, persistence) [AR] (Ch 8.1; Ch 8.3).

A third load-bearing claim is that *the situation matters more than dispositional accounts predict*. Social psychology demonstrates that people change their behaviour to match the composition of social groups, that the *fundamental attribution error* causes observers to overweight dispositional explanations and underweight situational ones (Ch 12.1, "Fundamental Attribution Error"), that conformity to group pressure occurs even on unambiguous perceptual tasks (Asch line studies, Ch 12.4), and that obedience to authority can override moral judgement (Milgram, Ch 12.4). The book argues this finding is culture-specific in degree: people from collectivistic cultures are less prone to the fundamental attribution error than people from individualistic cultures [AP] (Ch 12.1).

A fourth load-bearing claim is that *learning is the basic mechanism of behaviour change*. Chapter 6 organises three learning models — Pavlovian classical conditioning, Skinnerian operant conditioning, and Bandura's observational learning — and presents reinforcement schedules (fixed/variable × interval/ratio) as systematic determinants of response patterns. Variable-ratio schedules produce the highest and most extinction-resistant response rates [AP] (Ch 6.3, "Reinforcement Schedules"). Bandura's social-cognitive extension introduces *reciprocal determinism* and *self-efficacy* — "an individual's belief in their own capability to complete a task" [V] (Ch 10.1, "Self-efficacy and Social Motives"; Ch 11.4).

A fifth load-bearing claim is that *individual differences are real and consequential*. Personality is described through five major traditions — psychodynamic, neo-Freudian, learning/social-cognitive, humanistic, biological/trait — culminating in the Five Factor Model (openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, neuroticism). The book treats the Big Five as "the most popular theory in personality psychology today and the most accurate approximation of the basic personality dimensions" [V] (Ch 11.7). Intelligence is presented as multi-component (Cattell's fluid/crystallised; Sternberg's triarchic; Gardner's multiple intelligences; the Cattell-Horn-Carroll hierarchy), measured imperfectly by IQ tests that produce a bell-curve distribution with mean 100 and standard deviation 15 [AP] (Ch 7.4–7.6).

A sixth load-bearing claim is the *diathesis-stress integration*: psychological disorders typically arise from interactions between biological diatheses (genetic vulnerability, neurotransmitter imbalances) and environmental stressors (childhood adversity, traumatic events, chronic stress). The book applies this framework explicitly to schizophrenia (Tienari et al.), PTSD, depression, and personality disorders [AP] (Ch 15.3, "The Diathesis-Stress Model").

## Part I: The discipline of psychology — definitions, history, and contemporary subdivisions (Ch 1)

### Psychology as empirical science

Psychology is "the scientific study of the mind and behavior" [V] (Ch 1.1, "What Is Psychology?"). Psychologists "use the scientific method to acquire knowledge" — propose a hypothesis fitting a theory, make observations or run experiments, and publish results so others can replicate. The text emphasises that "psychological science is empirical, based on measurable data" and that "an empirical method for acquiring knowledge is one based on observation, including experimentation, rather than a method based only on forms of logical argument or previous authorities" [V] (Ch 1.1).

### Six historical schools and the cognitive revolution

Wilhelm Wundt and Edward Titchener developed *structuralism*, focused on identifying components of consciousness via introspection (Ch 1.2, "Wundt and Structuralism"). William James, John Dewey, and Charles Sanders Peirce developed *functionalism*, focused on the function of mental activities in adapting an organism to its environment (Ch 1.2, "Functionalism"). Sigmund Freud developed *psychoanalytic theory*, focused on the unconscious and early childhood; the text notes that "many of Freud's other ideas, however, are controversial" but that "modern iterations of Freud's clinical approach have been empirically demonstrated to be effective" [V] (Ch 1.2, "Freud and Psychoanalytic Theory"). Wertheimer, Koffka, and Köhler imported *Gestalt psychology* to the United States, emphasising that "how those parts relate to each other as a whole is often what the individual responds to in perception" [V] (Ch 1.2, "Wertheimer, Koffka, Köhler, and Gestalt Psychology"). Pavlov, Watson, and Skinner developed *behaviorism*, which the text describes as having "dominated experimental psychology for several decades" [V] (Ch 1.2, "Pavlov, Watson, Skinner, and Behaviorism"). Maslow and Rogers developed *humanism*, "a perspective within psychology that emphasizes the potential for good that is innate to all humans" [V] (Ch 1.2, "Maslow, Rogers, and Humanism"). The *cognitive revolution* of the 1950s and 1960s, catalysed by Chomsky's critique of behaviorism and the rise of computer science and linguistics, reintroduced mental processes as legitimate objects of scientific inquiry [AP] (Ch 1.2, "The Cognitive Revolution").

### Modern subdivisions of contemporary psychology

The book lists biopsychology and evolutionary psychology, sensation and perception, cognitive psychology, developmental psychology, personality psychology, social psychology, industrial-organizational (I-O) psychology, health psychology, sport and exercise psychology, clinical psychology, counseling psychology, and forensic psychology as the major contemporary subdivisions (Ch 1.3). I-O psychology "applies psychological theories, principles, and research findings in industrial and organizational settings" and is concerned with "personnel management, organizational structure, and workplace environment" (Ch 1.3, "Industrial-Organizational Psychology"). Health psychology applies the *biopsychosocial model*, the position that health and illness are determined by the interaction of biological, psychological, and sociocultural factors (Ch 1.3, "Health Psychology").

### A note on diversity in the discipline's history

The book repeatedly highlights the founding contributions of women and people of colour despite institutional barriers: Margaret Floy Washburn (first woman to earn a PhD in psychology, 1894); Mary Whiton Calkins (completed all PhD requirements at Harvard but was refused the degree because she was a woman; later first woman APA president, 1905); Francis Cecil Sumner (first African American to receive a PhD in psychology, 1920); Inez Beverly Prosser (first African American woman, 1933); Kenneth and Mamie Phipps Clark (research instrumental in *Brown v. Board of Education*) [AP] (Ch 1.2, "Multicultural and Cross-Cultural Psychology"; Ch 12.5).

## Part II: Research methods and ethics (Ch 2)

### Theory, hypothesis, falsifiability

A *theory* is "a well-developed set of ideas that propose an explanation for observed phenomena" and a *hypothesis* is "a testable prediction about how the world will behave if our idea is correct" [V] (Ch 2.1, "The Process of Scientific Research"). A scientific hypothesis must be *falsifiable* — "capable of being shown to be incorrect" — and the text uses this criterion to mark the boundary between scientific and non-scientific theorising, citing Freud's id/ego/superego as a non-falsifiable structure (Ch 2.1).

### Methods inventory

The book organises empirical methods into clinical/case studies, naturalistic observation, surveys, archival research, longitudinal versus cross-sectional designs, and experiments (Ch 2.2). Each method is described with its strengths and weaknesses: case studies provide depth but limit generalisability; naturalistic observation has high ecological validity but limits control; surveys collect breadth but rely on self-report honesty; experiments establish cause and effect via random assignment and manipulation of an independent variable but may sacrifice ecological validity. The text contrasts *correlation* with *causation* repeatedly: "correlations do not tell us anything about causation—regardless of how strong the relationship is between variables" [V] (Ch 2.3, "Correlation Does Not Indicate Causation"). The example of ice cream and crime, both correlated with temperature as a *confounding variable*, is the text's central illustration. Pearson r is interpreted by sign (positive vs negative) and magnitude (closer to ±1 = stronger).

### Experimental design components

The text identifies experimental versus control groups, *operational definitions*, *random sampling* and *random assignment*, *single-blind* and *double-blind* designs, *placebo effect*, *experimenter bias*, *independent* and *dependent* variables, *statistical analysis*, and *replication* as the working components of experimental research (Ch 2.3). The standard for *statistical significance* is described conventionally: "psychologists consider differences to be statistically significant if there is less than a five percent chance of observing them if the groups did not actually differ from one another" [V] (Ch 2.3, "Interpreting Experimental Findings"). The text discusses the *replication crisis* in psychology and other sciences openly: "some of the most well-known studies and scientists have produced research that has failed to be replicated by others" [V] (Ch 2.3, "Reporting Research").

### Reliability and validity

*Reliability* is "the ability to consistently produce a given result"; *validity* is "the extent to which a given instrument or tool accurately measures what it's supposed to measure" [V] (Ch 2.3, "Reliability and Validity"). The text distinguishes inter-rater, internal consistency, and test-retest reliability; ecological, construct, face, and predictive validity. "While any valid measure is by necessity reliable, the reverse is not necessarily true" [V].

### Research ethics

Modern human-subjects research is governed by Institutional Review Boards (IRBs) requiring *informed consent*, allowing *deception* only when necessary and followed by *debriefing*, and protecting confidentiality (Ch 2.4). The text uses the Tuskegee Syphilis Study (1932–72) as a historical example of profound ethical failure that "led directly to the National Research Act of 1974 and the strict ethical guidelines for research on humans described in this chapter" [V]. Animal research is governed by Institutional Animal Care and Use Committees (IACUCs).

## Part III: Biopsychology — the biological substrate of behaviour (Ch 3)

### Genetics and the gene-environment interaction

Charles Darwin's *theory of evolution by natural selection* underwrites the biological framing: organisms better suited to their environment survive and reproduce (Ch 3.1, "Human Genetics"). The book introduces *genotype* (genetic makeup), *phenotype* (observable inherited characteristics shaped by both genes and environment), dominant and recessive alleles, homozygous and heterozygous genotypes, and *polygenic* traits. Three frameworks are presented for the gene-environment relationship: (a) *range of reaction* — genes set boundaries within which environmental experience determines where the individual falls; (b) *genetic environmental correlation* — bidirectional influence in which genes shape environment-seeking and environment shapes gene expression; (c) *epigenetics* — the same genotype can yield different phenotypes through environmental modulation of gene expression [AP] (Ch 3.1, "Gene-Environment Interactions"). The Tienari et al. (2004) adoption study of schizophrenia is cited approvingly to support a diathesis-stress reading: "both genetic vulnerability and environmental stress are necessary for schizophrenia to develop" [BT] (Ch 3.1).

### Neurons, neurotransmitters, and drug action

The neuron is described in standard anatomical terms — soma, dendrites, axon, terminal buttons, synaptic vesicles, myelin sheath, Nodes of Ranvier (Ch 3.2, "Cells of the Nervous System"). Neuronal communication is "an electrochemical event": the action potential moves electrically down the axon and chemically across the synaptic cleft via neurotransmitter binding to receptors with a "lock-and-key relationship" [V] (Ch 3.2). The action potential is *all-or-none*. Major neurotransmitters and their effects are tabulated: acetylcholine (muscle action, memory); beta-endorphin (pain, pleasure); dopamine (mood, sleep, learning, reward, addiction); GABA (quieting/inhibition); glutamate (memory, learning); norepinephrine (arousal); serotonin (mood, sleep) [V] (Ch 3.2, Table 3.1). Psychoactive drugs act as *agonists* (mimic neurotransmitters), *antagonists* (block them), or *reuptake inhibitors* (prolong their presence in the synaptic cleft); SSRIs are the canonical example of the third class and are "commonly treated" antidepressants for depression linked to reduced serotonin [AP] (Ch 3.2, "Neurotransmitters and Drugs").

### Nervous system organisation and brain structure

The CNS comprises brain and spinal cord; the PNS divides into somatic (voluntary) and autonomic (involuntary), with the autonomic further divided into *sympathetic* (arousal, fight-or-flight) and *parasympathetic* (rest and recovery) (Ch 3.3). The forebrain is divided into four cortical lobes — frontal (motor cortex, prefrontal cortex, Broca's area), parietal (somatosensory cortex), temporal (auditory cortex, Wernicke's area), and occipital (primary visual cortex). The case of Phineas Gage anchors the discussion of frontal-lobe function in impulse control and emotion regulation (Ch 3.4). The case of H.M. anchors the role of the hippocampus in consolidating new explicit memories (Ch 3.4, "The Case of Henry Molaison"). Limbic structures (hippocampus, amygdala, hypothalamus) jointly regulate emotion, memory, and homeostasis.

### Neuroplasticity

*Neuroplasticity* — "how the nervous system can change and adapt" — operates through new synapse creation, pruning of unused synapses, glial cell changes, and even neurogenesis in some areas. The brain is most plastic when young but capable of "remarkable changes later in life" [V] (Ch 3.4, "Neuroplasticity").

### The endocrine system

Hormones are slower and more diffuse than neurotransmitters. Key glands: pituitary (master gland), thyroid (metabolism), pineal (melatonin), adrenal (epinephrine and norepinephrine for stress response), pancreas (insulin and glucagon), gonads (sex hormones) (Ch 3.5).

## Part IV: Consciousness, sensation, and perception (Ch 4–5)

### Consciousness, sleep, and circadian rhythms

*Consciousness* is awareness of internal and external stimuli; *biological rhythms* include 24-hour *circadian rhythms* regulated by the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus, with melatonin from the pineal gland as a key regulator (Ch 4.1). Sleep proceeds through three NREM stages (1, 2, 3) and a REM phase, distinguishable by EEG patterns (alpha → theta → sleep spindles and K-complexes → delta waves; REM resembles waking EEG with eye movements and muscle paralysis) (Ch 4.3). REM is associated with dreaming. Recommended sleep duration declines from 14–17 hours in newborns to 7–9 hours in adults (Ch 4.1, Table 4.1). Sleep deprivation has cognitive, physiological, and emotional consequences. Three theories of sleep function are noted: adaptive (predator avoidance, energy conservation), restorative, and cognitive/memory consolidation (Ch 4.2).

### Substance use and addiction

The DSM-5 frames substance use disorder as compulsive drug use despite adverse consequences, involving physical dependence (tolerance, withdrawal) and psychological dependence (craving) (Ch 4.5). Drug categories include depressants (alcohol, barbiturates, benzodiazepines — GABA agonists), stimulants (cocaine, amphetamines, MDMA, caffeine, nicotine — most acting on dopamine), opioids (heroin, morphine, oxycodone — endogenous opioid receptor agonists), and hallucinogens (LSD, psilocybin, mescaline — varied mechanisms). The text discusses the opioid epidemic in detail, attributing initial driver to pharmaceutical-company false claims of non-addictiveness [AP] (Ch 4.5, "The Opioid Crisis").

### Sensation, perception, and Gestalt

The text distinguishes *sensation* (sensory receptor activation) from *perception* (interpretation), "the way sensory information is organized, interpreted, and consciously experienced" [V] (Ch 5.1, "Perception"). Perception involves both *bottom-up* (data-driven) and *top-down* (knowledge- and expectation-driven) processing. Attention shapes perception; the Simons and Chabris (1999) gorilla study demonstrates *inattentional blindness*. Cultural background shapes perception (the Müller-Lyer illusion is stronger in "carpentered world" Western cultures than in the Zulu villages of round huts, Ch 5.1).

Gestalt principles for organising sensory input into perceptual wholes include *figure-ground relationship*, *proximity*, *similarity*, *good continuation* (continuity), and *closure*. "The whole is different from the sum of its parts" is the central Gestalt thesis [AP] (Ch 5.6, "Gestalt Principles of Perception"). Perceptual hypotheses — "educated guesses that we make while interpreting sensory information" — are shaped by personality, experience, and expectation, and can produce systematic biases including racially-biased weapon detection in laboratory tasks [AP] (Ch 5.6, "The Depths of Perception").

### Signal detection and depth perception

*Signal detection theory* describes the ability to identify a stimulus embedded in distracting background noise (Ch 5.1). Depth perception relies on binocular cues (binocular disparity, convergence) and monocular cues (linear perspective, interposition, relative size, closeness to horizon) (Ch 5.3).

## Part V: Learning (Ch 6)

### Three models

*Classical conditioning* (Pavlov) pairs a neutral stimulus with an unconditioned stimulus; the previously neutral stimulus becomes a conditioned stimulus eliciting a conditioned response. Higher-order (second-order) conditioning extends the chain. Acquisition, extinction, spontaneous recovery, stimulus discrimination, and stimulus generalisation are the central process concepts. Watson and Rayner's "Little Albert" experiment demonstrated classical conditioning of fear in a human infant (Ch 6.2). Taste aversion is presented as biologically prepared learning that can occur in a single trial across long delays (Garcia & Koelling) [BT] (Ch 6.2, "General Processes in Classical Conditioning").

*Operant conditioning* (Skinner via Thorndike's law of effect) shapes behaviour by consequences. The 2x2 reinforcement-punishment matrix is presented: positive reinforcement (add desirable stimulus, increase behaviour); negative reinforcement (remove undesirable stimulus, increase behaviour); positive punishment (add undesirable stimulus, decrease behaviour); negative punishment (remove desirable stimulus, decrease behaviour) [AP] (Ch 6.3, Table 6.2). The text takes a clear position: "today's psychologists and parenting experts favor reinforcement over punishment—they recommend that you catch your child doing something good and reward them for it" [V] (Ch 6.3, "Punishment").

### Reinforcement schedules and their effects

| Schedule | Description | Result | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fixed interval | Reinforcement after predictable time | Moderate response with significant pauses after reinforcement | Patient-controlled, doctor-timed pain relief |
| Variable interval | Reinforcement at unpredictable time | Moderate, steady response | Checking social media |
| Fixed ratio | Reinforcement after predictable number of responses | High response with pauses after reinforcement | Piecework |
| Variable ratio | Reinforcement after unpredictable number of responses | Highest and steady response, most resistant to extinction | Gambling |

[V] (Ch 6.3, Table 6.3). Variable-ratio schedules produce the most extinction-resistant behaviour, which the text uses to explain gambling addiction. *Shaping* uses successive approximations toward a target behaviour. *Latent learning* (Tolman) demonstrates cognitive maps form even without reinforcement.

### Observational learning (modelling)

Bandura's *social learning theory* extends behaviourism with cognition: learning through *modelling* depends on attention, retention, reproduction, and motivation. *Vicarious reinforcement* and *vicarious punishment* shape whether observers imitate. The Bobo doll experiments demonstrated that children imitate aggressive adult models, especially when models are not punished [BT] (Ch 6.4). The text identifies live, verbal, and symbolic models and notes prosocial and antisocial implications. Research suggests violent media may have antisocial effects, though the causal-correlational question remains contested in the literature [AP] (Ch 6.4).

## Part VI: Cognition, intelligence, and judgement (Ch 7)

### Concepts, prototypes, and schemata

*Cognition* is "thinking, including perception, learning, problem solving, judgment, and memory" [V] (Ch 7.1, "Cognition"). Concepts are "categories or groupings of linguistic information, images, ideas, or memories"; *prototypes* are best examples; *natural concepts* form through experience and *artificial concepts* through definition [AP] (Ch 7.1). *Schemata* are clusters of related concepts that organise memory and inference; they include *role schemata* (assumptions about role-occupants) and *event schemata* (cognitive scripts for how sequences typically unfold). Schemata speed processing but produce systematic errors when the assumed pattern does not fit.

### Problem-solving strategies

The text distinguishes *trial and error*, *algorithms* (step-by-step procedures producing the same result every time), and *heuristics* (general problem-solving frameworks; mental shortcuts) (Ch 7.3). Heuristics save time but "are not always the best method for making a rational decision" [V]. Five conditions invoke heuristic use: information overload, time pressure, low-stakes decisions, limited information, and a heuristic that comes to mind. *Working backwards* (start from the goal) and decomposition into sub-tasks are presented as useful general heuristics.

### Pitfalls — the bias inventory

The text catalogues five decision biases (Ch 7.3, Table 7.3):

- *Anchoring bias*: "tendency to focus on one particular piece of information when making decisions or problem-solving" [V].
- *Confirmation bias*: "focuses on information that confirms existing beliefs" [V].
- *Hindsight bias*: "belief that the event just experienced was predictable" [V].
- *Representative bias*: "making a decision or judgment based on perceived similarity to existing stereotypes or prior beliefs" [V].
- *Availability heuristic*: "decision is based upon either an available precedent or an example that may be faulty" [V].

Two additional pitfalls are *mental set* (persisting with a previously successful approach that is no longer working) and *functional fixedness* (inability to perceive an object being used for something other than its designed purpose; Duncker's candle problem is the canonical example, Ch 7.3, "Pitfalls to Problem Solving").

### Intelligence — competing theories

Spearman proposed a general intelligence factor *g*. Cattell distinguished *crystallized intelligence* (acquired knowledge and retrieval) from *fluid intelligence* (complex relationships, novel problem-solving) [AP] (Ch 7.4). Sternberg's *triarchic theory* identifies practical, analytical, and creative intelligence. Gardner's *Multiple Intelligences Theory* identifies eight intelligences (linguistic, logical-mathematical, musical, bodily-kinesthetic, spatial, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic) but the text notes Gardner's framework "has been heavily criticized for lacking empirical evidence" [V] (Ch 7.4). *Emotional intelligence* combines Gardner's inter- and intrapersonal categories. The *Cattell-Horn-Carroll (CHC) theory* is described as "the most comprehensive theory of intelligence to date" [V] (Ch 7.4).

### IQ measurement

Galton initiated broad intelligence testing; Binet developed the first reliable test for the French school system; Terman standardised it as the Stanford-Binet at Stanford; Wechsler's WAIS, WISC-V, and WPPSI-IV are now widely used (Ch 7.5). Test scores follow a bell curve with mean 100 and standard deviation 15; 68 per cent of the population scores between 85 and 115; 2.2 per cent score below 70 (one criterion for intellectual disability with adaptive deficits before age 18) (Ch 7.5). The *Flynn effect* describes the rise in average IQ scores across generations. Research indicates intelligence has both heritable and environmental components; childhood poverty has been documented to reduce prefrontal function in ways comparable to lesion damage (Kishiyama et al., 2009) [BT] (Ch 7.6, "High Intelligence: Nature or Nurture?").

### Creativity, divergent thinking

*Divergent thinking* arrives at "unique, multiple solutions to a given problem"; *convergent thinking* arrives at "a correct or well-established answer" [V] (Ch 7.4, "Creativity").

## Part VII: Memory (Ch 8)

### The Atkinson-Shiffrin model and Baddeley's working memory

Memory operates through encoding, storage, and retrieval (Ch 8.1). The Atkinson-Shiffrin model proposes three stages — sensory memory (brief), short-term memory (15–30 seconds, capacity 7±2 items per Miller; revised down to 4±1 per Cowan), and long-term memory (effectively unlimited) — with rehearsal moving information from short-term to long-term. Baddeley and Hitch's working memory model extends this with a central executive directing a phonological loop, visuospatial sketchpad, and episodic buffer. Encoding can be visual, acoustic, or *semantic*; semantic encoding produces the strongest memory traces, especially when augmented by the *self-reference effect* (information related to oneself is better remembered) (Ch 8.1).

### Long-term memory architecture

Explicit (declarative) memory subdivides into *episodic* (events you have experienced, what-where-when) and *semantic* (facts and concepts). Implicit memory subdivides into *procedural* (how-to skills), *priming*, and *emotional conditioning* (Ch 8.1, Figure 8.6).

### Brain regions involved in memory

The amygdala attaches emotional value to memory, especially fear; the hippocampus consolidates new declarative memories (loss in H.M. produced anterograde amnesia); the cerebellum supports procedural and classical-conditioning memories; the prefrontal cortex is involved in semantic encoding and retrieval (Ch 8.2).

### Errors, distortions, and the seven sins

Schacter's *seven sins of memory* fall into three groups (Ch 8.3, Table 8.1):

- Forgetting: *transience* (decay), *absentmindedness* (encoding failure from divided attention), *blocking* (tip-of-the-tongue).
- Distortion: *misattribution* (confusing source), *suggestibility* (false memory from external misinformation), *bias* (current beliefs reshape memories).
- Intrusion: *persistence* (intrusive unwanted memories).

Loftus's *misinformation effect paradigm* demonstrates that leading questions can alter eyewitness memory: in the classic Loftus and Palmer (1974) study, subjects who heard "smashed" estimated higher car speeds and falsely remembered seeing broken glass at higher rates than subjects who heard "contacted" [BT] (Ch 8.3, "The Misinformation Effect"). Eyewitness misidentification is a leading cause of wrongful convictions, illustrated in detail by the Ronald Cotton case (Ch 8.3, "Eyewitness Misidentification"). *Proactive interference* (old information hinders new) and *retroactive interference* (new information hinders old) both impair retrieval (Ch 8.3, "Interference"). Ebbinghaus's forgetting curve (1885): about 50 per cent of new information is lost after 20 minutes and 70 per cent after 24 hours [BT] (Ch 8.3, "Memory Errors").

### Memory enhancement

Effective study techniques the text recommends: elaborative rehearsal (linking new information to existing knowledge), self-reference effect, distributed practice rather than cramming, mnemonic devices (acronyms, acrostics, jingles, the method of loci/memory palace), expressive writing, and saying words aloud. The text recommends regular aerobic exercise (which "promotes neurogenesis") and adequate sleep (during which "the brain organizes and consolidates information") [V] (Ch 8.4, "How to Study Effectively").

## Part VIII: Lifespan development (Ch 9)

### Three domains, three tensions, two debates

Development unfolds across physical, cognitive, and psychosocial domains. The text discusses three central tensions in developmental theory: continuous versus discontinuous (gradual change versus stage-based change); one course versus many (universality of stages versus cultural and individual variation); and nature versus nurture (genetic versus environmental influence) (Ch 9.1).

### Stage theories

Freud's *psychosexual development* (oral, anal, phallic, latency, genital) is presented but the text notes that "most of Freud's ideas have not found support in modern research" [V] (Ch 9.2). Erikson's *psychosocial development* identifies eight stages each centred on a developmental task: trust vs mistrust (0–1), autonomy vs shame/doubt (1–3), initiative vs guilt (3–6), industry vs inferiority (7–11), identity vs confusion (12–18), intimacy vs isolation (19–29), generativity vs stagnation (30–64), integrity vs despair (65+) [AP] (Ch 9.2, Table 9.1). Piaget's *cognitive development* identifies four stages defined by qualitative shifts in reasoning: sensorimotor (0–2; object permanence develops between 5 and 8 months), preoperational (2–7; symbolic representation, but egocentrism and lack of conservation), concrete operational (7–11; logical thought about concrete events, conservation, reversibility), formal operational (12+; abstract and hypothetical reasoning) [AP] (Ch 9.2, Table 9.2). Some contemporary researchers propose a fifth, postformal stage in which decisions integrate logic with emotion and context. Kohlberg's *moral development* proceeds from pre-conventional to conventional to post-conventional reasoning; Gilligan critiqued Kohlberg for sampling bias (only male and upper-class subjects) and proposed that women reason more in terms of relational connection (Ch 9.2).

### Vygotsky's sociocultural theory

Vygotsky proposed that "human development is rooted in one's culture": language and thought emerge from the social world rather than from purely internal processes [V] (Ch 9.2, "Sociocultural Theory of Development").

### Attachment

Harlow's monkey experiments demonstrated that contact comfort (cloth surrogate) outweighed nourishment (wire surrogate) in producing attachment, contradicting the prevailing assumption that attachment was solely about feeding [BT] (Ch 9.3, "Attachment"). Bowlby developed *attachment theory* and the concept of *secure base*. Ainsworth's *Strange Situation* identified three attachment styles (secure, avoidant, resistant); Main and Solomon added *disorganized* (often seen in abused children).

### Parenting styles

Baumrind's typology: *authoritative* (warm, demanding, flexible — strongest predictor of child outcomes in American samples), *authoritarian* (cold, demanding, inflexible), *permissive* (warm, undemanding), *uninvolved/neglectful* (cold, undemanding). The text qualifies that "effective parenting styles vary as a function of culture and… the authoritative style is not necessarily preferred or appropriate in all cultures" [V] (Ch 9.3). Children's *temperament* (easy, difficult, slow to warm up; Thomas and Chess) interacts with parenting in predicting outcomes.

### Emerging adulthood and the lifespan

The text adopts Arnett's *emerging adulthood* concept (18 to mid-20s) as a culturally constructed developmental period in industrialised societies, reflecting prolonged education, delayed marriage, and labour-market shifts [BT] (Ch 9.3, "Emerging Adulthood"). Adulthood is divided into early (20s–early 40s), middle (40s–60s), and late (60+). Crystallised intelligence remains stable to mid-50s; fluid intelligence declines with age but mental and physical activity buffer the decline. Carstensen's *socioemotional selectivity theory* predicts that as people age they prefer fewer but closer relationships [BT] (Ch 9.3).

### Death and dying

Kübler-Ross's five stages of grief (denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance) are described as a common but not universal sequence. Hospice care and advance directives (living wills, DNR orders, healthcare proxies) are discussed (Ch 9.4).

## Part IX: Motivation and emotion (Ch 10)

### Motivation theories

*Motivation* describes "the wants or needs that direct behavior toward a goal" [V] (Ch 10.1). Intrinsic motivation arises from internal factors (personal satisfaction); extrinsic motivation arises from external factors (rewards from others). The *overjustification effect* describes how extrinsic rewards can erode intrinsic motivation: an activity once enjoyed for its own sake can be reclassified as work when extrinsic reinforcement is added [AP] (Ch 10.1). The text qualifies: tangible rewards (money) tend to undermine intrinsic motivation more than verbal praise; expected rewards undermine more than surprise rewards; cultural context matters (collectivistic cultures motivate more through group purpose).

Theoretical traditions include *instinct theory* (James, controversial), *drive theory* (homeostatic deviations create needs that direct behaviour back to homeostasis), *arousal theory* (the *Yerkes-Dodson law*: optimal arousal varies with task complexity — simple tasks favour higher arousal, complex tasks favour lower), and *self-efficacy theory* (Bandura: belief in one's capability to complete a task drives goal-setting and persistence) [AP] (Ch 10.1). Murray catalogued social motives including needs for achievement, affiliation, and intimacy.

### Maslow's hierarchy of needs

Maslow's hierarchy organises motives from physiological needs through safety, love and belonging, esteem, and self-actualisation, with later additions of cognitive, aesthetic, and self-transcendence needs. Maslow argued lower-level needs must be met before higher-level needs become motivating, though the text notes that the theory "has been criticized for its subjective nature and its inability to account for phenomena that occur in the real world" [V] (Ch 10.1). People sometimes sacrifice lower-level needs to pursue higher purposes (Gandhi's hunger strikes are cited).

### Hunger, eating, and body weight

Hunger and satiety are regulated by the hypothalamus integrating signals from blood glucose, the gut (cholecystokinin, ghrelin), and fat cells (leptin). The *set-point theory* of body weight regulation has limited empirical support. Obesity is presented as multi-causal (genetic, environmental, socioeconomic) and is associated with multiple health risks; the text notes the American Medical Association's 2023 recommendation that BMI be de-emphasised because it does not account for body composition or racial/ethnic variation (Ch 10.2).

### Emotion theories

*Emotion* is "a subjective state of being that we often describe as our feelings… result[ing] from the combination of subjective experience, expression, cognitive appraisal, and physiological responses" [V] (Ch 10.4). Emotion is distinguished from *mood* (longer-lasting, less intense, less object-directed). Major theories of emotion (Ch 10.4, Figure 10.21):

- *James-Lange*: physiological arousal causes emotion ("we are afraid because we tremble").
- *Cannon-Bard*: physiological arousal and emotional experience occur simultaneously and independently.
- *Schachter-Singer two-factor*: physiological arousal is interpreted in context to produce emotion (cognitive label is essential).
- *Lazarus's cognitive-mediational*: appraisal precedes both cognitive label and emotion; appraisal is often immediate and unconscious.
- *Barrett's constructed emotion*: emotions are concept-based predictions that construct experience; "Two identical physiological states can result in different emotional states depending on your predictions" [AP] (Ch 10.4).
- *Zajonc and LeDoux*: some emotional responses (especially fear) bypass cortical interpretation, processed via direct thalamus-to-amygdala pathways.

### Facial expression and cultural display rules

Ekman documented universal facial expressions of seven basic emotions (happiness, surprise, sadness, fright, disgust, contempt, anger) recognisable across cultures, including in pre-literate New Guinea [BT] (Ch 10.4). *Cultural display rules* govern when and how expressions are appropriate: the text cites Matsumoto's findings that Japanese individuals suppress negative emotional displays in social contexts more than Americans (Ch 10.4).

## Part X: Personality (Ch 11)

### Five major perspectives

Psychodynamic (Freud and successors) — id/ego/superego, defence mechanisms (repression, reaction formation, regression, projection, rationalisation, displacement, sublimation), psychosexual stages (Ch 11.2). Neo-Freudian — Adler (inferiority complex, social motives, birth order), Erikson (psychosocial stages across lifespan), Jung (collective unconscious, archetypes, introversion-extroversion as foundational dimensions), Horney (basic anxiety, three coping styles: moving toward, against, away from people; rejection of penis envy in favour of culturally-rooted gender differences) (Ch 11.3). Learning approaches — behaviourist (Skinner: personality as shaped behaviour), social-cognitive (Bandura: reciprocal determinism, observational learning, self-efficacy), Rotter's locus of control (internal vs external), Mischel's person-situation debate and the marshmallow test of self-regulation [AP] (Ch 11.4). Humanistic (Maslow's hierarchy and self-actualisation; Rogers's ideal self vs real self, congruence, unconditional positive regard) (Ch 11.5). Biological/trait — temperament (Thomas and Chess), Eysencks' two-factor (later three-factor) model, the Big Five / Five Factor Model (Ch 11.6–7).

### The Big Five

The Five Factor Model — openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, neuroticism (mnemonic: OCEAN) — is described as "the most popular theory in personality psychology today and the most accurate approximation of the basic personality dimensions" [V] (Ch 11.7). Conscientiousness shows positive correlation with academic and career success; the factors are relatively stable across the lifespan but conscientiousness and agreeableness tend to increase with age while neuroticism and extraversion tend to decline [AP] (Ch 11.7). The Big Five has been validated across many cultures, with some between-culture variation in distribution. The HEXACO model adds an honesty-humility dimension (Ch 11.7).

### Cultural understandings of personality

Individualistic cultures (United States, England, Australia) emphasise independence, competition, and personal achievement; collectivistic cultures (many Asian, African, and South American cultures) emphasise group harmony, social duty, and interdependence (Ch 11.8). Yang (2006) found people in individualistic cultures display more personally oriented traits and people in collectivistic cultures display more socially oriented traits [BT]. The text identifies three approaches to cross-cultural personality research — the cultural-comparative approach (testing Western models in other cultures), the indigenous approach (developing culture-specific instruments), and the combined approach.

### Personality assessment

The text distinguishes self-report inventories (MMPI-2 with 567 items and 10 clinical scales, including validity scales like the L Scale to detect "faking good"; 16PF; Big Five questionnaires) from projective tests (Rorschach inkblot test; Thematic Apperception Test; Rotter Incomplete Sentence Blank). Projective tests are less subject to intentional distortion but are time-consuming for the evaluator and have weaker validity. Culturally-specific projective tests (Williams's *Contemporized-Themes Concerning Blacks Test*; the *TEMAS Multicultural Thematic Apperception Test* for Hispanic youth) address cross-cultural test bias (Ch 11.9).

## Part XI: Social psychology (Ch 12)

### Situation versus disposition; the fundamental attribution error

Social psychology is "the study of how people affect one another" with attention to "the power of the situation" [V] (Ch 12.1). *Situationism* attributes behaviour to immediate environment; *dispositionism* attributes behaviour to internal traits and personality. The *fundamental attribution error* is the tendency to overweight dispositional factors and underweight situational factors when explaining others' behaviour. The Ross, Amabile, and Steinmetz (1977) quizmaster study is the classic demonstration: observers rated questioners as more knowledgeable than contestants despite the obvious situational asymmetry [BT] (Ch 12.1). The *halo effect* extends single positive attributes to overall character.

The fundamental attribution error is "much more common in individualistic cultures (e.g., United States, Canada, United Kingdom) than collectivistic cultures (e.g., Japan, China, Korea, India, Latin American countries, African countries)" because the latter "focus on the group more than on the individual" [AP] (Ch 12.1, "Is the Fundamental Attribution Error a Universal Phenomenon?"). The *actor-observer bias* describes the corollary asymmetry: we attribute others' behaviour dispositionally and our own behaviour situationally. *Self-serving bias* attributes our successes to disposition and our failures to situation, protecting self-esteem. The *just-world hypothesis* — the belief that people get what they deserve — leads to victim-blaming.

### Self-presentation: roles, norms, scripts

A *social role* is "a pattern of behavior that is expected of a person in a given setting or group" [V] (Ch 12.2). *Social norms* are group expectations of appropriate behaviour. *Scripts* are knowledge of expected event sequences. The Stanford Prison Experiment (Zimbardo, 1971) demonstrated the power of role assignment but has been criticised on ethical grounds and for design flaws including explicit guidance to guards; subsequent attempts have not fully replicated the original findings (Ch 12.2).

### Attitudes and persuasion

An *attitude* is an evaluation of a person, idea, or object with cognitive, affective, and behavioural components (Ch 12.3). *Cognitive dissonance* (Festinger) is psychological discomfort arising from inconsistent attitudes, behaviours, or cognitions; reduction may involve changing the behaviour, changing the cognition, or adding new cognitions. The *justification of effort* effect (Aronson and Mills, 1959) explains why difficult initiations increase liking for groups [BT] (Ch 12.3, "The Effect of Initiation").

Persuasion mechanisms include the Yale attitude change approach (Hovland: source credibility and attractiveness; message subtlety, sidedness, and timing; audience attention, intelligence, self-esteem, and age) and the *elaboration likelihood model* (Petty and Cacioppo: *central route* uses logic and data, requiring an analytical and motivated audience, producing durable change; *peripheral route* uses cues like celebrity endorsement and emotional association, requiring less effort but producing less durable change) [AP] (Ch 12.3). The *foot-in-the-door* technique exploits consistency: agreement to a small request increases the likelihood of agreement to a larger request (Freedman and Fraser, 1966) [BT].

### Conformity, compliance, obedience

Asch's line-judgement studies showed 76 per cent of participants conformed to group pressure on at least one trial despite the obvious correct answer; conformity is moderated by majority size (up to about seven), the presence of a dissenter (drops conformity to near zero), and public versus private response (private reduces conformity) [BT] (Ch 12.4). *Normative social influence* is conformity to fit in; *informational social influence* is conformity to learn the right answer. Milgram's obedience studies showed 65 per cent of participants administered what they believed to be lethal shocks under instruction from a researcher in a lab coat; obedience varied with proximity, authority of the experimenter, and physical setting [BT].

### Group dynamics

*Groupthink* (Janis): groups prioritise cohesion and unanimity over critical thinking, leading to faulty decisions; symptoms include perceived invulnerability, moral certainty, self-censorship, illusion of unanimity, and stereotyping out-groups. Preventatives include external opinions, private voting, leaders withholding initial positions, and contingency planning [AP] (Ch 12.4). *Group polarisation*: discussion strengthens initial group attitudes. *Social loafing*: reduced individual effort in pooled tasks; mitigated by individual evaluation, smaller groups, and challenging tasks; collectivistic cultures show less social loafing. *Deindividuation*: anonymity in groups reduces accountability.

### Prejudice and discrimination

*Prejudice* is a negative attitude toward a group; *stereotype* is a cognitive belief about group members; *discrimination* is negative action based on group membership (Ch 12.5, Table 12.3). Forms include racism, sexism, ageism, homophobia, and transphobia. The *dual attitudes model* distinguishes explicit (conscious, controllable) from implicit (unconscious, automatic) attitudes; modern measures often reveal implicit bias even where explicit bias is suppressed [AP] (Ch 12.5). *Self-fulfilling prophecy* and *confirmation bias* maintain stereotypes. *In-group bias* and *scapegoating* sustain inter-group hostility. The Robbers Cave study (Sherif et al., 1961) and the jigsaw classroom (Aronson) demonstrate that *superordinate goals* requiring cooperation between groups reduce prejudice [BT] (Ch 12.6).

### Aggression and prosocial behaviour

*Aggression* divides into *hostile* (anger-driven) and *instrumental* (goal-driven). Bullying involves repeated negative treatment with a power imbalance; cyberbullying is mediated by digital technology and adds anonymity and inescapability. The *bystander effect* (Latané and Darley) describes reduced help-giving as the number of witnesses increases, due to *diffusion of responsibility* [BT] (Ch 12.6). The Kitty Genovese case popularised the bystander concept although later reporting clarified that bystanders did intervene.

*Prosocial behaviour* is voluntary helping; *altruism* is helping others when costs outweigh benefits to oneself. Empathy is a key motivator. The text notes ongoing debate about whether pure altruism exists or whether helping is always partly self-serving (Ch 12.7).

### Forming relationships

Proximity and similarity are the strongest predictors of relationship formation; *homophily* (preference for similar others) limits diversity exposure (Ch 12.7). Reciprocity and self-disclosure maintain relationships. Sternberg's *triangular theory of love* identifies three components — intimacy, passion, commitment — and seven combinations including consummate love (all three). *Social exchange theory* models relationship satisfaction as a cost-benefit calculation [BT].

## Part XII: Industrial and organisational psychology (Ch 13)

### What I-O psychology studies

I-O psychology "studies how human behavior and psychology affect work and how they are affected by work" [V] (Ch 13.1). The field has three major divisions: *industrial psychology* (job analysis, selection, training, performance evaluation), *organisational psychology* (job satisfaction, motivation, leadership, organisational culture, teamwork), and *human factors psychology* (the worker-machine interface; ergonomics in European usage) (Ch 13.1, Table 13.1). Subfields include *occupational health psychology* (stress and well-being at work) and *humanitarian work psychology* (helping marginalised populations access work).

### History

Early contributors include Cattell, Münsterberg, Scott, Yerkes, Bingham, and Lillian Gilbreth. WWI catalysed personnel selection (Army Alpha and Army Beta tests); WWII expanded the field further. The Hawthorne studies (Mayo et al. at Western Electric, 1929–32) shifted attention from physical work conditions to psychological and social factors — and produced the *Hawthorne effect*: "the increase in performance of individuals who are aware they are being observed by researchers or supervisors" [V] (Ch 13.1, "Hawthorne Effect"). Frederick Taylor's *Principles of Scientific Management* (1911) emphasised time-motion studies and standardisation; his approach was criticised for treating workers as inherently lazy. Lillian Gilbreth — "the mother of modern management" — extended efficiency methods to home, office, and shop and authored *The Psychology of Management* (1914).

### Selection and evaluation

*Job analysis* is "accurately describing the task or job" via task-oriented and worker-oriented descriptions [V] (Ch 13.2). The U.S. Department of Labor's O*Net database catalogues KSAs (knowledge, skills, abilities) for thousands of occupations. Personality testing is used in selection but should be validated against job performance — too high agreeableness, for example, may impede a customer-service role where customer misperception must sometimes be corrected (Ch 13.2). Cutoff scores raise legal and ethical questions, illustrated by the Robert Jordan case in which a high IQ score disqualified a police-officer applicant.

Interviews divide into *unstructured* and *structured*; meta-analysis (McDaniel et al., 1994) shows structured interviews are more predictive of job performance [BT] (Ch 13.2). Nonverbal cues (eye contact, smiling), self-promotion behaviours, and first-impression effects influence interviewer ratings. Training begins with orientation (covering policies and *organisational culture*) and continues through formal training and informal *mentoring*. Mentoring shows significant but small positive effects on protégé compensation, promotions, and satisfaction; pairings of same gender or race/ethnicity tend to produce better psychosocial outcomes, while cross-pairings tend to produce greater career advancement and pay (Ch 13.2, "Training").

*Performance appraisals* use various methods including 360-degree feedback (input from supervisors, peers, subordinates, self, and sometimes customers). Performance appraisal systems are often used incorrectly or fit poorly with organisational culture, undermining their value (Ch 13.2, "Evaluating Employees").

### Bias and protections in hiring

The text catalogues U.S. anti-discrimination law: Title VII of the Civil Rights Act (1964) covers equal employment opportunity; the Pregnancy Discrimination Act (1978); the Equal Pay Act; the Age Discrimination in Employment Act; the Americans with Disabilities Act (1990). The 2020 Supreme Court ruling extended Civil Rights Act protections to sexual orientation and gender identity. *Bona fide occupational qualifications* (BFOQs) provide narrow exceptions, decided by the courts case-by-case (Ch 13.2).

### Organisational psychology

*Job satisfaction* is "the degree to which individuals enjoy their job" [V] (Ch 13.3). Work-content factors (variety, challenge, role clarity) are the strongest predictors of satisfaction; pay shows only weak correlation. Job satisfaction is reciprocally related to life satisfaction and predicts organisational citizenship and reduced absenteeism. Job stress is "the perception and response of an individual to events judged as overwhelming or threatening to the individual's well-being" [V] (Ch 13.3). Sources include role ambiguity, lack of progress, lack of control, isolation, work overload, discrimination, harassment, and bullying. Downsizing and mergers create predictable stress patterns affecting both departing and remaining employees; I-O psychologists are often involved in managing these transitions.

*Work-family balance* identifies time-, strain-, and behaviour-based conflicts in either direction (work to family or family to work). Lapierre and Allen (2006) found practical home support and supervisor support more effective than emotional support in reducing work-family conflict; flextime alone did not help and telecommuting actually worsened conflict in some contexts because home demands intensify when the worker is physically present at home [BT] (Ch 13.3).

### Management styles

McGregor's *Theory X* (managers assume workers dislike work and need control) versus *Theory Y* (managers assume workers seek inner satisfaction and respond to participation) is contrasted as the central management dichotomy (Ch 13.3, Table 13.3). Clifton's *strengths-based management* focuses on building on strengths rather than correcting weaknesses, though Kaiser and Overfield (2011) note that managers tend to overuse strengths and neglect weaknesses, both of which interfere with performance.

### Leadership styles

Bass distinguishes *transactional leadership* (focused on supervision and reward-punishment exchanges; status-quo-oriented; managerial in flavour) from *transformational leadership* (charismatic, inspirational, intellectually stimulating, considerate; oriented toward change and elevated performance) [AP] (Ch 13.3). Eagly et al.'s meta-analyses found women tend slightly more toward transformational and interpersonal leadership while men tend slightly more toward transactional and task-oriented leadership, though differences are small and contextual. Eagly et al. (2003) note transformational approaches are more effective than transactional ones, though most leaders use elements of both. *Followership* research is emerging, examining the leader-follower relationship from the follower's side and acknowledging that follower characteristics (extraversion, growth needs, need for structure) shape what works.

### Teams and organisational culture

Team types: problem resolution, creative, tactical, virtual. Team performance is undermined by social loafing, communication failures, conformity effects, and within-group conflict; the *team halo effect* gives teams credit for successes but blames individuals for failures (Ch 13.3). Hoogendoorn et al. (2013) found gender-balanced teams outperformed male-dominated teams on profit and sales metrics [BT].

*Organisational culture* "encompasses the values, visions, hierarchies, norms, and interactions among its employees" [V] (Ch 13.3, "Organizational Culture"). Ostroff et al. identify three layers: observable artifacts, espoused values, and basic assumptions. Diversity training aims to bridge cultural differences; Pettigrew and Tropp's meta-analysis confirms that contact between groups reduces prejudice when contact occurs under conditions of equal status, common goals, cooperation, and institutional support [BT].

### Sexual harassment and workplace violence

*Sexual harassment* per the EEOC: "Unwelcome sexual advances, requests for sexual favors, and other verbal or physical conduct of a sexual nature" that "explicitly or implicitly affects an individual's employment, unreasonably interferes with an individual's work performance, or creates an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment" [V] (Ch 13.3). *Quid pro quo* involves rewards-for-favours; *hostile environment* involves a pattern of offensive conduct.

*Workplace violence* — verbal abuse, threats, physical assault, and homicide — is the second leading cause of death in the workplace and the leading cause of death for women at work. *Procedural justice* — fairness of the processes determining outcomes — predicts retaliation and aggression; Greenberg's experiments showed that adequate explanations of pay decisions reduced theft compared to curt explanations [BT] (Ch 13.3, "Violence in the Workplace").

### Human factors / ergonomics

Human factors psychology focuses on integrating worker, machine, and environment to improve safety, productivity, and health. Areas include attention, cognitive engineering, task analysis, and cognitive task analysis (Ch 13.4, Table 13.4). Checklists are a standard intervention (airline pre-flight, WHO surgical safety checklist). Bruno and Abrahão (2012) found that as the volume of decisions per day increased among security analysts at a Brazilian bank, false alarms increased while missed real intrusions did not — illuminating a key trade-off in cognitive workload design [BT].

## Part XIII: Stress, lifestyle, and health (Ch 14)

### Defining stress

*Stress* is "a process whereby an individual perceives and responds to events that they appraise as overwhelming or threatening to their well-being" [V] (Ch 14.1, citing Lazarus and Folkman, 1984). *Stressors* are the demanding events. Lazarus's appraisal model distinguishes *primary appraisal* (judgement of harm or threat) and *secondary appraisal* (judgement of options to cope and their effectiveness). The text takes the view that "stress is largely in the eye of the beholder: it's not so much what happens to you as it is how you respond" [V] (Ch 14.1).

*Eustress* is moderate, motivating stress associated with optimal performance; *distress* is excessive, debilitating stress that erodes performance and health (Ch 14.1, "Good Stress?"). The Yerkes-Dodson-like inverted U applies.

### Physiological mechanisms

Cannon described the *fight-or-flight response*: sympathetic nervous system activation produces pupil dilation, heart-rate increase, blood-pressure rise, glucose release, and muscle tension. Selye's *general adaptation syndrome* describes three stages: *alarm reaction* (immediate), *stage of resistance* (sustained adaptation), *stage of exhaustion* (depleted resources, illness or death) [AP] (Ch 14.1). The *hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis* releases cortisol; sustained elevated cortisol weakens immune function (Ch 14.1, "Physiological Basis of Stress").

### Stressor categories

Traumatic events (combat, assault, disaster); life changes (Holmes and Rahe's *Social Readjustment Rating Scale* with 43 events scored in life-change units; death of spouse rates 100 LCUs); daily hassles (better predictors of physical and psychological health than major life events per DeLongis et al., 1982); occupation-related stressors. *Job strain* combines high demands with low control and elevates risk of hypertension, heart attack, weight gain, and depression. *Job burnout* — emotional exhaustion, depersonalisation, and reduced personal accomplishment — affects human-services workers most often and co-occurs with depression (Ch 14.2).

### Stress and illness

Chronic activation of stress responses produces *psychophysiological disorders* — physical conditions caused or worsened by emotional factors. Major categories: cardiovascular (hypertension, coronary heart disease), gastrointestinal, respiratory (asthma), musculoskeletal, skin (Ch 14.3, Table 14.3). Friedman and Rosenman's *Type A* (intensely driven, time-urgent, hostile) versus *Type B* (relaxed) framework was an early heart-disease risk model; subsequent research isolated *anger and hostility* as the most predictive components of Type A's link to cardiovascular disease [AP] (Ch 14.3, "Are You Type A or Type B?"). *Negative affectivity* (a tendency to experience distressed emotional states) and depression are also significant cardiovascular risk factors.

The immune system can be disrupted by stress; *psychoneuroimmunology* studies these links. Chronic stress slows wound healing, weakens vaccine response, and increases susceptibility to infection (Cohen et al., 1998: subjects with chronic stress lasting more than a month were considerably more likely to develop colds when exposed to rhinovirus) [BT] (Ch 14.3). Stress can shorten *telomeres*, accelerating cellular aging; this effect has been demonstrated in caregivers, victims of childhood violence, and even in young adults whose mothers experienced severe stress during pregnancy [BT] (Ch 14.3, "Stress and Aging").

### Asthma and tension headaches

Psychological factors are significant triggers of asthma exacerbation in many sufferers; high negative emotion is associated with airway obstruction. Tension headaches result from muscle tension and stress-elevated pain sensitivity (Ch 14.3).

### Coping and regulation

Lazarus and Folkman's two coping styles: *problem-focused coping* (direct action against the stressor; effective for controllable stressors) and *emotion-focused coping* (managing one's emotional response; effective for uncontrollable stressors like bereavement) (Ch 14.4). *Perceived control* — Bandura: "the intensity and chronicity of human stress is governed largely by perceived control over the demands of one's life" — predicts better health, lower reactivity, and lower mortality [V] (Ch 14.4). Higher socioeconomic status correlates with greater perceived control and better health outcomes.

*Social support* — "the soothing impact of friends, family, and acquaintances" — has been documented since the 1970s as a major predictor of health and longevity [V] (Ch 14.4). The Holt-Lunstad meta-analysis (148 studies, 300,000+ participants) found a 50 per cent greater survival likelihood among individuals with stronger social relationships, comparable in magnitude to smoking cessation. Social support buffers immune function and blood pressure during stressful tasks (Ch 14.4).

Stress reduction techniques: regular aerobic exercise (buffers stress reactivity, prevents telomere shortening, reduces depressive symptoms and anxiety in cancer survivors); the *relaxation response technique* (Benson: relaxed breathing, mantra repetition, passive mind-clearing); biofeedback (Schwartz: providing real-time data on autonomic activity to enable voluntary control). Discrimination is a chronic stressor with cumulative health effects, mitigated but not eliminated by social support and coping resources (Ch 14.4, "Stress and Discrimination").

### Happiness and positive psychology

Positive psychology (Seligman, Csikszentmihalyi) focuses on building strengths and well-being rather than treating pathology. Three elements of happiness: *the pleasant life* (daily pleasures), *the good life* (engagement of strengths), *the meaningful life* (use of strengths for greater good) (Ch 14.5). Predictors of happiness across nations include per capita GDP, social support, freedom of choice, healthy life expectancy, freedom from corruption, and generosity (Helliwell et al., 2013). Within nations, happiness rises with income up to about $75,000 per year (Kahneman and Deaton, 2010) and then plateaus [BT]. Marriage, family relationships, and friendship are stronger predictors of happiness than wealth alone. *Affective forecasting* — predicting future emotion — is systematically poor; people overestimate the lasting emotional impact of life events (the *adaptation* phenomenon). *Flow* (Csikszentmihalyi) — total absorption in challenging activity matched to skill — is associated with deep fulfilment.

## Part XIV: Psychological disorders (Ch 15) — overview only; spawn-prompt directive excludes clinical content from distillation projection

### Definition of psychological disorder

A psychological disorder per APA: "a syndrome characterized by clinically significant disturbance in an individual's cognition, emotion regulation, or behavior that reflects a dysfunction in the psychological, biological, or developmental processes underlying mental functioning" [V] (Ch 15.1). Wakefield's *harmful dysfunction* model defines disorder as the joint presence of (a) dysfunction in an internal mechanism and (b) harm to the individual or others by cultural standards. Atypicality alone is insufficient; cultural expectations vary substantially.

### Classification systems

The DSM-5 (American Psychiatric Association, 2013) is the standard U.S. classification with 237 disorders organised into categories. The ICD-11 (WHO) is more widely used internationally for clinical purposes. The DSM has been criticised for over-pathologising normal experience and for diagnostic inflation. Comorbidity — co-occurrence of multiple disorders — is common; substance use disorders frequently co-occur with mood and anxiety disorders (Ch 15.2).

### Major disorder categories

The text covers anxiety disorders (specific phobia, agoraphobia, social anxiety, panic disorder, generalised anxiety disorder), obsessive-compulsive and related disorders (OCD, body dysmorphic disorder, hoarding disorder), trauma- and stressor-related disorders (PTSD), mood disorders (major depressive disorder, persistent depressive disorder, bipolar disorder), schizophrenia, dissociative disorders (dissociative amnesia, depersonalisation/derealisation, dissociative identity disorder), neurodevelopmental disorders (ADHD, autism spectrum disorder), and personality disorders (Cluster A: paranoid, schizoid, schizotypal; Cluster B: antisocial, histrionic, narcissistic, borderline; Cluster C: avoidant, dependent, obsessive-compulsive personality disorder). The *diathesis-stress model* is the integrative framework for most disorders [AP] (Ch 15.3).

The text takes a strong position on three controversies: there is no scientific evidence linking childhood vaccines to autism (Wakefield's 1998 paper was retracted; subsequent studies including DeStefano et al. 2013 show no relationship) [AP] (Ch 15.10, "Child Vaccinations and Autism Spectrum Disorder"); homosexuality is not a mental disorder (removed from the DSM in 1973); conversion therapy is not effective and may be harmful (Spitzer publicly retracted his earlier paper supporting it) (Ch 10.3; Ch 15.2).

## Part XV: Therapy and treatment (Ch 16) — overview only; spawn-prompt directive excludes clinical content from distillation projection

### Types of treatment

*Psychotherapy* uses psychological methods; *biomedical therapy* uses medication and medical procedures (Ch 16.2). The text catalogues psychoanalysis (Freud: free association, dream analysis, transference; long-duration; declining popularity), play therapy (children), behaviour therapy (classical and operant conditioning techniques: counterconditioning, aversion therapy, exposure therapy with systematic desensitisation, virtual reality exposure, token economies), cognitive therapy (Beck: identifying and challenging cognitive distortions), cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT: ABC model — Action, Belief, Consequence; addressing all-or-nothing thinking, overgeneralisation, jumping to conclusions), and humanistic therapy (Rogers's client-centered/Rogerian therapy: nondirective, active listening, unconditional positive regard).

The text reports that meta-analyses find no consistent advantage of one therapy over another for most disorders; CBT is supported by extensive evidence for many conditions; and the therapeutic alliance is a primary predictor of outcome regardless of orientation (Ch 16.2, "Evaluating Various Forms of Psychotherapy").

### Treatment modalities

Individual, group, couples, and family therapy. Each is described with its specific use cases (Ch 16.3).

### The sociocultural model

*Cultural competence* is essential for effective therapy; Eurocentric therapeutic models can be ineffective for clients from collectivistic cultures or other non-Western backgrounds. Multicultural counseling integrates cultural patterns into a treatment approach tailored to the individual. Ethnic minorities tend to use mental health services less frequently than White, middle-class Americans even when access barriers are controlled — reasons include lack of bilingual treatment, stigma, fear of being misunderstood, family privacy, and lack of education about disorders (Ch 16.5).

## Positions the authors explicitly frame against

- *Punishment as a primary tool for behaviour change*: "Today's psychologists and parenting experts favor reinforcement over punishment—they recommend that you catch your child doing something good and reward them for it" [V] (Ch 6.3, "Punishment").
- *Dispositional explanations applied universally to others' behaviour*: the fundamental attribution error is presented as a systematic mistake — "people tend to overemphasize internal factors as explanations—or attributions—for the behavior of other people" — and "underestimate the power of the situation on the behavior of others" [V] (Ch 12.1, "Fundamental Attribution Error").
- *Stereotypes as factual baseline*: stereotypes are framed as cognitive shortcuts that overgeneralise group membership and cause discriminatory behaviour [AR] (Ch 12.5).
- *Strict Maslow-hierarchy prepotency*: the text notes Maslow's theory "has been criticized for its subjective nature and its inability to account for phenomena that occur in the real world" [V] (Ch 10.1).
- *Freud's theory of psychosexual development*: "most of Freud's ideas have not found support in modern research" [V] (Ch 9.2).
- *Theory X management*: McGregor's account is presented critically, with Theory Y (worker autonomy and participation) framed as more effective for modern organisations [AP] (Ch 13.3).
- *Forced ranking and adversarial performance evaluation*: not explicitly named, but the discussion of poorly-implemented performance appraisals and their failures notes that systems "often used incorrectly or are of an inappropriate type for an organization's particular culture" undermine their value [V] (Ch 13.2).
- *Vaccination as a cause of autism*: "there is no scientific evidence that a link exists between autism and vaccinations" [V] (Ch 15.10). Wakefield's original paper was retracted for fraud.
- *Conversion therapy*: "there is no credible scientific evidence to suggest that individuals can change their sexual orientation"; Spitzer publicly retracted his prior paper supporting it [V] (Ch 10.3).
- *BMI as a clinical health measure*: AMA 2023 recommendation that BMI be de-emphasised because it does not account for body composition or racial/ethnic variation in body composition (Ch 10.2).
- *Polygraph as lie detector*: "their validity and accuracy are highly questionable because there is no evidence that lying is associated with any particular pattern of physiological arousal" [V] (Ch 10.4).
- *Repressed-memory recovery via hypnosis or guided imagery*: Loftus's research demonstrating high suggestibility of memory under such methods is cited; the text qualifies that childhood sexual abuse is real and common but that the recovery method itself produces high rates of false memory [AP] (Ch 8.3, "Controversies over Repressed and Recovered Memories").
- *The MMPI's earliest version's normative sample*: the original sample was "small, limited… mostly of Minnesota farmers and psychiatric patients"; the updated MMPI-2 used a more representative national sample [V] (Ch 11.9).
- *Mental illness as personal weakness or character failure*: the text repeatedly insists that "those with psychological disorders suffer debilitating, often painful conditions that are not of their own choosing. These individuals deserve to be viewed and treated with compassion, understanding, and dignity" [V] (Ch 15.2, "The Compassionate View").
