
# Krivitsky, Larman & Flemm, Org Topologies Primer

**Source:** Alexey Krivitsky, Craig Larman & Roland Flemm (2025). *Strategic Org Design: The Primer*. Org Topologies™ 2025 Edition. February 2025. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0.
**Structure:** 27-page primer. Cover and licence; Management Summary; Why OT; the OT Map; horizontal and vertical dimensions; four organizational characteristics; four archetype groups; three named topologies (Resource, Delivery, Adaptive); the MADE method (Map → Assess → Design → Elevate) with Elevating Katas™; Strategic AI Adoption; Strategic Org Design vs. Framework Thinking; Custom ChatGPT prompts; Learning & Accreditation Journey; blank maps and a closing co-creation note.

## Author's Thesis (condensed)

Org Topologies (OT) is a "human-centric plus AI-friendly organizational change approach" that presents organizational performance as decisively shaped by org design. Different business goals — rapid delivery, global adaptability, resource optimization, amplified innovation — require deliberately different org designs. The authors frame this as *strategic org design*: leaders aligning business strategy, organizational goals, and the change process so they reinforce each other to be "fit for purpose", rather than adopting a pre-existing framework hoping it will solve their problems.

The mapping technique has two dimensions (scope of skills mandate, scope of work mandate), four organizational characteristics (incomplete/complete, output/outcome), sixteen named archetypes organised into four groups (Directing, Doing, Delivering, Driving), and three named topologies (Resource, Delivery, Adaptive). The MADE method — Map, Assess, Design, Elevate — uses the OT map as a visual thinking tool for organisational redesign, supplemented by Elevating Katas™ for the Elevate step. The 2025 Edition is distinguished by its treatment of AI as a cornerstone of the future workforce: "specialization and expertise are vanishing as limited resources, due to intelligence as a service."

The authors take a psychology-of-change discipline seriously: people "have to own – not rent – their change", and change fails when it is pushed onto people who didn't create it. This stance positions OT against canned-solution consulting and "cargo-cult" framework adoption.

## The Two Dimensions

The **horizontal axis** is the scope of skills mandate — how broad a unit's skill mandate is, equivalent in economic terms to transaction (overhead) costs to deliver value, and in Scrum terms to the increasing completeness of the Definition of Done. Five levels: Functional (movie costume department), Multi-Skill (surgical team), End-to-End (software feature team), Expanding (special-forces team), Unbounded.

The **vertical axis** is the scope of work mandate — how broad a unit's work mandate is, equivalent in economic terms to switching costs to work on something different, and in collaboration terms to the size of the "field of play". Five levels: Tasks (sous-chef), Capabilities (insurance claims-processing group), Partial-Business (Uber Ride vs Uber Eats), Whole-Business (two-person Instagram-like startup), Unbounded.

The two axes generate the OT map and the named archetype grid (CAPS-2, WHOLE-3, TASKS-1, PART-1 …) — a working vocabulary the authors position as deliberately framework-agnostic, "without resorting to 'agile' or other framework-specific jargon."

## The Four Characteristics and Four Archetype Groups

Each archetype is **complete or incomplete** (whether it can independently deliver customer value) and **output- or outcome-oriented** (whether it produces deliverables or downstream effects). Complete archetypes can become silos — "whether that's a virtue or vice depends." The well-known management principle of "maximizing outcomes while minimizing outputs" is invoked; more output usually means more cost, not more outcome.

The four **archetype groups** are the OT map's quadrants:

- **Directing** — oversee higher scope of work, lack capabilities to create customer value, design and coordinate (e.g. project manager).
- **Doing** — task-oriented, cannot see the whole, reliant on directives (e.g. sous-chef).
- **Delivering** — capable of delivering value within a narrow scope, often siloed (e.g. e-commerce Search Team).
- **Driving** — capable of understanding, creating, and delivering customer value in its broadest sense; the authors' car-with-passengers metaphor (any one of whom can drive). Example: three-person startup or five teams within a Team-of-Teams.

## The Three Topologies

- **Resource Topology** (Directing + Doing) — frozen functional archetypes optimised for 100% utilisation. Fit for cases like a movie producer hiring dancers for a scene. Learning is "largely confined to improving existing skills rather than discovering new ones."
- **Delivery Topology** (Directing + Delivering) — cross-functional teams that deliver completely-done work fast; sometimes called a "Feature Factory". Fit for domains where the challenge is "delivering new capabilities predictably with short lead times", such as a diner restaurant with short-order cooks. Warning: "no guarantee… that the output effectively solves the real problem"; feature bloat is a common consequence.
- **Adaptive Topology** (Driving) — merges directing, doing, and delivering into one Driving unit. Goal is adaptiveness and customer-centricity. Boundaries between teams are replaced by larger constructs (Team-of-Teams) operating across the entire business (Uber) or a partial business domain (Uber Eats). Emergent quality: "synchronicity of work". Fit for market disruptors and startups.

## The MADE Method (Map → Assess → Design → Elevate)

The change method begins by visually mapping existing units on the OT Map, then assessing the topology's fit for the strategic objective, then designing the target topology, then elevating with the Elevating Katas™.

- **MAP** practises systems thinking and the OT vocabulary; goes to the workplace to understand inputs, outputs, and value-chain dependencies. Warning against local optimisation: "if each element (e.g. policies, structures) is optimized in isolation, the performance of the whole system may actually decline for the desired goal (flow can get worse!)".
- **ASSESS** compares the existing map to the three topologies to test strategic alignment: which goal is the current org designed for; how well it serves that goal; how well that goal supports the business objective. Mismatches are common — "Org Topologies makes it easy for people to see, understand, and then get change going."
- **DESIGN** selects the org goal that delivers the capabilities the strategy requires. Possible goals: output predictability, utilisation, flow, customer delight, adaptiveness, etc. Multi-goal handling and conflict resolution are beyond the Primer's scope. Different parts of the organisation may carry different designs.
- **ELEVATE** moves the org design toward the upper-right of the OT map (broader skill and work mandate). Uses Elevating Katas™ — "a set of practices and principles that can be applied as thoughtful experiments to change the organizational structure, processes, policies, and so forth." Elevation is incremental; periodic re-mapping completes the learning loop. "We are never finished with the perfection journey."

## Strategic AI Adoption

"Any management approach that doesn't include AI as a central part of the future workforce, is of the past." A central design claim: specialisation is vanishing as a limited resource because intelligence is becoming a service; old assumptions like "we don't have enough experts, therefore…" should be discarded. Top-right Driving archetypes become more feasible as AI lowers the cost of broader skill and work mandates.

Three guiding questions: (1) Where in the org is the focus of OT-driven development? Concentrate AI investment there. (2) What are the bottlenecks in moving to the target archetypes? Find the early points where AI delivers outsized impact. (3) Do the AIs need human monitoring? "We predict that monitoring will become an important responsibility for human workers." Each archetype group has a characteristic AI application: Directing (predictive analytics, resource optimisation), Doing (intelligent automation, virtual assistants), Delivering (data integration, recommendation engines), Driving (adaptive machine learning, customer journey analytics, AI as subject-matter expert assisting expanding/unbounded learning).

## Strategic Org Design vs. Framework Thinking

Framework thinking — adopting a pre-built management framework as the solution — is named as the consulting industry's standard pitch. Without understanding, it produces three failure modes: root-cause neglect, no buy-in (people didn't figure it out themselves), and "cargo-cult" mentality leading to Religious Wars. OT positions itself as the alternative path: strategy → org goals → topologies → frameworks (frameworks are tools, not the goal). The Primer illustrates by mapping SAFe (mostly Tasks-level incomplete teams and Capabilities-level fast-flow teams) and Rendanheyi (RDHY — Haier's micro-enterprise model that elevates thousands of MEs that interact with users directly).

## Key Connections

- *Scrum.* The OT horizontal axis is mapped to "the increasing completeness of the Definition of Done" in Scrum.
- *Toyota / Kata (Mike Rother).* The Elevating Katas borrow the term from Toyota's "disciplined and repeating pattern for improving".
- *Systems thinking.* Treated as a practitioner discipline required in the MAP step.
- *SAFe, LeSS, Team Topologies, FAST Agile, Spotify Model / McKinsey Product Operating model, Haier's RDHY, Bayer's DSO.* Named as framework examples that can be mapped onto the OT grid (frameworks as tools, not as the answer).
- *Outcome-over-output principle.* Invoked as "a well-known management principle" without specific attribution.

## Signature Contrarian Positions

1. **Framework adoption is the wrong unit of change.** OT positions itself directly against "the pitch from consulting companies big and small" of adopting an industry-standard framework as the path to success.
2. **People have to own — not rent — change.** Change initiatives fail when people don't create the change themselves; "don't underestimate this psychological dynamic."
3. **Org design is internal and controllable; business outcomes are not.** Designing for fast flow is closer to deterministic; influencing business success requires influencing the internal factors first.
4. **More output ≠ more outcome.** Investing to increase outputs is suspect; outputs are leading metrics, not the goal. Delivery Topology's "feature bloat" is named as the common failure mode.
5. **The "we don't have enough experts" constraint is dissolving.** With AI, "spin up another 10,000 agents" — old organisational constraints around scarce expertise no longer apply, and elevation rightward and upward becomes structurally easier.
6. **Local optimisation degrades whole-system performance.** Systems-thinking discipline is required in MAP; optimising elements in isolation can make the desired goal (e.g. flow) worse.
