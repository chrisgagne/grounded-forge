# Software-Business Distillation Index

> **Purpose:** Situation-to-resource router for software-business work. "In phase X of a software-business question, which references should I reach for?"

This is the *when-to-use* layer for the software-business task. It partitions the task into six phases and lists distillations appropriate to each phase. For the *what-is* layer (descriptions of each reference), see the corpus catalogue at [`../../references/REFERENCE-INDEX.md`](../../references/REFERENCE-INDEX.md).

**Workflow:** Distillation index (when) → distillation (how) → deep reference (citations, if needed).

The software-business axis fires when a question sits at the *intersection* where the technical and the commercial constrain each other: a feature with both a price and a build-cost; a technical decision with a board-reporting consequence; an engineering structural choice with a customer-experience downstream. Generic management or generic engineering framings should route through `decision-making` or `stakeholder-engagement` instead.

Where a query is shaped by a clear reader-type (a founder briefing a board, a CTO explaining to engineering, a PM communicating with sales), check [`../../lenses/LENS-INDEX.md`](../../lenses/LENS-INDEX.md) before composing the answer; the `cto`, `business-executive-stakeholder`, and `pm-bounded-by-ba-role` lenses are the three strong-fire lenses for this axis.

## Cross-axis notes

Software-business is an *intersection*, not a superset. Questions that are primarily about generic decision-making process or generic stakeholder communication route better through `decision-making` and `stakeholder-engagement` respectively. Software-business catches the cases where the *software* part materially shapes the answer, where Jones's empirical findings, Letaw's process discipline, or the software-specific chapters of the business books carry the load.

**Relationship to `decision-making`.** Significant overlap on Phases 1, 2, and 4. The same source projects differently onto each axis: the software-business projection sharpens to the technical-commercial intersection where the decision-making projection covers the broader decision discipline.
