# Barbrook-Johnson & Penn, Systems Mapping — Software-Business Distillation

**Source:** Barbrook-Johnson, P., & Penn, A. S. (2022). *Systems Mapping: How to build and use causal models of systems*. Palgrave Macmillan / Springer Nature. 197 pages. **Licence:** CC BY 4.0 (Open Access). **Distribution note:** Unrestricted with attribution.

## Software-Business Relevance

*Systems Mapping* is a methods book about causal modelling of complex systems — neither a software book nor a business book — but it lands on the software-business intersection precisely because software businesses *are* complex adaptive systems: feature pricing, technical-debt servicing, capacity planning, build-vs-buy, incident response, and board communication all turn on causal structures that are tangled enough to defeat the practitioner's instinct toward linear cause-and-effect. The seven methods give the technical-business practitioner a structured way to make those structures visible — without smuggling in a particular operating ideology — so that a CTO can reason about reliability investment as a stock-and-flow dynamic, a founder can map the pricing-feature-effort causal loops competitors create, and a board memo can carry a Theory of Change diagram that names which assumptions the intervention rests on.

Four contributions land most directly on the software-business intersection:

1. **The appropriateness triangle as a method-selection discipline for software-business diagnosis.** Method choice is a triangulation between project purpose, system characteristics, and method nature (Ch 11, Fig 11.1) — not a personal preference or a one-tool-fits-all default. The frame applies whether the question is "should I model our reliability dynamics" or "should I run a Theory of Change session before fundraising" — and the named biases (familiarity, quantitative-vs-qualitative instinct, funder/client pressure) catch the failure modes a technical leader's training reinforces.

2. **System Dynamics for dynamical software-business sub-problems.** Capacity-vs-debt-vs-reliability over time, hiring ramps colliding with onboarding throughput, attractiveness-erosion-from-quality-loss (the Maldives tourism worked example in Ch 8 is structurally identical to a SaaS company's churn-from-degraded-experience problem) — these are *dynamical sub-problems* where System Dynamics' stock-and-flow apparatus prices the trade-offs Jones's empirical findings name (Ch 8).

3. **Bayesian Belief Networks for probabilistic software-business reasoning under uncertainty.** Security investment decisions, compliance milestones, incident-recovery probabilities, regulator-inquiry exposure: each is a conditional-probability problem that benefits from BBN's structural transparency over the more common "what does our gut say" or "what did Gartner say" defaults (Ch 7).

4. **Rich Pictures + Theory of Change for stakeholder-legible communication of software-business situations.** The board reading a CTO memo about platform modernisation is in the same epistemic position as the policy committee reading a Theory of Change about a poverty intervention: they need a structured causal narrative that surfaces the load-bearing assumptions and the failure modes. Theory of Change's "fill the gaps backward" and "what if no change?" prompts (Ch 3) discipline the artefact in ways that the standard board-memo template doesn't.

The fifth contribution — Causal Loop Diagram archetypes for strategic-positioning recognition — fires at Phase 1: Kim's (2000) eight archetypes (Ch 4) include several that recur across software-business strategy decisions (*fixes that fail* — short-term reliability patches that mask architectural debt; *limits to success* — successful products hitting capacity ceilings; *shifting the burden* — outsourcing dependencies that erode in-house capability; *tragedy of the commons* — shared engineering capacity depleted by uncoordinated team demands).

The distillation projects these contributions onto the software-business task axis. Where the book is silent — formal financial decision tools, real-time operational decision-making, agent-based modelling, AI-specific causal structures — the silence is acknowledged.

## Key Concepts for Software-Business

1.  **The appropriateness triangle.** Method choice triangulates project purpose / system characteristics / method nature. For software-business work, the triangulation looks like: *purpose* (board memo? capacity model? incident write-up? roadmap defence?), *system characteristics* (feedbacks present or not? specific dynamical sub-problem or whole-system question? data-rich or data-poor? participatory or expert-driven?), *method nature* (qualitative-quantitative position, participatory-or-not, intuitive-or-formal). The familiar-method bias is acute in technical leadership — engineers default to quantitative methods, product leaders default to ToC, ex-consultants default to CLD. The triangulation forces the alternative methods into view. (Source: Barbrook-Johnson & Penn, *Systems Mapping*, Ch 11, "Comparing Systems Mapping Methods")

2.  **System Dynamics' dynamical-sub-problem discipline.** "Despite having 'system' in the name, System Dynamics models tend to be focused on specific dynamical problems rather than whole systems." For software-business work, this means SD applies to *technical-debt-accumulating-versus-paying-down dynamics*, *capacity-versus-demand-over-quarters dynamics*, *reliability-investment-versus-customer-attractiveness dynamics* — not to "model the whole engineering org". The Maldives tourism example is the canonical worked case: stocks (tourists, attractiveness), flows (arrival rate, departure rate), and feedback through pollution. The software-business analogue is users, NPS, churn flows, with feedback through reliability and feature velocity. (Source: Ch 8, "What Is System Dynamics?"; Fig 8.1)

3.  **Stocks, flows, and soft factors.** Stocks are entities that accumulate or deplete (engineers, technical-debt items, customer trust, board confidence); flows are rates of change (hiring, deprecation, churn, escalations). Soft factors (*Attractiveness of the Maldives* in the worked example; *board confidence* or *engineering morale* in software-business analogues) can be included if their behaviour can be sensibly described going up and down. The authors push back on the bias toward only quantifiable elements: "All complex adaptive systems will have these components, and they will often play important roles in dynamics, so we cannot ignore them simply because they are more difficult to specify." For software-business work, the move is to model what matters — including the soft factors — and be honest about the analytical uncertainty their inclusion creates. (Source: Ch 8, "What Is System Dynamics?")

4.  **Bayesian Belief Networks' acyclic-probabilistic structure.** Networks of variables with conditional probabilities, no feedback loops (or feedbacks captured only via dynamic BBN with time-period instances), maximum two-three parent nodes per child node. For software-business work, BBN fits security-investment decisions (investment node → defence-strength node → breach-probability node → financial-impact node), compliance investment scoping, regulatory-inquiry-exposure modelling, and structured incident-response decision trees. The structure forces the probability tables onto the table, which is harder to hand-wave than verbal probability claims. (Source: Ch 7, "What Are Bayesian Belief Networks?")

5.  **BBN transparency advantage.** "Everything that has gone into the model is clearly visible in the network structure and probabilities. There are no 'hidden' decisions taken by a facilitator or modeller on which the outputs might depend crucially. (For example, like parameter choices in Fuzzy Cognitive Mapping or System Dynamics.)" For software-business work, this means BBN is the right tool when the board or auditor needs to see the assumptions explicitly — not when the question is dynamics-over-time. (Source: Ch 7, "What Are Bayesian Belief Networks Good and Bad At?")

6.  **BBN's wider-context probability handling.** Non-zero probabilities in the conditional probability table represent "everything else going on in the system" — the structural way BBN admits that the model is not the world. When both interventions are "on" and the outcome still has a 0.1 probability of not happening, that 0.1 is the wiggle room for unmodelled context. For software-business work, this discipline lets a security memo say "the model predicts a 0.05 residual breach probability even with both controls fully deployed" rather than claiming model-complete coverage. (Source: Ch 7, "What Are Bayesian Belief Networks?"; Table 7.2)

7.  **Causal Loop Diagram archetypes (Kim 2000).** Eight named patterns: *drifting goals* (commitment to quality erodes under date pressure; "good enough" sets the new bar); *escalation* (competitor matches your engineering investment; both spend more, neither pulls ahead); *fixes that fail* (a quick patch creates more incidents downstream); *growth and under-investment* (product success outpaces engineering capacity until quality breaks); *limits to success* (early product-market-fit hits a ceiling at scale where the original architecture cannot serve); *shifting the burden* (outsourcing dependencies erode in-house capability); *success to the successful* (the team with the demo budget gets more demos, more wins, more budget); *tragedy of the commons* (shared engineering capacity depleted by uncoordinated team demands; shared technical-debt-cleanup time always-deferred). When a software-business situation feels familiar, the named archetype is a starting hypothesis. (Source: Ch 4, "How Do You Create Causal Loop Diagrams?", citing Kim 2000)

8.  **The CLD core-engine discipline.** Build the CLD around a "core system engine" — the central reinforcing or balancing loop — and let the rest of the map cluster around it, with twenty nodes as the practical limit before unwieldiness. For software-business work, this is what stops a delivery-system map from sprawling into a horrendogram of every team, every tool, every metric: name the loop you care about (e.g., *feature-velocity → revenue → engineering-investment → feature-velocity* as a reinforcing loop, with *technical-debt → defect-rate → engineering-time-on-fixes → feature-velocity* as a balancing loop) and let the rest hang on that engine. (Source: Ch 4, "How Do You Create Causal Loop Diagrams?")

9.  **Theory of Change for software-business interventions.** Inputs → activities → outputs → outcomes → impacts (Rogers 2014's results chain). For a platform-modernisation programme, *inputs* are engineering headcount, modernisation budget, and architecture decisions; *activities* are sprint work, migrations, deprecations; *outputs* are migrated services, decommissioned legacy systems; *outcomes* are performance improvements, reduced incident rates, faster release cadence; *impacts* are customer retention, NPS, commercial growth. The structure forces an explicit account of how the engineering effort connects to the business outcome — the question the board is always implicitly asking. (Source: Ch 3, "What Is Theory of Change Mapping?", citing Rogers 2014)

10.  **The "fill the gaps backward" Theory of Change pattern.** Start with inputs and long-term impacts; *then* fill the middle by starting near the long-term impacts and working backwards through medium-term and short-term outcomes to the outputs that lead to them. The reverse-engineering pattern surfaces missing intermediary steps that forward-only construction tends to gloss. For a software-business memo, this catches the gap between "we will deploy AI tooling" (activity) and "engineering productivity will rise 30%" (impact) by forcing the intermediary outcomes — *what specifically changes in the engineer's day, what specifically reduces in cycle time, what specifically becomes measurable* — to the surface. (Source: Ch 3, "How Do You Create Theory of Change Diagrams?")

11.  **The "theories of no change / negative change" prompt.** The authors recommend also specifying 'theories of no change' or 'theories of negative change' in the diagram as a way to force realism into the planning exercise. For software-business work, this is the structured way to write the pre-mortem into a roadmap proposal: *if this initiative succeeds, what changes? If it doesn't change anything, why might that be? If it produces a negative outcome, what would the mechanism be?* The prompts force the assumptions the success-narrative ToC hides. (Source: Ch 3, "How Do You Create Theory of Change Diagrams?")

12.  **Theory of Change as theory, not map of reality.** "Though the clue is in the name, it is important to keep in mind that ToCs are theories, they represent the mental models of the people who constructed them. They are not maps of reality and they may contain gaps and ambiguities, as indeed may the theories they represent." For board-memo and investor-update communication, this distinction is load-bearing: the ToC diagram is making the *assumptions visible*, not certifying their truth. The board's job is to interrogate the assumptions; the artefact's job is to surface them. (Source: Ch 3, "What Is Theory of Change Mapping?")

13.  **Rich Pictures for stakeholder-communication of complex software-business situations.** Free-form drawing of a "situation" — no formal causal-arrow discipline, minimal facilitator prompts, participants discouraged from text. The method is at the extreme end of low-formality, designed for opening up exploration with stakeholders who might disengage from a CLD or ToC. For a software-business situation where a non-technical board needs to *feel* a system's tangle before being asked to make a decision, Rich Pictures sit before the structured methods, not in competition with them. (Source: Ch 2, "What Are Rich Pictures?")

14.  **The horrendogram failure mode.** "System maps are sometimes referred to as 'horrendograms', and much worse (!), when they show us the complexity of a system in an unfiltered manner." The 2007 UK obesity CLD is the canonical "high-profile" but "horrendogram" example. For software-business communication — board memos, all-hands engineering reviews, post-incident write-ups — the horrendogram failure mode is the most common technical-leader trap: the diagram that shows every team, every system, every dependency on one page, that lands as paralysis rather than navigation. The discipline: layer, filter, stage for the audience. (Source: Ch 1, "How Can Systems Mapping Be Useful?"; Ch 4, "Causal Loop Diagrams")

15.  **The half-resources-on-design rule.** For CLDs (and by extension other visually-driven methods), "allocate half of your resources to design and communication" — a striking quantitative recommendation. For software-business artefacts, this catches the engineering-culture failure mode of treating the analysis as the work and the communication as polish. (Source: Ch 4, "Common Issues and 'Tricks of the Trade'")

16.  **Process value versus product value.** "Be clear about whether you want value from the process, outputs, or both, of a mapping process." Often the process — discussions and mutual understanding generated through map-building — delivers more value to stakeholders than the map itself. For software-business work, the implication is that *running a workshop with engineering, product, and business* on a CLD of the delivery system may produce more alignment than circulating a finished diagram. The choice affects the engagement design and the budget. (Source: Ch 12, "Our Final Take-Home Messages")

17.  **Participation versus conceptual rigour.** "In an ideal world we could do deeply participatory work which also produced models which were completely conceptually and scientifically sound. In practice, this is rarely possible. Stakeholders will want things included which a neutral 'expert' wouldn't." For software-business work — particularly when running a workshop with mixed engineering and business stakeholders — the trade is real: a deeply participatory session produces ownership but a less conceptually clean model; a researcher-driven session produces a tighter model but less buy-in. The decision shapes the engagement design and the deliverable shape. (Source: Ch 12, "Our Final Take-Home Messages")

18.  **The Maslow's-hammer failure mode in software-business diagnosis.** "You have your hammer, and now everything looks like a nail." For technical leaders, the hammer is whichever method their training emphasised (System Dynamics for the operations-research-trained, CLDs for the consultant-trained, BBN for the data-scientist-trained, ToC for the development-economics-trained). The appropriateness triangle is the antidote, but the bias is institutional — leadership pressure to be seen using "the right" method shapes choice as much as the diagnostic question. (Source: Ch 12, "Our Final Take-Home Messages")

19.  **Combining methods as the normal pattern.** "Combining methods in some form will normally be useful or valuable" — sequentially (one method feeds another, e.g., Rich Pictures → CLD → System Dynamics) or as hybrids (one map combining elements of two or more methods). For software-business work, the typical combination chain is *Rich Pictures with non-technical board to scope the situation* → *Theory of Change with leadership to frame the intervention* → *CLD or System Dynamics with engineering to model the dynamics* → *BBN with risk/compliance to quantify residual exposure*. (Source: Ch 11, "Combining Systems Mapping Methods")

20.  **The participatory-steering stance.** "We must accept that we cannot force or control the complex adaptive systems we live in and make up, but we can work with them to steer and nurture the change we want." For software-business work, this is the meta-stance: a software business — feature flow, engineering culture, technical-debt dynamics, customer trust — is a complex adaptive system, not a controllable machine. Decisions in it are nudges and probes, not commands. (Source: Ch 12, "Our Final Take-Home Messages")

## Questions to Ask During Software-Business Work

### Phase 1: Strategic positioning (pricing, market entry, build-vs-buy, portfolio decisions, AI integration choices)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Does this strategic situation look like a familiar pattern? | Run through Kim's eight CLD archetypes: drifting goals, escalation, fixes that fail, growth and under-investment, limits to success, shifting the burden, success to the successful, tragedy of the commons. If one fits, that's a starting hypothesis for the core system engine. |
| Is the question structurally about *positioning* (a competitive dynamic) or about *dynamics* (a time-evolving system)? | Strategic positioning often fits CLD (qualitative dynamics, feedback recognition); strategic-dynamics-over-time fits System Dynamics if there's enough specificity. |
| Is the build-vs-buy choice creating a *shifting-the-burden* pattern? | Outsourcing to a partner that erodes in-house capability over time is the archetype. Map it; the dynamic surfaces the long-term cost. |
| Is the AI-integration decision creating a *fixes-that-fail* pattern? | Tactical AI deployment that masks underlying capability or process issues is the archetype. Map the short-term-fix vs long-term-issue dynamics before committing. |
| Am I defaulting to a familiar method because it's mine, not because it fits? | The appropriateness triangle (project purpose / system characteristics / method nature) is the antidote. Name your default; ask what an honest triangulation would choose. |

### Phase 2: Product and engineering economics (technical-debt servicing, capacity planning, ROI on engineering investment, reliability-as-business-decision)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Is this a dynamical sub-problem suited to System Dynamics? | Does it involve stocks (debt items, engineers, customer trust) and flows (debt-accumulation, debt-payment, hiring, churn)? Does the question turn on dynamics over time, not on whole-system structure? If yes, SD applies. |
| What are the stocks and flows for this question? | Engineers (stock), hiring rate (flow), attrition rate (flow); technical-debt items (stock), debt-creation rate (flow), debt-payment rate (flow); customer trust (stock), trust-building rate (flow), trust-erosion rate (flow). The model needs differential equations on each flow. |
| What soft factors must be included even though they're hard to quantify? | Engineering morale, board confidence, customer trust — all behave like stocks (accumulate and deplete) even though their values are estimates. Include them or model a system without the load-bearing dynamics. |
| Is the dynamics question one of reinforcing or balancing loops? | Reinforcing loops produce runaway dynamics (feature velocity → revenue → engineering investment → feature velocity); balancing loops produce stabilisation (technical debt → defect rate → engineering time on fixes → feature velocity, which constrains the reinforcing loop). |
| Am I trying to model the whole engineering org, or a specific dynamic? | System Dynamics is "for dynamical sub-problems, not whole systems". If the answer is "the whole org", scope down. |

### Phase 4: Operations and process (delivery cadence, governance, decision rights, change management)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| What's the purpose of this systems-mapping exercise? | Orientation (where are we?); diagnostic (what's the structure causing the issue?); intervention design (what should we change?); communication (how do we explain this?); simulation (how do dynamics play out?). The purpose drives the method choice. |
| What does the system look like — feedback-rich, intervention-driven, dynamics-dominated, or tangled-and-need-to-be-explored? | Feedback-rich and intervention-driven points to CLD; feedback-rich and quantitative-dynamics-needed points to System Dynamics; intervention-driven and stakeholder-shared points to Theory of Change; tangled-and-need-to-be-explored points to Rich Pictures or Participatory Systems Mapping. |
| Who needs to be in the room? | Engineering for technical accuracy; product for outcome assumptions; business for impact assumptions; finance for cost framing. Mixed groups produce better maps and more alignment but slower progress and lower conceptual purity. |
| Am I designing this exercise for process value, product value, or both? | Process value comes from the discussion and mutual understanding generated; product value from the finished artefact. Engineering culture typically over-weights product; participatory methods unlock process value the team may not know they need. |
| Where on the qualitative-quantitative spectrum should this fall? | Rich Pictures (most qualitative) → ToC → CLD → PSM → FCM → BBN → System Dynamics (most quantitative). The choice is set by the question; do not let "we want it quantitative" or "we want it qualitative" drive without justifying against the question's needs. |

### Phase 5: Risk, reliability, compliance (incidents, security, legal, regulatory, ethics)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Is this question genuinely probabilistic and acyclic? | If the question is *how likely is outcome X given evidence Y* and the causal chain has no feedback loops, BBN fits. If the question is *how does this dynamic play out over time*, BBN is the wrong tool — even with dynamic-BBN extensions, the structure is awkward. |
| What are the network nodes? | For security: investment level (root) → defence strength → attack-success probability → financial impact. For compliance: control coverage → audit-finding probability → fine probability. Acyclic; conditional dependencies; explicit state spaces. |
| What's in the wider-context probability slack? | The non-zero probabilities for outcomes when all interventions are "on" represent unmodelled context. State this explicitly in the model: "with both controls fully deployed, the model predicts a residual probability of X — this is the wiggle room for risks not in the network". |
| Is the model going to be used as decision-support or as audit evidence? | Decision-support tolerates expert-elicited probabilities; audit evidence often requires data-derived ones. The structural-transparency advantage of BBN over System Dynamics or FCM is more load-bearing for audit-evidence use. |
| What feedbacks am I forcing out of the model to make BBN work? | If feedbacks are central (security investment → attack surface → defence load → security investment, for example), BBN is the wrong tool. CLD or System Dynamics fit the cyclic structure. |

### Phase 6: Stakeholder communication (board reporting, exec alignment, investor relations, customer messaging)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Is the audience visually literate enough for a CLD or System Dynamics diagram? | "Tune the complexity of a map to the visual literacy of your audience." If not, simplify radically — and consider Rich Pictures or a simplified Theory of Change as the artefact, with the full diagram as appendix. |
| Have I avoided producing a horrendogram? | The single-page unfiltered display of every team, system, and dependency is the technical-leader trap. Layer the map; stage the narrative; produce a "real" version and a simplified version for slide-deck use. |
| Have I allocated half my resources to design and communication? | For CLDs specifically — but the recommendation generalises to other diagram-driven artefacts. Engineering culture under-weights this; the result is finished analysis that does not land. |
| Have I used Theory of Change to discipline the board memo? | The ToC structure (inputs → activities → outputs → outcomes → impacts) is the structured way to write "what we're investing in / what we're doing / what changes immediately / what changes downstream / what the business outcome is". The "fill the gaps backward" pattern catches the missing intermediary steps. |
| Have I included a theory-of-no-change or negative-change? | The artefact lands better when it names what would happen if the intervention failed or produced an unintended consequence. Boards interrogate assumptions; the artefact's job is to surface them so the interrogation is structured. |
| Am I treating the ToC diagram as theory (assumptions made visible) or as a map of reality (claims certified)? | The honest framing: "this diagram represents our current model of how the intervention will work; here are the load-bearing assumptions." This deflects the failure mode where the board reads the diagram as a commitment rather than as a model. |
| Have I planned post-workshop one-to-ones? | "Participants are often interested in following up one-to-one, and these can be some of the most useful spaces". Board members and executives often surface their real concerns in bilateral conversation; the workshop produces the shared artefact, the one-to-ones produce the real signal. |

## What to Look For

**Pattern: a strategic-positioning conversation that keeps circling a familiar dynamic without naming it.**
- *Signal:* Leadership discussion of a competitive situation that keeps coming back to "we need to invest more / they're investing more / nobody's pulling ahead", or "every fix creates new problems", or "we hit a ceiling we didn't expect."
- *Diagnosis:* Likely a CLD archetype (escalation, fixes that fail, limits to success). The conversation is recognising the pattern without the vocabulary to name it.
- *Follow-up:* Name the archetype as a starting hypothesis; sketch the core system engine. Don't claim certainty — sketch and ask whether the structure fits the stakeholders' experience. The sketch's primary job is to make the dynamic discussable.

**Pattern: a capacity-or-debt question being analysed as a static budget allocation rather than as a dynamic.**
- *Signal:* Discussion frames the question as "how much should we spend on debt vs features this quarter" — a single-period decision — rather than as "how do debt accumulation and debt payment interact with feature velocity over multiple quarters."
- *Diagnosis:* The system has dynamical structure that's being collapsed to a static decision. System Dynamics' stock-and-flow framing makes the dynamics legible.
- *Follow-up:* Sketch the stocks (debt items, engineers, customer trust) and the flows. Look for the balancing loop (debt → defect rate → engineering time on fixes → less time for features → less revenue → less hiring → less debt payment) that the static framing hides.

**Pattern: a board memo or investor update reading as either methodology-heavy or assumption-light.**
- *Signal:* The artefact either leads with how the analysis was conducted (over-methodology) or jumps from inputs to impacts with no intermediary steps visible (under-assumption).
- *Diagnosis:* Theory of Change discipline missing. The structural cascade (inputs → activities → outputs → outcomes → impacts) is what makes the causal chain interrogable.
- *Follow-up:* Reshape the artefact through the ToC frame. Add a "theories of no change / negative change" section. Lead with the impact and work backward through the cascade. The methodology section recedes; the assumption surface becomes the artefact's primary work.

**Pattern: a security or compliance investment proposal asserting an outcome probability without showing the conditional structure.**
- *Signal:* Claims like "this investment reduces breach risk by 60%" or "this control eliminates the audit finding" without showing the model.
- *Diagnosis:* The probability claim lacks the BBN-style structural transparency that would make it interrogable. The 60% is implicit on assumptions the artefact isn't surfacing.
- *Follow-up:* Sketch the BBN structure even if it's not formally computed: the parent nodes (the inputs and their states), the conditional probability tables (the assumptions), the child node (the outcome). The exercise either reveals the model is sound (the artefact now has structure) or reveals load-bearing assumptions the artefact was hiding.

**Pattern: a delivery-system map that shows every team, every tool, and every dependency on one page.**
- *Signal:* The "comprehensive" map that turns into a horrendogram — visually impressive, conceptually overwhelming, useless for decision-making.
- *Diagnosis:* The map has confused complexity-display with insight. The unfiltered structure paralyses rather than guides.
- *Follow-up:* Identify the core system engine — the 5-7 nodes most central to the question. Build the map around that engine. Layer or filter the rest for audiences who need it. Allocate half the resources to design and communication. Produce a real version and a simplified version.

**Pattern: a participatory workshop framed as "we'll just facilitate" without honest acknowledgment of the rigour trade-off.**
- *Signal:* The plan is to run a mapping workshop with mixed engineering, product, and business stakeholders, framed as "let's let the participants build the model."
- *Diagnosis:* The participation-versus-conceptual-rigour trade is real and unacknowledged. Stakeholders will include things a neutral expert would not; the model will gain ownership but lose tightness.
- *Follow-up:* Name the trade with the team before the workshop. Decide which to prioritise. If participation, accept the tangents and the inclusions; the model is the team's, and its rigour-cost is the price of ownership. If conceptual rigour, be explicit with stakeholders that expert decisions will be made and why — and consider hybrid designs (participatory at scoping and validation, expert-driven in between).

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for Barbrook-Johnson & Penn when a software-business question genuinely requires causal modelling of a complex system — when the practitioner's instinct toward linear cause-and-effect is producing a flat answer the situation's structure does not support. Specifically:

- **Method-selection problem.** A causal-modelling exercise is going to happen (strategic-positioning diagnosis, technical-debt-versus-capacity dynamics, security risk modelling, board memo on platform modernisation) and the question is *which method fits this question*. The appropriateness triangle (Ch 11) is the structured way to choose.

- **Dynamical sub-problem.** The question is genuinely about how a system evolves over time (technical debt over quarters, capacity-versus-demand, reliability-investment over a fiscal year) and System Dynamics' stock-and-flow apparatus prices the trade-offs more honestly than verbal reasoning.

- **Probabilistic risk reasoning.** Security, compliance, regulatory exposure, or incident-response questions where the causal chain is acyclic and the conditional structure benefits from BBN's transparency.

- **Stakeholder-legible causal communication.** Board memos, investor updates, customer messaging, or all-hands engineering reviews where a Theory of Change diagram or a Rich Picture lands harder than a bulleted memo.

- **Strategic-positioning pattern recognition.** A competitive dynamic or organisational dynamic that keeps showing up in leadership discussion without being named. The eight CLD archetypes are the starting vocabulary.

Reach for other corpus references first when:

- The question is *generic decision-making process* (the appropriateness-triangle frame is here but the corpus's generic decision-making discipline lives in the OpenStax Organizational Behavior, Principles of Management, and Business Ethics distillations).
- The question is *facilitation craft* for the workshop you're already going to run (this book has a strong workshop runbook in Ch 10, but the Liberating Structures handbook, the FLO Facilitation Guide, and the Open Practice Library distillations carry the broader facilitation discipline).
- The question is *empirical findings about software engineering* (Jones's *Evidence-Based Software Engineering* and Letaw's *Handbook of Software Engineering Methods* are the corpus's anchors for software-specific empirical claims; Barbrook-Johnson supplies methodology, not findings).
- The question is *individual or group dynamics within the engineering org* (OpenStax Organizational Behavior is the workplace-applicable psychology).
- The question is *cost-benefit analysis or financial decision-tooling* (OpenStax Accounting Vol 2's CVP, relevant-cost, and theory-of-constraints chapters; OpenStax Principles of Finance for capital allocation).

## Worked Example

A founder-CTO of a 150-engineer SaaS company in year 3 of a platform-modernisation programme is preparing to brief the board on a proposal to delay a major feature release by one quarter to pay down a specific cluster of technical debt that the engineering team estimates is causing 30% of recurring incidents. The board's instinct, based on the last two meetings, will be to push back: "every quarter we hear 'one more cleanup' and the features keep getting pushed; what's different this time?"

The founder-CTO reaches for Barbrook-Johnson & Penn (Ch 11) and runs the appropriateness triangle. The project purpose is twofold: *board communication* (Phase 6) and *internal alignment* on the trade-off (Phase 2). The system has feedback dynamics (debt → incidents → engineering time on fixes → less time for features → revenue pressure → pressure to deprioritise debt cleanup → more debt; balanced against debt-payment → fewer incidents → more time for features → more revenue → more capacity for further payment). The methods that fit: Theory of Change for the board communication (structured intervention narrative), CLD for the internal alignment session (feedback visibility), and System Dynamics if the time horizon and data quality justify it.

For the board memo, the founder-CTO builds a Theory of Change diagram (Ch 3). Inputs: 8 engineers reassigned for one quarter, $X opportunity cost in delayed feature revenue. Activities: refactor the authentication service, retire two legacy adapters, consolidate the monitoring layer. Outputs: 40% reduction in deployment-friction tickets, 60% reduction in monitoring-related on-call escalations. Outcomes (medium-term, 1-2 quarters out): 25% reduction in incident frequency, 15% improvement in feature-delivery throughput. Impacts (long-term, 3-4 quarters out): churn reduction tied to reliability perception, retention improvement at enterprise tier, capacity to take on the AI-integration roadmap board has been asking about. The memo uses the "fill the gaps backward" pattern (Ch 3) — starting from the long-term impact (commercial capacity for AI integration) and working back to why this specific debt cluster is the bottleneck — and includes a "theories of no change / negative change" section: *what if we delay and the incidents don't drop? What if we delay and incidents drop but commercial outcomes don't move? What if we don't delay — what does the bi-exponential incident pattern look like in two more quarters of accumulation?*

For the engineering town hall the week before the board meeting, the founder-CTO runs a 90-minute CLD session (Ch 4) with engineering, product, and finance leads. The core system engine: *feature delivery → revenue → engineering investment → feature delivery* (reinforcing loop, R1) and *technical debt → incident rate → engineering time on fixes → time available for features* (balancing loop, B1). The two loops are linked through the *engineering time* node. The session names the *fixes-that-fail* archetype (Ch 4, citing Kim 2000) as the historical pattern — quick patches that masked the architectural issue and let debt accumulate — and uses the diagram to ask: *if we don't break the cycle this quarter, what does the model predict?* The answer is visible on the page; the session ends with engineering, product, and finance leads holding the same model. The CTO allocates half the prep time to the diagram's design and the session's structure (Ch 4), not because the analysis was complex but because the communication is load-bearing.

The board memo lands. Two board members ask about the theories of negative change before asking about the timeline — exactly the conversation the artefact's structure was designed to invite. The board approves the delay. The CTO does the post-workshop one-to-ones the following week (Ch 10) and finds that the most-sceptical board member's real concern was not the timeline but the engineering team's ability to actually execute the cleanup at the promised quality — a concern the ToC diagram had not addressed at the right level. A follow-up artefact addresses it.

This is the book's pattern, projected onto software-business work: the methods are the discipline, the participation pattern is the engagement design, the artefacts are what lands with the audience.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **The Maslow's-hammer failure mode in causal modelling.** Defaulting to whichever method the practitioner's training emphasised — System Dynamics for the operations-research-trained, CLD for the consultant-trained, BBN for the data-scientist-trained, ToC for the development-economics-trained. The appropriateness triangle (Ch 11) forces alternatives into view.

- **Treating System Dynamics as a whole-system method.** Building a model that tries to capture "the whole engineering org" rather than a specific dynamical sub-problem (debt-versus-capacity, hiring-versus-onboarding, reliability-versus-attractiveness). The book is explicit: "models rarely aim to represent a whole system, but rather focus on carefully constrained sub-sets of a system with the relevant dynamics" (Ch 8).

- **Producing a horrendogram for board or all-hands communication.** Single-page unfiltered displays of every team, every system, every dependency. Visually impressive, conceptually paralysing (Ch 1, Ch 4). The discipline: layer, filter, stage for the audience; allocate half the resources to design.

- **Treating a Theory of Change diagram as a commitment certified, not as theory with assumptions visible.** The board reads the diagram as promises; the artefact's actual job is to surface the load-bearing assumptions so the board can interrogate them (Ch 3).

- **Forcing feedback dynamics into a BBN.** The structure is acyclic; "in complex systems, it is rare for there to be no feedback loops" (Ch 7). If feedbacks are central, the right method is CLD, FCM, or System Dynamics — not BBN with dynamic-BBN workarounds.

- **Letting threshold-function choice silently dominate FCM analysis.** The form of the threshold function can reverse model results in causal-mode FCM. For software-business work, the implication: do not present FCM outputs to a board without disclosing the sensitivity-analysis work, and consider PSM instead when the analysis is going to be high-stakes (Ch 6).

- **Hiding the soft factors from a System Dynamics model.** Engineering morale, board confidence, customer trust all behave as stocks in software-business dynamics even though their values are estimates. Excluding them in favour of only-quantifiable factors produces a model that misses the load-bearing dynamics. The authors are explicit: include them, and be honest about the uncertainty (Ch 8).

- **Treating the mapping process as overhead and the mapping artefact as the work.** Often the discussions and shared understanding generated through the workshop are worth more than the diagram itself. Designing the engagement as if only the artefact matters wastes the process value and produces shallower stakeholder commitment (Ch 12).

- **Pretending the participation-vs-rigour trade isn't real.** Deeply participatory mapping with mixed software-business stakeholders produces ownership and surface alignment but accepts that stakeholders will include things a neutral expert would not. Pretending the model will be both fully participatory and conceptually tight produces an artefact that is neither (Ch 12).

- **Audio-recording engineering or board workshops without budgeting for transcription.** Transcription "can easily take five or six times the length of the recording when there are multiple people involved" (Ch 10), and the recording itself can inhibit honest discussion. Default to dedicated note-taking unless the artefact specifically requires verbatim record.

## Through the CTO lens

For the CTO reading any of the artefacts this distillation supports — a Theory of Change for the board, a CLD for the engineering all-hands, a System Dynamics model for the platform-modernisation timeline, a BBN for the security-investment case — the lens reweights three things in particular.

First, **the constraint as operational mechanism, surfaced through the core system engine**. CLDs land for this reader-type when the diagram names the one balancing loop or reinforcing loop that is the constraint — owner, capability gap, repeatable gate — rather than displaying every dependency. A CLD that fires for this CTO has 5-7 nodes in the core system engine and the rest layered or filtered; a CLD with twenty nodes on the front page reads as Past-tempo evidence-display and gets skimmed past. (Source: Ch 4, "How Do You Create Causal Loop Diagrams?")

Second, **the Theory of Change diagram leading with what becomes possible, not what failed**. Standard ToC pedagogy leads with inputs and works forward; the fill-the-gaps-backward pattern (Ch 3) maps directly onto the CTO's opportunity-first reading register. The artefact should open with the long-term impact (the unlock — what AI capability, what reliability tier, what commercial capacity becomes available) and work back to the inputs through outcomes, outputs, and activities. The methodology section recedes; the assumption-surface becomes the artefact's primary work. (Source: Ch 3, "How Do You Create Theory of Change Diagrams?")

Third, **the AI-native angle in the systems-mapping exercise itself**. The CTO's bet is AI-as-substrate-for-org-redesign, not AI-as-productivity-tool. A systems-mapping exercise applied to AI integration should surface the role-bifurcation question (which engineer roles change, which appear, which disappear) as a structural feedback in the map — not as a productivity bullet. The book's silence on AI is a gap this lens fires; the practitioner brings the question even when the book doesn't.

**Triggers the CTO lens reaches for in this distillation:**
- Phase 1: archetype-recognition diagnosis when a strategic-positioning situation has the shape of *fixes-that-fail*, *limits-to-success*, or *shifting-the-burden* with AI-integration implications.
- Phase 2: capacity-versus-debt modelling that resolves to a single constraint with an owner and a timeboxed first step.
- Phase 4: choice of mapping method when the audience is engineering (CLD with core engine) versus when the audience is the executive team (Theory of Change with backward fill).
- Phase 6: board-memo shape using ToC structure to lead with the unlock and work backward to the inputs.

The lens recedes when the question is genuinely about workshop facilitation craft (other corpus references carry that better for this reader-type) or about generic decision-making process (route through `decision-making`).

## Through the business-executive-stakeholder lens

For the business-executive-stakeholder reading a systems-mapping-derived artefact — a Theory of Change diagram in a quarterly business review, a CLD shown in an executive offsite, a BBN-derived security memo, a Rich Picture in a strategy session — the lens reweights two things.

First, **the register-determines-reception dynamic**. A CLD that names a *tragedy-of-the-commons* dynamic in shared engineering capacity, or a *shifting-the-burden* archetype in vendor dependencies, lands with this reader if the artefact uses Paradigm-A vocabulary (commitment, accountability, plan, KPI, named owner) at the load-bearing positions and treats the systems-mapping vocabulary as a structured Paradigm-B reframe offered as a choice — not as a sermon. The single B-move heuristic applies: one structural reframe per artefact, surfaced as "this pattern reads as system-shaped rather than individual-shaped; the durable fix is structural" with an A-shaped action close. Multiple structural reframes in one artefact read as paradigm sermon and get filed as "not rigorous." (Source: Ch 4, archetypes; Ch 12, participation-vs-rigour trade)

Second, **the Theory of Change as accountability surface, not as systems-thinking proselytising**. The ToC structure (inputs → activities → outputs → outcomes → impacts) maps naturally onto Paradigm-A artefact requirements: the inputs are budget and headcount commitments, the outputs are deliverables with named owners, the outcomes are KPI deltas, the impacts are commercial outcomes the stakeholder can be accountable for. The "fill the gaps backward" pattern surfaces the intermediary KPIs the stakeholder needs to commit against. The "theories of no change / negative change" section meets the stakeholder's Paradigm-A reflex by naming the failure modes operationally rather than letting them surface as ambiguity-trigger in the meeting. (Source: Ch 3, "How Do You Create Theory of Change Diagrams?")

The participation-versus-conceptual-rigour trade (Ch 12) reshapes through this lens: the stakeholder's Paradigm-A operating register prefers conceptual rigour with named owners over deeply participatory ambiguity-rich outputs. The engagement design that lands is *participatory at scoping (to secure ownership), expert-driven in synthesis (to produce a rigorous artefact), participatory at validation (to confirm the artefact and re-anchor ownership)* — a hybrid that supplies both the warmth-and-acknowledgment surface and the A-shaped operational substance.

The horrendogram failure mode (Ch 1, Ch 4) is acute for this lens: a complex map shown to a Paradigm-A-operating executive without filtering reads as "this is a system failure, not an individual failure" without the operational specificity the stakeholder needs to act. The discipline of layered or staged maps — produce a real version and a simplified version (Ch 3's "two version" trick for ToC generalises here) — is load-bearing.

**Triggers the business-executive-stakeholder lens reaches for in this distillation:**
- Phase 1: CLD archetype-naming as a structural reframe surfaced as choice, not as critique.
- Phase 2: Theory of Change as accountability surface for technical-debt servicing or capacity-investment cases.
- Phase 6: any board-memo or executive-update artefact where the ToC discipline supplies the structural rigour the stakeholder needs, with parsimonious B-vocabulary in opening warmth only.

The lens recedes when the artefact is engineering-internal communication (the CTO lens carries that) or when the question is pure facilitation craft for a non-executive audience.

## Anti-patterns the lenses help avoid

- **Producing a horrendogram for a CTO who needs the constraint in the first two sentences.** The single-page complex CLD lands as Past-tempo evidence-display; the same dynamics with the core system engine surfaced and the rest layered lands as constraint-as-mechanism. (CTO lens, Source: Ch 4 + Ch 1)
- **Producing a Theory of Change diagram for a Paradigm-A-operating executive that opens in B-vocabulary.** "This is a system-shaped intervention with structural assumptions about emergent outcomes..." reads as sermon. "Proposed: 8 engineers, one quarter, target outcome 25% incident reduction; here is the assumption surface" lands. (Business-executive-stakeholder lens, Source: Ch 3 + Ch 12)
- **Allocating two B-moves into one artefact.** "This is a system failure, not an individual failure" + "this is a structural problem requiring structural intervention" + "this requires a learning loop, not a control loop" stacks paradigm reframes the stakeholder will not carry. One reframe per artefact, surfaced as choice, with A-action close. (Business-executive-stakeholder lens)
- **Treating the appropriateness triangle as an academic exercise rather than a constraint-discipline move.** The triangulation produces *one* method, with named reasons. The CTO reads multi-method recommendations as analysis paralysis. (CTO lens, Source: Ch 11)
- **Skipping the post-workshop one-to-ones because "the workshop produced the artefact".** Both lenses fire on this. The CTO uses one-to-ones to surface the constraint-as-mechanism question that didn't fit the workshop format; the business-executive-stakeholder uses them to surface the real concerns that the group setting suppressed. (Source: Ch 10, post-workshop)

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The seven methods rest on foundational works this corpus does not hold as primary references.

- *Donella Meadows, Thinking in Systems* — cited for the definition of a system as "an interconnected set of elements that is coherently organised in a way that achieves something" (Ch 1, "What Is Systems Mapping?"). Flagged [BT] in the deep ref.
- *Patton Rogers, Evaluating the Contribution of Development Interventions* (2014) — cited as the source of the inputs → activities → outputs → outcomes → impacts results chain underlying Theory of Change (Ch 3, "What Is Theory of Change Mapping?"). Flagged [BT].
- *Kim's eight CLD archetypes* (2000) — the eight named patterns (drifting goals, escalation, fixes that fail, growth and under-investment, limits to success, shifting the burden, success to the successful, tragedy of the commons) are attributed to Kim 2000 throughout (Ch 4, "How Do You Create Causal Loop Diagrams?"). Kim is not held as a primary reference; this distillation cites Barbrook-Johnson & Penn's presentation of these archetypes.
- *Box and Draper's "all models are wrong, but some are useful"* — cited in the definitional section of the deep ref (Ch 1, "What Is Systems Mapping?"). Flagged [BT]. Not held as a primary reference.
- *Williams and Hummelbrunner (2011)* — cited for the three-point system definition (elements, links, boundary). Flagged [BT].
- *Gojko Adzic, Impact Mapping* and *Sean Ellis, North Star Framework* — the Open Practice Library distillation refers to these; this distillation does not independently invoke them. Where the Integration section references `openstax-business-ethics`, the Grunig-Hunt lineage is the source's own (Ch 3.2), not borrowed-through.

**Named limits of the source.** The authors explicitly scope to seven methods; they exclude mind mapping, stakeholder mapping, giga-mapping, viable systems model, social network analysis, path analysis, concept mapping, participatory action research, design thinking, and permaculture (Ch 1, "What Methods Are Not in This Book?"). The book is a methodology text, not an empirical findings text: it tells practitioners how to model, not what any specific domain's model reveals. The authors are explicit that systems mapping is not primarily for forecasting or prediction; emergent effects require "other modelling approaches" (Ch 1, "Why Think About Systems Mapping Now?"). The book is silent on AI-specific causal structures.

**Evidence-marker continuity.** The deep reference carries the book's load-bearing claims with [V] markers (the horrendogram label; "combining methods in some form will normally be useful or valuable"; the participation-vs-rigour trade phrasing; the "all models are wrong" citation; "we cannot force or control the complex adaptive systems we live in and make up, but we can work with them to steer and nurture the change we want") and [AR] markers for the authors' own theoretical positions (the appropriateness-as-frame argument; process-value-over-product-value). This distillation paraphrases [V]-marked content throughout; no verbatim blockquotes appear here. The [BT] attributions for Kim's archetypes, Rogers's results chain, and Meadows's system definition are preserved as borrowed-through rather than the book's own original claims.
