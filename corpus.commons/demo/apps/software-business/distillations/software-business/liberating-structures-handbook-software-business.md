## Software-Business Relevance

The handbook applies to software-business work in the moments where the technical and commercial constrain each other and the resolution depends on a *room* — a board paper drafted with cross-functional input; a build-vs-buy decision held across product, engineering, and finance; an incident write-up that must land with engineers, legal, and an exec audience without one register flattening the others; a roadmap-vs-capacity argument where each function has a partial view. The handbook's structural claim — that ownership outperforms buy-in, and that structural change beats motivational change — is exactly the move a CTO or founder needs when the engineering function has been asked to "buy in" to a commercial commitment made without them, or when sales has been asked to "buy in" to a technical-debt servicing window decided without them (Source: *Engaging Everyone with LS Handbook*, "Ownership versus Buy-in").

A second strand is the handbook's Stacey-Zimmerman complexity classification. Software-business decisions sit across the simple/complicated/complex spectrum: a pricing rule with known cost structure is complicated; a market-entry-with-AI-integration decision under partner-roadmap uncertainty is complex. The handbook supplies the diagnostic to choose method before applying it — "Many of our strategies and heuristics for working on problems are designed for simple or complicated situations. These strategies don't work well when things are complex because the situation is emergent and unpredictable" (Source: "Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix"). For software-business specifically, the matrix names when an expert-led analysis followed by board approval will fail because the situation is complex and the affected community is not in the room.

A third strand is the catalogue's value as a *toolkit for the cross-functional moments* software-business work routinely demands. 1-2-4-Whole Group fits a decision-rights conversation where engineering, product, and sales each need to surface their view before commitment. Wise Crowds fits a CTO facing a build-vs-buy decision whose stakeholders haven't given them the option-space. Troika Consulting fits a peer cohort of engineering managers each working a parallel staffing decision. TRIZ fits a "what would make our reliability investment fail?" inversion before committing the budget. 15% Solutions fits engineering-led change inside an organisation where the 85% — corporate strategy, customer commitments, regulatory regime — is not under the engineering function's control. Wicked Questions fits strategic-positioning paradoxes ("how do we sustain enterprise-grade reliability while shipping at start-up cadence?") that are real tensions, not problems with hidden right answers.

The handbook is not a software-business *theory* source. It does not price the trade-offs Jones surfaces, does not provide Letaw's RACI mechanics, does not deliver the Mintzberg structures or Goldratt constraint discipline. Its software-business contribution is the in-the-room craft: the structures that make a cross-functional conversation produce an owned decision, the diagnostic for choosing structure by complexity, and the explicit anti-patterns of forum design that turn good analysis into ignored deliverables.

## Key Concepts for Software-Business

1.  **Ownership versus buy-in as a software-business decision-quality distinction.** Engineering "buying in" to a commercial commitment made without them — or sales "buying in" to a reliability-investment schedule decided without them — produces "lukewarm, pallid implementation and mediocre results." The handbook's diagnostic move: listen for the word *buy-in* in the room. It is "a danger signal telling you that your development and implementation process is missing the essential ingredient of involving all who should be involved." For software-business, the involvement question routinely crosses function boundaries (engineering, product, finance, sales, legal). (Source: *Engaging Everyone with LS Handbook*, "Ownership versus Buy-in")

2.  **Stacey-Zimmerman complexity classification before method choice.** The Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix names a software-business diagnostic move: classify the situation before reaching for a method. Recipe (simple) → known cost structure, known agreement; airplane (complicated) → many parts but known how to combine, expertise solves it; child (complex) → emergent, unpredictable, requires distributed framing. Build-vs-buy with a stable partner roadmap is complicated; market-entry-with-AI-integration under partner-roadmap uncertainty is complex. Methods designed for recipes fail on child-class problems. (Source: "Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix"; Stacey and Zimmerman as borrowed-through lineage)

3.  **Structural change, not motivational change, is the lever for software-business behaviour.** "For things to really change, structural elements need to change, too. Otherwise change is short-lived." For software-business, this rules out the persuasion-and-OKR pattern as a stand-alone change move — the structure (who is in which conversation, what decision rights exist, how the forum is shaped) usually has to change too. (Source: "Introduction")

4.  **1-2-4-Whole Group as the cross-functional-decision opener.** Individual reflection → pairs → groups of 4–6 → whole-group surfacing. "More than six is too big to give everyone a chance to talk." Fits software-business moments where engineering, product, finance, and sales each need to surface their view of a shared decision (pricing, roadmap commit, capacity allocation) before commitment. The structure scales without losing every-voice access. (Source: "1-2-4-Whole Group")

5.  **Wise Crowds for a single-owner decision with cross-functional stakeholders.** A CTO facing a build-vs-buy decision sits as "client" with 5–6 consulting-team peers (product, engineering leadership, finance, customer-success). Client describes the challenge ~10 minutes; then turns chair around and listens silently ≥15 minutes while peers converse; client returns with "Here's what I heard that intrigued, surprised, or resonated with me." The team's "job is not to decide 'this is what The Client ought to do'" — structurally separating option-broadening from option-selection in a way conventional cross-functional meetings collapse. (Source: "Wise Crowds")

6.  **Troika Consulting for a peer cohort with parallel software-business decisions.** Three engineering managers each facing a staffing decision; three product managers each facing a roadmap-vs-capacity decision; three founders each facing a pricing-against-engineering-cost decision. Ten-minute rounds; each gets a turn as focal person honed by two peers. Closing conversation about patterns across the three rounds. Produces three owned next-step decisions per troika without consultant-style external-authority dependence. (Source: "Troika Consulting")

7.  **TRIZ inversion for reliability, security, and technical-debt investments.** "Design a comprehensive system that makes it absolutely impossible to get that result." Apply to: "What would guarantee we *do not* hit our reliability SLA?" "What would guarantee our security investment is wasted?" "What would guarantee technical debt accumulates faster than we service it?" Surface the currently-invisible self-sabotaging structures before deciding on positive moves. Software-business value: many engineering-investment cases are rejected by boards because the failure mode is unstated and unaudited. (Source: "TRIZ"; Altshuller as borrowed-through lineage)

8.  **15% Solutions for engineering-led change inside a constrained 85%.** "Most people have about 15-percent control over their work situations. The other 85 percent rests in the broader context, shaped by the general structures, systems, events and culture in which they operate." For engineering leadership working inside an organisation whose strategy, contracts, and regulatory regime are not under their control, the 15% reframe converts a paralysing "we cannot fix this without C-suite change" into tractable "what is in our 15%?" — without denying the 85%. (Source: "15% Solutions"; Gareth Morgan as cited)

9.  **Wicked Questions for software-business strategic-positioning paradoxes.** "A question is 'wicked' if there is an embedded paradox or tension in the question… A wicked question is not a trick question." Software-business wicked questions: "How can we sustain enterprise-grade reliability while shipping at start-up cadence?" "How can we maintain platform discipline across teams while allowing local product-line innovation?" "How can we honour customer-contract commitments while servicing the technical debt those contracts were built on?" Naming the wicked-question quality changes how the room engages — surfacing the paradox before the room hunts for a hidden right answer. (Source: "Wicked Questions")

10.  **Discovery & Action Dialogues for surfacing positively-deviant in-system solutions.** Six-question protocol ending in "What has to happen next to make that happen? Who will do what when next?" Software-business application: when one team has already solved a version of the problem locally (a deployment-frequency improvement, a reliability practice, a hiring approach) and the broader engineering organisation hasn't surfaced their solution. The DAD ends in named-owner-and-next-step, the structural commitment that converts conversation into engineering action. (Source: "Discovery & Action Dialogues"; Plexus Institute / Positive Deviance as cited)

11.  **What/So What/Now What as the post-incident or post-release debrief backbone.** "WHAT? What is emerging here? What data stands out? What actually happened? SO WHAT? What do the data imply? Do you see a pattern? Does it make a difference? NOW WHAT? What action may help us move forward? Who else should be here?" In order. For software-business incident reviews, release retrospectives, and quarter-end debriefs, the sequence forbids skipping to Now-What before the data and pattern questions have landed. (Source: "After Action Debrief"; Center for Creative Leadership as borrowed-through lineage)

12.  **Storyboard before agenda for software-business forum design.** "Try using a storyboard instead of an agenda… Unlike an agenda, it also includes and illustrates something about what the participants will experience." Diagnostic: "if your storyboard has a whole lot of podium sessions in a row, it's not going to be very engaging!" For a quarterly board update, an exec offsite on AI integration, or a cross-functional decision workshop on portfolio prioritisation, the storyboard surfaces engagement-risk that the agenda hides. (Source: "Storyboard Design and Planning")

13.  **Engage the Unusual Suspects upstream of software-business decisions.** "Who's not here that could contribute to our conversation?" — "one of the most important questions you can ask." For software-business: which engineering line manager is not in the pricing conversation; which legal counsel is not in the AI-integration conversation; which customer is not in the reliability-investment conversation; which finance partner is not in the technical-debt conversation. Decisions made without the people most affected miss critical context and fail at the implementation step. (Source: "Engage the Unusual Suspects"; "Nothing About Me Without Me")

14.  **Surfacing tools are not committing tools.** 25-to-10 is named explicitly: "This is not recommended to make a decision. It's a way to get a sense of the wisdom of the crowd." Mini Constellations similarly surface positions without producing commitment. For software-business work, conflating a surfacing exercise (a poll of engineering preference; a constellation on risk tolerance) with a decision rule converts a perception-broadening tool into a vote and loses the distributed-intelligence signal. (Source: "25-to-10"; "Mini Constellations")

15.  **Simple Rules for software-business policy and process design.** Five categories — How To, Boundary, Priority, Timing, Exit. The test: "What would happen if you left that rule out? Is this really required versus just an artifact of what we've been doing all along?" Apply to engineering process policies (code-review rules, deployment-approval rules, incident-response rules), product-decision rules, or vendor-engagement rules. Strip accumulated artefacts that no longer earn their keep. (Source: "Simple Rules")

## Questions to Ask During Software-Business Work

### Phase 1: Strategic positioning (pricing, market entry, build-vs-buy, portfolio decisions, AI integration choices)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Classify the situation before choosing method | Where does this strategic question sit on the Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix? Is it a recipe (known cost structure, known agreement), an airplane (many parts but known how to combine), or a child (emergent, unpredictable)? A complex strategic-positioning question with unstable agreement and unstable certainty requires distributed framing, not expert-then-board-approval. |
| Test for buy-in framing on the strategic decision | Is the strategy being built by a small executive team with the intent to bring it to engineering, product, and finance "for buy-in"? If yes, predict the outcome: "lukewarm, pallid implementation and mediocre results." Re-frame as participatory upstream of the decision, not after it. |
| Frame strategic-positioning paradoxes as wicked questions | Is the underlying question a paradox — sustaining reliability while shipping fast; maintaining discipline while allowing innovation; honouring contract commitments while servicing the debt those contracts created? Name the wicked-question quality so the room stops hunting for the hidden right answer. |
| Run Participatory Scenarios on AI-integration strategy | Rather than experts building two-by-two scenarios for the board, build them with the engineering leadership, product, and the affected customer voices in the room. "Lack of ownership on the part of the whole community means that the scenarios don't really play a part in decision making." |
| Engage the Unusual Suspects on the strategic-positioning question | Who's not here that could contribute? For a build-vs-buy decision: the engineering line manager who would maintain the build, the customer-success owner who would handle the buy. For an AI-integration decision: legal counsel, the data-engineering lead, the customer most affected. For a market-entry decision: the salesperson with field knowledge. |
| TRIZ-invert the strategy | What would guarantee this strategic move fails? Surface the self-sabotage structures before committing to the positive move. |

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Run What/So What/Now What as the incident-debrief backbone | Three sequential questions, in order. For a security event, a reliability outage, or a customer-facing defect: "WHAT actually happened? SO WHAT do the data imply, what pattern? NOW WHAT action — and who else should be in the next conversation?" Refuse the temptation to jump to Now-What. (Pair with NHS Just Culture for the structured individual-vs-system locus decision.) |

| 1-2-4-Whole Group on the risk-and-reliability investment case | Individual reflection → pairs → groups → whole. Use when engineering, product, finance, legal, and customer-success each need to surface their view of an investment before the case goes to the board. |
| Storyboard the breach or outage communication arc | The storyboard discipline applies to the communication sequence — what stakeholders will experience over the days following the event, not just the timeline of facts. |
| Apply 15% Solutions when the broader regulatory regime cannot move | What's in the engineering function's 15% on this compliance question? Move there without waiting for the 85% (the regulator, the auditor, the customer-contract regime) to change. |

### Phase 6: Stakeholder communication (board reporting, exec alignment, investor relations, customer messaging)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Compose the question deliberately | "Conceptual containers created by the way a question is phrased" — one of the four pattern-language primitives. For a board paper, the question the paper answers shapes whether the board engages with the technical substance or skims to the recommendation. Test the question's shape (wicked? appreciative? operational?) before drafting. |
| Storyboard the exec-memo arc | Storyboard the memo as a sequence of participant experiences — what the exec reader will think, feel, and notice across the opening, the data, the analysis, the decision-being-requested. Move components around to test what lands. |
| Refuse Q-A-Q-A in the board read-out | "Q A Q A Q A Q A pattern… reinforces the 'central tendency' versus the distributed intelligence of the group." Batch questions in 3–4 at a time. For a board update, collect questions on cards or post-its; group them; respond in batches. |
| Honour the ≥20-second wait | After asking the board "what do you think about this trade-off?" wait at least 20 seconds. Look down at your shoes. The 6-second average suppresses board contribution and signals the CTO has the answer they want. |
| Surface the Unusual Suspects for the exec offsite | Who's not at the exec offsite on AI integration that would change what the room thinks? The data-engineering lead; the affected customer; legal counsel; the partner whose roadmap conditions the integration. |
| Choose space and aesthetics for high-engagement exec sessions | A standard conference-room round table is "too large for juicy conversation." For an exec offsite designed to produce ownership rather than buy-in, set up "4-tops"; choose natural light; provide long walls for graphic recording. Space is structural infrastructure, not logistics. |

## What to Look For

### Pattern 1: The cross-functional decision being made through serial buy-in
- **Signal.** A pricing decision, build-vs-buy call, roadmap commitment, or strategic-positioning move is being built by one function (typically executive or product) and circulated to other functions ("engineering, please buy in"; "finance, please approve"; "sales, please commit").
- **Diagnosis.** The structure is producing the buy-in failure mode the handbook names directly. "Aiming for buy-in creates lukewarm, pallid implementation and mediocre results" (Source: "Ownership versus Buy-in"). For software-business decisions where the technical and commercial constrain each other, the function carrying the constraint must be in the development conversation — not added to the implementation conversation.
- **Follow-up.** Reframe upstream. Identify the Unusual Suspects ("Who's not here that could contribute?"). Convene 1-2-4-Whole Group or Wise Crowds with the cross-functional set. The structural cost of slowing the decision is recovered in implementation quality.

### Pattern 2: A complex problem treated with complicated-problem methods
- **Signal.** A market-entry-with-AI-integration decision, a reliability strategy under uncertain customer-mix, a hiring plan under uncertain scaling trajectory — being approached with expert analysis, NPV calculation, and board approval as if the situation were a recipe or an airplane.
- **Diagnosis.** "Many of our strategies and heuristics for working on problems are designed for simple or complicated situations. These strategies don't work well when things are complex because the situation is emergent and unpredictable" (Source: "Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix"). The Stacey-Zimmerman frame names what's mismatched: the situation has unstable agreement, unstable certainty, or both, and complicated-problem methods cannot resolve it.
- **Follow-up.** Classify the situation explicitly. If complex, route to methods designed for complexity: Participatory Scenarios, Wicked Questions, Discovery & Action Dialogues, the wisdom-of-the-crowd-surfacing tools. Reserve NPV and decision trees for the recipe and airplane parts.

### Pattern 3: The forum is structurally hostile to the decision
- **Signal.** Cross-functional decision in a large conference room with one big table; fluorescent lighting; agenda built around 30-minute presentations followed by 10 minutes of questions; report-outs from breakouts; one strong voice (typically the most-senior person) anchoring the conversation early.
- **Diagnosis.** The forum is producing the structural failure modes the handbook names. Big tables are "too large for juicy conversation"; fluorescent light makes people "wilt"; report-outs "drain the life out of whatever conversation that took place in the small group"; Q-A-Q-A patterns "reinforce the 'central tendency' versus the distributed intelligence of the group." For a software-business decision that needs cross-functional ownership, the room is suppressing the contribution the decision needs (Source: "Choosing Space"; "Aesthetics Are Important"; "A Note about Report Outs"; "Taking Questions").
- **Follow-up.** Change the room. 4-tops or knee-to-knee groups; natural light; long walls for graphic recording; storyboard the session before the agenda; refuse report-outs in favour of Gallery Walk or bring-the-experience-forward; batch questions 3–4 at a time; hold the ≥20-second wait after questions.

### Pattern 4: Engineering investment cases rejected without articulated failure mode
- **Signal.** A reliability investment, a technical-debt remediation, a security spend, or a platform-engineering case repeatedly comes to the board and is deferred or rejected. The case has ROI, has scope, has timeline. The failure mode is implicit — the board cannot quite say what they're worried about.
- **Diagnosis.** The case has stated positively (what we will achieve, what we will spend) but the inverse (what would make this spend wasted) is unstated. TRIZ names the move: "Design a comprehensive system that makes it absolutely impossible to get that result. What policies, practices, and ways of operating would make it 100% certain there is no way any of the things you want can happen" (Source: "TRIZ").
- **Follow-up.** Run a TRIZ inversion before the next board cycle. With the cross-functional team, design the system that *guarantees* the investment fails. Compare the adverse-design to the current state; surface and eliminate similarities. The case that returns to the board names the failure mode and addresses it.

### Pattern 5: Surfacing tools used as committing tools
- **Signal.** A polling-the-engineering-team session, a positions-constellation on risk tolerance, a 25-to-10 idea-rating exercise, or a "thumbs-up around the table" is being treated as the decision rather than as a surfacing input.
- **Diagnosis.** The handbook is explicit: 25-to-10 is "not recommended to make a decision. It's a way to get a sense of the wisdom of the crowd" (Source: "25-to-10"). Mini Constellations surface positional diversity; they do not produce commitment. Treating either as a decision-rule converts a perception-broadening exercise into a vote and loses the distributed-intelligence signal.
- **Follow-up.** Name the structure as surfacing. Identify the legitimate decision-maker (the CTO, the founder, the engineering manager, the convening leader). Route from surfacing to committing through a DAD ("Who will do what when next?") or a Wise Crowds client decision.

### Pattern 6: The post-incident debrief jumping to Now-What
- **Signal.** A reliability incident, security event, or customer-facing defect has occurred. The leadership team convenes and immediately discusses fixes, owners, and timelines.
- **Diagnosis.** The What/So What/Now What sequence is being skipped — "WHAT actually happened? SO WHAT do the data imply?" are being assumed rather than answered. Decisions made without the data-and-pattern step land badly and often re-create the conditions that produced the incident. (Source: "After Action Debrief")
- **Follow-up.** Enforce the order. Run What with the operational data on the table. Run So-What with the pattern across this incident and the previous N. Only then run Now-What — and end with "Who else should be here?" looping back to the Unusual Suspects.

## When to Use This Reference

- A *cross-functional software-business decision* needs structural change to produce ownership rather than buy-in across engineering, product, finance, sales, and customer-success.
- A *strategic-positioning question* is showing characteristics of complexity (unstable agreement, unstable certainty) and complicated-problem methods are not landing.
- A *post-incident debrief, release retrospective, or quarter-end review* needs the discipline of the What/So What/Now What sequence.
- A *cross-functional workshop, exec offsite, or board working session* is being designed and the convener needs a storyboard-and-room-design pass before agenda-and-slides.
- A *single owner has a software-business decision* (build-vs-buy, hiring, capacity) and would benefit from being structurally separated from the option-generating conversation (Wise Crowds).
- A *peer cohort has parallel software-business decisions* (engineering managers staffing teams; product managers prioritising roadmaps) and would benefit from peer-honed next-steps (Troika).
- An *engineering investment case* (reliability, security, technical debt, platform) has been repeatedly rejected without articulated failure mode — TRIZ inversion before the next board cycle.
- An *engineering-led change effort* is paralysed by the broader context (corporate strategy, contracts, regulators) — 15% Solutions to make the work tractable inside the constrained 85%.
- An *organisation's after-event commitment is fading* (the handbook's "half-life problem") despite an energetic engagement event — distinguish the structural changes that landed from the motivational ones that didn't.

## Worked Example

A product-led SaaS company has decided to integrate a generative-AI capability into its core product. The CEO, the head of product, and a small AI-strategy task force have spent six weeks producing a 40-page strategic memo with five workstreams, ROI projections, and a 12-month roadmap. The memo will be circulated to engineering, customer-success, legal, sales, and finance — "for buy-in" — at a one-day exec offsite the following month.

The CTO reads the memo, recognises three things, and routes the question through the LS handbook's frame.

**First: the framing is buy-in.** The strategy was built by a small expert team; the broader leadership is being asked to implement. The handbook diagnoses buy-in as a structural error symptom, not a communication problem: "Aiming for buy-in creates lukewarm, pallid implementation and mediocre results." The word itself is the diagnostic signal: "Anytime you or someone around you thinks or talks about buy-in, beware! It is a danger signal telling you that your development and implementation process is missing the essential ingredient of involving all who should be involved." (Source: Heft & Pattillo, *Engaging Everyone with LS Handbook*, "Ownership versus Buy-in") The CTO names the trap to the CEO before the offsite: an AI-integration strategy implemented through buy-in will not be owned by the functions who must carry it.

**Second: the situation is complex, not complicated.** The AI capability's product-market fit, the regulatory regime around training data, the customer mix's appetite, the platform partner's roadmap, and the engineering team's readiness are all in motion. Stacey-Zimmerman names this as the child case — emergent and unpredictable. "Many of our strategies and heuristics for working on problems are designed for simple or complicated situations. These strategies don't work well when things are complex because the situation is emergent and unpredictable." (Source: "Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix") The CTO recommends that the offsite re-frame from "approve the strategy" to "co-author the strategy with the people who will implement it."

**Third: the offsite forum design is structurally hostile to ownership.** Six 45-minute presentations followed by Q&A across 16 people around a single large conference table in a hotel boardroom under fluorescent lighting. The handbook names each failure mode: "the round tables typically available in most conference centers and hotels are too large for juicy conversation"; "most people wilt when meeting all day in artificial — especially fluorescent — light"; serial presentations "drain/deadening the energy in the meeting, which happens when people have to listen to any one voice for more than 5 minutes." (Source: "Choosing Space"; "Aesthetics Are Important"; "Celebrity Interviews") The CTO storyboards the day with the CEO — "try using a storyboard instead of an agenda to design and plan a meeting" — and the engagement risk becomes visible: "if you find your storyboard has a whole lot of podium sessions in a row, it's not going to be very engaging!" (Source: "Storyboard Design and Planning")

**The redesigned offsite.** Morning: Impromptu Speed Networking on three provocative questions about AI integration — "everyone gets to speak early in the meeting" and "in a 30-minute session, everyone will have had 12-15 minutes of air time — no matter how many participants." (Source: "Impromptu Speed Networking") Then 1-2-4-Whole Group on "what is the strategic question we are actually trying to answer about AI?" — individual reflection, pairs, groups of 4-6 ("more than six is too big to give everyone a chance to talk"), then whole-group surfacing. (Source: "1-2-4-Whole Group") Five wicked questions emerge, including "how can we honour customer-contract commitments while shipping AI capability that may change those commitments?" Mid-day: Wise Crowds with the CTO as client and six cross-functional peers as consulting team on the build-vs-buy decision; the client turns chair around and listens silently while peers converse — "the team's job is not to decide 'this is what The Client ought to do.'" (Source: "Wise Crowds") Afternoon: Troika Consulting with three groups of product-and-engineering pairs, each working a parallel AI-integration sub-decision. Closing: What/So What/Now What in order — WHAT happened today, SO WHAT do the patterns imply, NOW WHAT actions and owners — surfacing five owned next-steps with named owners and 30-day timeboxes. (Source: "After Action Debrief: What, So What, Now What?")

**Outcome.** The strategy that emerges is 60% the original memo's content reshaped, 40% new. Engineering leadership, customer-success, legal, and the sales-engineering function own the framing — not because they bought in, but because they were in the development conversation. Twelve months later, four of five workstreams have shipped at or ahead of plan; the fifth was killed at the 90-day check after engineering surfaced a regulatory risk the original memo had under-weighted.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

### Anti-pattern 1: The persuasion-and-OKR-only change effort
- **Signal.** An engineering-culture change, a delivery-cadence change, or a quality-discipline change is being driven through executive messaging, all-hands speeches, and OKR cascades — without structural change to the rooms, the decision-rights, or the forum design.
- **Diagnosis.** "For things to really change, structural elements need to change, too. Otherwise change is short-lived" (Source: "Introduction"). The handbook's claim is that motivational change without structural change does not survive contact with default ways of meeting and deciding.
- **Follow-up.** Identify the structural change the effort needs alongside the motivational one. Which forums change? Which decision rights move? Which seating arrangements, room choices, or storyboard designs make the new behaviour easier to enact than the old?

### Anti-pattern 2: The expert-built scenario followed by community buy-in
- **Signal.** Strategic-planning sessions producing two-by-two scenarios built by an executive task force, brought to the broader organisation for "alignment" or "buy-in" or "calibration."
- **Diagnosis.** "Many companies develop scenarios to support strategic planning. These scenarios are often created by 'experts' or small teams. As a result, many companies discover that lack of ownership on the part of the whole community means that the scenarios don't really play a part in decision making" (Source: "Participatory Scenarios").
- **Follow-up.** Build scenarios with the implementing community in the room. Use the five-step Participatory Scenarios method. Accept that the scenarios will be different from the expert-built ones; that's the feature, not the bug.

### Anti-pattern 3: The board presentation as a serial monologue
- **Signal.** A quarterly board update delivered as 6–8 presentations of 15–25 minutes each, with Q&A at the end. The board engages with the surface-level summary; the substantive technical content is not discussed.
- **Diagnosis.** The forum is producing the listening-fatigue failure mode the handbook names — "draining/deadening the energy in the meeting, which happens when people have to listen to any one voice for more than 5 minutes" (Source: "Celebrity Interviews"). Combined with the Q-A-Q-A "central tendency" pattern in the closing discussion, the structure suppresses board engagement with the technical substance the CTO needs them to engage with.
- **Follow-up.** Use Celebrity Interviews to replace conventional presentations (a board chair interviewing the CTO with generative questions: "What surprised you about reliability this quarter? What would you do differently?" instead of "What does the reliability data say?"). Batch board questions 3–4 at a time. Take questions on cards and group them before responding.

### Anti-pattern 4: The implementation-stage RACI workshop
- **Signal.** A change-management initiative is being implemented; mid-implementation, decision-rights ambiguity surfaces; the leadership team convenes a one-day RACI workshop to "lock down ownership" before the next phase.
- **Diagnosis.** A RACI workshop convened after decision-rights ambiguity has manifested in delivery problems is treating the symptom (the decision rights) without surfacing the structural problem (the decision-making forum design upstream). The handbook's frame: the structural change has not landed; the motivational pressure to "lock down ownership" cannot substitute for it.
- **Follow-up.** Run 1-2-4-Whole Group on "where is the actual decision-rights ambiguity in our work" before the RACI workshop. Surface the unspoken disagreements. Combine with Disagree-and-Commit framing from the Open Practice Library when divergent views need to land in a single direction without performing false consensus.

### Anti-pattern 5: The all-hands as the engagement event
- **Signal.** A company-wide all-hands, town hall, or quarterly meeting is being designed as the engagement vehicle for a major strategic shift, a re-org, or an AI-integration announcement. Format: 90 minutes, CEO presenting, 15 minutes of Q&A.
- **Diagnosis.** The all-hands format produces the half-life problem the handbook names — "The half-life of the energy and commitment to new ways of being after these events can be short when participants return to their organizations and fall back into default ways of meeting" (Source: "A Pattern Language for Engagement"). Without structural change in the smaller forums people return to, the all-hands' effect dissipates.
- **Follow-up.** Pair the all-hands with structural changes in the team-level forums. Cascade Discovery & Action Dialogues into engineering teams; rotate Troika Consulting across managers; run 15% Solutions exercises with line teams to surface what they can do in their scope. Treat the all-hands as one engagement event, not the engagement event.

### Anti-pattern 6: The wicked question framed as a problem
- **Signal.** A strategic-positioning paradox ("ship fast and ship reliable"; "platform discipline and product autonomy"; "customer-contract commitments and technical-debt servicing") is being treated as a problem with a hidden right answer. The team is searching for the trick, the missing piece, the framework that resolves the tension.
- **Diagnosis.** "A question is 'wicked' if there is an embedded paradox or tension in the question. A wicked question is not a trick question. With a trick question, someone knows the answer. Wicked questions do not have obvious answers. Their value lies in their capacity to open up options, inquiry and bring to the surface the fundamental issues that need to be addressed" (Source: "Wicked Questions"). Treating a wicked question as a problem with a hidden right answer either produces premature closure on a partial solution or paralysis.
- **Follow-up.** Name the wicked-question quality explicitly. Run Wise Crowds or 1-2-4-Whole Group on the named paradox. The output is not the resolution; it's the option-space the paradox makes visible.

## Integration with Other References

- **Letaw, Handbook of Software Engineering Methods** (Ch 2, 3, 6): Letaw provides the software-engineering decision-rights mechanics (RACI, triple constraint, fist-of-five) that the LS handbook does not. The two combine: use LS structures upstream of a RACI workshop (1-2-4-Whole Group to surface the actual ambiguity) and to ratify the outcome (Disagree-and-Commit-style commitment after the workshop). Use Letaw's fist-of-five inside a Scrum-style team for commitment-vote; use LS structures when the goal is cross-functional option-generation upstream of any vote. Letaw's Tuckman team-stage map and the LS catalogue overlap on team-development craft.
- **Jones, Evidence-based Software Engineering**: Jones brings the empirical findings — bi-exponential fault patterns, the post-1980 evidence collapse, agency theory in vendor-client relations, cognitive-capitalism — that price the trade-offs LS structures help surface. Where the LS handbook assumes good-faith conversation, Jones surfaces the information-asymmetry, agency, and incentive distortions that distort the conversation. Use Jones to name the structural distortion; use LS to design the conversation that mitigates it. For example: Jones's agency-theory analysis of vendor-client moral hazard is named before a Wise Crowds session on a build-vs-buy decision, ensuring the consulting team can speak to the distortion the contract dynamics create.
- **Scrum Guide (Schwaber & Sutherland) + Approach Perfect Field Guide to Scrum Events**: Scrum's empirical cadence — Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective — is the rhythmic *container* the LS handbook names as a primitive. The Scrum Guide deliberately under-prescribes technique inside events; the LS catalogue is one source of those techniques. Use 1-2-4-Whole Group inside Sprint Planning Topic-One; use What/So What/Now What as Sprint Retrospective backbone; use Wise Crowds when one Product Owner needs option-broadening; use Troika across managers running parallel team decisions.
- **Open Practice Library**: OPL explicitly names LS structures (1-2-4-All, Wise Crowds, Troika Consulting, Disagree-and-Commit) in its foundation, discovery, options, and delivery phases. The two sources cross-feed: OPL provides the phase context for *where* in the change-management arc each LS structure fits; the LS handbook provides the operational detail of *how* to run each structure.
- **OpenStax Principles of Management** (Ch 5, 8, 13, 14, 16): OpenStax provides Mintzberg's structures, Five Forces, PESTEL, and the broader management-level frames that sit *above* the LS catalogue. Use OpenStax for the strategic-positioning theory; use LS for the room in which the strategic-positioning decision actually gets made. The two combine on AI-integration decisions: PESTEL surfaces the environmental factors; Participatory Scenarios builds them with the implementing community.
- **OpenStax Accounting Vol 2** (Ch 2, 5, 6, 7): Accounting Vol 2 provides Goldratt's theory of constraints, CVP analysis, relevant-cost discipline, and break-even framing — the financial discipline software-business decisions need. LS structures provide the cross-functional room in which the financial analysis is discussed and owned. Use Accounting Vol 2 to build the financial case; use LS to make the case stick in the cross-functional conversation.
- **OpenStax Business Ethics** (Ch 3): the Mitchell-Agle-Wood stakeholder salience model and the Donaldson-Preston three approaches give the stakeholder-prioritisation theory that the LS handbook's "Unusual Suspects" question operationalises. Use Business Ethics for who has standing on a software-business question (AI ethics, customer data, externalised cost); use LS for how to bring them in.
- **NHS Just Culture Guide + TC 25-20 Army AAR + LFUO Learning Review Guide**: For software-business incidents specifically (outages, breaches, defects, customer-facing failures), the incident-response sources provide the structured decision protocols the LS handbook does not — NHS's individual-vs-system locus decision tree; TC 25-20's AAR-vs-critique distinction; LFUO's no-recommendations FLA versus recommendation-producing Learning Review with administrative firewall. Use the LS What/So What/Now What as the dialogue scaffolding inside these protocols; use the structured protocols for the defensible decision the incident requires.
- **Approach Perfect Field Guide to Scrum Events** (Sprint Retrospective): the Field Guide names Kerth's Prime Directive and the structural conditions for productive retrospectives. The LS catalogue (What/So What/Now What, Wise Crowds, Troika Consulting, Appreciative Interviews) populates the Sprint Retrospective with technique. Use the Field Guide for the retrospective's conditions; use LS for the structures inside it.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The handbook's structure catalogue cites several external lineages not held as primary references in this corpus. The Stacey-Zimmerman Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix (also called the Stacey Matrix) is used as the complexity-classification tool but the underlying Stacey and Zimmerman publications are not held; the matrix is cited as [BT] in the deep reference. TRIZ is attributed to Altshuller and his Soviet-era inventive-principles research; the underlying Altshuller corpus is not held. The 15% Solutions frame cites Gareth Morgan's organisational-imagery work; Morgan's text is not held. Discovery & Action Dialogues cite the Plexus Institute and the positive-deviance tradition (Sternin); neither is held independently. The After-Action Debrief cites the Center for Creative Leadership's What/So What/Now What sequence; the CLL publication is not held. Peter Block's knee-to-knee conversation design is cited as [BT]. The "A Pattern Language for Engagement" essay cites Christopher Alexander (1977) and Johnson-Lenz; neither is held. (Source: Heft & Pattillo, *Engaging Everyone with LS Handbook*, deep reference, Parts I–II and "Origins")

**Named limits of the source.** The handbook is explicitly "a helpful reminder, not complete instructions" for people who have attended an LS-based event — it is a participant reference, not a facilitator training manual. It does not carry the depth of facilitation guidance available in the Lipmanowicz and McCandless full *Liberating Structures* volume (2014), which is not in this corpus. The handbook does not name or specify roles for participants beyond "client" and "consulting team" in Wise Crowds and Troika; it does not address how to recover a session that has gone wrong. The complexity-classification tool (Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix) is presented descriptively; the full Stacey Matrix literature — including the Cynefin framework, which overlaps — is not represented. The handbook predates generative-AI facilitation tools and does not address hybrid (in-person / remote) format adaptations. (Source: deep reference front matter and structure note)

**Evidence-marker continuity.** All normative claims in this distillation — that buy-in produces "lukewarm, pallid implementation," that serial presentations "drain and deaden energy," that Wise Crowds structurally separates option-generating from option-selecting — are [V] in the deep reference, drawn directly from the handbook's text. They are presented here as the authors' stated positions, not as independently evaluated empirical findings. The quantitative claims in the worked example (12-15 minutes of air time per person in Impromptu Speed Networking; "more than six is too big to give everyone a chance to talk" in 1-2-4-Whole Group) are [V] in the deep ref and cited accordingly. The Stacey-Zimmerman complexity-classification mechanism is [BT] in the deep ref; it is presented here as the handbook's borrowed diagnostic, not as the handbook's own primary claim.
