# OpenStax Principles of Accounting Vol 1 (Financial Accounting), Software-Business Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2019). *Principles of Accounting, Volume 1: Financial Accounting*. Rice University. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/principles-financial-accounting. Senior contributing authors Mitchell Franklin (lead), Patty Graybeal, Dixon Cooper; 28 contributing authors.

## Software-Business Relevance

OpenStax *Principles of Accounting Vol 1* covers the financial-accounting half of the discipline — what publicly reported numbers a software business produces, how those numbers are governed (GAAP, FASB, SEC, PCAOB, IFRS), and how trust between management and external stakeholders is operationalised through internal controls, auditor independence, and Sarbanes-Oxley attestation. For a founder, CTO, engineering manager, or technical product leader, the volume is load-bearing in four specific places where the *software* part of a software-business question turns on a financial-reporting consideration that general business framing cannot resolve.

First and most consequentially, *FASB ASU 2014-09 (Topic 606)*, the five-step revenue-recognition model (Source: OpenStax, *Accounting Vol 1*, Ch 9.1), is the canonical SaaS revenue-recognition framework. The five steps — identify the contract, identify separate performance obligations, determine the transaction price, allocate the price to the performance obligations, recognise revenue when each obligation is satisfied — replace the prior industry-specific revenue-recognition practices with a principle-based approach. For a software business pricing a multi-element contract (subscription + implementation + professional services + support), choosing between term and perpetual licences, structuring rebates or service-level credits, or negotiating multi-year prepayment terms, Topic 606 determines what revenue can be recognised when, which determines the income statement, the balance sheet (deferred revenue), and the cash-flow framing. Phase 1 software-business decisions cannot be made cleanly without it.

Second, *SOX Sections 302 and 404, plus the COSO Internal Control-Integrated Framework's five components* (control environment, risk assessment, control activities, information and communication, monitoring) (Source: Ch 8.2, Ch 8.5), frame software-business compliance investment as a financial-reporting question rather than as an engineering virtue. Once a software business is publicly traded — or contemplating becoming so — SOX 302 (CEO/CFO personal certification of internal control representations) and SOX 404 (annual internal-control audit and attestation) make security, reliability, and change-management investment a personally-certified line. Investment in audit-trail integrity, segregation of duties, and access controls is no longer a discretionary engineering preference; it is a financial-statement-attestation requirement. Modern SOX compliance has expanded into cybersecurity disclosure (Source: Ch 8.7), so a software business's data-breach posture is now a SOX-attested concern.

Third, the *FASB contingent-liability matrix* (Source: Ch 12.3) — combining likelihood of occurrence (probable, reasonably possible, remote) with measurability (estimable or inestimable) to determine journalise / footnote-disclose / omit — is the framework for accruing software-specific risk events: security incidents in progress, defect-driven litigation, regulator inquiries, breach notification obligations, indemnification triggers under customer contracts. The US GAAP probable threshold (~80%) versus IFRS "more likely than not" (~50%) creates a routine decision point for software businesses reporting under both standards. The recording rule (US GAAP records the low end of a range; IFRS records the discounted midpoint) materially affects reported earnings during an active risk event.

Fourth, the *auditor-independence and capitalisation-vs-expensing patterns* anchored in the Arthur Andersen / Enron case (Source: Ch 8.2) and the WorldCom capitalisation fraud (Source: Ch 11.2) frame two decisions specific to software businesses: choosing and managing auditors (with SOX-era restrictions on non-audit services from the same firm) as a going-concern integrity question, and deciding which software-development costs are capitalised long-term assets versus which are operating expenses. WorldCom's fraud was substantially the latter pattern — capitalising costs that should have been expensed — and the same line lives in every software business that builds proprietary software with engineering payroll as a candidate for internal-use-software capitalisation.

This distillation consolidates these four threads into a working pattern for software-business decisions and software-business deliverables.

## Key Concepts for Software-Business

1.  **Topic 606 / FASB ASU 2014-09: the five-step revenue-recognition model.** Identify the contract with the customer; identify the separate performance obligations; determine the transaction price; allocate the transaction price to the performance obligations; recognise revenue when each performance obligation is satisfied. Replaces industry-specific revenue recognition with a principle-based approach. Foundational for SaaS pricing, multi-element contracts, professional-services bundling, and multi-year prepayment structures. (Source: OpenStax, *Accounting Vol 1*, Ch 9.1)

2.  **Accrual basis as the operating premise.** Accrual accounting records transactions when they occur regardless of cash timing; cash basis records when cash exchanges. The text takes the position that accrual is "theoretically preferable" because it separates the timing of activity from the timing of cash. For software businesses with subscription revenue collected up front but earned over time, the distinction is the entire deferred-revenue conversation. (Source: Ch 2.1, Ch 4.2)

3.  **The eight GAAP principles, assumptions, and concepts.** Revenue recognition, expense recognition (matching), cost, full disclosure, separate entity, conservatism, monetary measurement, going concern, time period. The conservatism principle in particular biases software-business decisions involving estimation under uncertainty: estimated losses and uncertain expenses are recognised; estimated gains and uncertain revenues are not. (Source: Ch 3.1)

4.  **Capitalisation versus expensing for software development.** To capitalise is to record a long-term asset on the balance sheet and allocate cost over its economic life through depreciation or amortisation; to expense is to record the cost in the period incurred. The capitalised cost includes all costs necessary to place the asset into service. Routine maintenance is expensed; improvements extending useful life or capacity are capitalised. For software businesses, internal-use-software capitalisation (engineering payroll as a long-term asset rather than operating expense) is a routine accounting choice with material income-statement consequences. The WorldCom fraud was substantially this line crossed in the wrong direction. (Source: Ch 11.2, Ch 11.3)

5.  **Three depreciation methods.** Straight-line (cost minus salvage value divided by useful life); units-of-production (tied to usage); double-declining-balance (accelerated). The text takes a sharp position that management teams "typically fail to invest either time or attention into making or periodically revisiting and revising reasonably supportable estimates of asset lives or salvage values." For software businesses with hardware, data-centre infrastructure, or capitalised internal-use software, the estimate is consequential and revisable. Waste Management's $1.7B fraud was depreciation-estimate manipulation. (Source: Ch 11.3)

6.  **The contingent-liability four-treatment matrix.** Combine likelihood (probable, reasonably possible, remote) with measurement (estimable, inestimable) to decide journalise plus disclose, disclose only, or omit. US GAAP probable threshold is around 80%; IFRS uses "more likely than not" (~50%). For range estimates, US GAAP records the low end; IFRS records the discounted midpoint. Frames software-business security-incident accrual, defect-litigation accrual, regulator-inquiry accrual, breach-notification accrual, contractual-indemnification accrual. (Source: Ch 12.3)

7.  **COSO Internal Control-Integrated Framework's five components.** Control environment, risk assessment, control activities, information and communication, monitoring. The structural framework underlying all SOX-era internal-control work, and the lens for software-business compliance investment. Six elements of a strong internal control system: clear responsibilities, proper documentation with off-site backups, adequate insurance, separation of assets from custody, separation of duties, use of technology. (Source: Ch 8.2, Ch 8.3)

8.  **Sarbanes-Oxley Sections 302 and 404.** Section 302: CEO and CFO personally certify they have reviewed the internal control report and that financial information fairly states financial conditions. Section 404: management performs an annual audit of internal-control effectiveness, with selected executives attesting. The PCAOB oversees auditors of public companies. The penalty structure (up to $5M fines, 20-25 years prison) and the record-retention requirement (at least five years for business records, electronic records, electronic messages) make software-business compliance a personally-attested line, not a department's discretion. (Source: Ch 8.2, Ch 8.5, Ch 8.7)

9.  **The fraud triangle: opportunity, rationalisation, incentive (or pressure).** Cressey's framework. Internal controls bite on opportunity because that is where the discipline can act; rationalisation and incentive sit outside control design. For software-business compliance investment, the opportunity axis is the design target; the other two axes diagnose where culture and compensation may invite future failure. (Source: Ch 8.1)

10.  **Cybersecurity as a SOX-era control.** Modern SOX compliance has expanded beyond financial reporting to information-technology activities, with significant growth in cybersecurity disclosures. Equifax's 2017 breach affecting over 143 million people anchors the scope: data breaches affect stakeholder economic interests directly and now sit inside SOX-attested control coverage. (Source: Ch 8.7)

11.  **Burn rate framing of current liabilities.** Investors and creditors look to current liabilities to calculate annual burn rate — the monthly and annual cash needs of a company, used to determine how long the company can maintain operations before becoming insolvent. For early-stage software businesses pre-positive-cash-flow, burn rate is a stakeholder-facing artefact constructed from current-liability classification choices. (Source: Ch 12.1)

12.  **Unearned (deferred) revenue as a current liability.** Customer's advance payment for product or service not yet provided is a liability, not revenue. For SaaS businesses billing annually in advance, deferred revenue is typically the largest current liability on the balance sheet. Amazon Q4 2016 unearned revenue recognised: $4.8B (Source: Ch 12.1) — the scale of deferred-revenue accounting in subscription businesses. The mechanics shape what cash-versus-earnings looks like to investors. (Source: Ch 12.1)

13.  **Auditor independence and the audit committee.** External auditors must be independent of the company they audit; the audit committee, not management, oversees the external auditor's work; internal auditors are not independent. The Enron-Arthur Andersen case (Andersen providing both audit and consulting, $40B stockholder loss when Enron failed) is the canonical anti-pattern. SOX limits non-audit services from the same firm and mandates audit-partner rotation every seven years with a two-year break. For a software business choosing and managing an auditor, this is the integrity architecture. (Source: Ch 3.1, Ch 8.2)

14.  **Earnings management versus earnings manipulation.** Earnings management works within GAAP constraints to improve stakeholder views of financial position; earnings manipulation ignores GAAP rules to alter earnings significantly and shades into fraud. The boundary is not always sharp: changing bad-debt percentages, aging-category boundaries, depreciation estimates, or revenue-recognition timing are all candidate moves. The text's position is that auditors are the discipline; the front-page test is the practitioner's. (Source: Ch 9.4)

## Questions to Ask During Software-Business Work

### Phase 1 — Strategic positioning (pricing, market entry, build-vs-buy, AI integration choices)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify the Topic 606 performance obligations | What are the separate performance obligations in this contract — subscription access, implementation, professional services, support, custom development, training? Each may be a distinct obligation triggering its own revenue-recognition pattern. |
| Allocate the transaction price | If multiple performance obligations are bundled, how is the transaction price allocated? Standalone selling prices, residual method, expected value — each allocation choice affects the income statement. |
| Recognise revenue against obligation satisfaction | When does each performance obligation count as satisfied? Subscription access is typically recognised over the service period; one-time implementation is recognised at completion (or over time if the criteria are met); professional services depend on the engagement structure. |
| Surface deferred-revenue mechanics | What share of customer cash collected is unearned revenue (a liability) versus recognised revenue? For a SaaS business billing annually in advance, the answer shapes the entire cash-versus-earnings story to investors. |
| Apply the matching principle to feature build | Where does engineering effort to deliver a feature meet revenue recognition? Engineering payroll spent in advance of contracted delivery sits in either capitalised internal-use software or current expense; the choice affects how the period's income matches the period's commercial activity. |
| Apply the build-vs-buy distinction to capitalisation | If buying, the cost is typically capitalised as an intangible asset (software licence) or operating expense depending on the structure. If building, internal-use-software capitalisation criteria apply. The financial-statement effect of the choice is itself part of the decision. |

### Phase 2 — Product and engineering economics (technical-debt servicing, capacity planning, ROI, reliability as financial decision)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Decide capitalise versus expense for engineering work | Does this engineering effort meet the capitalisation criteria (extending useful life of an existing asset, building new internal-use software past the preliminary project stage)? If the answer is "no" or "ambiguous," the cost belongs in operating expense. WorldCom's $11B fraud was substantially this line crossed. |
| Estimate useful life for capitalised software | What is the realistic useful life of the capitalised asset, given software industry obsolescence patterns? An optimistic useful-life estimate flatters near-term earnings; a realistic one acknowledges the volatility of software's useful life. |
| Choose a depreciation method | Straight-line is the default; units-of-production may apply if usage is measurable; double-declining-balance accelerates expense recognition. Which best matches the asset's actual economic consumption pattern? |
| Apply conservatism to reliability investment | If the cost-benefit case for reliability investment is uncertain, does the analysis lean toward recognising potential losses (the conservatism principle) rather than uncertain gains? Reliability spend prevents costs that would otherwise materialise; the matching principle and conservatism together support the investment. |
| Decide whether to accrue a contingent liability for known defect risk | If a known defect or reliability issue is probable to require remediation expenditure, and the cost is estimable, the FASB matrix requires accrual *and* disclosure, not disclosure alone. If probable but inestimable, disclosure only. If reasonably possible, disclosure only. If remote, no action. |
| Surface the "cash is king" framing for the cash-flow story | Negative operating cash flow with positive net income is a signal worth attending to in software businesses; positive operating cash flow with negative net income is the inverse signal (often a deferred-revenue effect). What does each section of the statement of cash flows tell stakeholders about the business's actual cash mechanics? |

### Phase 3 — Team and capability building (hiring, structure, levelling, culture, succession)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Frame engineering payroll's accounting treatment | How much of engineering payroll is operating expense (R&D, maintenance, customer-support engineering) versus capitalised (internal-use software past the preliminary project stage)? The split affects reported income and is a discretionary boundary worth surfacing. |
| Apply segregation of duties to engineering authorisation | Where does the COSO control activity for segregation of duties apply to engineering work — deploy access, production-data access, code-review authority, payment-processing access? Segregation is not bureaucracy; it is opportunity-axis control of the fraud triangle. |
| Surface the tone-at-the-top question for engineering | Does engineering leadership model the integrity the SOX 302 attestation requires? Tone at the top is a leading indicator that flows from leadership behaviour to control effectiveness. |
| Test contractor and vendor accounting | If hiring contractors or vendors for software work, what is the accounting treatment (operating expense, capitalised cost, prepaid expense) and what audit-trail evidence does the engagement leave? Vendor work without a clean audit trail is a control weakness. |
| Frame succession risk in financial-reporting terms | If a critical engineer departs and an associated control or capability lapses, what is the contingent-liability exposure (regulatory, contractual, customer-trust)? Succession risk has a financial-reporting tail. |

### Phase 4 — Operations and process (delivery cadence, governance, decision rights, change management)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply the audit-trail discipline to delivery work | Does the delivery process leave a complete audit trail from request through deployment through outcome? The audit trail is what allows internal investigation, vendor disputes, customer complaints, and external-auditor testing. |
| Surface internal controls over change management | What controls govern who can deploy, who can change configuration, who can access production data? Each is an opportunity-axis defence inside the COSO control-activities component. |
| Test the documentation requirement | Is the documentation sufficient to support SOX 302 certification (the CEO/CFO attestation that the internal control report is accurate)? Insufficient documentation transfers personal liability to executives. |
| Apply consistency to method choices | If accounting methods affect operations (revenue recognition timing for a delivery milestone, capitalisation policy for engineering work), have those methods been applied consistently across periods? Inconsistent application requires disclosure under full-disclosure. |

### Phase 5 — Risk, reliability, compliance (incidents, security, legal, regulatory, ethics)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply the FASB contingent-liability matrix | For a security incident, defect-driven litigation, regulator inquiry, breach-notification obligation, or indemnification trigger, what is the likelihood (probable, reasonably possible, remote) and what is the measurement (estimable, inestimable)? The four-treatment matrix decides journalise plus disclose, disclose only, or omit. |
| Frame compliance investment as financial-decision cost | What is the cost of the compliance investment, and what is the contingent liability it reduces? SOX/COSO compliance, security investment, cyber-insurance, certification work — each has a cost and an avoided-cost that can be modelled. |
| Test the auditor's likely view on a judgement call | What would the external auditor say if asked to defend this judgement in an SOX 404 attestation? If the auditor would push back, that is signal worth attending to before the choice is made. |
| Apply the front-page test | If this decision were on the front page of the *Wall Street Journal* tomorrow, would I be defending it or apologising? Bristol-Myers Squibb, Enron, WorldCom, HealthSouth, Waste Management all failed this test in advance and reaped the consequences. The same test applies to software-business compliance choices. |
| Engage the audit committee early | Has the audit committee been informed of significant judgements (impairment, capitalisation policy change, contingent-liability accrual, restatement) before the close, or only after? The audit committee's structural role is oversight, not ratification. |
| Distinguish error from fraud in incident response | Is this an honest mistake (correctable through restatement and disclosure) or an intentional misrepresentation (fraud-triangle case requiring escalation)? The classification affects who runs the response. |
| Frame cybersecurity disclosure as SOX-era reporting | Has the security incident been classified for SOX-disclosure adequacy? Modern SOX compliance covers cybersecurity disclosures; suppressed or incomplete disclosure compounds rather than reduces exposure. |
| Test the auditor-independence position | Has the external auditor provided non-audit services that compromise independence? Audit-partner rotation honoured? The Arthur Andersen / Enron pattern is the going-concern case. |

### Phase 6 — Stakeholder communication (board reporting, exec alignment, investor relations, customer messaging)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Map the six stakeholder categories | Who relies on this report or this disclosure — stockholders, creditors and lenders, regulators, customers, employees, broader community? Each reads the financial statements differently; tailor the framing. |
| Apply the full-disclosure principle | What information could a reasonable stakeholder need to evaluate the financial statements that is not on the face of the statements? Note disclosure exists for this; method changes, contingent liabilities, related-party arrangements, accounting estimates all qualify. |
| Frame revenue-recognition judgement for the board | For a board reading the income statement of a software business, what is the deferred-revenue picture and what are the Topic 606 judgements driving recognition timing? Surface the assumptions, not only the numbers. |
| Test the "cash is king" framing for investor messaging | Is the cash-flow story coherent with the income-statement story? Negative cash flow with growth-stage investing inflows is one shape; positive cash flow with negative earnings due to deferred-revenue mechanics is another. The interpretation requires looking at all three sections of the statement of cash flows together. |
| Compose disclosure that survives the front-page test | Are management's intent and the underlying economics both clear, or could the disclosure be read as obscuring something? If a journalist read this, what would they conclude? |
| Engage the lender on covenant exposure | Are debt covenants being monitored and reported transparently? Forward visibility into potential covenant breaches builds long-term lender trust; surprise breaches damage it. |
| Address material uncertainties | Are going-concern issues, material contingencies, and significant subsequent events disclosed in management discussion or in footnotes? The full-disclosure principle's operational mechanism is the management-discussion-and-analysis section. |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: Engineering payroll capitalised aggressively just before a reporting deadline.** Signal: a sudden increase in the share of engineering payroll classified as capitalised internal-use software, coinciding with quarter-end or year-end. Diagnosis (in source vocabulary): possible improper capitalisation — the WorldCom pattern (Source: Ch 11.2). Follow-up: test whether the capitalised costs meet the criteria of "necessary to place the asset into service" past the preliminary project stage, not merely "engineering effort during the period." Document the support; engage the external auditor.

- **Pattern: Multi-element SaaS contract with revenue recognised entirely at signing.** Signal: a multi-year subscription, implementation, professional-services bundle recognised as revenue on contract close rather than allocated across performance obligations and recognised against obligation satisfaction. Diagnosis: Topic 606 (Source: Ch 9.1) misapplication — possible cookie-jar/bill-and-hold pattern (Source: Ch 4.1, Ch 8.7). Follow-up: re-run the five steps, identify the separate performance obligations, allocate the transaction price, build the recognition schedule against obligation satisfaction.

- **Pattern: Security incident in active mitigation, no contingent-liability accrual on the books.** Signal: a known active incident with probable financial consequence (regulatory fine, customer remediation, indemnification claim) and a footnote-only disclosure. Diagnosis: possible avoidance of journal entry to keep income statement clean — the FASB matrix's accrual requirement (Source: Ch 12.3) not honoured. Follow-up: re-test probability and estimability; if probable and estimable, accrue *and* disclose; record the low end of the range under US GAAP, the discounted midpoint under IFRS.

- **Pattern: Auditor's consulting revenue from the engineering organisation exceeds audit revenue.** Signal: the same firm doing the audit is also doing significant consulting (cloud migration advisory, SOX-readiness consulting, SaaS-implementation consulting) for the same client. Diagnosis: SOX-era independence concern — the Arthur Andersen / Enron pattern in software-business form (Source: Ch 8.2). Follow-up: under SOX, certain non-audit services are prohibited; audit-committee oversight applies; consider engaging a separate firm for the consulting work.

- **Pattern: Useful-life estimate for capitalised software extended at year-end without disclosure.** Signal: depreciation expense drops materially because internal-use software's assumed useful life has been revised upward (5 years to 7, 7 to 10). Diagnosis: possible estimate manipulation — the Waste Management pattern (Source: Ch 11.3). Follow-up: require explicit support for the change in estimate; force disclosure under full-disclosure principle; engage the audit committee on the rationale.

- **Pattern: Deferred-revenue balance growing materially while bookings-to-billings ratio shrinks.** Signal: deferred revenue accumulating because annual billing in advance is rising, but new bookings are slowing relative to billings. Diagnosis: stakeholder-trust risk — the deferred-revenue balance flatters the balance sheet but the income statement will catch up as the bookings-to-billings ratio is the leading indicator. Follow-up: surface the dynamic in management discussion; explain the bookings-vs-billings story to analysts and the board.

- **Pattern: Software-business "we'll restate later" position on a current-quarter judgement.** Signal: a contested accounting judgement (Topic 606 allocation, capitalisation eligibility, contingent-liability accrual) is being deferred to a future period in the hope it goes away. Diagnosis: stakeholder-trust risk — self-disclosed restatement preserves long-term trust; forced restatement compounds the trust loss. Follow-up: engage the audit committee and external auditor on the judgement now; restate now if material.

- **Pattern: Internal-control documentation fragmented across engineering tools, no single source-of-truth audit trail.** Signal: deploys logged in one system, change tickets in another, access changes in a third, code reviews in a fourth — none integrated. Diagnosis: COSO information-and-communication weakness with SOX 404 attestation risk (Source: Ch 8.2). Follow-up: build a unified audit trail; the documentation must be sufficient to support SOX 302 certification, which means the CEO/CFO must be able to attest accurately to the internal control report.

- **Pattern: Engineering judgement concentrated in one engineer without segregation of duties.** Signal: same engineer can deploy, change configuration, access production data, and modify access controls. Diagnosis: COSO control-activities weakness and fraud-triangle opportunity-axis risk (Source: Ch 8.1, Ch 8.3). Follow-up: rebuild segregation of duties (separate deploy from access-grant; separate production-data access from code-change authority); document the boundary; introduce surprise audit procedures.

## When to Use This Reference

- A SaaS pricing or contract decision is in front of you with multi-element revenue recognition under Topic 606 (subscription, implementation, professional services, support, custom development).
- A capitalise-versus-expense judgement on engineering work is being made (internal-use software past the preliminary project stage, infrastructure upgrades, capitalised configuration of acquired software).
- A depreciation-method choice or useful-life estimate is being made or revised for hardware, data-centre assets, or capitalised internal-use software.
- A security incident, defect-driven litigation, regulator inquiry, breach-notification obligation, or contractual-indemnification trigger requires a contingent-liability decision under the FASB matrix.
- A SOX 302 or 404 attestation is being prepared and the accuracy of the certification is in question.
- A COSO internal-control design choice (separation of duties, audit trail, documentation, monitoring) is being weighed against engineering velocity.
- An auditor selection, audit-partner rotation, or non-audit-services question is being decided (the Arthur Andersen / Enron pattern is the going-concern case).
- A board paper or 10-Q is being composed with deferred revenue, capitalised software, contingent liabilities, or cybersecurity disclosure as material items.
- A method change (revenue recognition timing, capitalisation policy, depreciation method, contingent-liability threshold) is being weighed against the cost of disclosure and stakeholder reaction.
- A restatement question is being weighed against the cost of admitting a prior misstatement.

## Worked Example

A series-B SaaS company is preparing for its Q3 board review. The CFO and CTO have three software-business-specific accounting questions in front of them.

**Question 1: Topic 606 allocation for a new enterprise contract.** A two-year, $1.2M contract has been signed: $800K subscription, $200K implementation, $200K professional services. The implementation is bundled "no charge" in the contract language; the professional services have a separately-scoped deliverable. The CFO's first instinct is to recognise $400K at signing and the $800K subscription over 24 months. The source's five-step revenue-recognition model disciplines the analysis: identify the contract; identify the separate performance obligations; determine the transaction price; allocate the transaction price to the performance obligations; recognise revenue when each obligation is satisfied. Working through these five steps, the CFO and the controller identify three performance obligations: subscription access (a stand-ready obligation over 24 months), implementation (a one-time deliverable at completion, even though it is bundled-no-charge), and professional services (a distinct deliverable with its own scope). They reallocate the transaction price using standalone selling prices, which assigns roughly $80K to implementation as its allocation share even though the customer paid no incremental amount for it. The recognition pattern is now: $80K at implementation completion (Q4), $200K at professional-services completion (forecast Q2 next year), and $920K rateably across 24 months. The Q3 board paper reflects the corrected allocation; the controller documents the standalone-selling-price methodology for the audit file. (Source: OpenStax, *Accounting Vol 1*, Ch 9.1)

**Question 2: Capitalisation policy for the new platform rebuild.** The engineering team has been rebuilding the core platform for nine months; engineering payroll for the rebuild has been recorded as operating R&D expense throughout. The CFO asks whether costs should be capitalised as internal-use software. The source draws the relevant line: capitalised costs include "all costs necessary to place the asset into service" past the preliminary project stage; routine maintenance is expensed; improvements extending useful life or capacity are capitalised. The WorldCom fraud was substantially this line crossed in the wrong direction — capitalising costs that should have been expensed. The CTO and controller agree that the project crossed into the application development stage in month 4. The team has spent $1.8M in engineering payroll across months 4-9 that meets the capitalisation criteria. The CFO consults the external auditor early, who supports capitalising prospectively with full-disclosure of the policy decision in the Q3 footnotes. The CFO declines retroactive capitalisation because the documentation supporting the month-4 boundary is thin. The decision is documented; the audit committee is briefed before the board meeting. (Source: Ch 11.2, Ch 11.3)

**Question 3: Contingent liability for an active security incident.** Two weeks ago, the company discovered a vulnerability that may have exposed customer data; the incident is in active mitigation, customer notification has begun, and a regulator has opened a preliminary inquiry. The CFO must decide what the Q3 financial statements say. The source's four-treatment matrix — combining likelihood (probable, reasonably possible, remote) with measurability (estimable, inestimable) — is the decision frame. Likelihood of regulatory fine: probable (the regulator has opened an inquiry; the company has notification obligations). Measurement: a range is estimable ($500K to $3M based on comparable enforcement cases), but the precise amount depends on the regulator's determination of scope. The source's rule: "Under US GAAP, the company must journalise the low end of the estimable range *and* disclose the range in a footnote." The CFO accrues $500K, discloses the range, the methodology, and the ongoing mitigation in the footnote, and adds discussion of the incident, the remediation plan, and the cybersecurity-control rebuild in management discussion and analysis. The audit committee is briefed before the Q3 close. The CFO's logic is the front-page test the source names: a $500K accrual with full disclosure looks like a credible response; no accrual on a probable-and-estimable contingent liability would not survive the front page. (Source: Ch 12.3)

The board paper now presents a cleaner story: Topic 606 revenue allocation corrected and transparent; capitalisation policy explicitly chosen and disclosed; contingent liability accrued and disclosed in the FASB-compliant shape. The SOX 302 attestation will be defensible because each judgement has been engaged with the auditor, documented, and surfaced to the audit committee — consistent with the source's framing of Section 302 as personal certification by the CEO and CFO. (Source: Ch 8.5)

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Recognising multi-element SaaS contract revenue at signing without Topic 606 allocation.** The five-step model is unambiguous on separate performance obligations and recognition against obligation satisfaction (Source: Ch 9.1). Recognising the entire transaction price at contract close, or treating subscription and services revenue as if they were a single bundle, fails Topic 606 and shades into the bill-and-hold and improper-cutoff fraud patterns (Source: Ch 8.7).

- **Capitalising engineering payroll that should be expensed.** The WorldCom case (Source: Ch 11.2) is the canonical version. The capitalisation criterion is narrow (internal-use software past the preliminary project stage, with the cost necessary to place the asset into service); "engineering effort during the period" is broad and not the criterion. Aggressive capitalisation flatters near-term earnings and creates a long write-down liability when the assumption breaks.

- **Footnoting probable-and-estimable contingent liabilities instead of accruing them.** The FASB matrix (Source: Ch 12.3) is unambiguous: probable *and* estimable warrants accrual *and* disclosure, not disclosure alone. Footnote-only treatment to keep the income statement clean fails the matrix and reads as earnings management approaching manipulation (Source: Ch 9.4).

- **Treating SOX 302/404 attestation as a control-team task.** Section 302 makes the CEO and CFO personally responsible for the truthfulness of internal-control representations (Source: Ch 8.5). Concealing or downplaying weakness compounds penalty rather than mitigating it. Tone at the top is structural, not rhetorical.

- **Selecting the auditor's affiliated consulting firm for major non-audit engineering work.** The Arthur Andersen / Enron pattern (Source: Ch 8.2) is the going-concern case. SOX limits prohibited non-audit services and mandates audit-partner rotation; the structural defence against the dependence pattern is honest about why it exists.

- **Extending useful-life estimates for capitalised software at year-end without disclosure.** The Waste Management pattern (Source: Ch 11.3). Useful-life estimates are revisable, but every revision requires explicit support and disclosure under full-disclosure. Quietly extending lives to flatter depreciation expense fails the front-page test.

- **Treating cybersecurity disclosure as a security-team responsibility separate from financial reporting.** Modern SOX has expanded into cybersecurity disclosures (Source: Ch 8.7); the Equifax 2017 breach (143M+ people) anchors the scale. Data-breach disclosure that lags behind the regulatory or stakeholder expectation compounds the original incident.

- **Concentrating engineering judgement and access in one engineer without segregation of duties.** The COSO control-activities component and the fraud-triangle opportunity axis are the structural defence (Source: Ch 8.1, Ch 8.3). Velocity arguments against segregation of duties are also arguments for opportunity-axis fraud risk.

- **Treating deferred-revenue balance growth as growth.** Deferred revenue is a current liability, not revenue (Source: Ch 12.1). Balance-sheet flattery from annual-prepay subscription billing is not the same as income-statement performance; conflating the two misreads the business to stakeholders.

- **Defending GAAP-technical compliance against the front-page test.** "It's allowed" is not the same as "it represents the underlying economics" (Source: Ch 3.1 full-disclosure principle). The front-page test catches what technical compliance permits but stakeholders find indefensible.
