# OpenStax Principles of Accounting Volume 2, Software-Business Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2019). *Principles of Accounting, Volume 2: Managerial Accounting*. Rice University. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/principles-managerial-accounting. Senior contributing authors Mitchell Franklin, Patty Graybeal, Dixon Cooper; 28 contributing authors.

## Software-Business Relevance

Volume 2 is the load-bearing managerial-accounting source for the software-business intersection. Where a generic finance text gives the practitioner ratios and statements, this volume gives the practitioner *decision-relative cost classification* — the move that lets a CTO or founder treat the same line of code as a variable cost in one decision, a sunk cost in another, and an opportunity cost in a third (Source: Ch 2.2, "Identify and Apply Basic Cost Behavior Patterns"). That move is what makes managerial accounting useful for software work, where the financial-statement view of an engineering team systematically misclassifies the costs that actually drive the decisions.

Five chapters carry most of the software-business weight. Ch 10 ("Short-Term Decision Making") catalogues the five canonical short-term decision frames — special order, make-or-buy, keep-or-discontinue, sell-or-process-further, constrained-resource allocation — each with the relevant-cost discipline that distinguishes avoidable from sunk and allocated costs. *Make-or-buy* is the analytical bone of build-vs-buy. *Constrained-resource allocation* maps onto Goldratt-style throughput thinking applied to engineering bottlenecks. *Keep-or-discontinue* applies to deprecation and technical-debt remediation decisions where allocated platform costs distort the segment-economic picture. Ch 11 ("Capital Budgeting Decisions") supplies NPV, IRR, payback, and ARR — the toolkit for evaluating multi-year engineering investments, platform rebuilds, and acquisitions. Ch 3 ("Cost-Volume-Profit Analysis") supplies contribution margin, break-even analysis, sensitivity analysis, and target pricing — the toolkit for pricing features and products where the underlying engineering cost has a fixed-vs-variable structure that conventional cost-plus pricing misses. Ch 12 ("Balanced Scorecard and Other Performance Measures") supplies the four-perspective scorecard, ROI / RI / EVA, and the WACC-based EVA calculation that lets a CTO frame engineering investments in the board's financial language. Ch 13 ("Sustainability Reporting") supplies GRI, SASB, and Integrated Reporting — the materiality frameworks for software-business questions that increasingly turn on ESG, sustainability, and stakeholder-broader reporting.

For software-business work, the volume's contrarian moves are load-bearing: cost classification is decision-relative not intrinsic (Source: Ch 2.2); short-term and long-term decisions warrant *different* analytical frames (Source: Ch 10.1; Ch 11); financial measures alone are insufficient (Source: Ch 12.1); ROI alone creates goal-incongruence that drives high-ROI segments to reject value-creating projects (Source: Ch 9.3; Ch 12.3); absorption costing's GAAP-compliance does not make it the right tool for internal decisions (Source: Ch 6.5). Each move pushes back on a default the software-business practitioner often inherits from generic-finance training.

## Key Concepts for Software-Business

1.  **Decision-relative cost classification.** Costs are not intrinsically fixed, variable, direct, indirect, product, period, relevant, or irrelevant — they earn those labels relative to the decision under consideration (Source: OpenStax, *Principles of Accounting Vol 2*, Ch 2.2). For software work this means that an engineer's salary is a fixed cost in a next-month capacity decision, a variable cost in a multi-year platform-rebuild decision, an opportunity cost in a feature-allocation decision, and a sunk cost in a post-launch retrospective. Asking "fixed for what decision?" rather than "is this fixed?" is the load-bearing software-business move.

2.  **Relevant-cost analysis as the build-vs-buy frame.** A cost or revenue is relevant if it differs between alternatives. Sunk costs, unavoidable costs, and future costs that do not differ are irrelevant. Avoidable costs and opportunity costs are typically relevant (Source: Ch 10.1, "Identify Relevant Information for Decision-Making"). For build-vs-buy, the prior engineering investment in an existing in-house system is sunk and irrelevant; only the *avoidable* in-house cost going forward (engineering hours plus avoidable hosting plus avoidable on-call) compares to the vendor cost.

3.  **Make-or-buy with avoidable-fixed-cost discrimination.** Variable costs are typically avoidable; fixed costs may or may not be. The volume's central illustration (Thermal Mugs / Plato Plastics, Ch 10.3) shows that whether $0.12 of allocated fixed cost is avoidable swings the decision. For software, the equivalent is whether shutting down an in-house module releases engineering headcount, releases licence costs, or releases on-call rotation — or whether it merely reallocates those costs to other modules. The analysis discriminates avoidable from reallocated. (Source: Ch 10.3, "Evaluate and Determine Whether to Make or Buy a Component")

4.  **Theory of Constraints and the five focusing steps.** Goldratt's TOC frames continuous improvement as identifying the single binding constraint, exploiting it, subordinating everything else to it, elevating it, and repeating (Source: Ch 1.5, "Lean Practices"). For software, the binding constraint is rarely engineering capacity in aggregate; it is usually a single team, a single integration path, or a single approval gate. The discipline of identifying the *one* constraint and not improving non-constraints is what distinguishes constraint-thinking from generic productivity work.

5.  **Contribution-margin-per-unit-of-the-constraint ranking.** When a resource constrains production, rank products by contribution margin per unit of the *constraining* resource — not by unit contribution margin (Source: Ch 10.6, "Make Decisions Using Constrained Resources"). For software, this maps onto prioritising features by the ratio of revenue or strategic value to the engineering hours they consume on the bottlenecked team or system, not by absolute revenue.

6.  **Contribution margin and CVP for feature pricing.** Contribution margin is the amount by which a product's selling price exceeds its variable cost per unit (Source: Ch 3.1, "Explain Contribution Margin"). For software, contribution margin reframes pricing as a fixed-and-variable-recovery exercise: the first sale of a feature contributes nothing to fixed engineering cost; later sales accumulate contribution until break-even, after which each marginal sale flows to profit. Target pricing inverts the calculation: work backwards from what customers will pay through desired margin to required cost (Source: Ch 3.3, "Change in Sales Price"). For software where marginal cost is near-zero, the contribution-margin lens often justifies aggressive pricing experiments that cost-plus-style frameworks would reject.

7.  **Operating leverage and the engineering cost structure trade-off.** Higher fixed costs produce higher operating leverage: at higher volumes the payoff is greater, but the downside is also greater (Source: Ch 3.5, "Operating Leverage"). For software, the trade-off shows up as in-house engineering (high fixed cost; high operating leverage) versus contractors and consumption-priced services (lower fixed cost; lower leverage but also lower risk). The leverage decision is structural and is made years before the volumes that test it appear.

8.  **Capital budgeting for multi-year engineering investments.** Capital decisions involve large outlays, long horizons, and the time value of money; payback and ARR are screens; NPV and IRR are the substantive tools (Source: Ch 11.1; Ch 11.2; Ch 11.4). For software, capital-budgeting analysis applies to platform rebuilds, major migrations, ML-model training infrastructure, and acquisitions — anywhere the cash outflow precedes the benefit by more than a year. The volume's discipline of using both NPV and IRR (and, for mutually exclusive different-sized projects, the profitability index) prevents picking the wrong tool for the question.

9.  **Sunk-cost discipline.** Sunk costs "have no bearing on future events and are not relevant in decision-making" (Source: Ch 10.1, "Sunk Costs"). The volume warns that sunk costs are difficult to ignore "due to human nature." For software, the canonical sunk-cost trap is defending a legacy system because of prior engineering investment; the volume's discipline says the only relevant question is what each alternative costs from here forward.

10.  **Variable-versus-absorption costing for internal decisions.** Variable costing treats fixed manufacturing overhead as a period cost and is more useful for internal decision-making; absorption costing inventories fixed overhead and can incentivise income manipulation through overproduction (Source: Ch 6.5, "Compare and Contrast Variable and Absorption Costing"). For software, the Big-Three-autos pattern that the volume cites translates into the temptation to defend engineering headcount by allocating overhead to "the platform" or "the product" rather than naming the truly fixed engineering cost as a period cost.

11.  **ROI's goal-incongruence problem.** Under pure-ROI evaluation, a high-ROI segment manager will reject a project below segment ROI even when the project benefits the company; residual income (income minus invested capital times cost of capital) and EVA (after-tax income minus WACC times invested capital) accept any positive contribution above the firm-wide threshold (Source: Ch 9.3, "Investment Centers"; Ch 12.3). For software, this manifests as profitable engineering teams refusing investments that would reduce their headline productivity numbers but would generate company-wide value.

12.  **Balanced scorecard's four perspectives.** Financial, internal-process, customer, and learning-and-growth measures together produce more durable evaluation than financial measures alone (Source: Ch 12.4, "Describe the Balanced Scorecard and Explain How It Is Used"). For board reporting on engineering, the four perspectives map onto: revenue and unit economics (financial); cycle time, defect rate, deployment frequency (internal-process); customer NPS and feature-adoption (customer); engineering retention, training, and capability building (learning-and-growth). The BSC structure makes engineering reporting legible to a board that does not read code.

13.  **Standard costs and variance analysis as feedback.** Standard-cost variances (price/rate and quantity/time for materials, labour, overhead) provide signals about where actual operations deviate from expectation (Source: Ch 8.1; Ch 8.5). For software, the equivalent of standard-cost variance is the gap between estimated and actual story points, between planned and actual cycle time, between budgeted and actual operational cost — analysed not as performance failure but as a learning signal. The volume warns against "managing to the variance" (short-term decisions that meet the threshold at cost of long-term value) and against "budget slack" (inflated standards to make actuals look favourable).

14.  **Capacity-aware special-order analysis.** With excess capacity, fixed costs are typically irrelevant; any price above variable cost contributes. Without excess capacity, accepting the order may require new fixed costs that must be covered (Source: Ch 10.2, "Identify and Apply Basic Methods of Cost Accounting for Special Order Decisions"). For software, this maps onto custom-build engagements, integration projects, or feature requests outside the standard roadmap: when the engineering team has slack, take the engagement at any price above marginal cost; when it is at capacity, the order requires either new headcount or displaced existing work.

15.  **Sustainability reporting and stakeholder-broader board communication.** Three frameworks (GRI for stakeholder-broad, SASB for investor-focused, IIRC's Integrated Reporting for six-capitals integration) each define materiality differently (Source: Ch 13.3). For software companies, the materiality question increasingly turns on energy consumption (cloud compute, model training), data-handling practices, and the social-and-relationship capital that platform companies depend on. The volume's framework choice (GRI vs SASB vs IIRC) is a strategic decision for any software business with stakeholder-broader reporting demands.

16.  **Ethics framework and the eight-step process.** The IMA's four standards (competence, confidentiality, integrity, credibility) plus the eight-step ethics framework (recognise; establish facts; recognise competing values; determine alternatives; evaluate; recognise consequences; decide and act; evaluate the decision) anchor decisions where the technical and the ethical intersect (Source: Ch 1.4, "Business Ethics" and "Ethics Legislation"). For software, this lands on AI deployment, data-handling, dark patterns, and the Ford-Pinto-style trade-off (Source: Ch 10.6, "Ethical Considerations") between feature ship-speed and known-defect tolerance.

## Questions to Ask During Software-Business Work

### Phase 1: Strategic positioning (pricing, market entry, build-vs-buy, portfolio decisions, AI integration)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| For a pricing decision tied to engineering cost | What is the contribution margin per unit at current pricing? How much fixed engineering cost does each unit need to cover before break-even? Could a target-pricing analysis (start from customer willingness-to-pay, work back through margin to required cost) shift the product roadmap toward lower-cost variants? |
| For build-vs-buy | What in-house costs are *avoidable* if I buy externally — engineering headcount that can be released, on-call rotation that can be retired, licence cost that can be dropped? What in-house costs are unavoidable regardless? Use only avoidable internal cost in the comparison; ignore allocated platform overhead that would remain. |
| For a market-entry decision with engineering implications | What is the operating leverage of the target market — high fixed engineering cost with high upside, or low fixed cost with bounded upside? Is the entry being analysed with the right time-frame (capital-budgeting NPV/IRR for multi-year; relevant-cost for under-twelve-month)? |
| For an AI-integration decision | Is the AI investment being framed as a short-term operational expense (run rate, vendor contract) or a long-term capital investment (model training, platform rebuild)? The frame determines the analytical tool — relevant-cost for the former, NPV/IRR for the latter. |
| For a competitive-response decision | Where is the constraint that competitor pressure is exposing — is it engineering capacity, time-to-market, feature breadth, or unit economics? The TOC five-step (identify, exploit, subordinate, elevate, repeat) applies to the single binding constraint; addressing non-constraints first wastes effort. |

### Phase 2: Product and engineering economics (technical-debt servicing, capacity planning, ROI, reliability)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| For a technical-debt-vs-revenue decision | Frame the technical-debt remediation as a make-vs-buy or keep-or-discontinue: which engineering capacity is *avoidable* if the debt continues unaddressed? Which becomes avoidable after remediation? If the answer is "the debt forces ongoing avoidable cost," the remediation has a contribution margin and a payback period; compute both. |
| For a reliability-investment-vs-milestone decision | What is the variable cost per incident (response time, customer-credit cost, brand damage) versus the fixed cost of the reliability investment? At what incident rate does the investment break even? Apply CVP analysis as the volume develops it (Ch 3.2). |
| For a capacity-planning decision | Is the constraint engineering hours in aggregate, or specific to one team or system? Goldratt's TOC: identify the *single* constraint, exploit it, subordinate everything else, elevate it. Generic hiring across all teams is rarely the right answer; hiring at the constraint is. |
| For a roadmap-capacity mismatch | Rank features by contribution-margin-per-engineering-hour-on-the-bottleneck, not by absolute revenue or strategic-tier. The highest-revenue feature may not be the highest contribution-per-hour feature at the bottleneck (Ch 10.6 analogue). |
| For a "speed vs quality" tension | The volume's variance analysis warning applies: short-term decisions that meet the variance threshold at the cost of long-term goals erode capability. Standard-cost framing (Ch 8.5) makes the trade-off visible — are we managing to the speed variance at the cost of latent defects? |

### Phase 3: Team and capability building (hiring, structure, levelling, culture, succession)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| For a hiring or role-structure decision | Frame headcount as a *fixed cost* in the operating leverage sense (Ch 3.5): higher fixed cost yields higher leverage at scale but greater downside risk. Is the hire structural (long-term capability) or load-driven (current-quarter capacity)? The frame determines whether the analysis is capital-budgeting (NPV-shaped) or relevant-cost (next-twelve-months-shaped). |
| For an engineering-management culture decision | The volume's ethics framework (Ch 1.4) and the IMA's four standards (competence, confidentiality, integrity, credibility) anchor the cultural decisions a CTO makes about transparency, escalation, and information flow. The eight-step ethics framework is a usable checklist. |
| For a levelling, performance, or compensation decision | The variance-analysis warning applies (Ch 8.5): performance metrics can be gamed via budget slack (inflated standards) or short-term decisions that meet the threshold at long-term cost. The balanced-scorecard frame (Ch 12.4) suggests pairing engineer-level financial measures (efficiency, throughput) with internal-process (defect rate, cycle time), customer (satisfaction with the engineer's deliveries), and learning-and-growth (skill acquisition, mentoring contribution). |
| For a succession or knowledge-transfer concern | The volume's responsibility-accounting framework (Ch 9.3) names whose accountability needs to hold each function during the transition. Allocated common costs are not the right framing for "who is responsible for what knowledge"; controllable factors only (Ch 9.4). |
| For a contractor / vendor / hybrid-team decision | Make-or-buy analysis (Ch 10.3): what costs are avoidable if the work is outsourced? Variable engineering cost typically; fixed-overhead allocation typically not. The Thermal Mugs pattern (avoidable fixed cost of $0.12 is the swing variable) maps onto the question of whether contractor engagement displaces specific engineering capacity or just shifts headcount-allocation lines on the org chart. |

### Phase 4: Operations and process (delivery cadence, governance, decision rights, change management)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| For a delivery-cadence question | Standard costs and variances (Ch 8) frame velocity, cycle-time, and deployment-frequency targets as standards; deviations are variances; both favourable and unfavourable variances warrant explanation. The volume's prescription: do not "manage to the variance"; treat both kinds of variance as learning signals (Ch 8.5). |
| For a change-management situation | The four budgeting approaches (top-down, bottom-up, combination, zero-based, Ch 7.1) map onto engineering planning styles. Zero-based ("every line must be justified") is the discipline for technical-debt remediation; rolling budgets ("continuously updated") are the discipline for AI-platform-shaped roadmaps where the underlying capability changes mid-cycle. |
| For a governance or decision-rights ambiguity | The five responsibility-centre types (cost, discretionary cost, revenue, profit, investment — Ch 9.3) name the structural shape of the decision rights question: is this a profit-centre that should own revenue and cost trade-offs, or a cost-centre that should not be evaluated on revenue? Evaluating a cost-centre on revenue produces goal-incongruence. |
| For a transfer-pricing analogue (internal platform team charging product teams) | Transfer pricing (Ch 9.4) is the analytical frame: what price across the internal boundary produces goal congruence with company-wide strategy? Variable cost only is right with excess capacity; full cost or market price is right at capacity. The transfer-price decision affects which team will accept the work and at what intensity. |
| For a retrospective or learning-loop need | The standard-cost variance framing (Ch 8.5) is usable as a retro frame: what was the standard (planned cycle time, planned defect rate, planned story points), what was the actual, and what does the variance signal — managing-to-the-threshold, budget-slack, capability change, external disruption? |

### Phase 5: Risk, reliability, compliance (incidents, security, legal, regulatory, ethics)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| For an incident-response or post-incident-learning concern | The IMA eight-step ethics framework (Ch 1.4) and the SOX whistleblower-protection framing (Ch 1.4) anchor the decision-rights of post-incident response. SOX Section 302 (CEO/CFO sign-off) creates accountability hooks above engineering. Engineering-side learning-loop discipline pairs with NHS Just Culture and TC 25-20 AAR work; this volume's contribution is the *cost-classification* of post-incident remediation (avoidable vs sunk, controllable vs uncontrollable). |
| For a legal-exposure decision (contract, IP, employment) | The Foreign Corrupt Practices Act and SOX framings (Ch 1.4) anchor cross-border and audit risk. The cost of compliance investment is fixed (build the control system); the cost of non-compliance is contingent and potentially ruinous (Ford Pinto pattern, Ch 10.6). |
| For a compliance-investment-with-engineering-cost decision | Cost-as-decision-relative (Ch 2.2) — is the compliance investment a cost-of-doing-business (already-committed fixed) or a controllable investment (relevant for evaluation)? Frame it as the latter for board-level discussion; frame it as the former for engineering-priority discussion. |
| For a security or reliability event with PR + customer dimensions | The J&J Tylenol case (Ch 13.1) and the eight-step ethics framework (Ch 1.4) anchor the decision-rights of crisis response. The Tylenol pattern: protect people first; protect the product second; act on principle before on cost-minimisation. |
| For an AI-ethics or extractive-cost question | The Ford Pinto case (Ch 10.6) is the volume's central illustration that pure cost-minimisation in ethically charged decisions produces decisions that look profitable but turn out ruinous. The eight-step ethics framework is the disciplined alternative; the IMA four standards (competence, confidentiality, integrity, credibility) anchor the practitioner's own obligations. |

### Phase 6: Stakeholder communication (board reporting, exec alignment, investor relations, customer messaging)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| For a board-paper concern with technical content | The balanced scorecard (Ch 12.4) gives the structural shape: financial, internal-process, customer, and learning-and-growth. Engineering metrics map onto each perspective; the board reads engineering through this structure more easily than through engineering-native vocabulary. Pair with EVA (Ch 12.3): WACC-based valuation of engineering investments in board-financial language. |
| For a fundraising or investor-update need | The capital-budgeting frame (Ch 11) — NPV, IRR, profitability index — is the investor-native language. Translate engineering investments into NPV at the investor's required rate of return; compare projects across the portfolio with the profitability index when sizes differ. |
| For a cross-function negotiation (engineering vs product vs sales) | The transfer-pricing frame (Ch 9.4) and the responsibility-centre framing (Ch 9.3) name the structural source of cross-function tensions: each function is evaluated on different measures and may prefer different prices for the cross-boundary work. Naming the structure — rather than negotiating the surface — surfaces the goal-incongruence problem and reframes the negotiation. |
| For a customer-communication concern with technical content | The volume's stakeholder-information-needs framing (Ch 13.2) supplies the audience shape: what does this customer actually need to know to make their decision? The framework choice (GRI for stakeholder-broad, SASB for investor-focused) is structurally analogous to the technical-communication choice (executive summary, technical detail, plain-language explanation). |
| For a CTO-to-board translation problem | The balanced scorecard (Ch 12.4) and the EVA-with-WACC formulation (Ch 12.3) bridge engineering and board vocabularies. The board reads engineering through ROI, RI, EVA, and the four BSC perspectives; the CTO translates engineering investments into this language without distorting the underlying engineering reality. |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: A build-vs-buy decision rests on allocated platform overhead.** Signal: the "build" cost in the comparison includes a share of platform engineering overhead that would remain whether or not the team builds in-house. Diagnosis: allocated common costs treated as avoidable; the comparison overstates internal cost and biases toward buy. Follow-up: redo the analysis using only avoidable internal cost (engineering headcount actually released, licences actually retired, on-call actually retired); the build option often becomes more attractive.

- **Pattern: Technical-debt remediation rejected on payback grounds without comparison to ongoing avoidable cost.** Signal: a debt-remediation project is rejected because the up-front cost exceeds annual savings, without measuring the recurring engineering capacity the debt consumes. Diagnosis: missing recurring-avoidable-cost framing; the remediation is being compared to zero rather than to the cost of continuing to absorb the debt. Follow-up: measure the engineering capacity the debt consumes each quarter; compute the payback period of remediation; compare to the project's expected horizon.

- **Pattern: Feature roadmap ranked by absolute revenue at the bottleneck.** Signal: features are prioritised by expected revenue without normalising by engineering hours at the constrained team or system. Diagnosis: ranking is wrong in a constrained-resource environment; high-revenue features may consume disproportionate bottleneck capacity. Follow-up: rank by contribution-margin-per-bottleneck-hour; expect the priority order to change.

- **Pattern: An engineering hire approved as a capital decision but evaluated as an operating expense.** Signal: hire was justified on multi-year capability grounds (NPV-shaped argument), but performance is being evaluated on quarterly output (relevant-cost-shaped measurement). Diagnosis: mismatched analytical frames; the long-horizon capability investment is being measured against short-horizon throughput. Follow-up: align the evaluation timeframe with the decision timeframe; capability investments take quarters to show in throughput.

- **Pattern: AI tooling evaluated by short-payback only.** Signal: AI investment proposals approved or rejected on whether the tool pays back in twelve months without longer-horizon NPV analysis. Diagnosis: payback-only screening misses post-payback value; the AI capability may compound over multiple years. Follow-up: compute NPV at the required rate of return over the realistic capability horizon (often 3-5 years); compare to short-payback alternatives.

- **Pattern: ROI-based engineering-team evaluation rejecting cross-team-value-creating projects.** Signal: an engineering team rejects a project that benefits the company but lowers the team's headline ROI. Diagnosis: goal-incongruence built into the metric. Follow-up: shift the team's evaluation to residual income or EVA; both reward any positive contribution above the firm-wide threshold.

- **Pattern: Board reporting on engineering through financial measures only.** Signal: board sees cost, headcount, and time-to-delivery but not cycle time, defect rate, customer satisfaction, or engineering retention. Diagnosis: incomplete information; the board cannot evaluate engineering trajectory from financial measures alone. Follow-up: introduce balanced-scorecard reporting with all four perspectives; financial, internal-process, customer, learning-and-growth.

- **Pattern: A reliability or security investment defended on principle without break-even analysis.** Signal: the investment is justified as "the right thing" without quantifying the incident cost it prevents. Diagnosis: missing CVP framing; the investment may be cost-effective or wasteful but the conversation can't tell. Follow-up: compute variable cost per incident (response, credit, brand damage); compute fixed cost of investment; identify the incident rate at which the investment breaks even; compare to historical incident rate.

- **Pattern: A sunk-cost defence of a legacy system.** Signal: arguments include "we've already spent N engineer-years on this platform; replacing it would waste that investment." Diagnosis: classic sunk-cost fallacy; prior investment is irrelevant to the forward-looking decision. Follow-up: reframe as "what is the best decision from here forward?"; compute the cost of continuing versus the cost of migrating, both excluding prior investment.

- **Pattern: A short-horizon engineering decision evaluated with multi-year NPV.** Signal: a make-or-buy decision for the next quarter is run through a five-year NPV model. Diagnosis: wrong analytical frame; relevant-cost analysis is sufficient. Follow-up: simplify to relevant-cost; the NPV adds discount-rate noise without information for an under-twelve-month horizon.

- **Pattern: A multi-year engineering investment evaluated with relevant-cost analysis only.** Signal: a multi-year platform investment is evaluated on next quarter's cash flow only. Diagnosis: wrong analytical frame; ignoring future cash flows and time value distorts the comparison. Follow-up: shift to NPV; for size-divergent alternatives, profitability index.

- **Pattern: Variance-management on engineering metrics producing latent technical debt.** Signal: cycle time, deployment frequency, or defect-rate metrics are hitting target through expedients (skipped tests, deferred refactors, under-staffed on-call). Diagnosis: managing to the variance — meeting the threshold at long-term cost. Follow-up: surface the latent debt being absorbed; reframe the variance discussion around sustainable rate rather than threshold compliance.

- **Pattern: A "free internal platform" creating goal-incongruence.** Signal: a central platform team provides services to product teams at zero transfer price; product teams over-consume; the platform team's costs grow but its productivity metrics look poor. Diagnosis: transfer pricing at zero creates demand without discipline; the platform is funded as a cost-centre but operates as a profit-centre. Follow-up: introduce explicit transfer pricing aligned with the platform's marginal cost; structure the evaluation framework around the responsibility-centre type the platform actually is.

## Worked Example

CloudForge, a mid-sized B2B SaaS company with 220 engineers across product, platform, and reliability teams, faces four overlapping software-business decisions in a single quarter. Each illustrates a distinct pattern from the volume.

**Decision 1 (build-vs-buy for an observability platform).** CloudForge's reliability team has built and maintained an in-house observability stack for four years. A vendor (PageDash) offers a comparable product at $480K annual licence. The reliability team's annual cost for the in-house stack: $1.2M (5 engineers fully allocated at $200K loaded cost, plus $200K hosting). Conventional reading: build appears more expensive than buy. The relevant-cost discipline is the load-bearing move here: "A cost or revenue is relevant if it differs between alternatives. Sunk costs, unavoidable costs, and future costs that do not differ are irrelevant." Of the 5 engineers, 2 would actually be reassigned to higher-priority reliability work (saving $400K only in opportunity-cost terms; the headcount remains); 3 carry domain knowledge that is load-bearing and cannot be released. The $200K hosting is genuinely avoidable. The avoidable in-house cost is $200K (hosting only); the vendor cost is $480K. Build, not buy. The conventional comparison overstated avoidable cost by treating engineer headcount as releasable when it is not — the Thermal Mugs pattern the volume names: "whether $0.12 of allocated fixed cost is avoidable swings the decision." (Source: OpenStax, *Principles of Accounting Vol 2*, Ch 10.1, "Identify Relevant Information for Decision-Making"; Ch 10.3, "Evaluate and Determine Whether to Make or Buy a Component")

**Decision 2 (technical-debt remediation against revenue work).** A legacy authentication module consumes approximately 3 engineer-weeks per quarter in maintenance, on-call escalation, and slow feature integration. Replacing it would cost approximately 12 engineer-weeks of effort and would eliminate the recurring cost. The conventional reading: 12 weeks is four times the quarterly burden, so the remediation pays back in four quarters. The relevant-cost analysis adds two pieces: (a) the avoidable recurring cost is real and ongoing, so the remediation has positive NPV over any realistic time horizon; (b) the legacy module is the constraint slowing time-to-market for three queued features. The volume's Theory of Constraints five-step applies directly: "identifying the single binding constraint, exploiting it, subordinating everything else to it, elevating it, and repeating." Relaxing the constraint creates revenue opportunity not in the original framing. Combined analysis: payback under four quarters on direct cost; an additional contribution from accelerated feature delivery. Decision: remediate now. (Source: Ch 1.5, "Lean Practices"; Ch 10.1)

**Decision 3 (capital investment in an AI infrastructure platform).** The CTO proposes a $4.8M investment in an internal AI infrastructure platform (model training, evaluation, deployment) over 18 months. Expected benefits: $1.5M annual cost savings from eliminating duplicate ML infrastructure across teams, plus a less-quantifiable acceleration of AI-feature development. Payback period: 3.2 years (passes a 5-year screen). The volume's capital-budgeting discipline: "Capital decisions involve large outlays, long horizons, and the time value of money; payback and ARR are screens; NPV and IRR are the substantive tools." NPV at 12 per cent required rate of return over 5 years: present value of $1.5M annuity is approximately $5.4M; NPV is $5.4M minus $4.8M = $0.6M, positive but thin. IRR: approximately 14 per cent, modestly above the hurdle. The profitability-index check gives $5.4M / $4.8M = 1.13, exceeding 1.0 by enough margin to justify the investment if the qualitative AI-acceleration benefit holds; milestone gates protect against over-commitment. The conversation with the CFO uses NPV and IRR; the conversation with the board uses EVA at WACC to express the investment in shareholder-value terms. (Source: Ch 11.1; Ch 11.2; Ch 11.4; Ch 12.3)

**Decision 4 (board reporting on engineering for the upcoming quarterly review).** The CEO has asked the CTO for a board paper summarising engineering performance. Pure financial reporting (cost, headcount, time-to-delivery) is read as inadequate by the CTO; the board has been receiving similar reports for two years. The volume's position: "Financial measures alone are insufficient"; the four-perspective balanced scorecard produces more durable evaluation. The CTO designs a balanced-scorecard structure: financial (engineering cost per active customer, revenue per engineer); internal-process (deployment frequency, mean-time-to-recovery, defect-escape rate); customer (feature-adoption rate, customer-satisfaction with engineering deliveries); learning-and-growth (engineering retention rate, ratio of internal promotions to external hires, certifications and training completion). Each perspective gets one chart with a target line, two-year trend, and a brief narrative interpretation. The CTO closes with an EVA calculation at the company's 9 per cent WACC: engineering EVA contribution for the trailing twelve months is $14.2M, expressed as the after-tax income attributable to engineering minus the WACC charge on engineering's invested capital. The board sees engineering as a value-creating function expressed in its own financial language for the first time. (Source: Ch 12.4, "Describe the Balanced Scorecard and Explain How It Is Used"; Ch 12.3)

These four decisions illustrate the volume's central software-business patterns: avoidable-cost discipline in make-or-buy, recurring-cost framing for technical-debt remediation, capital-budgeting analysis for multi-year platform investments, and balanced-scorecard structure for board reporting. The volume's decision-relative cost classification — "fixed for what decision? avoidable for what alternative? sunk relative to which forward question?" — runs underneath all four. (Source: Ch 2.2, "Identify and Apply Basic Cost Behavior Patterns")

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Treating allocated platform overhead as avoidable in make-or-buy.** The in-house cost in a build-vs-buy comparison should include only what's *actually* released by the buy decision (Ch 10.3). Including allocated overhead biases toward buy.
- **Defending legacy systems on sunk-cost grounds.** Prior engineering investment is sunk and irrelevant; only the forward-looking cost of each alternative matters (Ch 10.1).
- **Mixing short-term and long-term analytical frames in engineering decisions.** A quarterly capacity decision evaluated with five-year NPV adds noise; a five-year platform investment evaluated only on next-quarter cash flow distorts the comparison (Ch 10.1; Ch 11.1).
- **Ranking features by absolute revenue in a constrained environment.** Capacity-aware ranking is contribution-margin-per-unit-of-the-constraint; otherwise high-revenue but high-bottleneck-cost features starve higher-contribution-per-hour alternatives (Ch 10.6).
- **Evaluating engineering teams on ROI alone.** Goal-incongruence is built into the metric: high-ROI teams will reject value-creating projects below their headline number. Residual income or EVA aligns segment and company-wide value (Ch 9.3; Ch 12.3).
- **Pure financial-measure board reporting on engineering.** The four-perspective scorecard (financial, internal-process, customer, learning-and-growth) produces more durable evaluation; financial measures alone lag, are gameable, and miss the capability dimension (Ch 12.4).
- **Pure cost-minimisation in ethically charged software decisions.** The Ford Pinto pattern applies to data-handling, dark patterns, AI deployment, and known-defect tolerance (Ch 10.6). The eight-step ethics framework is the disciplined alternative (Ch 1.4).
- **Budget slack and managing to engineering variance.** Inflated estimates that make actuals look good degrade the long-term performance signal; short-term decisions to hit cycle-time or velocity targets at the cost of latent debt erode capability (Ch 7.5; Ch 8.5).
- **Absorption-style engineering-cost allocation for internal decisions.** Allocating fixed engineering cost to products to defend headcount or platform investment is the Big-Three-autos pattern in software shape; the volume prescribes variable costing for internal decisions, absorption only for external reporting (Ch 6.5).
- **Reactive crisis communication after a security or reliability event.** The J&J Tylenol pattern (protect people first, protect product second, act on principle before cost-minimisation) is the volume's case-study contrast to the Bhopal pattern (Ch 13.1).

## Through the cto lens

The CTO's opportunity-first, constraint-disciplined operating tempo reshapes which of this volume's frameworks are load-bearing.

**Surfaced as load-bearing.** The Theory of Constraints five-step (Ch 1.5) and contribution-margin-per-bottleneck-unit ranking (Ch 10.6) are the volume's most CTO-native moves — *one lever, not a list*. Decision-relative cost classification (Ch 2.2) is the discipline that distinguishes the CTO who names the constraint *as an operational mechanism* from one who names it as a concept; *fixed for what decision* is the CTO frame. The capital-budgeting NPV / IRR / profitability-index toolkit (Ch 11) is the language for multi-year platform investments where the CTO is making the AI-native bet and needs to defend it to the board. Variable-versus-absorption costing (Ch 6.5) maps onto the CTO's reluctance to absorb engineering into product cost: variable costing is the internal-decision discipline; absorption is for external reporting only.

**Reshaped questions.** The build-vs-buy question (Phase 1) is the CTO's: state the constraint as *which capacity the buy actually releases* (owner + capability gap + repeatable gate); not as *which option is cheaper on paper*. The technical-debt question (Phase 2) is the CTO's: the constraint is the binding bottleneck; the remediation lever exploits-and-elevates the constraint. The AI-integration question (Phase 1) is the CTO's substrate question — AI as org redesign with role bifurcation (AI-platform-expert vs domain-expert), framed by the cost-classification discipline and the capital-budgeting frame for multi-year investment. The board-reporting question (Phase 6) is the CTO's translation problem — balanced scorecard plus EVA-at-WACC bridges engineering into board-financial language without distorting the underlying engineering.

**Recedes for this reader.** The pedagogical scaffolding of the volume — career roles, certifications, the IMA Statement, the eight-step ethics framework — is contextual orientation, not load-bearing for the CTO's queue. The sustainability-reporting chapter (Ch 13) recedes unless a specific ESG question is in play. The horizontal-and-vertical-analysis appendix (Appendix A) is finance-functional, not CTO-functional.

**Native vocabulary to use when shaping for this lens.** *The constraint as operational mechanism, the lever, what unlocks, capability not productivity, decision-relative, avoidable in this comparison, contribution per bottleneck hour, NPV at our hurdle rate, EVA at WACC, the decision needed (assign / fund / approve), timeboxed first step, success measures.* Strip hedges; lead with what becomes possible; name the constraint by the end of the second sentence; close with the decision plus owner plus first step plus measurable success.

## Through the business-executive-stakeholder lens

The business-executive-stakeholder reads engineering investment in Paradigm-A vocabulary (commitment, accountability, plan, KPI, named owner) with occasional Paradigm-B language in low-heat moments. The volume's frameworks reshape under this lens toward register-fit.

**Surfaced as load-bearing.** The balanced scorecard (Ch 12.4) is this reader's native shape — four perspectives, each with measurable indicators, each owned. The capital-budgeting NPV / IRR analysis (Ch 11) speaks investor-native language; the executive reads NPV and IRR without translation. The responsibility-centre framework (Ch 9.3) supplies the named-owner discipline this executive looks for; the cost / profit / investment / revenue / discretionary-cost distinction names whose accountability holds which function. The variance-analysis framing (Ch 8.5) supplies the planned-vs-actual-vs-explanation structure this executive expects in business-review artefacts.

**Reshaped questions.** Board reporting (Phase 6) lands in this executive's queue via the BSC structure — each perspective with named owner, target, actual, variance, and the decision being asked of the executive (approve, fund, escalate). Variance analysis (Phase 4) lands as the standard-cost framing this executive recognises; the volume's warning against managing-to-the-variance is the parsimonious Paradigm-B move available inside an otherwise A-shaped artefact. Sustainability-reporting choice (Phase 5) lands as a framework decision (GRI vs SASB vs IIRC) with stakeholder-audience reasoning the executive can act on.

**The parsimonious B-move available.** The volume's *cost-classification is decision-relative* move (Ch 2.2) — fixed for what decision, avoidable for what alternative — is the one Paradigm-B reframe most likely to land with this reader inside an A-shaped artefact. It does not ask the executive to operate from B; it offers a sharper cost-classification discipline that produces better A-decisions. The variance-as-learning-signal framing (Ch 8.5) is a second B-move available, surfaced when the artefact carries the management-as-control-system tension; use one or the other, not both, in a single artefact.

**Recedes for this reader.** The pedagogical chapter scaffolding (terms, summaries, exercises) is irrelevant. The detailed cost-estimation methods (high-low, regression, Ch 2.3) recede unless a specific cost-modelling question is in play. The deeper ethics-framework material (Ch 1.4) recedes unless an ethically charged decision is in front of the executive; when it is, the IMA four standards (competence, confidentiality, integrity, credibility) are the load-bearing reference.

**Shape for this lens.** Opening: 1-2 sentences of warmth-and-acknowledgment when post-event; 1 sentence operational opening when pre-event. Operational frame: the plan, the actual, the variance, the consequence, the accountable owner, in Paradigm-A vocabulary throughout. *One* Paradigm-B move: decision-relative cost classification or variance-as-learning-signal, offered as a choice. Closing: named owner, timeboxed first step, measurable success criteria, the decision being requested.

## Through the pm-bounded-by-ba-role lens

This is a read-only lens; no produce-for surface. The PM bounded by a BA role reads this volume's frameworks as artefacts that arrive in their workflow, asking them to do work the role's actual authority often cannot support.

**Authority assumption the volume's frameworks regularly carry.** The volume frequently assumes the reader can *decide* a make-or-buy, *commit* capital, *approve* an investment, *set* a transfer price, *evaluate* segment performance. For a PM bounded by a BA role, those verbs typically describe activity above the role's decision rights. The volume's analytical frame is correct; the artefact landing on the PM that *uses* the frame should be read for whether it assigns the deciding-and-committing work to the PM (it should not), or assigns the *coordinating-translating-tracking* work to the PM (it likely does). The lens names the gap.

**Coordination cost the volume creates when its frameworks land on the PM.** A make-or-buy analysis (Ch 10.3) lands on the PM as coordination work: gather avoidable-cost estimates from engineering, gather vendor-cost estimates from procurement, reconcile the two, write the story or memo summarising the choice, schedule the decision meeting with stakeholders, capture the decision in the backlog. None of this appears in the framework itself; all of it appears in the PM's day. A capital-budgeting NPV analysis (Ch 11) creates similar coordination work: gather cash-flow estimates from finance, gather engineering-hour estimates from the engineering manager, format for the investment committee, follow up on decision criteria, schedule the review. The lens surfaces the coordination cost.

**Where the volume's authority assumptions exceed the PM's role.** The Ch 10 short-term decision-making chapter assigns the decision to "management" — which the PM bounded by a BA role typically supports but does not own. The Ch 11 capital-budgeting decisions sit at the executive layer; the PM coordinates inputs but does not approve. The Ch 9 responsibility-centre evaluation belongs to the executive or finance layer; the PM tracks against the metrics but does not set them. The Ch 12 balanced-scorecard design is a CTO or executive decision; the PM contributes data but does not own the measurement choices. Every framework in this volume that ends with "decide" or "approve" or "commit" is naming work that lives above the PM bounded by a BA role.

**Where the volume's frameworks are role-accurate.** The variance-analysis framing (Ch 8.5) and the standard-cost framing (Ch 8) are role-accurate for what a PM tracks and reports — planned vs actual for stories, cycle time, defect rate. The PM can run variance analysis without exceeding their authority. The retrospective-as-variance-discussion framing (Phase 4) similarly sits inside the PM's role. The data-coordination work involved in Phase 6 board-reporting belongs to the PM; the framework choices (which BSC perspectives, which BC measures) belong above.

**What gets absorbed silently when the volume's frameworks are applied without acknowledgment.** Translation of decision-criteria across the finance / engineering / product / stakeholder boundaries; reconciliation of cost estimates that arrive in different units; refinement of stories whose underlying assumption is a framework choice that hasn't been made; soothing of stakeholders whose preferred framework (cost-plus pricing, ROI-only evaluation) is being displaced by the volume's recommended alternative; tracking the decision through approval and into the backlog. None of this appears in the framework; all of it appears in the PM's day.
