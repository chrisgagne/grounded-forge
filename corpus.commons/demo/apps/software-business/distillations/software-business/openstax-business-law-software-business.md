# OpenStax Business Law I Essentials, Software-Business Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2019). *Business Law I Essentials*. Rice University. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/business-law-i-essentials. Lead author Mirande Valbrune (Esq.); six contributing authors.

## Software-Business Relevance

OpenStax's Business Law I Essentials provides the legal-primitive layer that prices several recurring software-business trade-offs the operator otherwise resolves on instinct. Three threads land cleanly on the technical-commercial intersection. Ch 6 and Ch 8 frame product-liability tort and UCC warranty doctrine, which together govern when a software defect becomes a legal exposure rather than a backlog item — relevant to reliability-as-business-decision and to incident response. Ch 7-8 set out contract formation and remedies, which govern vendor selection, build-vs-buy commitments, customer terms-of-service drafting, and the SLA-vs-warranty boundary. Ch 9 carries the employment-law primitives that bound hiring, contractor mix, levelling, and discipline decisions — Title VII, ADA, ADEA, FMLA, OSHA, FLSA, NLRA, at-will doctrine — and frames the legal floor under team-and-capability-building decisions. Ch 12 carries the FTC unfair-trade-practice categories that govern marketing claims about software products. Ch 2 supplies the TKI five-mode negotiation vocabulary that the software-business axis explicitly inherits for engineering-vs-product, vendor, and customer negotiations.

The Lanham Act, patent, copyright, and trade-secret material is *named* in the task spec but only *peripherally surveyed* in this source: IP appears in Ch 1.3's survey of business law's component disciplines and in Ch 8's warranty-against-infringement, not as standalone IP chapters. Practitioners reaching for IP-as-build-vs-buy frame will find scaffolding here but not depth. Similarly, GDPR/CCPA/COPPA are flagged in the source's Ch 12 framing of data privacy as code-of-conduct regulatory ethics, but not developed as independent regimes; for data-protection compliance depth the practitioner needs counsel or specialist regulatory reference outside this corpus.

This distillation consolidates the legal-primitive layer into a software-business working pattern: where legal exposure prices a technical decision, where contract law shapes a commercial commitment, and where employment and consumer-protection law shape the operating envelope of a software company.

## Key Concepts for Software-Business

1.  **TKI five negotiation modes.** Forcing (high self, low other), collaborating (high both), compromising (moderate both), avoiding (low both), yielding (low self, high other). Forcing on a continuing relationship "often undermines the party's long-term success"; yielding may signal an unfair power differential. Maps to engineering-vs-product, engineering-vs-sales, vendor, and customer negotiations where both relational and outcome goals are in play. (Source: OpenStax, *Business Law I Essentials*, Ch 2.1, "Negotiation Style")

2.  **Relational vs outcome goals; integrative vs distributive framing.** Continuing-relationship counterparties (key vendors, anchor customers, internal peers) warrant integrative framing; one-time interactions tolerate distributive framing — but the long-term reputational cost of distributive moves should be priced before being deployed. (Source: Ch 2.1, "Negotiation Types and Objectives")

3.  **Tiered ADR ladder.** Negotiation, mediation, arbitration, litigation form a structured escalation. Mediation supplies confidentiality, creativity, and retained control; arbitration produces a judge-like decision with limited appeal rights. The Chevron exemplar prices the savings empirically ($25,000 mediation vs $700,000 outside-legal vs $2.5M litigation). Software companies with binding-arbitration clauses in standard customer contracts should price the stakeholder-fairness cost before invoking them. (Source: Ch 2.1-2.3)

4.  **Forced-arbitration clauses as ethics question, not legal optimisation.** The source notes that under forced-arbitration clauses "consumers agree to settle all disputes through arbitration, effectively waiving their right to sue a company in court," and some "cause the other party to forfeit their right to appeal an arbitration decision or participate in any kind of class action lawsuit" [V] (Source: OpenStax, *Business Law I Essentials*, Ch 2.3, "Ethics of Commercial Arbitration Clauses"). The source explicitly frames against using such clauses "as a way to keep wrongdoings 'quiet' or to limit consumers' abilities to obtain rightful retribution." Software ToS clauses bear directly on this.

5.  **Five contract-formation elements.** Offer and acceptance, genuine agreement, consideration, capacity, legality. Each is a checkpoint when a software company commits to a vendor SaaS, a partnership agreement, an enterprise customer SOW, or a contractor engagement. (Source: Ch 7.1)

6.  **Material vs minor breach and five remedies.** Material breach (substantially different from what was promised) discharges obligations; minor breach (e.g., late delivery without "time is of the essence") still requires performance. Remedies: money damages, restitution, rescission, reformation, specific performance — the last *never* for personal service (would be unconstitutional forced labour, ruling out compelling a contractor or employee to perform). Court factors for materiality: benefit received, compensation adequacy, performance extent, breaching-party hardship, intent, possibility of completion. (Source: Ch 7.3)

7.  **UCC Article 2 sales contracts and the software-as-goods boundary.** Most terms (price, payment, delivery, duration) can be left open with reasonableness defaults; *quantity* must be specified except in requirements ("as much as I need") or output ("as much as you can produce") contracts. Firm offers default to 3-month irrevocability; the mirror-image rule does not apply; sales over $500 must be written. Software licences "have been treated variously as goods, mixed sales, and pure services" — a doctrinal uncertainty practitioners should treat as a flag, not as resolved. (Source: Ch 8.1)

8.  **Warranties under the UCC.** A warranty is "a guarantee on the good that comes as part of the sales contract" treated as an additional binding contract; if the standard is unmet, "the contract provides a specific remedy, such as a replacement or repair" [V] (Source: Ch 8.2, "Warranties"). Express warranties arise from seller statements ("wrinkle-free," "made in the USA"); implied warranties arise automatically — including the warranty against infringement (third-party IP claims) and warranty of fitness for normal use. Puffery ("the best in the world") is lawful; the reasonableness-of-reliance test divides puffery from warranty. Software vendor "marketing claims" that cross from puffery into specific guarantees become enforceable warranties.

9.  **Product liability and the unreasonably-dangerous-product standard.** Three sources of liability: manufacturing flaw, design defect (when safer alternatives are economically feasible), insufficient warnings or instructions. *Res ipsa loquitor* can shift the burden when normal use produces unexplained harm. Software practitioners considering reliability-as-business-decision should treat insufficient warnings (e.g., undocumented system limitations) as a distinct liability surface from the defect itself. (Source: Ch 6.2)

10.  **Negligence: duty, breach, causal connection, measurable harm.** The reasonable-person standard governs duty of care; foreseeability bounds it. Special relationships (vendor-customer, employer-employee) imply elevated duties. Foreseeability is the test that runs through both tort liability and contract anticipation. (Source: Ch 6.1)

11.  **At-will employment and its statutory limits.** As the source puts it: "all employees are considered to be at-will unless they are employed under a collective bargaining agreement, or under a contract for a set duration. Employers can still fire employees for any reason, but they cannot be fired for illegal reasons, as set out in the U.S. or state constitutions, federal law, state statutes, or public policy" [V] (Source: Ch 9.1, "Employment, Worker Protection, and Immigration Law"). The "illegal reason" set is the substantive content of Title VII, ADA, ADEA, FMLA, OSHA, NLRA, and public-policy doctrine.

12.  **Title VII and disparate-impact analysis.** Covers commercial employers with 15+ employees; protected classes are race, sex, national origin, colour, religion (plus pregnancy via the PDA, disability via ADA, age 40+ via ADEA). Two proof routes: disparate treatment (three-step burden-shifting — *prima facie*, employer reason, pretext) and disparate impact (statistical, *Griggs* analysis: black men with HS diploma at 12% vs white at 34%; intelligence-test pass rate 6% black vs 58% white). Software hiring filters (degree requirements, coding tests, AI-mediated screening) can produce disparate impact even without intent. (Source: Ch 9.3)

13.  **Three Title VII defences.** BFOQ (must be actual qualification, never stereotype; no race or colour BFOQs at all); merit (validated tests manifestly job-related); seniority (lawful if not rooted in discrimination and applied equally). (Source: Ch 9.3)

14.  **FMLA and ADA accommodation envelope.** FMLA: 12 unpaid weeks in 12 months for birth, adoption, foster placement, family illness, own serious health condition; 50+ employees, 12-month tenure, 25 hours/week eligibility. ADA: reasonable accommodation required absent undue burden; structured interactive review. Both are bounding constraints on engineering manager and HR decisions. (Source: Ch 9.1)

15.  **NLRA and the union-engagement architecture.** Rights to organise and collective bargaining; three union-security agreement types (closed, union, agency shop); three election types (consent, contest, decertification); 60-day cooling-off before strikes; four illegal strike types. Software shops with technical staff organising should engage labour counsel and respect NLRB-supervised procedures. (Source: Ch 9.2)

16.  **FTC unfair-trade-practice categories.** Six named: product guarantees and false endorsements, unfair advertising (bait-and-switch), taking advantage of customers (vulnerable populations), misrepresenting a product (specific term-use rules), giving misleading price information (false sales, hidden terms), failing to disclose pertinent information. Plaintiffs do not need to prove intent. Software marketing claims, dark-pattern UX, hidden subscription terms, and AI-capability puffery cross into this regime. (Source: Ch 12.1)

17.  **Antitrust primitives.** Sherman Act (criminal; restraints of trade; penalties up to $100M corporations / $1M individuals / 10 years; price-fixing, market-allocation agreements). Clayton Act (civil; mergers, price discrimination, exclusive dealing, interlocking directorates). FTC Act (unfair methods of competition). Software platform decisions — exclusive-dealing terms, acquisition pricing, distribution restrictions — sit in this regime. (Source: Ch 11.2)

18.  **Six-step ethical decision-making model (Michigan State).** Understand and gather facts; separate facts from assumptions; note all concerns (people, laws, ethical guidelines); construct a potential solution; evaluate against ethical aspects; recommend the solution and required actions. A structured pause for legally-and-ethically-consequential software decisions (data use, AI capability, surveillance feature). (Source: Ch 3.1, "Ethical Decision-Making Policies")

## Questions to Ask During Software-Business Work

### Phase 1: Strategic positioning (pricing, market entry, build-vs-buy, AI integration)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Surface IP exposure in build-vs-buy | If we buy or licence this component, what IP regime governs (copyright, patent, trade-secret, trademark)? What licence terms (permissive, copyleft, proprietary, dual)? What warranty against infringement does the vendor provide (UCC 8.2)? |
| Surface contract-formation gaps in vendor selection | Does the offer specify quantity (or invoke requirements/output exception)? Is consideration concrete? Is capacity present on both sides? Is the subject matter legal in all relevant jurisdictions? |
| Surface antitrust exposure in market-entry or partnership | Does this arrangement create or enable monopoly power, price-fixing, exclusive dealing, or interlocking directorates? Is the merger above the 1976 Clayton Act notification threshold? |
| Surface forced-arbitration ethics in customer ToS | Does our standard customer ToS include forced arbitration or class-action waiver? If we invoke it, are we using it as a legitimate dispute-resolution mechanism or as silencing? |
| Surface AI-integration legal envelope | If we add AI capability, what marketing claims are we making (FTC scrutiny on AI puffery)? What employment-decision automation are we enabling (Title VII disparate-impact exposure)? What data are we processing (consumer-protection regimes)? |

### Phase 2: Product and engineering economics (tech-debt, capacity, reliability-as-decision)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Price reliability investment against product-liability exposure | If a defect causes user harm, are we exposed to negligence, strict liability, or product-liability claims? Have safer alternatives existed at economically feasible cost (design-defect test)? Are our warnings and instructions adequate? |
| Price tech-debt remediation against breach-of-warranty exposure | Are we shipping product whose actual behaviour diverges materially from express marketing claims? Could a customer claim breach of express warranty? Is the implied warranty of fitness for normal use at risk? |
| Surface SLA-vs-warranty boundary | Are we calling something an SLA when it is functioning as an express warranty? Have we created reliance the reasonableness-of-reliance test will honour? |
| Surface specific-performance impossibility | Are we negotiating a customer commitment that would require us to compel a specific contractor or engineer to deliver? Specific performance is unavailable for personal service. |

### Phase 3: Team and capability building (hiring, structure, levelling, discipline)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Surface protected-class exposure in hiring decisions | Is the hiring criterion (degree, coding test, AI screen, network referral) producing disparate impact across protected classes (race, sex, national origin, colour, religion, age 40+, disability, pregnancy)? Have we run the *Griggs* statistical check? |
| Surface BFOQ pseudo-defences | If a role-specification names what someone "is like" rather than what the job requires, we have a stereotype-based BFOQ that will fail under Title VII and create discrimination liability. Rebuild on actual qualifications. |
| Surface contractor-vs-employee misclassification risk | Is the worker actually a contractor (control, equipment, schedule autonomy, multiple clients) or an employee misclassified? Wage-hour, FLSA, workers' comp, FMLA, and tax consequences follow. |
| Surface ADA accommodation envelope | Has a disclosed disability been followed by structured interactive review of reasonable accommodation, with explicit undue-burden analysis if denied? |
| Surface FMLA eligibility before deny | Does the employee meet FMLA eligibility (12 months, 25 hours/week, 50+ employer)? Is the leave for a qualifying event (birth, adoption, foster placement, family illness, own serious condition)? |
| Surface NLRA exposure in technical-staff organising | Are management actions during organising respecting NLRB procedures? Are restrictions on employee speech about working conditions touching protected concerted activity? |
| Surface at-will limits in discipline decisions | What is the asserted reason for the adverse action? Is it documented? Could a reasonable observer read it as pretext for an illegal reason? |

### Phase 4: Operations and process (delivery, governance, change management)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Surface contract-modification discipline | When we modify a sales contract under the UCC, is the modification in good faith? (New consideration is not required; good faith is.) |
| Surface OSHA exposure in physical-engineering work | If engineering work involves physical hazards (data-centre electrical, lab equipment, field deployment), are specific-duty and general-duty OSHA standards being followed? |
| Surface workers'-comp boundary | Are workplace injuries (including RSI, ergonomic, stress-related where state law recognises) being handled through workers' comp exclusively, or is there an intentional-injury exception in play? |
| Surface decision-rights ambiguity in cross-functional governance | Where governance authority is unclear, are decisions being escalated to the right structural position (legal counsel, board, regulator) rather than absorbed by under-authorised individuals? |

### Phase 5: Risk, reliability, compliance (incidents, security, legal, regulatory, ethics)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Frame the incident's legal envelope | What legal regimes apply to this incident — contract (SLA / warranty breach), tort (negligence, product liability, strict liability), employment (employee safety, retaliation), consumer protection (FTC, data privacy), securities (material undisclosed event, 8-K trigger)? |
| Surface foreseeability test | Was the harm reasonably foreseeable? Foreseeability is the test that runs through both tort liability and contract anticipation. |
| Surface warranty-of-fitness exposure | Does the defect affect the warranty of fitness for normal use? Is the warranty against infringement implicated (e.g., a component shipped that infringes a third party's IP)? |
| Surface FTC exposure in consumer-facing language | Does our incident communication, in remediating customer concern, cross into bait-and-switch, false endorsement, or misleading price information? Are we offering remedies that constitute consequential or punitive damage avoidance? |
| Surface code-of-conduct and whistleblower process | If the incident surfaces an internal ethics violation, is the whistleblower protected from retaliation (per source's explicit position)? Is the investigation appropriately separated from the reporter's employment status? Have third-party investigators been engaged for executive-level cases? |
| Surface Schedule 13D / Form 8-K disclosure trigger | If we are publicly held, does this incident trigger 8-K disclosure (within 4 business days)? Is the materiality threshold met for insider-trading silence obligations? |
| Surface securities-fraud risk in incident comms | Are we accurately stating financial-impact estimates, or constructing puffery the SEC could read as material misrepresentation? |
| Surface IP-trade-secret exposure in breach | If the incident exposes trade secrets, what loss-of-protection consequences follow (trade-secret status requires reasonable measures to maintain secrecy)? |
| Run the six-step ethical decision frame | Have we gathered facts, separated assumptions, noted all concerns (people, laws, guidelines), constructed a solution, evaluated it ethically, and recommended action? |

### Phase 6: Stakeholder communication (board reporting, exec alignment, customer messaging)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Choose TKI mode for cross-function negotiation | When engineering and product disagree on a commercial commitment, or engineering and sales on customer terms — which TKI mode fits? What signal would say we are using the wrong mode? |
| Apply relational-vs-outcome frame | Is this a continuing relationship (warranting integrative framing) or one-time (potentially distributive)? Does the chosen framing serve long-term reputation? |
| Surface positions-vs-interests in engineering-vs-sales | What is the underlying interest each party is protecting (engineering capacity, customer trust, revenue-recognition timing)? Can we find an integrative solution that serves both? |
| Surface stakeholder fairness in customer comms | If our customer comms invoke a forced-arbitration clause or class-action waiver, are we using legitimate dispute mechanism or silencing? Would we defend this in public? |
| Frame CSR and brand alignment | Does our commitment to data privacy, AI ethics, or sustainability survive operational scrutiny, or is it CSR-as-marketing the source warns against? |
| Run the foreseeability check on board comms | Have we surfaced reasonably foreseeable risks in board reporting? Hiding foreseeable risk creates governance liability beyond the operational risk itself. |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: A vendor or partnership negotiation is heading toward forcing-style without relational accounting.** Signal: discussion focused on extracting maximum value with no concession; partner expected to absorb. Diagnosis: forcing-style applied to a continuing relationship, which the source flags as undermining long-term success. Follow-up: shift to collaborating or compromising; price the reputational cost of the move before deploying it.
- **Pattern: A build-vs-buy decision treats licence terms as legal-team detail.** Signal: business case complete; legal review treated as rubber-stamp. Diagnosis: contract-formation elements (offer, acceptance, consideration, capacity, legality), UCC warranties (against infringement, fitness for normal use), and IP regime (copyright, patent, trade-secret, trademark) materially shape the economics. Follow-up: surface the legal exposure as part of the economic case, not after it.
- **Pattern: A hiring filter is producing differential outcomes across protected classes without disparate-impact analysis.** Signal: degree requirement, coding-test threshold, AI screen, or referral channel produces a candidate pool unrepresentative of the labour market in a protected class. Diagnosis: potential disparate-impact liability under Title VII; the *Griggs* statistical analysis applies regardless of intent. Follow-up: run the disparate-impact statistics; if the filter produces disparate impact, demonstrate manifest job-relatedness (validated business necessity) or reform.
- **Pattern: An incident is being communicated in terms that cross from operational into liability-creating.** Signal: incident comms include specific guarantees ("this will never happen again"), false endorsements ("our security team is the best in the industry"), or hidden remedy conditions. Diagnosis: comms language is creating express warranties, FTC exposure, or potential securities-fraud surface. Follow-up: legal review of incident comms before publication; separate empathy-and-acknowledgment from substantive remedy commitments.
- **Pattern: A customer ToS or product agreement is being invoked to block legitimate claims.** Signal: customer experiencing real harm; company invoking arbitration clause or class-action waiver to block remedy. Diagnosis: source explicitly flags this use as ethical violation, not legal optimisation. Follow-up: distinguish legitimate dispute mechanisms (good-faith ADR) from silencing tactics; consider stakeholder-reputation cost.
- **Pattern: An employment decision is being justified as at-will without checking for illegal reasons.** Signal: "we can fire for any reason or no reason." Diagnosis: at-will doctrine does not protect firing for illegal reasons under Title VII, ADA, ADEA, FMLA, NLRA, OSHA retaliation, or public policy. Follow-up: document the actual non-illegal reason; surface to counsel if any protected class or concerted activity is in play.
- **Pattern: A software product is being marketed with claims that approach specific guarantees.** Signal: "100% accurate," "zero downtime," "completely secure," "automatically detects all X." Diagnosis: claims have crossed from puffery into express warranty under UCC 8.2; reasonable reliance is likely. Follow-up: scope claims to what we can actually deliver, or carry the warranty exposure consciously.
- **Pattern: A contractor is being asked to perform a specific task they are refusing.** Signal: company seeks legal compulsion to force the contractor to deliver. Diagnosis: specific performance for personal service is unconstitutional forced labour; not available as a remedy. Follow-up: structure relief as money damages or substitute performance; consider whether the relationship can be repaired by addressing underlying interests.
- **Pattern: A regulatory inquiry is being treated as adversarial litigation by default.** Signal: legal team posture is full litigation defensive; regulators framed as opposition. Diagnosis: voluntary compliance through consent order may produce better long-term outcomes; regulators are stakeholders, not adversaries. Follow-up: evaluate negotiation, then mediation, then formal proceedings; the Chevron cost arithmetic generalises to regulatory contexts.
- **Pattern: A data-protection question is being treated as legal-only without operational-engineering input.** Signal: privacy policy authored without engineering review of what data is actually collected, retained, processed. Diagnosis: misrepresentation in a privacy policy is FTC unfair-trade-practice (failure to disclose pertinent information; misrepresenting a product). Follow-up: align stated policy with implemented engineering; the operational and the legal must be one document, not two.

## When to Use This Reference

- A vendor selection, partnership, or build-vs-buy decision is being structured where contract terms or IP regime materially shape the economics.
- A pricing or commitment negotiation is approaching where TKI mode and relational-vs-outcome framing change the outcome.
- A hiring, role-design, contractor-vs-employee, or discipline decision is being made where Title VII, ADA, ADEA, FMLA, NLRA, OSHA, or at-will limits are in play.
- A reliability investment is being priced against potential product-liability, warranty, or negligence exposure.
- An incident, breach, or defect has surfaced and the legal envelope around the response needs to be mapped.
- A customer-facing communication (ToS, marketing copy, incident comms, support response) is being drafted where FTC unfair-trade-practice categories could apply.
- A board-paper or exec-memo on legal exposure is being authored.
- A securities disclosure trigger is being evaluated (8-K, 10-Q, 10-K, Schedule 13D, proxy).
- A merger, acquisition, or strategic-partnership structure is being reviewed against antitrust and corporate-law primitives.
- An international transaction or compliance question is being scoped (CISG, FCPA, sovereign-immunity, common-vs-civil-law jurisdiction).
- The practitioner needs the legal-primitive layer underneath a decision they are otherwise resolving on instinct.

## Worked Example

A 200-engineer SaaS company has built an AI-augmented hiring product that screens engineering candidates. The product is in private beta with three enterprise customers. The CEO and head of product are deciding whether to launch publicly, with marketing claims emphasising "fairness-tested" screening and "60% time savings." The CTO has flagged that the underlying model produces a 14% lower pass rate for one protected class than another on a benchmark. The general counsel and the CEO meet to decide.

**Phase 1 framing.** The decision sits at the intersection of three legal surfaces. First, FTC unfair-trade-practice categories apply to the marketing claim itself: the six named categories include misrepresenting a product and giving false impressions, and FTC plaintiffs do not need to prove intent. (Source: OpenStax, *Business Law I Essentials*, Ch 12.1, "The FTC and Unfair Trade Practices") Second, Title VII disparate-impact analysis applies not only to direct employers but to software products that function as hiring filters — the *Griggs* two-route framework (disparate treatment and disparate impact) is the operative test for the customers who deploy the screening output. (Source: Ch 9.3, "Title VII of the Civil Rights Act") Third, the "fairness-tested" claim has moved from puffery into an express warranty under UCC Article 2, because the reasonableness-of-reliance test would honour a sophisticated customer's reliance on a specific fitness claim. (Source: Ch 8.2, "Sales Warranties")

**Bounding the decision.** The decision is not whether the product is fair abstractly. It is which marketing claims to make and which contractual warranties to extend. The reasonable-person standard governs the negligence surface; foreseeability runs through both tort and contract: the company can foresee that customers will use the output to make adverse employment decisions and that the 14% gap will produce *Griggs* statistics at the customer level. (Source: Ch 6.1, "Tort Law — Negligence"; Ch 7.3, "Contract Remedies")

**Deciding.** The general counsel concludes that the "fairness-tested" claim fails the reasonableness-of-reliance test given the documented gap — the claim has crossed into express warranty and a disclaimer does not cure its centre of gravity. The company applies the six-step ethical decision frame: gather facts, separate facts from assumptions, note concerns across people, laws, and ethical guidelines, construct a solution, evaluate it ethically, then recommend. (Source: Ch 3.1, "Ethical Decision-Making Policies")

The decision: drop "fairness-tested" from all marketing; lead with the "60% time savings" operational claim; document the gap transparently in product documentation for customer HR functions; allocate disparate-impact-analysis responsibility explicitly in the customer ToS, which UCC permits between sophisticated commercial parties. (Source: Ch 8.2, "Sales Warranties"; Ch 12.1, "The FTC and Unfair Trade Practices")

**Outcome.** Three months post-launch, two customers have run their own disparate-impact analysis, found the gap, and adjusted their use of the product. No lawsuits. The CTO commits to a six-month model-retraining initiative with quarterly fairness audits. The lesson captured: software products that mediate legally-consequential decisions inherit the legal envelope of the underlying decision; marketing-claim drafting must be co-authored by product, legal, and engineering from the start.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Treating IP regime as legal-team-only when it materially shapes the build-vs-buy economics.** Copyright (most software protection), patent (algorithmic claims), trade-secret (kept-secret algorithms and process), and trademark (brand) carry distinct rules that change the cost curve of the decision. The source surveys this at Ch 1.3; the practitioner reaches for it before, not after, the commercial case is built.
- **Drafting customer marketing claims as if all puffery is safe.** The reasonableness-of-reliance test divides safe puffery ("the best in the world") from creating-warranty specific claims ("100% accurate"). UCC 8.2 applies; FTC unfair-trade-practice applies independently. Software vendors should treat marketing copy as legal artefact.
- **Treating hiring filters as neutral when *Griggs* disparate-impact analysis would catch them.** Degree requirements, coding-test thresholds, AI-screen pass marks, and referral channels can all produce disparate impact regardless of intent. The defence is manifest job-relatedness or validated business necessity, not "we didn't mean it."
- **Demanding specific performance from a contractor or employee.** Unconstitutional under the Thirteenth Amendment as forced labour. Money damages or substitute performance is the only remedy; the underlying relationship is sometimes salvageable by addressing interests.
- **Invoking forced-arbitration clauses to silence legitimate claims.** The source explicitly frames this as ethics violation, not legal optimisation. Software ToS clauses bear directly on this; long-term reputation cost typically exceeds short-term litigation-avoidance benefit.
- **Treating regulators as adversaries.** Voluntary compliance through consent order produces better long-term outcomes than litigation. Regulators are stakeholders.
- **Communicating an incident in language that crosses from operational acknowledgment into legal liability.** Specific guarantees ("never again"), false endorsements, hidden remedy conditions, and unhedged financial-impact estimates all create exposure beyond the incident itself.
- **Hiding material undisclosed events to manage news cycle.** 8-K disclosure within 4 business days; Schedule 13D within 10 days; insider-trading rules on material nonpublic information. Hiding the event compounds the underlying problem with disclosure liability.
- **Drafting privacy or data-protection statements without engineering verification.** A privacy policy that misrepresents what is collected, retained, or processed is FTC unfair-trade-practice (failing to disclose pertinent information; misrepresenting a product) — independent of any specific privacy regime.
- **Misclassifying employees as contractors to side-step employment-law obligations.** FLSA, FMLA, workers' comp, tax, and Title VII obligations follow actual relationship, not contractual label. Liability accumulates silently until it surfaces.

Practitioners needing IP depth, GDPR compliance depth, or non-US commercial law will find scaffolding here but not doctrine.
