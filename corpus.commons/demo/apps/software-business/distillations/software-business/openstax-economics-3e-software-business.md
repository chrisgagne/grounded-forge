# OpenStax Principles of Economics 3e, Software-Business Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2022). *Principles of Economics 3e*. Rice University. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/principles-economics-3e. Lead author Steven A. Greenlaw; nine contributing authors.

## Software-Business Relevance

*Principles of Economics 3e* supplies the price-the-trade-off vocabulary that turns software questions into business questions, and business questions into decisions the practitioner can actually defend with numbers. A founder pricing a feature, a CTO defending a reliability investment, a PM making a build-vs-buy call against an opaque partner roadmap, an exec briefing a board about an AI-integration externality — each of these sits on a microeconomic foundation the book maps directly: opportunity cost and the production possibilities frontier (Ch 2), supply-demand-and-elasticity (Chs 3-5), consumer choice including behavioural anomalies (Ch 6), firm theory with explicit profit-maximisation rules across market structures (Chs 7-10), externalities and public goods (Chs 12-13), information asymmetry and signalling (Ch 16), and the public-choice realism that explains why regulators, boards, and special-interest stakeholders behave as they do (Ch 18).

The book is particularly load-bearing on five software-business pressure points. First, **information asymmetry and the lemons problem** (Ch 16.1): when a buyer cannot tell good software from bad software (or cannot tell a competent vendor from an incompetent one), Akerlof's adverse-selection dynamic predicts the trustworthy will exit the market and signalling mechanisms (warranties, brand, third-party certification) become the structural response. Second, **monopolistic competition** (Ch 10.1): most differentiated software products live here — many firms, free entry, downward-sloping demand for each because of differentiation, long-run zero economic profit, neither productive nor allocative efficiency. Variety and innovation are the offsetting benefits. Third, **marginal analysis with sunk costs as irrelevant** (Ch 2.1, Ch 7.3): the textbook discipline for engineering-investment decisions. Fourth, **negative externalities** (Ch 12.1): the software-product externality frame for compliance, ethics, and AI-extraction questions. Fifth, **behavioural anomalies — loss aversion, mental accounting, fungibility failure** (Ch 6.3): the calibration tools for SaaS subscription pricing, retention design, and upgrade-path framing where customer perception departs systematically from the standard rationality assumption.

This distillation projects the book's microeconomic and behavioural-economic vocabulary onto the six-phase software-business framework.

## Key Concepts for Software-Business

1.  **Opportunity cost as the right cost concept for engineering investment.** The cost of any engineering choice is the value of the next-best foregone alternative, not the cash outlay. The Sky-Marshal example — $3B per year for marshals versus $8B/year in opportunity cost of passenger time — generalises directly to software: a six-month migration's true cost includes the customer features not shipped, not just the migration headcount. (Source: OpenStax, *Principles of Economics 3e*, Ch 2.1)

2.  **Sunk costs are irrelevant; forward marginal analysis is the discipline.** Firms systematically defend failing software products on the basis of prior investment. The textbook position is direct: forget the money and time already gone; evaluate the marginal cost and benefit of current and future options. (Source: Ch 2.1, "Sunk Costs")

3.  **Production possibilities frontier with increasing opportunity cost.** Resources are not equally productive in all uses, so as you scale up one engineering activity (say, reliability investment) the marginal cost rises because resources increasingly pulled in are less well-suited. The PPF is curved, not straight; engineering capacity allocation lives on the same geometry. (Source: Ch 2.2)

4.  **Comparative advantage in build-vs-buy and partner decisions.** A firm should specialise where its opportunity cost is lowest, even if it is absolutely better than the alternative at every activity. The wholesale-distribution-vs-DTC and platform-vs-vendor decisions live here. (Source: Ch 2.2, "The PPF and Comparative Advantage"; Ch 33.1)

5.  **Elasticity and tax incidence as power-asymmetry indicators in pricing decisions.** If customer demand for your software is inelastic, you can pass through cost increases; if it is elastic (substitutes available), the market disciplines you. Tax incidence depends on relative elasticities — the legal target is rarely the economic bearer. (Source: Ch 5.3)

6.  **Behavioural-economics anomalies for SaaS pricing and retention.** Kahneman-Tversky loss aversion: a $1 loss pains 2.25 times more than a $1 gain helps. Mental accounting: people treat the same money differently depending on source (the "training budget" versus the "tooling budget"). Lack of self-control: people pay to constrain their own future choices. Failure of fungibility: the same money has the same value regardless of label. For SaaS, this means cancellation framing dominates upgrade framing, mental accounting predicts willingness to pay across budget categories, and pre-commitment mechanisms (annual prepayment, locked-in seat counts) systematically win against month-to-month even when annualised cost is identical. (Source: Ch 6.3)

7.  **Profit-maximisation rules across market structures applied to software pricing.** P = MC for the perfectly-competitive case (commodity SaaS, infrastructure passthrough); MR = MC for any pricing power (differentiated SaaS, branded platform, network-effect-protected position). Break-even at P = ATC; shutdown at P = AVC. The shape of long-run cost curves and the entry-and-exit dynamics differ by structure. (Source: Ch 7.3, Ch 8.2, Ch 9.2, Ch 10.1)

8.  **Monopolistic competition as the modal market structure for differentiated software.** Many firms, free entry, differentiated products, downward-sloping demand for each firm. Long-run economic profit drives to zero through entry; firms operate above minimum LRAC and at P > MC — neither productively nor allocatively efficient, but the variety and innovation are the offsetting benefits. The strategic implication: defensible differentiation (brand, switching costs, network effects, location, intangible reputation) is the only thing that protects margin in the long run. (Source: Ch 10.1)

9.  **Information asymmetry and the lemons problem in software vendor selection and build-vs-buy.** Akerlof's used-car argument: buyers who cannot tell good cars from bad pay the average price, sellers of good cars exit, the market unwinds. The software analogue is direct — when a buyer cannot tell a competent vendor from an incompetent one (technical credibility opaque, references managed, demos staged), the trustworthy are penalised and adverse selection dominates. Signalling devices reduce asymmetry: warranties, money-back guarantees, occupational licences, reputation, brands, cosigners, collateral. (Source: Ch 16.1)

10.  **Moral hazard and adverse selection in software-as-a-service insurance-like arrangements.** Where a vendor underwrites risk on behalf of a customer (SLA-backed reliability, usage caps, fair-use policies), moral hazard predicts the customer will behave more riskily once protected; adverse selection predicts the riskiest customers will most eagerly buy the protection. Deductibles, copayments, partial coverage, mandates, group coverage, and risk-pool design are the structural responses. (Source: Ch 16.2)

11.  **Negative externalities and the Coase theorem in software-product compliance and AI ethics.** A negative externality is a market exchange that affects a third party. Software products produce externalities — privacy harm to data-subject third parties, environmental cost of model training, market-distortion effects of recommendation systems. With clear property rights and low transaction costs, parties can negotiate efficiently regardless of who has the rights (Coase). With many parties, ambiguous causation, and high transaction costs, regulatory or market-based responses (pollution charges, cap-and-trade, mandatory disclosure) are required. (Source: Ch 12.1, Ch 12.3)

12.  **Public goods, common resources, and free-riding in open-source and shared-infrastructure decisions.** Open-source libraries, shared cloud infrastructure, common security standards, and industry-wide threat-intelligence pools all have public-good or common-pool characteristics. The free-rider problem predicts under-funding through voluntary contribution; the institutional responses are taxation-style mandates, social-pressure mechanisms, or Ostrom-style local self-governance with monitoring and graduated sanctions. (Source: Ch 13.3)

13.  **Public-choice realism for board, regulator, and special-interest stakeholders.** Rational ignorance: voters (and board members, and customers) lack incentive to become well-informed about low-individual-impact decisions. Special-interest groups exploit concentrated benefits and dispersed costs. Regulatory capture: the regulated industry can capture the regulator and turn protection-of-consumers into protection-of-incumbents. For software-business questions involving boards, regulators, or organised stakeholder groups, these mechanisms predict behaviour better than appeals to public-interest motivation. (Source: Ch 11.4, Ch 18)

14.  **Equilibrium analysis and the four-step process for market events.** When analysing how an event affects a software market (a competitor's pricing move, a regulatory change, an exchange-rate shift, an AI-capability release), the discipline is: (1) sketch the demand-supply diagram before the event; (2) decide whether the event affects demand or supply; (3) decide direction of shift; (4) compare new equilibrium to old. The discipline distinguishes movement-along-a-curve from shift-of-the-curve. (Source: Ch 3.3)

## Questions to Ask During Software-Business Work

### Phase 1: Strategic positioning (pricing, market entry, build-vs-buy, AI integration)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply opportunity cost to engineering effort | What feature, customer, or market are we giving up by spending this engineering quarter on this product? Is the cost concept we are using opportunity cost, or are we anchoring on the cash development cost? |
| Apply comparative advantage to build-vs-buy | Is our opportunity cost of building this lower or higher than the vendor's opportunity cost of providing it? Are we conflating "we can build it" (absolute) with "we should build it" (comparative)? |
| Diagnose information asymmetry in vendor selection | Where is the asymmetry — does the vendor know things we cannot verify, or do we know our use-case in ways the vendor cannot anticipate? Which signalling devices (warranties, third-party certification, reference customers we control, reputation, contractual remedies) credibly reduce the asymmetry? |
| Identify the market structure | Is the software product entering a market closer to perfect competition (commodity, undifferentiated, no pricing power), monopolistic competition (differentiated, many firms, free entry, modest pricing power), oligopoly (few firms, strategic interdependence), or monopoly (single firm, barriers to entry)? The applicable strategy and pricing rule depends on this. |
| Surface partner-roadmap externalities | If we buy, what externality from the partner's roadmap decisions will we absorb — feature drift, pricing change, deprecation, integration breakage? Is the partner internalising any of this externality, or are we bearing it without compensation? |

### Phase 2: Product and engineering economics (technical debt, capacity, ROI, reliability)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply marginal analysis to technical-debt remediation | Is the marginal benefit of next-quarter debt servicing greater than the marginal cost (revenue work not done)? If yes, fund it; if no, do not — and revisit the test next quarter rather than committing to a multi-year debt programme. |
| Apply increasing opportunity cost to engineering scale | As we scale up reliability investment (or any single engineering capability), are the resources we are pulling away equally suited to that work? If not, expect rising marginal cost; do not extrapolate early returns linearly. |
| Surface sunk-cost capture in product portfolio | Is a struggling product being kept alive because of cumulative prior investment? Apply the test: would we start this product today, given everything we now know? If no, prior investment is irrelevant; what matters is forward marginal contribution. |
| Apply break-even and shutdown discipline to a struggling product line | At what revenue does this product cover its average total cost (break-even)? At what revenue does it cover variable cost only (shutdown)? Above shutdown, operating at a loss may be correct in the short run if it covers some fixed costs; below shutdown, it should not operate. |
| Surface short-run-vs-long-run distinction | Is this decision a short-run one (some factors fixed — team composition, vendor contracts, tooling stack) or a long-run one (all factors variable — entire org structure on the table)? Decision rules and elasticities differ; conflating the two produces over-commitment to current capabilities. |

### Phase 3: Team and capability building (hiring, structure, levelling, succession)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply derived demand to engineering hiring | Demand for engineering is derived from demand for the firm's products. What is the underlying product demand we are sizing the team against, and is that demand stable, growing, or shrinking? Sizing the team without naming the product-demand assumption produces over- or under-hiring. |
| Apply comparative advantage to contractor / vendor / hybrid-team decisions | Is the contractor's opportunity cost of doing this work lower than ours, in a way that justifies the agency costs of indirection? Where is the genuine comparative-advantage case versus the false absolute-advantage case ("they're cheaper" without considering integration and coordination cost)? |
| Surface labour-market structure (monopsony, bilateral, competitive) | Is the talent market for this role competitive (many employers competing for the talent), monopsonistic (we are one of few employers in this geography or specialty), or bilateral-monopoly (a single highly-specialised candidate with significant bargaining power)? Compensation and retention design differ by structure. |
| Apply opportunity cost to levelling decisions | What is the opportunity cost of keeping a senior engineer in an IC role versus promoting them to management? What is the opportunity cost of promoting an underqualified senior to a manager role versus running with a smaller team? |

### Phase 4: Operations and process (delivery cadence, governance, decision rights, change)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply marginal analysis to delivery-cadence trade-offs | Is the marginal benefit of one more release per quarter greater than the marginal cost (more coordination overhead, more risk of defects, more interruption cost)? Cadence is a marginal decision, not an all-or-nothing one. |
| Apply equilibrium analysis to a process change | What is the equilibrium response to a new process — what will the affected parties (engineers, PMs, stakeholders) actually do, given their incentives? Apply the four-step process: sketch the before-state, identify demand/supply changes, predict the shift, compare equilibria. |
| Surface elasticity in capacity planning | If we add 20% capacity, how responsive is throughput — elastic (throughput rises more than 20%) or inelastic (throughput rises by less, often because the constraint is not capacity)? Inelastic throughput response means the constraint is elsewhere (queue management, gating, dependencies). |
| Apply public-choice realism to internal governance | Are decision rights being captured by an internal group with concentrated interests against the broader org? Decision-rights ambiguity is a regulatory-capture analogue inside the firm. |

### Phase 5: Risk, reliability, compliance (incidents, security, legal, regulatory, ethics)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply externality analysis to software product compliance | Whose costs (data-subject third parties, communities, the environment) are being externalised by this product? Are the third parties at the table, or are we treating their costs as zero? |
| Apply the Coase theorem to externality remediation | Are property rights clearly assigned (who has the right to the data, to the model output, to the platform behaviour)? Are transaction costs low enough to permit negotiation, or are regulatory or market-based interventions (charges, permits, mandatory disclosure) needed? |
| Apply moral-hazard analysis to SLA and warranty design | Will customers behave more riskily once the SLA is in place? Will high-risk customers preferentially purchase the warranty (adverse selection)? Deductibles, copayments, and partial coverage are the standard mitigations. |
| Apply incidence analysis to compliance-cost allocation | The party legally targeted by a compliance cost is rarely the economic bearer. Where will the burden actually land — on the firm, on customers (via price), on suppliers (via input cost), on employees (via wage)? |
| Apply public-choice realism to regulator engagement | Is the regulator at risk of capture by the larger industry? If we engage, are we contributing to capture, mitigating it, or simply being captured ourselves? |

### Phase 6: Stakeholder communication (board, exec, investor, customer messaging)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply loss-aversion framing to customer-pricing perception | Are we framing a pricing change as a loss (price increase) or a gain (added value at marginally higher cost)? Loss-framing produces 2.25x the perceived weight; consider whether the framing is structurally honest or manipulative, then choose with full knowledge of the asymmetry. |
| Apply mental accounting to SaaS upgrade-path framing | Are we asking the customer to spend from a budget category where the spend feels constrained (the "tooling budget" with a quarterly cap) when the value would justify spend from a different category (the "growth budget" with elastic limit)? Re-categorising the spend in the customer's framing changes willingness-to-pay. |
| Apply fungibility-failure to revenue accounting | The board and the investor read different fictional categories — recurring revenue, services revenue, expansion revenue, gross-of-discount revenue, net-of-refund revenue — as if they were different kinds of money. They are the same money. Surface the fungibility failure rather than exploit it. |
| Apply public-choice realism to board behaviour | Board members are rationally ignorant of detail and motivated by reputational and fiduciary concerns. A board memo addressed to fully-engaged technical readers will fail; a board memo that names the decision, the trade-off, and the owner will land. |
| Apply elasticity-driven incidence to customer-pricing announcements | If we raise price, who actually bears the cost — customers who cannot easily switch (inelastic demand: enterprise long-contract customers), customers who can substitute (elastic demand: SMB month-to-month), or competitors (if the move pulls volume to them)? The legal price change is one thing; the actual incidence depends on elasticities. |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: A SaaS pricing decision is being made without elasticity analysis.** Signal: discussion centres on cost-plus or competitor-match; nobody is checking how responsive customer demand actually is. Diagnosis: elasticity has not been examined; incidence is being asserted, not analysed. Follow-up: estimate price elasticity by customer segment (enterprise vs SMB vs self-serve); ask who can substitute, who can exit, who is locked in by contract or integration. Use the elasticity to predict where the burden lands.

- **Pattern: A build-vs-buy decision is being defended on absolute capability rather than comparative advantage.** Signal: "we can build it" or "we already have the skills" without comparing opportunity costs. Diagnosis: confusion of absolute with comparative advantage. Follow-up: compute the opportunity cost (what customer features or markets are deferred by the build), compare to the vendor cost; only build when comparative advantage favours it.

- **Pattern: A failing software product is being defended on sunk-cost grounds.** Signal: "we've already spent X years and Y million on this"; "we can't kill it now after all that effort." Diagnosis: sunk-cost capture. Follow-up: apply the test — would we start this product today, given what we now know? Treat the prior investment as gone; evaluate the marginal forward case freshly.

- **Pattern: A vendor selection is failing despite due diligence.** Signal: chosen vendor underperforms references; signals turn out to be staged; technical fit was overstated. Diagnosis: lemons problem — the asymmetry was not credibly closed. Follow-up: re-examine which signalling devices were actually load-bearing (independent third-party certification, reference customers under our control, escrow / collateral arrangements, contractual remedies with teeth) versus which were performance theatre (managed demos, vendor-selected references).

- **Pattern: An SLA or fair-use policy is producing perverse customer behaviour.** Signal: usage spikes once the policy is in place; the riskiest customers preferentially adopt the tier; the protection is bleeding margin. Diagnosis: moral hazard plus adverse selection. Follow-up: introduce deductibles, partial coverage, or risk-pool design; align customer incentives so the protected party retains skin in the game.

- **Pattern: A compliance investment is being evaluated on intentions rather than incidence.** Signal: framing is "we will reduce harm" or "we will improve trust"; nobody is asking where the cost will actually land. Diagnosis: missing incidence analysis. Follow-up: trace the incidence by elasticity — will the cost be absorbed by the firm (inelastic supply), passed to customers (inelastic demand), absorbed by suppliers (inelastic input supply), or borne by employees (inelastic labour supply)? The legal target is rarely the bearer.

- **Pattern: An AI-integration externality is being treated as zero.** Signal: training data sources, model outputs affecting third parties, recommendation-system downstream effects are absent from the deliberation. Diagnosis: externalities not internalised. Follow-up: surface the third parties explicitly; consider Coasean (property-rights) or regulatory (charges, mandatory disclosure, cap) responses; do not treat the externality as a cost the firm escapes by not naming it.

- **Pattern: A roadmap-versus-capacity mismatch is being addressed by adding capacity.** Signal: the response to roadmap overload is "hire more"; nobody is asking whether the constraint is capacity or something else. Diagnosis: elasticity of throughput with respect to capacity is being assumed without check. Follow-up: identify the actual constraint (queue management, gating, dependencies, decision latency); test by adding small capacity increments and measuring throughput response.

- **Pattern: A SaaS retention initiative is fighting loss aversion without naming it.** Signal: cancellation framing dominates upgrade framing; the team is surprised that customers value avoiding the loss more than they value the equivalent gain. Diagnosis: loss aversion not surfaced (2.25x asymmetry). Follow-up: redesign the choice architecture so retention plays into the asymmetry (frame the cancellation as the loss the customer is about to incur) rather than against it.

- **Pattern: An open-source contribution or shared-infrastructure decision is being treated as a voluntary-funding problem.** Signal: appeals to community goodwill; surprise at under-funding; resentment of the few who carry the cost. Diagnosis: public-good underprovision through voluntary contribution; free-rider problem. Follow-up: shift to mandate-style funding (mandatory contribution as a condition of participation), commercial licence-tier sponsorship, advertising-supported provision, or local-self-governance with enforceable membership.

## When to Use This Reference

- A pricing, packaging, or upgrade-path decision needs elasticity and incidence analysis.
- A build-vs-buy, vendor-selection, or platform-choice decision turns on information asymmetry, comparative advantage, or partner-roadmap externality.
- An engineering-investment, technical-debt, or reliability decision needs marginal-analysis discipline against sunk-cost capture.
- A market-structure or competitive-positioning question requires diagnosis (perfect competition, monopolistic competition, oligopoly, monopoly) before strategy.
- A compliance, ethics, or AI-externality question needs the externality / Coase / property-rights frame to make the third-party costs visible.
- A customer-pricing-perception, retention, or upgrade-path decision needs behavioural-economics calibration (loss aversion, mental accounting, fungibility).
- A board, regulator, or special-interest stakeholder behaviour is to be predicted using public-choice realism rather than public-interest assumption.
- An open-source, shared-infrastructure, or industry-collaboration question needs the public-goods / common-pool framing to predict why voluntary contribution will under-fund.

## Worked Example

A 200-engineer enterprise SaaS company is deciding whether to build a usage-analytics module in-house or buy from a specialist vendor. The company has the engineering skills to build; the vendor has a more mature product but charges $480K/year and locks the customer to a multi-year contract with an opaque pricing-renewal mechanism. The board is split. Two engineering directors favour the build ("we have the skills, we'll own the IP, no vendor lock-in"); the CFO and one PM favour the buy ("it's cheaper than diverting an eight-engineer team for two years"). The CTO has been asked for a recommendation in two weeks.

**Phase 1 (strategic positioning).** The relevant cost concept is opportunity cost, not the cash development cost. A two-year, eight-engineer in-house build would cost roughly the same cash as the vendor's three-year fully-loaded total, but the binding cost is what those eight engineers are not doing — the customer features deferred, the markets not entered, the platform debt not serviced. The source is direct: "the lesson of sunk costs is to forget about the money and time that is irretrievably gone and instead to focus on the marginal costs and benefits of current and future options." (Source: OpenStax, *Principles of Economics 3e*, Ch 2.1, "Sunk Costs") The comparative-advantage test: the vendor's opportunity cost of providing analytics is low (they specialise; this is their product); the firm's opportunity cost of building analytics is high (their engineers are best suited to the core product). Comparative advantage favours the buy even though the firm has the absolute capability to build — because "a country can produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than another country" is the criterion, not absolute ability. (Source: Ch 2.2, "The PPF and Comparative Advantage")

Information asymmetry is the next question. The vendor's roadmap and renewal-pricing mechanism are opaque; the firm cannot tell a competent committed vendor from a vendor about to be acquired. Akerlof's adverse-selection dynamic predicts that when buyers cannot tell good from bad, the trustworthy exit and "the market unwinds"; signalling devices are the structural response. (Source: Ch 16.1, "The Problem of Imperfect Information and Adverse Selection") Devices to require: third-party certification, reference customers the firm controls (not vendor-selected), source-code escrow, contractual remedies with teeth.

**Phase 2 (engineering economics).** If the firm builds, the marginal-analysis test applies each quarter: is the marginal benefit of one more quarter of analytics-build investment greater than the marginal cost of customer features deferred? The law of increasing opportunity cost predicts that "as production of a good or service increases, the marginal opportunity cost of producing it increases as well" because resources pulled in are progressively less suited. (Source: Ch 2.2, "The Production Possibilities Frontier and Social Choices") If the firm buys, sunk-cost capture is the year-three risk — the temptation to renew on bad terms because of prior spend. The guard is an explicit forward-marginal review at month 30.

**Phase 5 (risk, reliability, compliance).** The vendor option introduces a partner-roadmap externality — the firm bears the cost of any vendor pricing change, deprecation, or strategic pivot. The Coase theorem frames the fix: with "clear property rights and low transaction costs, parties can negotiate efficiently." (Source: Ch 12.1, "Externalities") The contract is the internalisation mechanism; the firm should price the externality into the negotiation: not "what is the vendor's price?" but "what is the price plus the value of the externality protections?"

**Phase 6 (stakeholder communication).** The CTO uses loss-aversion framing carefully — the loss of optionality (locking in a vendor) versus the loss of focus (engineers building analytics instead of product). Kahneman and Tversky documented that "a $1 loss pains us 2.25 times more than a $1 gain helps us"; naming both losses explicitly avoids asymmetric framing that skews the board's perception. (Source: Ch 6.3, "Behavioral Economics: An Alternative Framework for Consumer Choice") The CTO also names the fungibility failure: the board reads the $480K/year from the vendor budget as different money from the eight-engineer-team allocation from the engineering budget. They are the same money. (Source: Ch 6.3, "Behavioral Economics: An Alternative Framework for Consumer Choice")

**Recommendation.** Buy, with contract terms designed to internalise the partner-roadmap externality (escrow, named references, third-party certification, exit assistance); set an explicit forward-marginal review at month 30 to guard against sunk-cost capture; redirect the displaced engineering capacity to the core product where the firm's comparative advantage is.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Cash-only development cost framing.** Counting only the headcount cost of an in-house build misses the opportunity cost of features not shipped; produces systematic over-building of capabilities the firm has no comparative advantage in.
- **Absolute-advantage build-vs-buy reasoning.** "We can build it" or "we have the skills" without comparing opportunity costs leads to build decisions that maximise capability and minimise focus.
- **Sunk-cost capture in product portfolio decisions.** Defending failing products on the basis of cumulative prior investment, or defending vendor renewals on the basis of cumulative prior spend.
- **Ignoring lemons-problem dynamics in vendor selection.** Treating vendor due diligence as adequate when the signalling devices are vendor-selected (managed demos, vendor references) rather than firm-controlled.
- **Treating SLAs and warranties as commitment without moral-hazard / adverse-selection design.** Predictable cost overruns follow when protected customers behave more riskily and high-risk customers preferentially buy the protection.
- **Treating compliance and AI externalities as zero-cost.** The cost of externalities does not disappear by not naming them; it lands on third parties (and eventually on the firm through regulation, litigation, or reputation).
- **Voluntary-contribution framing for open-source and shared-infrastructure decisions.** Predictable under-funding follows; the structural fix is mandate, commercial sponsorship tier, or local-governance with enforceable membership.
- **Public-interest framing of board and regulator behaviour.** Treating boards and regulators as fully-engaged public-interest actors rather than as rationally-ignorant rational-economic actors produces predictably surprised outcomes.
- **Loss-aversion blindness in customer pricing.** Symmetrically-priced gains and losses do not feel symmetric to customers; design choices that ignore the 2.25x asymmetry leak value persistently.
- **Mental-accounting blindness in SaaS upgrade-path design.** Asking customers to spend from a constrained budget category when the value justifies spend from an elastic category produces avoidable willingness-to-pay constraints.
