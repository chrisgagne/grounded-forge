# OpenStax Introduction to Business, Software-Business Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2018). *Introduction to Business*. Rice University. CC BY 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/introduction-business. Lead author Lawrence J. Gitman; six senior contributing authors.

## Software-Business Relevance

OpenStax's *Introduction to Business* is the broad-coverage primer of the corpus and the only OpenStax title under CC BY 4.0 (the remaining eleven are CC BY-NC-SA). For a founder, CTO, engineering manager, or technical product leader, the book supplies the survey-textbook scaffolding that translates a software-business problem into the named subdomains a board, an auditor, or a regulator already speaks: the seven-environment forces wheel for strategic positioning (Ch 1.2), Carroll's four-component CSR pyramid for ethics decisions affecting users (Ch 2.3), six entry modes ordered by risk and commitment for international product launches (Ch 3.6), the make-or-buy criteria and computerised resource-planning tools (MRP, MRP II, ERP) for system-selection decisions (Ch 10.4), Gantt / CPM / PERT for delivery scheduling (Ch 10.5), the post-Enron Sarbanes-Oxley regime that governs how publicly traded software companies certify financials and disclose internal controls (Ch 14.2), and the legal-and-tax environment for contracts, intellectual property, product liability, and antitrust (Appendix A).

The text sits at *breadth*, not depth. Where Letaw's handbook will carry process discipline and Jones's empirical findings will carry the post-1980 evidence collapse, *Introduction to Business* contributes the cross-functional vocabulary: the integration points between operations, accounting, marketing, finance, HR, and IT that a CTO must speak to a board that does not read code, and that a founder must navigate when an early-stage company first encounters consumer-protection law, securities regulation, or product liability. The IT chapter (Ch 13) is the corpus's only treatment of MIS / ERP / VPN / ASP / e-commerce as line items in a business survey; the operations chapter (Ch 10) is the corpus's only structured treatment of the make-or-buy decision as a procurement primitive that generalises to in-house-vs-vendor software decisions.

## Key Concepts for Software-Business

1.  **Seven environmental forces wheel.** External environment decomposes into seven sub-environments — economic, political-and-legal, demographic, social, competitive, global, technological. Managers control internal operations (supplies, employees, products, locations) but external conditions are "generally beyond the control of management and change constantly", and "managers are primarily adapters to, rather than agents of, change". For a software business, the strategic-positioning scan runs across all seven, not just the technological. (Source: OpenStax, *Introduction to Business*, Ch 1.2)

2.  **Carroll's four-component CSR pyramid.** Economic responsibility (the foundation — without profit, the others don't matter), legal responsibility (obey the law), ethical responsibility (do what is right, just, and fair), philanthropic responsibility (be a good corporate citizen). Business activity sorts into three categories: illegal-and-irresponsible; irresponsible-but-legal; and legal-and-responsible. For a software company, the irresponsible-but-legal category is where most data-handling, AI-deployment, and dark-pattern decisions sit. (Source: Ch 2.3)

3.  **Four stakeholder groups.** Employees, customers, society (general public, environment, communities), investors. Each group has distinct engagement modalities and indicators of trust. For software-business work, the four-group frame is the minimum-viable stakeholder map for any board paper or incident write-up. (Source: Ch 2.4)

4.  **Six entry modes for global trade, ordered by risk and commitment.** Exporting (least risky), licensing, franchising, contract manufacturing, joint ventures, direct foreign investment (greatest reward and risk). For a SaaS product entering a non-domestic market, these map onto reseller agreements, white-label / OEM partnerships, regional joint ventures, and full local-entity operation. (Source: Ch 3.6)

5.  **Make-or-buy decision criteria.** Quantity needed, secrecy of design, supplier reliability and quality. Low quantity and reliable suppliers favour buy; high secrecy and unreliable suppliers favour make. The frame generalises directly to in-house-vs-vendor decisions on software components, infrastructure, and operational tooling. (Source: Ch 10.4)

6.  **Computerised resource-planning hierarchy.** *MRP* (materials requirement planning) ensures right materials/labor/equipment at right times from a master schedule; *MRP II* extends to integrate finance, marketing, accounting, engineering, and manufacturing; *ERP* (enterprise resource planning) further incorporates suppliers and customers. For an internal-tooling or buy-vs-build decision, the hierarchy names the integration depth the system selection is committing to. (Source: Ch 10.4)

7.  **Gantt charts, CPM, PERT for scheduling under uncertainty.** *Gantt charts* show scheduled vs actual production as bars. *Critical Path Method (CPM)* identifies the longest chain through linked activities — tasks on this path must finish on time or the project slips. *PERT* extends CPM by assigning three time estimates per activity (optimistic, most probable, pessimistic) to anticipate delays. For a software release with a commercial commitment, the CPM-vs-PERT choice turns on whether the activity-time variance is large enough to warrant the three-estimate discipline. (Source: Ch 10.5)

8.  **Lean manufacturing and just-in-time vulnerability.** *Lean manufacturing* eliminates non-value-added steps. *Just-in-time (JIT)* materials arrive exactly when needed; relies on close vendor coordination; vulnerable to supply disruptions. The text's vulnerability examples are physical (9/11, Hurricane Maria/Harvey); for software, JIT-equivalents include zero-buffer dependency-injection pipelines, single-vendor cloud bills, and contractor-only critical-path roles. (Source: Ch 10.6)

9.  **Total Quality Management and Six Sigma.** TQM (Deming): quality as company-wide goal; *continuous improvement* — "a commitment to constantly seek better ways of doing things" [V] (Source: OpenStax, *Introduction to Business*, Ch 10.6, "Looking for a Better Way: Improving Production and Operations"). *Six Sigma*: no more than 3.4 defects per million; uses *DMAIC* (Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control). Both frame quality investment as a managed long-term program rather than an episodic engineering virtue.

10.  **Risk-return trade-off as universal financial principle.** "The higher the risk, the greater the return that is required". The financial manager balances *return* (opportunity for profit) and *risk* (chance of loss). The principle extends beyond financial management to any software-business decision where loss probability and gain magnitude can be characterised — feature investment, reliability spend, security hardening, technical-debt remediation. (Source: Ch 16.1)

11.  **Four ratio categories for financial-health reading.** *Liquidity* (current ratio, acid-test, working capital), *profitability* (net profit margin, return on equity, EPS), *activity* (inventory turnover), *debt* (debt-to-equity). Industry-specific norms matter — a grocery store may have <1% net margin while a jewelry store exceeds 10%. For a SaaS company's board paper, the equivalent industry-specific norms are gross margin, rule-of-40, CAC payback, NRR — but the *category structure* (liquidity / profitability / activity / debt) is the discipline. (Source: Ch 14.7)

12.  **Sarbanes-Oxley and the post-Enron audit regime.** SOX (2002) redefined the auditor-corporation relationship. The source defines auditing as "the process of reviewing the records used to prepare financial statements and issuing a formal auditor's opinion indicating whether the statements have been prepared in accordance with accepted accounting rules" [V] (Source: Ch 14.2, "Public Accountants"). SOX placed accountability on CEOs and CFOs (financial-statement certification with criminal penalties); required internal-control procedures with annual report on implementation; restricted non-auditing consulting work for client; established the PCAOB. Auditing work papers retained for seven years. For a publicly traded software company, SOX governs the financial-statement and internal-control disclosure architecture that engineering organisations must support.

13.  **Sources of law and the UCC.** Four primary sources: common law (judicial-decision-derived), statutory law (legislatures — main source), administrative law (federal/state/local agencies), business law (commercial dealings; *Uniform Commercial Code (UCC)* standardises laws across 49 states). For software contracts, the UCC and contract-law primitives (mutual assent, capacity, consideration, legal purpose, legal form) frame what a master services agreement and an end-user licence agreement must satisfy. (Source: App A, "Contract Law")

14.  **Intellectual property regimes.** *Patent* (20 years exclusive right for new, unique, useful invention); *copyright* (life of creator plus 70 years for printed materials, art, photographs, movies); *trademark* (design/name/distinctive mark); *servicemark* (symbol/name/design identifying a service). For a software-business, the copyright regime governs source code by default; the patent regime governs software-implemented inventions (with jurisdictional variation); the trademark regime governs product naming. (Source: App A, "Patents, Copyrights, and Trademarks")

15.  **Strict product liability.** Makes manufacturer/seller liable for defective products even if all possible care was taken. The text frames product liability through the asbestos-lawsuit total cost (>$70 billion). For software, strict-liability exposure rises in regulated domains (medical, financial, transport) and is a load-bearing consideration for any product-launch decision touching consumer safety. (Source: App A, "Product-Liability Law")

16.  **Five-step decision-making process for non-programmed decisions.** Recognise / define the problem or opportunity; gather information to identify alternative solutions; select one or more alternatives after evaluating strengths and weaknesses; put the chosen alternative into action; gather feedback on effectiveness. The text warns: "It can be easy (and dangerous) for managers to get stuck at any stage" — citing analysis paralysis. For software-business decisions where the technical and commercial constrain each other, the discipline of staging the process explicitly is what stops the technical depth swallowing the commercial timing. (Source: Ch 6.6)

17.  **Six promotion goals and the integrated marketing communications (IMC) discipline.** Promotion is "an attempt by marketers to inform, persuade, or remind consumers and B2B users to influence their opinion or elicit a response" [V] (Source: Ch 12.5, "Promotion Strategy"). Six promotion goals: creating awareness, getting consumers to try, providing information, retaining loyal customers, increasing use frequency, identifying target customers, teaching the customer. *IMC* coordinates traditional advertising, personal selling, sales promotion, public relations, social media, e-commerce so the customer receives a consistent unified message across channels. For a software-business launch, the IMC discipline names the multi-channel coordination cost that engineering-only thinking under-prices.

## Questions to Ask During Software-Business Work

### Phase 1: Strategic positioning

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Scan all seven environmental forces | Have I scanned all seven environments (economic, political-legal, demographic, social, competitive, global, technological), or have I treated this as a technological-and-competitive question only? |
| Position the decision on the CSR pyramid | Is this an economic decision (foundation), a legal-compliance decision, an ethical decision, or a philanthropic decision? Where on Carroll's pyramid does the choice land — and have the lower levels been honoured before pursuing the higher? |
| Test the market-structure assumption | What is the competitive structure (perfect, monopolistic, oligopoly, monopoly) in the target market? Pricing power and entry barriers differ qualitatively across the four. |
| Choose the entry-mode for cross-border launches | Of the six modes (exporting, licensing, franchising, contract manufacturing, joint venture, direct foreign investment), which matches the risk appetite, capital available, partner-control needs, and regulatory environment? |
| Diagnose whether the decision is strategic, tactical, operational, or contingency | What is the time horizon (1-5 years; less than 1 year; current; alternative for crisis)? The four planning types govern who decides, with what specificity, and under what level of uncertainty. |

### Phase 2: Product and engineering economics

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply the make-or-buy criteria to a system or component choice | What is the quantity (low quantity favours buy)? How critical is design secrecy (high secrecy favours make)? How reliable is the supplier (uncertain reliability favours make)? Is this competitive-advantage-worthy in-house? |
| Choose the resource-planning depth | Does the decision call for materials-level coordination (MRP), functional integration (MRP II), or supplier-and-customer integration (ERP)? The hierarchy names the integration cost the system selection commits to. |
| Use CPM or PERT for schedule risk | For a release with a commercial commitment, is the activity-time variance large enough to warrant PERT's three-estimate discipline, or is CPM's single-estimate critical path sufficient? |
| Stress-test JIT-equivalent dependencies | Where does the delivery system have zero-buffer dependencies (single vendor, single contractor, single cloud region, single key engineer)? Lean is not free — JIT's vulnerability surface must be priced. |
| Apply the risk-return trade-off to engineering investment | What is the magnitude of the return (revenue, retention, reliability improvement)? What is the magnitude of the risk (technical debt accreted, opportunity cost, vendor lock-in)? Does the return justify the risk given the firm's risk appetite? |

### Phase 3: Team and capability building

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Choose departmentalization | Is the engineering org best served by functional, product, process, customer, or geographic departmentalization? Most large organisations combine; the choice shapes coordination cost and customer responsiveness. |
| Choose mechanistic vs organic structure | Does the strategy demand innovation (favours organic — low specialisation, wide spans, decentralised decision-making) or cost-control (favours mechanistic — high specialisation, narrow spans, centralised)? |
| Apply leadership-style situational selection | Given the maturity and competency of those completing the task, does the situation call for autocratic, participative (democratic, consensual, consultative), or free-rein leadership? The text notes the trend is *away* from directive autocratic styles. |
| Test the recruitment-and-selection process | Does the six-step selection process (initial screen, employment testing, in-depth interview, background/reference check, physical/drug testing, decision) match the role's criticality and risk? |
| Diagnose the motivation gap | Are workers underperforming because hygiene factors (policy, supervision, working conditions) are absent (Herzberg)? Because effort-to-performance, performance-to-outcome, or outcomes-to-needs links are broken (expectancy)? Because outcome-input ratio comparisons are unfavourable (equity)? Because goals are vague or unaccepted (goal-setting)? |

### Phase 4: Operations and process

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Choose the production-process pattern | Is the work mass production (low cost, uniform output), mass customization (mass techniques to a point, then per-order features), or full customization (one-at-a-time)? The choice shapes the operations stack — from factory floor to managed-service onboarding. |
| Choose the system-of-record stack | Where on the MRP / MRP II / ERP spectrum does the operational ambition sit? Each step up integrates more functions and raises switching cost. |
| Apply TQM / Six Sigma to quality program design | Is quality being treated as a company-wide goal (TQM, continuous improvement) or as an episodic engineering virtue? Does the program have a DMAIC structure (Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control), or is it ad hoc? |
| Use CPM critical-path identification | Where is the critical path in the current delivery? Have tasks on it been protected with buffer, while tasks off it are left to absorb their own variance? |
| Choose the IT integration model | Is the IT chapter's stack (MIS, ERP, VPN, ASP, e-commerce) being built in-house, bought as SaaS, or hybrid? The selection criteria are the make-or-buy primitives applied to information systems. |

### Phase 5: Risk, reliability, compliance

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply the four CSR levels to compliance decisions | Has the decision been tested against all four levels (economic, legal, ethical, philanthropic), or has it been collapsed to "is this legal"? The collapse is the most common ethics failure mode. |
| Apply the two ethical-decision tests | Does the decision pass the *feelings test* (after deciding, does discomfort persist?) and the *newspaper / social-media test* (how would the front page describe it)? |
| Test the product-liability surface | Is this a product whose defect could cause physical or financial harm to users? *Strict liability* applies even when all possible care was taken — for software in regulated domains (medical, financial, transport), the exposure is substantial. |
| Test the IP-protection posture | Is the work protected by patent (20-year invention right), copyright (life-plus-70 for printed/digital work), trademark (brand identifier), or servicemark? Are licence agreements aligned with these regimes? |
| Test the SOX / financial-control posture | For a publicly traded entity, do the internal controls support CEO/CFO certification with criminal penalties for misstatement? Are auditing work papers retained for the required seven years? |

### Phase 6: Stakeholder communication

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Map the four stakeholder groups | For the deliverable in front of you (board memo, customer-facing notice, employee broadcast, investor disclosure), which of the four groups (employees, customers, society, investors) is the primary audience? Which are secondary readers whose interests must still be honoured? |
| Identify the power-base mix | Which power bases (legitimate, reward, coercive, expert, referent) are in play? The mix shapes which communication register the audience expects. |
| Apply the leadership-styles continuum | Does the communication match the leadership style appropriate to the situation? Autocratic in crisis with clear authority; participative when the team is mature; free-rein when expectations are clear and feedback channels work. |
| Use the IMC discipline | Across the channels in play (announcements, web copy, support documentation, partner notification, regulator filings), is the message unified? Inconsistent multi-channel messaging is the standard stakeholder-confusion source. |
| Test the financial-disclosure shape | For investor communication, are the four ratio categories (liquidity, profitability, activity, debt) represented or have they been collapsed into a single hero metric? |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: A "tech vs business" framing collapses the seven environments into two.** Signal: a strategy memo names competitive and technological forces but omits political-legal, social, or demographic shifts. Diagnosis: the seven-environment scan has been compressed; the four omitted forces are where the next surprise will come from. Follow-up: complete the scan explicitly and name what each environment is doing.
- **Pattern: Ethics framed only as legal compliance.** Signal: discussion turns on "is this legal" but not on "is this ethical or philanthropic". Diagnosis: Carroll's pyramid has been collapsed to its second level. Follow-up: separate the four levels explicitly; apply the feelings test and the newspaper test.
- **Pattern: Make-or-buy decision skipped on a multi-year commitment.** Signal: in-house build chosen without comparison to vendor offerings, or vendor chosen without comparison to in-house capability. Diagnosis: the four make-or-buy criteria (quantity, secrecy, reliability, competitive-advantage) have not been applied. Follow-up: score the four criteria explicitly; surface the trade-offs the analysis exposes.
- **Pattern: ERP / MRP-II decision made without integration-depth discussion.** Signal: a system selection happens on feature-fit and price; the integration scope (operations only? plus finance? plus suppliers / customers?) is unspecified. Diagnosis: the resource-planning hierarchy has been treated as a single product category. Follow-up: name the integration scope explicitly; price the switching cost at each level.
- **Pattern: JIT-equivalent dependencies treated as efficiencies.** Signal: zero-buffer single-vendor dependencies celebrated as cost-discipline. Diagnosis: the JIT vulnerability surface is unpriced. Follow-up: name the single points of failure; price the disruption scenario.
- **Pattern: Critical-path tasks unprotected; non-critical tasks buffered.** Signal: schedule reviews focus on visible-but-non-critical work while critical-path activities slip. Diagnosis: CPM not in use, or used as project-management theatre. Follow-up: identify the critical path explicitly; reallocate buffer to it.
- **Pattern: Financial reports collapse to a hero metric.** Signal: a board paper presents one ratio (gross margin, revenue, NRR) without the four-category context. Diagnosis: ratio analysis has been used selectively to support a conclusion rather than diagnostically. Follow-up: present the four categories (liquidity, profitability, activity, debt) with industry-specific norms.
- **Pattern: SOX controls treated as accounting team's problem.** Signal: engineering changes that affect financial controls (revenue recognition, billing logic, audit-trail integrity) made without involving finance or audit. Diagnosis: SOX has been organisationally siloed. Follow-up: route financially material engineering changes through the internal-control review.
- **Pattern: Product-liability surface unexamined on regulated-domain launch.** Signal: a launch into medical, financial, or transport domains lacks a strict-liability analysis. Diagnosis: the regulated-domain product-liability exposure has been treated as a general-counsel afterthought. Follow-up: surface the strict-liability test before launch commitment.
- **Pattern: Multi-channel communication landing inconsistently.** Signal: support docs, sales messaging, board memo, and regulator filing tell subtly different stories. Diagnosis: IMC discipline absent. Follow-up: anchor on one stakeholder-specific message per group and audit channels for consistency.

## When to Use This Reference

- A founder or CTO is preparing a strategic-positioning analysis and needs the seven-environment vocabulary that translates a software question into board-readable terms.
- A buy-vs-build decision on infrastructure, internal tooling, or product components needs the make-or-buy criteria applied as a structured frame rather than a vendor-spreadsheet exercise.
- A delivery schedule with a commercial commitment needs CPM or PERT discipline applied to the critical path.
- A publicly traded (or pre-IPO) software company needs the SOX / GAAP / ratio-analysis vocabulary to align engineering controls with financial-disclosure obligations.
- A product launch into a regulated domain (medical, financial, transport) needs the strict-liability and consumer-protection framing.
- A multi-channel launch needs IMC coordination across customer communication, partner notification, internal announcement, and (where applicable) regulator filing.
- A team-and-capability decision (departmentalization, mechanistic-vs-organic, leadership-style selection) needs the structural vocabulary the rest of the corpus assumes.
- An ethics decision needs Carroll's pyramid and the two decision tests (feelings, newspaper) to separate legal from ethical from philanthropic considerations.
- A first-pass orientation across functional areas — operations, accounting, finance, marketing, HR, IT, legal — when the practitioner is unfamiliar with one functional vocabulary and needs the corpus's broadest treatment.

This reference is most useful as the *breadth* anchor. Depth in any single area routes to the function-specific siblings: `principles-management` for strategic-analysis frameworks, `principles-marketing` for marketing-mix depth, `accounting-vol2` for managerial cost discipline, `business-ethics` for ethics theory, `business-law` for legal depth, `entrepreneurship` for startup-stage specifics, `principles-finance` for finance depth.

## Worked Example

A founder of a Series A medical-imaging SaaS company faces a decision: their largest hospital customer has asked for an on-premise deployment of the inference engine to satisfy a regional data-residency rule. The current product runs cloud-only. Engineering estimates six months to retrofit; the customer represents 22% of ARR; signing the contract requires committing to a 3-year on-premise SLA.

**Phase 1 — strategic positioning.** The founder applies the seven-environment scan: economic, political-and-legal, demographic, social, competitive, global, and technological. Political-and-legal (the data-residency rule) and competitive (two competitors already offer on-premise) are obvious. The scan surfaces three less obvious: demographic (hospital IT staffing trends suggest on-premise support burden will rise), technological (containerised inference makes on-premise newly feasible), and global (the data-residency rule is a leading indicator for three other jurisdictions). The source's argument is that "external environmental conditions are generally beyond the control of management and change constantly"; managers are "primarily adapters to, rather than agents of, change." The scan reframes the decision as a product-strategy decision, not a customer-accommodation decision. (Source: OpenStax, *Introduction to Business*, Ch 1.2, "Understanding the Business Environment")

**Phase 2 — product economics.** The founder runs the make-or-buy criteria: quantity (if this is one customer, buy; if the first of many, make), secrecy (the inference engine is core IP — high secrecy favours make), supplier reliability (uncertain operational reality favours make), competitive advantage (on-premise is becoming table-stakes in the regulated-domain segment — in-house build is competitive-advantage-worthy). Decision: make. (Source: Ch 10.4, "Make or Buy") Schedule discipline: CPM identifies the critical path through containerisation, installer, customer-data-flow audit, and SLA-compliant monitoring. The team adopts PERT three-estimate discipline because installer scope variance is genuinely large — PERT assigns "three time estimates per activity (optimistic, most probable, pessimistic) to anticipate delays." (Source: Ch 10.5, "Scheduling")

**Phase 3 — team and capability.** The on-premise build forms a small organic team with wide spans and decentralised decision-making, separate from the cloud-only delivery team. Leadership style trends consultative — the work has high uncertainty and benefits from the team's input, but SLA commitments require single-owner accountability. The source catalogues autocratic, participative (democratic, consensual, consultative), and free-rein styles; situational leadership selects among them based on the maturity and competency of those completing the task. (Source: Ch 6, "Management and Leadership in Today's Organizations")

**Phase 4 — operations.** MRP-level coordination is insufficient because finance, support, and customer-success must integrate against the same milestones; the founder commits to MRP-II-equivalent integration across functions. TQM is applied: the on-premise delivery cannot ship with episodic quality; a continuous-improvement programme is structured around DMAIC for support-incident handling. The source frames TQM as "a commitment to constantly seek better ways of doing things" and Six Sigma as "no more than 3.4 defects per million" using the DMAIC cycle. (Source: Ch 10.6, "Quality Management and Lean Manufacturing")

**Phase 5 — risk and compliance.** The product-liability surface widens — strict liability applies even when all possible care was taken; exposure rises in the medical-imaging context. Carroll's pyramid is applied: the legal level is satisfied by the on-premise deployment; the ethical level is tested by asking whether patients whose images flow through the system would consent to the arrangement — the answer surfaces a patient-consent gap that requires a product change. (Source: App A, "Product-Liability Law"; Ch 2.3, "Managing a Socially Responsible Business")

**Phase 6 — stakeholder communication.** The founder prepares four artefacts in IMC discipline — board paper, customer email, internal announcement, investor-update note — with a unified message across channels. IMC "coordinates traditional advertising, personal selling, sales promotion, public relations, social media, e-commerce so the customer receives a consistent unified message across channels." The board paper uses the four-ratio category structure (liquidity, profitability, activity, debt) with industry-specific norms rather than a single hero metric. (Source: Ch 12.5, "Promotion Strategy"; Ch 14.7, "Ratio Analysis")

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Tech-and-competitive-only environmental scan.** Skipping the other five environmental forces (political-legal, demographic, social, global, economic) produces decisions that are surprised by regulation, demographics, or trade dynamics that were visible months in advance.
- **Ethics-as-compliance.** Collapsing Carroll's pyramid to its second level (legal responsibility) leaves the ethical and philanthropic levels unaddressed and produces decisions that are technically legal but stakeholder-damaging.
- **Make-or-buy by default rather than by criteria.** Defaulting to in-house build (or to vendor purchase) without applying the four criteria produces under-priced commitments either way.
- **Single-channel launch communication.** Skipping IMC discipline produces stakeholder confusion when partners, customers, employees, and regulators hear subtly different versions of the same event.
- **JIT celebrated as efficiency.** Treating zero-buffer single-vendor dependencies as cost-discipline ignores the JIT-vulnerability tradition documented across the operations chapter; the disruption cost is real and must be priced.
- **One-ratio financial reporting.** Presenting a single hero metric (revenue, gross margin, NRR) without the four-category context permits selective storytelling and obscures the financial-health pattern.
- **SOX as accounting silo.** Treating internal-control requirements as the finance team's problem produces engineering changes that quietly break financial controls and surface during audit as material weaknesses.
- **Regulated-domain launch without strict-liability analysis.** Treating product-liability as a general-counsel afterthought on a regulated-domain launch under-prices the strict-liability exposure that applies even when all possible care was taken.
- **Free-rein leadership applied where expectations are unclear.** Free-rein style without clear expectations and feedback channels produces frustration rather than autonomy — a common failure mode when scaling early-stage teams that operated informally.
- **Matrix-as-default for cross-functional work.** Matrix structure's two-supervisor design routinely produces accountability ambiguity. The text's Unilever-moved-back-to-product example warns against matrix as a default for ambiguous-priority cross-functional work.
