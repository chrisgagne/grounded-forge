# OpenStax Organizational Behavior, Software-Business Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2019). *Organizational Behavior*. Rice University. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/organizational-behavior. Lead author J. Stewart Black; 11 contributing authors.

## Software-Business Relevance

OpenStax's *Organizational Behavior* is the corpus's anchor on individual, group, and organisational psychology — the human side of the software-business intersection. Software businesses are organisations that build and sell technical work through people; the technical-commercial trade-offs this axis prices are almost always *mediated* by motivation, perception, group dynamics, conflict, leadership, and culture. A pricing decision is a decision under bounded rationality and confirmation bias (Ch 6.4). A team-structure decision activates the Argyris incongruity thesis on mature engineers in fragmented work (Ch 2.5) and the Tuckman stages on team formation (Ch 9.1, Ch 10.2). A roadmap-vs-capacity argument with sales is process conflict that productively reshapes options when surfaced, or relationship conflict that pushes both sides into reactive-system processing when not (Ch 6.4, Ch 14.2). A board memo on technical debt is power and influence work that turns on French and Raven's bases and Hickson's strategic contingencies (Ch 12.3, Ch 13.3).

The source's distinct contribution to software-business is *the language for naming what is happening between people inside a software-business decision*. Where Jones (`jones-evidence-based-sweng`) prices the technical trade-offs empirically and Letaw (`letaw-handbook-sweng-methods`) supplies the process scaffolding, OpenStax OB supplies the diagnostic vocabulary for the organisational behaviour layer that determines whether either of the other two actually lands. Hire a strong engineer into a Theory-X management culture and motivation collapses regardless of compensation (Ch 12.3, Ch 7.2). Surface a technical-debt argument as relationship conflict and the reflective-system analysis the CTO needs from the board evaporates (Ch 6.2, Ch 14.1). Adopt a deficit-based Kotter change script for an AI-platform transition and engineers' tacit knowledge stays underground because the change frame doesn't ask for it (Ch 16.3).

This distillation projects the source onto the six software-business phases by reading each phase's typical situations through the source's organisational-behaviour vocabulary, then pre-positions diagnostic questions for the founder, CTO, engineering manager, or technical product leader to ask in the room.

## Key Concepts for Software-Business

1. **Reflective versus reactive systems in technical-commercial decisions.** The brain processes decisions through one of two routes at a time: reflective (logical, analytical, deliberate, prefrontal) and reactive (quick, intuitive, basal ganglia and amygdala). Strong emotion — board pressure, founder ego, missed-quarter heat — pulls software-business decisions toward the reactive route, where pricing, build-vs-buy, and incident-response judgements get made on impulse rather than evidence. (Source: OpenStax, *Organizational Behavior*, Ch 6.2, "How the Brain Processes Information to Make Decisions")

2. **Six barriers to decision quality.** Bounded rationality, escalation of commitment, time constraints, uncertainty, personal biases (similar-to-me, confirmation), and conflict avoidance. Each fires in software-business work: escalation keeps a failing acquisition integration funded, confirmation bias selects the vendor demo that fits the prior, conflict avoidance lets engineering-vs-product drift continue. (Source: Ch 6.4)

3. **Process conflict versus relationship conflict.** Process conflict (about *how* to do something) can improve decisions through deeper exploration. Relationship conflict (about *who* is right) degrades decisions and pushes participants into reactive-system processing. The software-business signature: an engineering-vs-product disagreement starts as process conflict over scope or sequencing and degrades into relationship conflict over competence and ownership when left unmanaged. (Source: Ch 6.4, "Conflict"; Ch 14.1, "The Positive and Negative Sides of Conflict")

4. **Five conflict-resolution modes (Thomas).** Competing, collaborating, compromising, avoiding, accommodating, on assertiveness × cooperativeness axes. Each fits different software-business situations: collaborating for a high-stakes engineering-product-sales joint roadmap; competing on an unpopular but vital security-investment call; accommodating where the engineering decision matters more to product than to engineering. (Source: Ch 14.2, Exhibit 14.4)

5. **Five motivation theories anchoring team-and-capability work.** Maslow's hierarchy (with the caveat that research does not support strict prepotency), Herzberg's motivator-hygiene distinction, McClelland's nAch/nAff/nPow, expectancy theory (effort → performance → outcome × valence), and self-determination theory's intrinsic-versus-extrinsic claim ("as the level of extrinsic rewards increases, the amount of intrinsic motivation decreases"). The collective implication: compensation is hygiene; mastery, autonomy, and purpose are motivators; reward systems must link effort to performance to outcome to be motivating. (Source: Ch 7.2, "Content Theories of Motivation"; Ch 7.3, "Process Theories of Motivation")

6. **Equity theory and procedural justice.** Adams's equity model: employees compare their outcome-input ratio to a referent other's; perceived inequity produces restorative behaviour (alter inputs, alter outcomes, distort perception, change referent, leave). Two amplifiers: people overrate their own performance and overrate others' outcomes. Layoffs based on seniority are perceived as more procedurally just than layoffs based on supervisors' opinions — process fairness sustains commitment when distributive outcomes disappoint. (Source: Ch 7.3, "Reducing Underreward Inequity"; Ch 7.4, "Equity Theory")

7. **French and Raven's five power bases.** Reward, coercive, referent, expert, legitimate. Influence outcomes differ sharply: rationality, expertise, and moralistic appeal produce commitment; coercive power produces alienation and resistance. A CTO defending technical-debt remediation to a board relies primarily on expert and referent power; reliance on legitimate power alone tends to produce compliance without commitment. (Source: Ch 12.3, "Leadership as an Exercise of Influence"; Ch 13.1, "Types of Power")

8. **Hickson's strategic contingencies and resource dependence.** Subunit power accrues to units that cope with uncertainty for the rest of the organisation, are non-substitutable, and sit centrally in the workflow. Engineering's organisational power in a software business is shaped by these three; engineering loses power when the work becomes commoditised, when the technical uncertainty drops, or when leadership routes around it. The Salancik-Pfeffer resource-dependence model adds the diagnostic question: who controls the critical resources others need? (Source: Ch 13.3, "Power and the Control of Strategic Activities")

9. **Distributive versus integrative bargaining and BATNA.** Distributive (win-lose, fixed pie, short-term) versus integrative (win-win, expanded pie, long-term). Most software-business stakeholder relationships warrant integrative framing; specific transactions (a partner-roadmap negotiation under deadline) may warrant distributive. BATNA — Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement — determines bargaining stance; "in general, the party with the more attractive BATNA gets the better of the deal." (Source: Ch 14.4, "Bargaining Strategies"; "Concluding Thoughts about Conflict and Negotiations")

10. **Tuckman's stages and Lencioni's dysfunctions.** Group/team development moves through forming, storming, norming, performing (and adjourning). Lencioni's five dysfunctions stack: absence of trust prevents conflict, which prevents commitment, which prevents accountability, which prevents results. A new engineering team will pass through storming; trying to skip it by enforcing harmony stalls the team at performing-shaped politeness with relationship conflict held below the surface. (Source: Ch 9.1, "Stages in Group Development"; Ch 10.2; Ch 10.4)

11. **Mintzberg's managerial role clusters.** Three clusters of roles: interpersonal (figurehead, leader, liaison), informational (monitor, disseminator, spokesperson), decisional (entrepreneur, disturbance handler, resource allocator, negotiator). Stewart's finding that managers spend 47 per cent of their time with peers and 12 per cent with superiors locates the liaison role at the centre of cross-function software-business work. (Source: Ch 11.3, "The Roles Managers Play")

12. **Communication is invention; the receiver creates meaning.** "A company, for example, is not in default until a team of auditors sits down to examine the books and review the matter. Only after extended discussion do the accountants conclude that the company is, in fact, in default." Information never speaks for itself; context drives meaning; a messenger always accompanies a message. For software-business communication — a technical-debt board memo, an incident write-up, a customer breach disclosure — what the artefact *means* is produced in the reader's reading, not the writer's intent. (Source: Ch 11.5, "Communication Is Invention")

13. **Bureaucracy, the mechanistic-organic continuum, and the organisational life cycle.** Mechanistic structures fit stable simple environments; organic structures fit unstable complex environments. The four-phase life cycle — entrepreneurship (loose, organic), survival, sustained success (mechanistic structures strengthen), renewal or decline — names that the *right* engineering-organisation structure changes over time. Six structures along the continuum: functional → divisional → geographic → matrix → networked team → virtual. (Source: Ch 15.3, "Organizational Designs and Structures"; Ch 16.1; Ch 16.2)

14. **Competing Values Framework: four cultural types.** Cameron and Quinn's CVF: Adhocracy (external × flexibility — innovation, risk-taking), Clan (internal × flexibility — relationships, mentoring), Hierarchy (internal × control — efficiency, process), Market (external × control — competition, results). Software cultures shift along these axes as the firm matures; Amazon's mixed pattern (external adhocracy combined with internal hierarchy) illustrates that cultures can be internally inconsistent. (Source: Ch 15.5, "Corporate Cultures")

15. **Four change models with different assumptions.** Lewin's unfreeze-change-refreeze (stable contexts); Kotter's eight-step (top-down, deficit-based, mechanistic); Cooperrider's Appreciative Inquiry (bottom-up, abundance-based); Olson-Eoyang Complex Adaptive Systems (emergent, self-organising). The text argues that "the more complex the potential change, the greater the need to involve employees." Software-business changes — AI-platform transitions, post-incident process redesign, modernisation programmes — typically warrant the more emergent models rather than Kotter's mechanistic frame. (Source: Ch 16.3)

## Questions to Ask During Software-Business Work

### Phase 1: Strategic positioning (pricing, market entry, build-vs-buy, portfolio, AI integration)

| Need | Question | Source-tag |
|---|---|---|
| Identify the system in play | Am I making this pricing or build-vs-buy call through the reflective system or the reactive system? Is there board, founder, or customer pressure pushing me into impulse? If emotion is high, can I delay until reflection is possible? | Ch 6.2 |
| Surface confirmation bias | What evidence about this market-entry, vendor, or AI-integration choice would change my view, and am I actively seeking it? Am I generating three to five real alternatives or stopping at two? | Ch 6.4 |
| Test for escalation | Is there a prior strategic commitment I am defending rather than evaluating freshly? Where would the cost-of-reversal calculation actually sit if I had not made the prior choice? | Ch 6.4 |
| Engage diverse perspectives | Have I sought input from colleagues whose backgrounds, technical specialties, or commercial roles differ from mine? Diverse input alters majority cognition for the better; in groupthink-shaped rooms, strategic decisions look unanimous until they fail. | Ch 6.6, Ch 10.5 |
| Surface the moral dimension | Does this AI integration or vendor choice have ethical implications I should make explicit before proceeding? Whose interests are affected — workers, customers, communities — who are not in the room? | Ch 6.5 |
| Apply critical thinking to the case | Am I noticing logical fallacies in the strategic argument (appeal to tradition, bandwagon, false cause)? Is the recommendation standing on evidence or on momentum? | Ch 6.5 |

### Phase 2: Product and engineering economics (technical-debt servicing, capacity, reliability investment)

| Need | Question | Source-tag |
|---|---|---|
| Frame the trade-off as variance from a reference scenario | Is the technical-debt remediation argument competing for the same capacity as revenue work? What is the reference scenario the variance is being measured against, and is everyone in the room using the same reference? | Ch 6.3, Ch 6.4 |
| Detect motivation-collapse risk | Will the reliability or quality-investment decision change which work engineers do day-to-day? Does the change move work toward or away from autonomy, competence, and relatedness — the SDT intrinsic-motivation factors? | Ch 7.2 |
| Check for hygiene-versus-motivator confusion | Are we treating compensation, conditions, or supervision as a motivator, when the text predicts they prevent dissatisfaction without driving motivation? What motivators (achievement, recognition, work itself, growth) is the proposed investment touching? | Ch 7.2 |
| Apply expectancy logic | Does the proposed effort have a clear performance path? Does performance link to an outcome of value? Is the link being asserted in slideware or actually visible to the engineers? | Ch 7.3 |
| Surface equity-driven restorative behaviour risk | If the reliability or capacity decision changes some teams' workloads more than others, what restorative behaviour is the equity model predicting — alter inputs, distort perception, change referent, leave? | Ch 7.3 |
| Apply procedural justice to the rollout | Has the *process* for allocating capacity or pay been visibly fair, even if the *outcome* disappoints some? Procedural justice sustains commitment when distributive outcomes don't. | Ch 7.4 |

### Phase 3: Team and capability building (hiring, structure, levelling, culture, succession)

| Need | Question | Source-tag |
|---|---|---|
| Diagnose the management paradigm | Are we operating under Theory X (people require control, lack self-direction) or Theory Y (people self-direct, want to participate) assumptions in this hire or role-structure decision? The 14-country study found most managers held Theory-X assumptions even when publicly endorsing participation; whose actual assumptions are shaping the spec? | Ch 12.3 |
| Detect Argyris incongruity | Is the role we are designing structured for adult engineers' development needs (active, independent, varied, long-perspectived), or are we replicating a mechanised fragmented work design that mature engineers will leave? | Ch 2.5 |
| Choose the structure for the stage | What life-cycle stage are we in — entrepreneurship (loose, organic), survival, sustained success, renewal? Is the structural shift we are considering (functional, divisional, matrix, networked team, virtual) appropriate to that stage, or are we replicating the structure of a different stage? | Ch 15.3, Ch 16.2 |
| Plan for Tuckman storming | Have we acknowledged that this newly-formed team will pass through forming, storming, and norming before performing? Are we creating the conditions for productive storming or trying to skip it? | Ch 9.1, Ch 10.2 |
| Stack against Lencioni's dysfunctions | Has the team built the trust that allows conflict? Without trust there is no conflict; without conflict there is no commitment; without commitment there is no accountability; without accountability there are no results. Where in the stack is this team blocked? | Ch 10.4 |
| Read the culture being installed | Which CVF type are we installing — Adhocracy (innovation, risk-taking), Clan (relationships, mentoring), Hierarchy (efficiency, process), Market (results, competition)? Is the installed culture consistent with what we are asking the team to deliver? | Ch 15.5 |
| Identify motivation-theory mismatch | Are we using extrinsic rewards (bonus structures, ranking) in roles where SDT predicts they erode intrinsic motivation? What is the cost of that erosion? | Ch 7.2 |
| Address levelling without forced ranking | The text reports that GE backed away from forced ranking after Welch retired because it "fostered internal competition and undermined collaboration and teamwork." If we are proposing a forced ranking, what is our answer to that observation? | Ch 17.3 |
| Plan succession through tacit transfer | Are we structuring knowledge transfer for codifiable explicit knowledge only, or does the role's value reside in tacit knowledge that needs Nonaka-shaped transfer through socialisation and observation? | Ch 17.6 |

### Phase 4: Operations and process (delivery cadence, governance, decision rights, change)

| Need | Question | Source-tag |
|---|---|---|
| Match change model to context | Are we using Lewin (stable context, unfreeze-change-refreeze), Kotter (top-down, deficit-based, mechanistic), Appreciative Inquiry (bottom-up, abundance-based), or CAS (emergent, self-organising)? Is the model's assumption-set consistent with the change we are actually trying to make? | Ch 16.3 |
| Locate the constraint as bounded rationality | What can we actually grasp about the operational delta, given bounded rationality? Where are we papering over uncertainty with false precision? | Ch 6.4 |
| Place the liaison role | Who is in the liaison position — spending the 47 per cent of time with peers across organisational boundaries that Mintzberg's role-cluster work surfaces? Are we structurally supporting that work or treating it as overhead? | Ch 11.3 |
| Diagnose intergroup interdependence | Are the teams whose work needs to integrate operating under pooled (largely independent), sequential (one's output is another's input), or reciprocal (mutual dependence) interdependence? Are the integration mechanisms (rules, member exchange, linking roles, task forces, decoupling) matched to the type? | Ch 9.4 |
| Address change as identity work | Is the change we are making structural, technological, or cultural? The text frames cultural change as "the most difficult, requires reshaping core identity"; are we treating a cultural change as if it were structural? | Ch 16.2 |
| Read communication as invention | The artefact we send (process redesign, governance update, retrospective summary) does not carry its own meaning; meaning is produced in the reading. Have we composed for the reader's reading or the writer's intent? | Ch 11.5 |
| Surface the resource-dependence map | Which subunits control which critical resources others need? Where is the power asymmetric, and does the proposed change either ratify or shift that asymmetry? | Ch 13.3 |

### Phase 5: Risk, reliability, compliance (incidents, security, legal, regulatory, ethics)

| Need | Question | Source-tag |
|---|---|---|
| Avoid attributional drift | After the incident, are we drifting into the fundamental attribution error — over-weighting internal/personal causes, under-weighting situational? Are we treating "human error" as sufficient explanation? | Ch 3.3 |
| Surface escalation of commitment | Is the prior compliance, security, or reliability investment being defended against new evidence that costs are mounting? What would I do if I had not made the prior choice? | Ch 6.4 |
| Hold conflict productively | Does the post-incident discussion stay in process conflict (what went wrong, how to do it differently) or drift into relationship conflict (who is to blame)? When it drifts, do we reframe back? | Ch 6.4, Ch 14.1 |
| Apply Rest's four-component ethical model | Where are we in the chain — moral sensitivity (do we even see the issue as ethical), moral judgment (which way is right), moral motivation (do we intend to do right), moral character (do we act)? Failure at any point produces unethical action. | Ch 6.5 |
| Choose the right reinforcement mode | Is the post-incident response structured around positive reinforcement combined with extinction (the text's preferred mode), or around punishment that the text predicts will alienate and emphasise the negative? | Ch 4.2 |
| Counter groupthink in incident review | Has dissent been suppressed in the post-incident review because participants would rather keep peace than antagonise others? Is a devil's advocate assigned to systematically critique the proposed corrective action? | Ch 6.6 |

### Phase 6: Stakeholder communication (board, exec alignment, investor relations, customer messaging)

| Need | Question | Source-tag |
|---|---|---|
| Choose the power base for the influence attempt | Which of French and Raven's five bases is this board memo, investor update, or customer-comms artefact relying on — reward, coercive, referent, expert, legitimate? Rationality, expertise, and moralistic appeal produce commitment; coercive produces alienation. | Ch 12.3 |
| Map the counterpower the audience can draw on | What other power sources can the board, investor, or customer draw on that buffer our influence attempt? Where is push-back going to come from? | Ch 13.1 |
| Choose distributive or integrative bargaining frame | Is this a one-shot transaction (distributive) or part of a long-term relationship (integrative)? Most stakeholder relationships warrant integrative framing; few moments warrant distributive. | Ch 14.4 |

Are we treating a signed contract as beginning of final-points negotiation (some cultures) or as closure (others)? | Ch 14.4 |
| Read the political conditions | Are ambiguous goals, limited resources, changing technology, non-programmed decisions, or organisational change creating the conditions for political behaviour around this stakeholder communication? | Ch 13.3 |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: A founder is defending a feature, pricing, or technology choice in the face of mounting counter-evidence.** Signal: investments increase as outcomes worsen; criticism is dismissed; the founder cannot articulate which evidence would change their view. Diagnosis: escalation of commitment, often combined with confirmation bias and similarity bias (the founder hears most clearly from people who think like them). Follow-up: re-evaluate freshly; treat sunk costs as sunk; ask the precommitment question — *what would I do if I had not made the prior choice?* Bring in a deliberate dissent voice. (Source: Ch 6.4)

- **Pattern: Engineering-product-sales meetings cycle without converging.** Signal: same discussion repeats; no decisions; meeting fatigue. Diagnosis: the decision is non-programmed but the team is using programmed-decision shortcuts, *or* relationship conflict has overtaken process conflict and the room has shifted to reactive-system processing. Follow-up: explicitly bound the decision; pause and ask whether the dispute is about *what to do* (productive process conflict to surface) or about *who is right* (relationship conflict to interrupt and reframe). (Source: Ch 6.3, Ch 6.4)

- **Pattern: A high-performing engineering team's output drops after a re-org or compensation change.** Signal: velocity falls; senior engineers go quiet in standups; informal departures begin. Diagnosis: equity model predicts restorative behaviour when the outcome-input ratio against a referent shifts; the visible response is reduced inputs, perception distortion, or exit. Procedural justice may be the missing variable — even when the outcome is fair, an opaque process erodes commitment. Follow-up: ask which referent the team is now using and why; surface the process the change followed and where the procedural-justice gaps are. (Source: Ch 7.3, Ch 7.4)

- **Pattern: A CTO's board memo lands as "interesting but unactioned."** Signal: the board nods, asks no questions, no decisions are recorded; the same memo recurs in a future quarter. Diagnosis: the influence base is wrong (relying on legitimate power where expert or referent would land); *or* the memo asks a non-programmed decision of a board operating in programmed-decision mode; *or* the communication-as-invention problem — the board's reading produces a meaning the memo did not intend. Follow-up: reground the influence base on expert and referent power; rewrite for the board's reading rather than the writer's intent; specify the decision being requested. (Source: Ch 11.5, Ch 12.3, Ch 13.1)

- **Pattern: A change initiative (AI platform, modernisation, post-incident process) launches with momentum and stalls at the team level.** Signal: leadership communications continue but team behaviour does not shift. Diagnosis: the change model is mis-matched — Kotter's mechanistic deficit-based frame for a change that needs Appreciative-Inquiry or CAS-shaped emergent involvement. Or the cultural-change dimension (CVF type shift) was treated as structural and not engaged at the identity level. Follow-up: surface the model assumption explicitly; ask whether the change actually fits Lewin's stable-context assumption; consider whether the people closest to the work need to be involved in shaping the change rather than absorbing it. (Source: Ch 16.2, Ch 16.3)

- **Pattern: Sales is angry that engineering missed a date; engineering is angry that sales committed without consulting; both are escalating to the CEO.** Signal: cross-function emails, blame language, "this keeps happening" framing. Diagnosis: intergroup interdependence type is mis-named — likely reciprocal interdependence being managed as if it were pooled or sequential. Coordination mechanism (linking roles, task forces, integrators) may be missing. Conflict has likely shifted from process to relationship. Follow-up: name the interdependence type; install the matching integration mechanism; reframe the dispute to process conflict (about how) rather than relationship conflict (about who). (Source: Ch 9.4, Ch 14.1)

- **Pattern: A new senior hire is failing despite the right credentials.** Signal: 6-9 months in, performance below expectations; rapport with team thin; original sponsors quiet. Diagnosis: motivation theory predicts the gap — the role may meet hygiene factors (compensation, conditions) without supplying motivators (achievement, growth, recognition); or the Theory-X/Theory-Y mismatch between the hire's expectation and the operating context; or Tuckman's storming-stage management mishandled. Follow-up: separate hygiene from motivators in the diagnosis; check the management-paradigm mismatch; treat the team-formation work as substantive rather than incidental. (Source: Ch 7.2, Ch 9.1, Ch 12.3)

- **Pattern: Engineering organisational power has eroded over 12-18 months.** Signal: engineering's input on roadmap, hiring, vendor selection has been progressively routed around; CTO's authority appears diminished. Diagnosis: strategic contingencies have shifted — engineering may no longer be coping with critical uncertainty, may have become substitutable (commodity engineering, outsourcing has expanded the supply), or may have lost workflow centrality (other functions now sit closer to value creation). Follow-up: assess which of the three Hickson dimensions has shifted; do not respond by reasserting legitimate power (the wrong base for the situation); rebuild expert-power-grounded contributions on the dimensions that have changed. (Source: Ch 13.3)

## When to Use This Reference

- A founder or executive is making a high-stakes strategic-positioning decision under time and emotional pressure; this reference surfaces the dual-system risk and the bounded-rationality / escalation / confirmation-bias barriers.
- A CTO or engineering manager is structuring teams or changing compensation in a software business and needs the motivation, equity, and Argyris-incongruity vocabulary to predict response.
- A change initiative (AI platform, modernisation, post-incident redesign) is being planned and the four change models need to be matched to the actual change shape.
- A board memo, investor update, or customer-comms artefact is being written and the French-and-Raven bases, BATNA, and communication-as-invention framing apply.
- An engineering-versus-product or engineering-versus-sales conflict has reached the leadership chain and needs both a Thomas-mode and a process-versus-relationship-conflict diagnosis.
- A post-incident review is at risk of attributional drift (toward "human error" as sufficient explanation), or of escalation of commitment defending a prior reliability investment.
- A succession or knowledge-transfer concern involves substantial tacit knowledge that doesn't move through explicit documentation alone.

This is not the reference for the *empirical software-engineering* question (KLOC mythology, fault-report patterns, productivity statistics) — that's Jones (`jones-evidence-based-sweng`). It is not the reference for the *process-design* question (RACI, microservices boundaries, scrum facilitation) — that's Letaw (`letaw-handbook-sweng-methods`), Scrum, Kanban, and the Open Practice Library. It is the reference for *the organisational-behaviour layer* that sits underneath those technical and process choices and determines whether they actually land.

## Worked Example

A founder-CEO of a 180-engineer B2B SaaS company is preparing a board memo recommending an $8M reliability investment over the next four quarters. The CTO has supplied the technical case (incident frequency, customer impact, post-mortem patterns); the CFO has supplied a cost-of-incidents reference scenario. The founder-CEO drafts the memo in the standard format: incident-data tables, cost estimates, ROI calculation, recommended approval. The CTO reviews it and flags that the same memo (with different numbers) was rejected at the prior board meeting nine months ago.

Slowing down: the founder is in reactive-system territory after a major customer escalation that morning. The board memo as drafted is *defending* the prior rejected recommendation rather than evaluating freshly — escalation of commitment, possibly compounded by confirmation bias toward the data that supports the prior view. The source identifies both patterns as barriers to decision quality: escalation of commitment and personal biases (including confirmation bias) are two of the six barriers the source names. (Source: OpenStax, *Organizational Behavior*, Ch 6.4)

Reframing: the founder rewrites with deliberate attention to the four-component ethical-decision dimension (whose interests are affected — customers without voice, engineers absorbing the on-call burden, investors whose returns are exposed); to procedural justice (was the prior process the board followed visibly fair, and if so, what evidence has changed that warrants revisiting the decision); and to the French-and-Raven base on which the memo will rely (expert power from the CTO, referent power from the CEO, *not* legitimate-position arguments about CEO authority to direct capital). The source's finding is that "rationality, expertise, and moralistic appeal produce commitment; coercive power produces alienation and resistance." (Source: Ch 12.3, "Leadership as an Exercise of Influence"; Ch 6.5 for the ethical-decision framing; Ch 7.4 for procedural justice)

Composing for the reader: the founder considers what the board will *read*, not what the founder intends to *write*. The source is explicit on this: "A company, for example, is not in default until a team of auditors sits down to examine the books and review the matter. Only after extended discussion do the accountants conclude that the company is, in fact, in default." Information never speaks for itself; meaning is produced in the reading. Last quarter, the board's reading produced "this is engineering asking for more money" — a confirmation-bias reading the memo did not intend but the format invited. The new draft leads with the customer escalation as concrete evidence of the cost of inaction, names the prior rejection explicitly, and frames the decision as one the board's procedural-justice norms require revisiting given the new evidence. The recommendation is for $4M in the next two quarters (half the original ask) with explicit success criteria and a Q3 reassessment gate — a structure designed to interrupt the escalation pattern by building in reversibility. (Source: Ch 11.5, "Communication Is Invention")

In the board meeting: the chair raises a relationship-conflict framing (questioning the CTO's competence). The founder reframes back to process conflict ("the question is not whether the CTO is reading the data correctly; the question is what response the data warrants given the customer impact we have evidence of"). The source names this distinction: process conflict about *how to do something* can improve decisions through deeper exploration; relationship conflict about *who is right* degrades decisions and pushes participants into reactive-system processing. The founder's reframe is designed to keep the room in process-conflict mode. The board approves the $4M with the reassessment gate. (Source: Ch 6.4, "Conflict"; Ch 14.1, "The Positive and Negative Sides of Conflict")

This worked example illustrates several key concepts from the source: dual-system processing, escalation of commitment, similarity and confirmation biases, communication as invention, French-and-Raven bases, procedural justice, process versus relationship conflict, and the structural design of decisions to interrupt escalation through reversibility.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Treating compensation as a motivator.** Herzberg, Maslow, and SDT all predict that pay is hygiene — it prevents dissatisfaction but does not motivate. Engineering compensation increases that solve "we need to motivate the team" rarely do; the work itself, autonomy, mastery, and purpose carry the motivation load. The cost of misreading hygiene as motivator is that the next quarter's "motivation problem" returns despite higher comp. (Source: Ch 7.2)
- **Skipping Tuckman storming.** Treating a newly-formed engineering team's early conflict as a personnel problem to be smoothed over rather than as a structural stage of group development. The cost: the team locks into performing-shaped politeness with unresolved relationship conflict held below the surface, and emerges months later as a "team chemistry problem" no-one can name. (Source: Ch 9.1, Ch 10.2)
- **Coercive power as default influence in technical-debt or reliability-investment arguments.** Relying on legitimate-position-authority arguments to push through a reliability investment that the rest of the C-suite hasn't engaged with on the evidence. The cost: alienation and resistance even when the technical case is correct; subsequent rounds harder, not easier. (Source: Ch 12.3, Ch 13.1)
- **Forced ranking as a long-term performance management tool.** The text reports GE's reversal post-Welch because the practice "fostered internal competition and undermined collaboration and teamwork." In software work where collaboration is load-bearing, the cost of forced ranking is hidden until it appears as cross-team velocity collapse. (Source: Ch 17.3)
- **Top-down Kotter-shaped change for complex emergent challenges.** Applying Kotter's eight-step mechanistic frame to an AI-platform transition, a culture change, or a post-incident process redesign whose actual shape is closer to CAS-emergent. The cost: leadership communications run; team behaviour does not change. (Source: Ch 16.3)
- **Treating cultural change as structural.** Reorganising the org chart and expecting cultural shift to follow. The text frames cultural change as "the most difficult, requires reshaping core identity"; structural change alone does not displace the underlying CVF type. The cost: a new org chart with the old culture. (Source: Ch 15.5, Ch 16.2)
- **Reading communication as transmission rather than invention.** Assuming the board memo, customer-comms artefact, or incident write-up carries its meaning. The reader creates the meaning; the artefact at best constrains the range of plausible readings. The cost: the writer's intent and the reader's reading drift apart, and the artefact either fails to land or lands wrong. (Source: Ch 11.5)
- **Punishment as primary post-incident response.** Disciplinary action as the primary response to incidents; the text argues that "positive reinforcement combined with extinction is the most suitable way to bring about desired behavior" and that punishment alienates and emphasises the negative. The cost: incident reporting goes underground; subsequent incidents are detected later and at higher cost. (Source: Ch 4.2)
- **Diffusing accountability into "the team" or "the org."** Equity theory predicts disengagement when the link between effort, performance, and reward is unclear; expectancy theory predicts the same. Decisions about accountability are themselves high-stakes decisions; the cost of diffusion is that high performers leave first. (Source: Ch 7.3, Ch 7.4)

## Through the CTO lens

When the reader is an opportunity-first, AI-native, constraint-disciplined CTO mid-modernisation, several questions and patterns reweight.

- **Phase 1 (Strategic positioning):** the dual-system framing (Ch 6.2) does not slow the CTO down — it sharpens the question of which decisions need reflective-system processing and which can run on operational-pattern reactive heuristics. The CTO reaches for the source on novel non-programmed decisions (AI integration choices, build-vs-buy with strategic dependency, acquisition-target engineering health) and routes programmed decisions through delegated mechanisms. Critical thinking (Ch 6.5) lands as constraint-discipline-protection: when the case is built on appeal to tradition, bandwagon, or false cause, the constraint is being smuggled in dressed as evidence.
- **Phase 3 (Team and capability):** the Argyris incongruity thesis (Ch 2.5) and the SDT intrinsic-motivation claim (Ch 7.2) reweight most heavily for this lens. The CTO's bifurcating engineer profile (AI-platform-expert or deep-domain-expert) actively raises Argyris's question: are we structuring roles for the developmental needs of these mature engineers, or are we replicating fragmented mechanised work in new vocabulary? Forced ranking (Ch 17.3) is treated as a non-starter for this lens given the collaboration loss it produces in AI-platform and modernisation work.
- **Phase 4 (Operations and process):** CAS-emergent change (Ch 16.3) is the CTO's default model for AI-native transitions rather than Kotter. The text's claim that "the more complex the potential change, the greater the need to involve employees" lands directly. Demand governance and explicit prioritisation gates map onto the source's bounded-rationality and Mintzberg-disturbance-handler framings: the constraint is what enters the queue, not how the queue is groomed.
- **Phase 6 (Stakeholder communication):** the French-Raven bases (Ch 12.3) and communication-as-invention (Ch 11.5) carry the load. The CTO defends technical-debt remediation and AI investments to the board through expert and referent power; legitimate-position arguments are treated as evidence the underlying case is weak. The artefact is composed for the board's reading, not the writer's intent, with the constraint stated by the end of the second sentence and the decision needed (assign, fund, approve) named explicitly.

The CTO lens recedes much of the source's pedagogical scaffolding (Maslow as such, Hofstede as a comparative framework, the catalogue of seven logical fallacies as a checklist) in favour of the constraint-and-mechanism questions: which lever does this concept name, who owns it, what is the timeboxed first step, what is the success measure?

## Through the Business-Executive Stakeholder lens

When the reader is a Paradigm-A-operating peer C-suite executive who occasionally espouses Paradigm-B language, the source reweights for register-and-reception, not content. The substantive content of the source — motivation theories, conflict frames, change models, communication-as-invention — carries content the stakeholder typically already espouses; the question is which moves the artefact makes in Paradigm-A register the stakeholder will receive, with one Paradigm-B move offered parsimoniously.

- **Phase 5 (Risk, reliability) and Phase 6 (Stakeholder communication):** the conflict frames (Ch 6.4, Ch 14) and the communication-as-invention claim (Ch 11.5) carry the lens's load. Post-incident framing as "system not people" lands on this stakeholder as the Paradigm-B move; the artefact pays for it by leading with what was committed, what was actual, the variance, the accountable owner, and the decision being requested. The Thomas modes (Ch 14.2) provide the Paradigm-A vocabulary the stakeholder uses natively (competing, collaborating, compromising, avoiding, accommodating) — the lens treats these as register-match rather than as the lesson.
- **Phase 3 (Team and capability):** equity theory and procedural justice (Ch 7.4) are the source's most Paradigm-A-shaped contribution: the stakeholder receives "people compare ratios and respond to perceived unfairness" as an accountability frame rather than as a sympathy claim. The Argyris thesis (Ch 2.5) and SDT (Ch 7.2) are typically the *one* Paradigm-B move the artefact can carry — offered as a structural reframe ("this pattern reads as role-shaped, not individual-shaped"), not as a sermon.
- **Anti-patterns reweight:** "diffusing accountability into the team or the org" is treated as a load-bearing failure for this lens, because diffuse accountability triggers Model-I defensive reasoning in the stakeholder and the artefact gets filed as "not rigorous." Named owners and KPIs throughout; one structural reframe; the artefact terminates in Paradigm-A action.

The lens recedes most of the source's vocabulary about *people's interior life* (motivation as inner experience, perception as construction, attitudes as predispositions) in favour of the *operational consequences* of those interior states. The stakeholder will not engage with "people are motivated when their work meets autonomy, competence, and relatedness needs"; they will engage with "the proposed compensation change predicts 15-20 per cent voluntary attrition in the engineering org based on equity-theory restorative behaviour, with named at-risk roles and a procedural-justice fix that reduces the predicted attrition by half." Same source, register-shaped output.

## Through the PM Bounded by BA Role lens

When the reader is a PM whose title implies product authority but whose actual role is bounded by BA-shaped coordination, the source reweights narrowly — the conflict, group-dynamics, and intergroup-interdependence chapters carry the most relevant content; the leadership and power chapters carry content about authority the PM does not have but the artefact may assume they do.

- **Phase 4 (Operations and process):** intergroup interdependence (Ch 9.4 — pooled, sequential, reciprocal) and integration mechanisms (rules, member exchange, linking roles, task forces, decoupling) name the work the PM actually does — most PM work in this archetype is *integration* across teams whose interdependence is reciprocal but treated as if sequential. The lens reads the source as providing vocabulary for *naming* what the PM absorbs silently, not as authority to redesign the integration mechanisms (that authority sits with the leadership chain).
- **Phase 6 (Stakeholder communication):** the Thomas modes (Ch 14.2) reweight for the PM's typical use — the PM is structurally *accommodating* (the issue often matters more to other stakeholders), or *compromising* (time-pressured, equal-power-with-no-real-authority). The lens reads the source as a diagnostic: when the artefact assumes the PM will *compete* or *collaborate* in a high-stakes integrative mode, it is assuming authority the role doesn't carry.
- **Anti-patterns reweight:** the "diffusing accountability into the team" anti-pattern is read inverted for this lens — the PM is often *the* accountability surface for cross-team integration work that the org's measurement systems don't see, and "diffusing into the team" is sometimes the org's way of treating the PM's invisible absorption as if it were distributed work.
- **Cost the artefact creates:** if a software-business artefact assigns the PM to "drive the change," "set priorities for engineering capacity," or "decide the trade-off," the lens names the authority-assumption gap. The OB source provides vocabulary (legitimate vs expert vs referent power, French-Raven bases) for naming *whose authority the artefact actually needs to route to* — usually the C-suite, the engineering manager, or the business stakeholder — rather than the PM who can only absorb the ask.

The lens recedes the source's leadership and motivation framings (Theory X/Y, Maslow, McClelland) as content the PM has no structural authority to act on; it does not have hire-fire authority, comp authority, or culture-change authority. It surfaces the source's conflict and intergroup-coordination framings as vocabulary the PM can use *in the artefacts the PM produces* to name the coordination work that goes invisible.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The source draws on a large cluster of foundational behavioural-science researchers this corpus does not hold as primary references.

- *Chris Argyris* — the basic incongruity thesis (mature workers vs mechanised roles) is cited [BT] (Ch 2.5). Argyris's primary work (*Personality and Organization*, 1957) is not in this corpus.
- *Abraham Maslow, Frederick Herzberg, David McClelland, Edward Deci and Richard Ryan (SDT), Victor Vroom, Lyman Porter, J. Stacy Adams* — content and process motivation theories are all cited [BT] (Ch 7.2-7.3). None held as primary references; this text is the corpus's primary treatment of all five theory families.
- *James Rest* — the four-component ethical decision model is cited [BT] (Ch 6.5).
- *John French and Bertram Raven* — five bases of power are sourced [BT] (Ch 12.3; Ch 13.1).
- *D. J. Hickson et al., strategic contingencies; Gerald Salancik and Jeffrey Pfeffer, resource dependence* — subunit power models are cited [BT] (Ch 13.3).

**Named limits of the source.** The text is a general OB textbook, not a software-specific source. The text does not address AI-native roles, remote-first engineering teams, or the motivational dynamics of AI-augmented work; these are inference territory, not source claims. Hofstede's four-dimension model is the text's primary cultural framework; more nuanced cultural treatments (GLOBE adds dimensions) are acknowledged but not developed as the primary reference.
