
# Heft & Pattillo (Group Jazz), Engaging Everyone with Liberating Structures Handbook (light reference)

**Source:** Heft, L. & Pattillo, A. (compilers), with materials by H. Lipmanowicz and K. McCandless (2010). *Engaging Everyone with Liberating Structures Handbook*. Washington, DC: Group Jazz. Licence: CC BY-NC-SA 3.0 Unported. https://www.grassrootsgrantmakers.org/wp-content/uploads/Engaging-Everyone-31.pdf.
**Structure:** 60-page participant-facing handbook ("a helpful reminder, not complete instructions") with seven framing sections (Ownership vs Buy-in, Choosing Space, Aesthetics Are Important, Working with the Space You're Given, Storyboard Design and Planning, Nothing About Me Without Me, Engage the Unusual Suspects), an activity catalogue of ~32 named Liberating Structures (LS) interaction designs, three Seeing-Complexity exercises, five Random-Thoughts framing-aids, an Origins section attributing the term to Torbert (1991) and de Bono (1991), and a closing "Pattern Language for Engagement" essay grounded in Alexander (1977) and the Johnson-Lenz primitives.

## Author's Thesis (condensed)

Liberating structures are frameworks that "make it possible for people and organizations to create, to do new things, to be innovative" by being "messy" and "complex" yet with "just enough structure to channel the energy." The central metaphor is jazz: structure that "gives enough structure so that people can create together" while making "possible infinite degrees of freedom." Structural change is non-optional: "For things to really change, structural elements need to change, too. Otherwise change is short-lived."

A second load-bearing claim is the *ownership-versus-buy-in* distinction: ownership is participation in development; buy-in is being convinced after the fact. Buy-in is treated as a structural error symptom — the word in conversation is a "danger signal telling you that your development and implementation process is missing the essential ingredient of involving all who should be involved." The whole catalogue is presented as instruments for ownership.

A third load-bearing claim is the *pattern-language* framing: LS are best understood as named, combinable patterns drawn from four primitives — rhythm/timing, boundaries (group size and inclusion), physical containers (space and room set-up), and conceptual containers (question phrasing). The handbook positions itself as a reminder for people who have attended an LS-based event, not a stand-alone instructional manual; the underlying patterns are claimed to be combinable "in dozens of different ways."

## Framing Principles

**Ownership versus buy-in.** Ownership is "your choice freely made" through participation in development; buy-in is "the exact opposite" — implementing someone else's idea, producing "lukewarm, pallid implementation and mediocre results." For "intractable socio-technical behavioral problems in systems the notion of buy-in is just not useful – people in the system need to own the new behaviors."

**Choosing space and aesthetics.** Space is structural, not decorative. NO TABLES is often best; small "4-tops" beat the large round tables typical of conference centres. Natural light is critical ("most people wilt… in fluorescent light"). Long walls or easeled poster boards enable graphic recording. When space is fixed, bend it: have people stand, turn around, group in threes between rows.

**Storyboard design and planning.** Use a storyboard rather than an agenda. A storyboard "also includes and illustrates something about what the participants will experience." Diagnostic: "if your storyboard has a whole lot of podium sessions in a row, it's not going to be very engaging!" Operational: post-it notes for components, moved around to test combinations.

**Nothing About Me Without Me / Engage the Unusual Suspects.** When designing for positively-deviant behaviour change, "broaden your conversations to include the people most involved." Companion question: "Who's not here that could contribute to our conversation?" — described as "one of the most important questions you can ask." Mind the invitation craft: many people have been invited as "window dressing" and may be suspicious.

## The LS Activity Catalogue (selected interaction designs)

**Conversation-starting and opening.**
- *Knee-to-Knee Conversation.* Small groups sitting close, no table; Peter Block's framing. Set up the chairs before participants arrive.
- *One Minute of Silence.* Reflection time before an exercise; "not just for the introverts." Stage the time-stretch with a script.
- *Impromptu Speed Networking.* Bell-paced 5–10-minute partner rounds, three rounds usual. Everyone speaks early; in a 30-min session, 12–15 min of air time per person regardless of group size.
- *1-2-4-Whole Group.* Individual → pairs → groups of 4–6 ("more than six is too big") → whole-group open question.

**Surfacing knowledge and reflection.**
- *Celebrity Interviews.* "Talk show" replacement for a panel; avoid having "to listen to any one voice for more than 5 minutes."
- *Taking Questions.* Take questions in batches of 3–4 rather than 1-by-1; avoids the Q-A-Q-A "central tendency" trap.
- *Take 10 (Minutes).* Independent reflection with a juicy question; "What's been missing from our conversation so far?"
- *Take a Panel.* Each participant works ~20 minutes on their own flip-chart sheet against 8–10 questions; then small groups share ~4 minutes each.
- *A Note about Report Outs.* "BORING!!!" Drain the life out of small-group conversation. Bring the experience into a new conversation instead.
- *Gallery Walk.* Poster-based alternative to report-outs; "What was surprising? What was new? How does this change our conversation?"

**Working-with-experts.**
- *Wise Crowds.* 5–6 person groups, one "client" and a "consulting team." Client describes ~10 min, turns back ≥15 min while team converses, then rejoins ~15 min. The consulting team's "job is not to decide 'this is what The Client ought to do.'"
- *Troika Consulting.* Three-person 10-minute rounds; each gets a turn as focal person.
- *Appreciative Interviews.* Stories about "a time when…" with named characteristics (Connection Through Empathy, "Third Ear and Third Eye", Generative Questioning, etc.).
- *Discovery & Action Dialogues.* Six-question Plexus Institute / Positive Deviance protocol surfacing existing solutions and identifying volunteers to experiment.

**Question-asking and assumption-surfacing.**
- *5 Whys.* Pair-based, "at least five" repetitions; explicitly relaxable.
- *Wicked Questions.* Questions with embedded paradox or tension; not trick questions. Examples: "How can we sustain quality standards across the system while allowing for local innovation?"
- *Q Storming.* Post-it brainstorm of "courageous and provocative" and "silly" questions; Marilee Adams' protocol.
- *TRIZ.* A single move from Altshuller's Theory of Inventive Problem Solving: "Design a comprehensive system that makes it absolutely impossible to get that result" — then look for similarities to the current state.

**Decision-tools and prioritisation.**
- *25-to-10.* Five rounds of milling-and-rating index cards 1–5; surface high-scoring ideas via countdown. "Not recommended to make a decision" — a wisdom-of-the-crowd surfacing tool.
- *Mini Constellations.* Spatial positioning around a floor object; speak from the distance-position as a "role."
- *15% Solutions.* "Most people have about 15-percent control" — the question is "Where do you have freedom to act? What's in your 15%?" (Gareth Morgan).
- *Simple Rules.* Five categories — How To, Boundary, Priority, Timing, Exit. Test each: "What would happen if you left that rule out?"
- *Six Words.* Hemingway-anecdote compression; Mulago Foundation's <8-words-with-verb-and-target criteria.
- *Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix.* Ralph Stacey's two axes; Brenda Zimmerman's recipe/airplane/child distinction.
- *Portfolio Planning.* Zimmerman's forest-fire eco-cycle applied to project portfolios.

**Group-storytelling and dialogue.**
- *After Action Debrief: What, So What, Now What?* Three sequential questions, "in the following order." Centre for Creative Leadership.
- *Conversation Café Dialogue.* Six named agreements (Open-mindedness, Acceptance, Curiosity, Discovery, Sincerity, Brevity); two structured rounds plus open dialogue plus final round, using a talking object.
- *World Café.* Four-per-table, three-or-more 20-minute rounds, table host stays, others move.
- *Fishbowl.* Inner circle of 5–6 people with direct experience talk to each other, not the audience; outer circle asks questions; then dialogue between the circles.
- *Shift & Share.* Team "stations" in the room; participants travel rather than serial keynotes.
- *Just In Time Topics.* Self-organising agenda from Open Space lineage; "Whoever comes are the right people" / "law of two feet."

**Scenario-building and network.**
- *Participatory Scenarios.* Critique of expert-only strategic scenarios; five-step community-driven 2×2 scenario build.
- *Network Weaving.* Map the network; close triangles, integrate clusters, expand the periphery.

**Levels of Accountability.** Seven-rung ladder from "Take Action and Learn" / "Make it happen, renewal" at the top through "Find Solutions," "Accept ownership," "Acknowledge situation," "Wait 'n Hope," "Blame self," "Blame others"; bottom layer "Denial / 'This has nothing to do with me.'" Top band labels: Learn & Grow / Empowered. Bottom band labels: Protect & Defend / Disempowered. Closing question: "What do we need to do to work 'above the line?'"

**Improv Games.** Three named exercises — One Word at a Time Story, Yes And ("the most common response is 'yes and but'"), Coffee Filters (Tom Sparough). Open-up-rather-than-close-down conversational practice.

## "Seeing Complexity" Exercises

- *Complex Systems Game: How Many Ways?* Lunch bags of random objects; groups invent classifications; surfaces how "people can 'see' very different things based on their experience."
- *Tic Tac Toe.* No talking, no touching, in the air. 3×3 tractable; 4×4 "impossible." Framing: "We spend a lot of time in meetings doing the equivalent of playing 4 x 4 tic tac toe in the air."
- *A Place in the System.* Stand and get between Person A and Person B; then equidistant from them. Surfaces what it's like to not be "in control" when everything is moving.

## "Random Thoughts" Framing-Aids

- *Waving Hands versus Clapping.* Less mood-breaking than clapping for in-meeting acknowledgement.
- *Talking Objects.* Cultural caveat: respect for the talking-stick lineage (Dr. Locust on Native American practice); use a talking *object* (anything held in the hand — even a rubber duck) when avoiding spiritual borrowing.
- *Music & Poetry.* Create conditions for creativity by adding these to the mix.
- *Tingsha Bells.* Solving the recall problem without yelling or microphone-tapping. "Kid's xylophones also work."
- *Take 20 Seconds (Pause and look at your shoes).* Calibration figure: facilitators on average start talking after 6 seconds; prescription is to wait at least 20. "If you start talking too soon, you truncate that process and communicate, unwittingly, that you're the one with the answers."

## Key Connections

The handbook is dense with attributions; LS is presented as a synthesis of named lineages rather than a closed system:

- **Term origin:** William Torbert, *The Power of Balance* (Sage, 1991); contemporaneous Edward de Bono, *Teaching Thinking* (Penguin, 1991).
- **Pattern-language framing:** Christopher Alexander, *A Pattern Language* (Oxford U, 1977); Peter and Trudy Johnson-Lenz (rhythms/boundaries/containers).
- **Large-group-method lineage:** Dick Axelrod, *Terms of Engagement* (Berrett-Koehler, 2010), naming Open Space, Appreciative Inquiry, the Conference Model, and others as sharing principles of widening involvement, connecting, action-community, democratic practice.
- **Specific structure lineages:** Peter Block (small groups, asmallgroup.net); Plexus Institute / Positive Deviance (DADs); Genrich Altshuller (TRIZ); Ralph Stacey (Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix); Brenda Zimmerman (recipe/airplane/child; forest-fire eco-cycle); Gareth Morgan (15% Solutions); Diana Whitney and Marilee Adams (Q Storming, AI); Mulago Foundation (Six Words criteria); Larry Smith/SMITH Magazine and Hemingway anecdote (Six Words); Center for Creative Leadership (What/So What/Now What); Tom Sparough (Coffee Filters); Open Space Technology / openspaceworld.org (Just In Time Topics); Conversation Café (conversationcafe.org); World Café (theworldcafe.com); Appreciative Inquiry (appreciativeinquiry.case.edu); family-therapy Constellations (familyconstellationwork.net); Dr. Locust 1998 (Native American talking-stick passage); The Grove (Portfolio Planning templates).

## Signature Contrarian Positions

1. **Buy-in is the wrong goal.** Aiming for buy-in is a structural error producing "lukewarm, pallid implementation and mediocre results"; the word itself is a "danger signal."
2. **Structural change beats motivational change.** "For things to really change, structural elements need to change, too. Otherwise change is short-lived."
3. **Large conference-centre round tables are anti-conversation.** Too far apart, requiring yelling. Use small "4-tops" or no tables at all.
4. **Storyboards beat agendas.** Agendas hide participant experience; storyboards make it visible — visibly bad in the case of "podium sessions in a row."
5. **Listening to one voice for more than 5 minutes drains energy.** Hence Celebrity Interviews replacing panels; hence Take 20 Seconds; hence batched questions rather than Q-A-Q-A.
6. **Report-outs from breakouts are BORING.** "Most of the time, we don't really need to hear serial summaries of all the discussions." Bring the experience into the next conversation instead.
7. **Wise-Crowds consulting teams do not decide for the client.** Their job is to think out loud while the client listens; the client decides what intrigued, surprised, or resonated.
8. **Strategic scenarios built by experts alone do not get used.** "Lack of ownership on the part of the whole community means that the scenarios don't really play a part in decision making." Build them participatorily.
9. **25-to-10 is not a decision tool.** "This is not recommended to make a decision. It's a way to get a sense of the wisdom of the crowd."
10. **Wicked questions are not trick questions.** Trick questions have hidden answers; wicked questions have embedded paradoxes and no obvious answers. Complaints about other groups are not wicked.
11. **Yelling and microphone-tapping are not facilitator tools.** Find another way to call the group back.
12. **Facilitator silence after a question is load-bearing.** Average start-speaking time is 6 seconds; prescription is ≥20. Talking too soon "communicate[s], unwittingly, that you're the one with the answers."
13. **Strategies for simple/complicated problems do not work on complex ones.** "Complex" is emergent and unpredictable (Stacey/Zimmerman); recipe-and-airplane methods fail on child-raising-class problems.
14. **The "Unusual Suspects" question is one of the most important you can ask.** Engagement design starts upstream of the room.
