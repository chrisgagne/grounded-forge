
# OpenStax, Business Ethics (light reference)

**Source:** OpenStax (2018). *Business Ethics*. Rice University. Senior contributing authors Stephen M. Byars (USC Marshall) and Kurt Stanberry (University of Houston–Downtown), with seven contributing authors. Licence: CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/business-ethics.
**Structure:** Eleven substantive chapters (Why ethics matter; ethics from antiquity to the present; defining and prioritising stakeholders; three special stakeholders — society, environment, government; impact of culture and time; what employers owe employees; what employees owe employers; recognising the rights of all; professions under the microscope — entrepreneurship, advertising, insurance, health care; changing work environments and future trends; epilogue on becoming an ethical professional). Three substantive appendices: A (lives of ethical philosophers), B (profiles of ten contemporary ethical leaders), C (the lead author's succinct theory). Coverage: substantive content only — Key Terms, Summary, Assessment Questions, Endnotes per chapter excluded; Answer Key and Index excluded.

## Author's thesis (condensed)

Business ethics is the conduct by which companies and their agents abide by the law and respect the rights of their stakeholders. The text's central conviction is that ethical business is not in tension with profitability across the long run; the most-admired companies tend to be among the most profitable, and ethical conduct generates *goodwill* — reputation, brand value, employee commitment, customer loyalty — which in turn supports profit. Milton Friedman's claim that the only "social responsibility of business is to increase profits" is registered as historically influential but treated as inadequate to the realities of contemporary business and the social contract a corporation enters into when society grants it the right to incorporate.

Five load-bearing claims structure the book. First, *all stakeholders matter*, not just shareholders; identifying, categorising, and prioritising stakeholder claims is a core managerial competence. Second, a *single, universally applied ethical standard* is rationally superior to ethical relativism — utilitarianism, deontology, and virtue ethics all converge on this conclusion. Third, the *moral minimum* (legal compliance) is rarely sufficient; ethical conduct often requires going *above and beyond* the law, especially where the law lags. Fourth, the lead author's "succinct theory" prefers *deontology over utilitarianism* in business: it is the means that ennoble the ends, not consequences alone. Fifth, *the formation of conscience* matters more than compliance training; ethics is "a way of life, not a hurdle to overcome", and *tone at the top* is the most effective lever.

## Foundations: integrity, ethics, the law

Ethics is the set of standards of behaviour to which we hold ourselves. Integrity is unity between what we say and do. Stakeholders are individuals and entities affected by a business's decisions: customers, clients, suppliers, employees, investors, retailers, communities, governments, media, the environment. Compliance is the baseline minimum; ethical observance builds on it. An action may be legal yet unacceptable. The text introduces three normative families recurring throughout: examining ends (utilitarianism and consequentialism), examining means (deontology, Kant), and examining character (virtue theory, Aristotle and Confucius). Aristotle's *phronesis* — practical wisdom acquired from experience — is the foundational ethical virtue invoked across chapters.

## Ethical theory: antiquity to the present

Aristotelian virtue ethics is built on *eudaimonia* (flourishing), the *golden mean* between vices of excess and defect, and the formation of character through habituated action. Confucian virtue ethics centres on *li* (proper order), the *junzi* (gracious, magnanimous person), and *quan* (expediency or counterbalance to achieve fairness). Both East and West share the theme of self-control. Bentham and Mill develop *utilitarianism* — the greatest good for the greatest number — with Mill adding the *harm principle*. Kant develops *deontology* and the *categorical imperative*: act only according to a maxim you can will to be a universal law; treat others as ends, never merely as means. Rawls's *justice as fairness* introduces the *original position* and *veil of ignorance* as a method for arriving at fair social arrangements; the *difference principle* permits inequality only insofar as it benefits the least-advantaged. Robert Nozick is registered as the principal critic of Rawlsian redistribution.

## Stakeholders: definition, weighing claims, prioritisation, CSR

Donaldson and Preston's three approaches to stakeholder claims — *descriptive* (model and balance interests), *instrumental* (good stakeholder management improves the bottom line), *normative* (stakeholders are ends in themselves) — frame the chapter; the book endorses the normative approach as fundamental. Grunig and Hunt's organisational linkage model categorises stakeholders as *enabling* (regulators, board, stockholders), *normative* (industry peers, competitors), *functional* (suppliers, employees, customers, distributors), and *diffused* (NGOs, voters, mass media). Four publics — nonpublic, latent, aware, active — describe degrees of stakeholder mobilisation. *Stakeholder prioritisation* uses a power × interest matrix combined with *exigency* (urgency). Two distinctions anchor the practical chapter: *ethical maximum* (strongest available action) versus *ethical minimum* (legally minimal); *moral minimum* names the ethical floor a firm must meet to claim ethical positioning at all. CSR, the *triple bottom line* (people, planet, profit, due to John Elkington), and the warning against *greenwashing* close the chapter.

## Three special stakeholders: society, environment, government

A century-long judicial debate over *shareholder primacy* runs from *Dodge v. Ford Motor Company* (1919, strict shareholder primacy) through *Shlensky v. Wrigley* (1968, business judgment rule discretion) to *Burwell v. Hobby Lobby* (2014, "corporate law does not require for-profit corporations to pursue profit at the expense of everything else"). The *moral minimum* is honouring obligations to all stakeholders. *Sustainability* is good business: McKinsey and Grant Thornton surveys document cost-cutting as the leading sustainability driver; the Global 100 outperforms typical index funds. *Carbon footprint*, *cap and trade*, *carbon tax*, and *Pigovian taxes* form the policy toolkit; the Climate Leadership Council carbon-tax proposal of $45/metric ton would yield ~$200 billion/year in revenue. *Corporate personhood* (extended to free speech in *Citizens United*, 2010) raises the symmetrical question whether the environment should have legal standing — Cullinan and Berry's *earth jurisprudence* argument; Ecuador's 2008 constitution; Christopher D. Stone's "Should Trees Have Standing?" Sarbanes-Oxley (2002) governs reporting transparency post-Enron; the BP Deepwater Horizon disaster illustrates the cost of regulatory capture (Norway and Brazil mandate acoustic shutoff valves; the US did not).

## Culture and time: geography, history, religion, universal ethics

*Enculturation* (acquiring native culture) versus *acculturation* (cross-cultural exchange) frames how culture shapes business ethics. Business is not culturally neutral; the dominant English-speaking, individualistic frame must be balanced against local cultural norms. Time as resource, contact-versus-noncontact cultures, *localisation* of products and practices recur across chapters. The historical sweep covers mercantilism (extractive ethics in colonial trade), Industrial Revolution (Tarbell on Rockefeller's "scruples in an electric dynamo"), and the Information Age (codes of professional conduct and individual ethical formation). The Petrobras Operation Car Wash and Siemens international-bribery scandals exemplify the corruption risk in cross-cultural business. The text takes the strong position that absolute values exist (cheating, defrauding, lying, murder are objectively wrong) and that compliance-as-checkbox ethics training is inadequate; *humanistic business* and the addition of humanities (literature, history, philosophy) to business education are recommended for forming moral agency.

## What employers owe employees

The OSHA framework (1970) requires safe workplaces. EEOC enforcement of Title VII covers harassment; *sexual harassment* is widespread and the law's enforcement imperfect (Ford's two Chicago plants drew $22 million in 1990s and another $10 million settlement in 2017). The WARN Act (1989) requires sixty days' advance notice of mass layoffs. Happy employees are more productive; the book catalogues practices that build a satisfied workforce — recognising progress, soliciting ideas, encouraging exercise and sleep, allowing employees to disengage. Generations differ in valued benefits.

The federal minimum wage of $7.25/hour has declined 23% in inflation-adjusted terms since 1960; 29 states have higher minima. Pew Research finds middle-income workers' wages rose 6% over 35 years versus high-income workers' 41%. The *gender pay gap* persists at ~80%; women in senior positions earn ~$600,000 against men's $800,000. The Equal Pay Act protects "equal pay for equal work" but allows job-title manipulation; New Jersey's Diane B. Allen Equal Pay Act extends protection to "substantially similar work". Iceland (2018) makes paying men more than women illegal. Eight US states ban salary-history questions in hiring. Dan Price at Gravity Payments raised the floor to $70,000 and cut his own pay to $70,000; Pharmalogics, Tower Paddleboards, and other firms have implemented similar pay experiments — most found revenue and retention rose.

US union membership fell from 33% in the 1950s to ~10% in 2016; public-sector membership is 35%, private 6.5%. *Codetermination* — German-style worker board representation — is documented as producing fewer strikes, better pay, safer conditions. The US CEO-to-worker pay ratio is ~300×, against UK 22×, France 15×, Germany 12×. CEO pay rose ~1,000% from 1978 to 2014 against worker pay's 11%. The text takes a strong position that the pay disparity hurts teamwork.

The Electronic Communications Privacy Act of 1986 governs workplace monitoring with two exceptions — *business purpose* and *consent*. Employers can monitor company-owned equipment broadly. *Employment at will* covers ~85% of US private-sector workers; drug testing is legal in most states. *Ontario v. Quon* (2010) limits expectations of privacy on employer-issued devices.

## What employees owe employers

The *duty of loyalty* (common law of agency) requires that employees not act contrary to the employer's interest; it rises to *fiduciary duty* at higher trust levels. The *duty of confidentiality* protects trade secrets, IP, employee records, customer data, often via *nondisclosure agreements*. *Non-compete agreements* cover ~20% of the US private-sector workforce and ~17% of those earning under $40,000; California (2017) voided most post-employment non-competes. The Jimmy John's case illustrates the over-application of non-competes to low-wage workers.

Branding and customer service: employees should help build the brand; *internal marketing* is getting employees to believe in the product. Negative employee social-media events (Taco Bell, Domino's, Burger King) damage brands swiftly. Employees do not owe customers tolerance of harassment (the Pizza Hut/Lockard case).

NIOSH categorises workplace violence into four types: traditional criminal intent, worker-on-worker, personal-relationship, customer/patient. Codes of conduct cover compliance, fraud, conflict of interest, company property, cybersecurity, social media, sexual harassment.

*Insider trading* (Section 10(b)5 of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934) extends to corporate insiders and "temporary insiders" (bankers, brokers, attorneys, accountants, journalists). The Drexel Burnham Lambert case ($1.1 billion total penalties on Milken; DBL bankrupted) is the canonical example. *Bribery* under the *Foreign Corrupt Practices Act* (1977) is broadly prohibited; the OECD Anti-Bribery Convention (1997) extends parallel laws to 43 signatories. BHP Billiton, GlaxoSmithKline, Telia are recent enforcement targets. Federal employee gift policies model corporate gift-acceptance norms.

Ten US states (and federal contractors per Obama's 2014 executive order) ban *pay secrecy*; NLRB rulings since 2012 protect pay discussions. The First Amendment does *not* protect at-will employees from criticism-related termination (Adrian Duane / IXL case). *Whistleblowing* is protected by over 20 federal laws; the False Claims Act (1863) qui tam provision allows relators to recover 15-30% of government recoveries. Birkenfeld ($104 million), the Pfizer ten ($102 million), and HCA ($100 million) illustrate the scale. The Sherron Watkins / Enron case shows the protection gap when whistleblowers go to the press; the Sallie Krawcheck / Merrill Lynch case shows the career cost even when staying internal.

## Recognising the rights of all

The *diversity dividend*: McKinsey & Company finds top-quartile gender diversity correlates with 15% higher financial returns; top-quartile racial/ethnic diversity with 35% higher; executive/board top-quartile with 50% higher returns on equity. Diversity must be paired with *inclusion* — Howard Ross's "diversity is invitation, inclusion is dance". Tech sector lags: Google is ~30% women, fewer than 20% in technical roles, ~56% White, ~35% Asian; backlash includes a 2017 leaked memo and class-action suit.

The ADA (1990) requires *reasonable accommodation* unless it causes *undue hardship*. The Verizon $20 million EEOC settlement (2011) for an inflexible no-fault attendance policy is the largest single ADA settlement. Title VII protects religious belief (and per *Welsh v. United States*, nontheistic equivalents). The Abercrombie & Fitch hijab case extended this. *Bostock v. Clayton County* (2020) interpreted "sex" in Title VII to include sexual orientation and gender identity.

US income inequality: top 10% earns 10× the bottom 90%; top 1% earns >40×. Top 1% share of US income rose from 8% to 22% over 35 years. Middle-income share of US households fell from ~60% in 1970 to 47% in 2014. Lloyd Blankfein and Bill Gross are quoted on the dangers of income inequality; Robert Reich is cited for the diagnostic that "the American middle class is the core of our customer base". Costco, IKEA, Starbucks, Target, McDonald's, and Wells Fargo / PNC / Fifth Third Bank ($15/hour) have voluntarily raised minimum wages. Department of Labor data: benefits cost employers ~32% beyond direct wages — the 30%+ saved by hiring contractors instead of employees "is often the margin between being in the middle class and falling below it". Neumark and Wascher are registered as a counter-position favouring Earned Income Tax Credit over minimum-wage increases.

The animal-rights movement spans ASPCA, HSUS, the Humane Slaughter Act and Animal Welfare Act. The book describes EU/Norway/Switzerland/India/etc. cosmetic-testing bans and the failed US Humane Cosmetics Act. Pet-industry sales tripled in 15 years to ~$70 billion projected for 2018.

## Professions under the microscope

*Entrepreneurial culture*: 75% of venture-backed start-ups fail (Ghosh, HBS); 50% of US companies fail within 5 years and 70% within 10. The Uber/Kalanick case illustrates the toxic-culture failure mode. Max Weber on bureaucracy as advanced-capitalism feature is approvingly cited. The Paul Orfalea / Kinko's reflection on anger and the cost of building a 23,000-employee start-up over 25 years is offered as cautionary advice for would-be entrepreneurs.

Advertising: *psychological appeals* are the most successful technique. John Kenneth Galbraith's *dependence effect* warns that advertising may create demand for frivolous goods. Children under 7-8 view advertising as credibly as trustworthy adults. *Subliminal advertising* claims (Vance Packard, James Vicary) are inconclusive but a 2015 study found brief, primed beverage-choice effects.

Insurance: profits arise when claims are few; the social-good versus profit tension underlies the industry's ethical questions. Hurricane Harvey's $30+ billion in property losses were only 40% insured because flood insurance is a federal add-on (FEMA, since 1968). The California Earthquake Authority parallels FEMA at state level. *Redlining* in auto insurance is documented in a 2017 Consumer Reports/ProPublica study showing minority-neighbourhood pricing disparities exceed underlying risk differences.

Health care: the US multipayer system contrasts with single-payer systems in UK and Canada; Commonwealth Fund data show the US spends most yet has poorer outcomes. Prescription drug prices are 17% of US healthcare spending (up from 7% in the 1990s). Wellness programs reduce premium costs but raise privacy concerns (Hiltzik's caution). The *Affordable Care Act (ACA, 2010)* — preceded by the 2006 Massachusetts plan — is the central reform. California's Healthy California Act (SB 562) would create a $370-400 billion single-payer state plan.

## Changing work environments and future trends

*Telecommuting* is documented as productivity-positive: HBR call-centre study, +13.5% calls; JD Edwards, 20-25% more productive; American Express, 43% more productive. About 4 million US workers telecommute at least part-time; 1 in 5 globally. Drawbacks: collaboration loss (Yahoo, IBM, Aetna, Bank of America reversals), corporate culture transmission, manager bias against remote workers.

*Workplace campuses* (Apple, Microsoft, Facebook, Google/Alphabet, Amazon) raise the parallel to 19th-century company towns and the *monopsony* labour-market concern. Facebook's Willow Village (1500 units) and Acuity Insurance's campus (Sheboygan, with a working Ferris wheel) illustrate the trend.

*Job sharing* and *flextime*: pros (less absenteeism, retention, family obligations, training) and cons ("two Mondays effect"; employer abuse to avoid benefits). The *access economy* (Airbnb, Uber, RelayRides) and the *gig economy* (~35% of US workforce per Freelancers Union; projected 40% by 2020) raise major ethical questions about benefits, payroll taxes, and the *new precariat*. Microsoft's $97 million 2000 settlement for misclassification is the cautionary case. The text takes a strong position that unpaid internships are exploitative.

*Robotics* and *AI*: McKinsey 2018 estimates 800 million jobs lost globally by 2030 (20% of workforce); US 39-73 million. Service / healthcare / education / care jobs and skilled trades are less affected. The text raises governance questions: should companies disclose AI interaction? EU lawmakers have begun establishing legal frameworks for robot liability, including for self-driving cars.

## Becoming an ethical professional (Epilogue and Appendix C)

Ethical formation is a developmental process; conscience is shaped over time. The Rajat Gupta and General Motors ignition-switch cases illustrate the failure modes. The text takes a strong position on workplace relationships and ethics: dissatisfied employees and unethical superiors produce unethical workplaces; example at the top is the most effective lever. Personal mission statements (Branson, Morrison) anchor career navigation. Costco is held up as positive exemplar — insurance for part-time workers, above-industry pay, 9.58/10 employee satisfaction. UPS and Xerox are cited as eleven-year-running members of Ethisphere's "World's Most Ethical Companies".

The lead author's "succinct theory" (Appendix C) endorses deontology over utilitarianism in business; warns that profits-versus-ethics is a false dichotomy; insists all stakeholders are significant; argues that excessive pay disparity hurts teamwork; calls for stewardship of social and physical environments; and identifies "restoring the broken sense of obligation that owners and employees owe each other" as "the most important task of business leadership now and going forward".

## Signature contrarian positions

1. **Shareholder primacy as exclusive duty is rejected.** All stakeholders are significant; the moral minimum requires honouring obligations to non-shareholder stakeholders.
2. **Ethical relativism is rejected.** A single ethical standard applies across cultures, contexts, and times.
3. **Compliance-as-checkbox training is inadequate.** Culture and conscience-formation matter more.
4. **Deontology is preferred over utilitarianism in business** (the lead author's credo). The means ennoble the ends; consequences alone are insufficient justification.
5. **The profits-versus-ethics dichotomy is false.** Ethical business is profitable business across the long run.
6. **Excessive CEO-worker pay ratios damage teamwork.** Pay disparity should not be many multiples; the US ~300× ratio is presented as an outlier.
7. **Pay secrecy degrades equity and motivation.** Transparency is recommended; ten US states and federal contracting law have moved this direction.
8. **Inflexible "no-fault" attendance policies that disregard ADA accommodation are unethical** (Verizon $20M settlement).
9. **Non-compete agreements applied to low-wage workers are exploitative** (Jimmy John's pattern); California's 2017 voiding of post-employment non-competes is endorsed.
10. **Greenwashing is rejected.** Authentic CSR is distinguished from PR substitute by stakeholder engagement and integrated commitment, not by donation amount.
11. **Misclassification of workers as contractors to avoid benefits is unethical** (the Microsoft cautionary tale).
12. **Unpaid internships function as exploitation when they substitute for paid labour**; pay or academic credit is required.
13. **Bribery as cultural accommodation is rejected.** The FCPA and OECD Convention frame the position; "when in Rome" is not a defence.
14. **Insurance redlining by neighbourhood demographics is unethical** even when not legally prohibited.
15. **Employer-provided wellness programmes carry privacy risk** that managers must respect; voluntariness and data limits matter.
16. **Hiring on merit must not be cover for bias.** The lead author's credo: "race, ethnicity, creed, sex, sexual orientation, age, and disability all are irrelevant to the abilities that they bring to the workplace".
17. **Subliminal advertising claims are inconclusive** but the demand-creation power of psychological appeals is documented and warrants ethical scrutiny.
18. **Restoring management-labour reciprocity** is the most important task of business leadership now and going forward (App C closing).
