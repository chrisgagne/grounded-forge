## Authors' thesis (paraphrased from source)

The five briefs together introduce *systems thinking* as a perspective and *system dynamics* as a set of tools for engaging with complex problems where linear cause-and-effect reasoning fails. The series' working argument is that the social challenges practitioners care about "feel like messy, wicked problems" because they share characteristics of *complexity* — dynamic over time, multi-stakeholder with conflicting goals, time delays between action and result, unintended consequences, and accumulations with history dependence — and that the traditional linear program-design tools (logic models, cause-effect interventions) fail on these problems despite the planner's best intentions [AR] (Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education"; Brief 1.02, "+System dynamics offers tools for working with complex problems").

The series' load-bearing pedagogical move is that *transforming systems requires transforming mental models* — the cognitive maps each person carries of how a system works — because system structures are "supported and held in place by our underlying beliefs, mindsets, and goals" [V] (Brief 1.03, opening header, citing Brief 1.01 *Systems Thinking Iceberg* which is not in the present ingest). Mental models are inherently incomplete (Brief 1.03's blindfolded-elephant parable), and system dynamics tools — causal loop diagrams, stock and flow diagrams, group model building — exist to "get our mental models out of our minds and onto the paper" so they can be challenged, negotiated, and made more complete [AR] (Brief 1.03, "+CONCEPT").

A second load-bearing claim is that the *feedback perspective* — looking at cause and effect "as not just moving in one direction" — replaces the linear "X causes Y" frame with a recognition that "we look for causal relationships in both directions" and that "feedback structure" explains why problems persist despite well-intentioned interventions [V] (Brief 1.05, "+Linear vs. Feedback Perspective"). Causal Loop Diagrams (CLDs) are the named tool for the feedback view, and the brief is explicit that a CLD "is a hypothesis — there is not one single 'right' answer" and is informed by each participant's mental model [V] (Brief 1.05, "+CONSIDERATIONS").

A third load-bearing claim is that *accumulations* — things that build up or drain over time, like water in a bathtub — provide critical insight into "how misperceptions, delays, and counter-intuitive system responses occur" [V] (Brief 1.06, opening). Stock-and-flow diagrams are the visual tool; the headline empirical claim, cited from Cronin, Gonzalez & Sterman (2009), is that even mathematically trained students "make persistent mistakes understanding even simple dynamic systems that include accumulations" [V] (Brief 1.06, "+CONSIDERATIONS WHEN APPLYING THE CONCEPT OF ACCUMULATIONS"). Reasoning intuitively about accumulation is unreliable, and the discipline is to make stocks, flows, and history-dependence visible.

A fourth load-bearing claim is that *system archetypes* — generic, recurring system structures expressed in CLD form — are pattern-recognition aids for "common patterns of problems" found "in schools, in businesses, in non-profit organizations, in health systems, and in communities" [V] (Brief 1.07, opening; Brief 1.07, "+ APPLYING SYSTEM ARCHETYPES IN K-12 EDUCATION"). The brief introduces five archetypes — *fixes that fail*, *success to the successful*, *shifting the burden*, *drifting goals*, *limits to growth* — adapted from Kim's *Systems archetypes I: Diagnosing systemic issues and designing high-leverage interventions* (1993) [BT] (Brief 1.07, "+ CONCEPT"; "+ SOURCES" reference 4). Archetypes are explicitly *not prescriptive*: "system archetypes are not meant to erase the uniqueness of a situation" and oversimplifying by jumping to an archetype "can impede the creative and iterative process that makes system dynamics modeling so useful" [V] (Brief 1.07, "+ CONSIDERATIONS").

A fifth thread running through every brief: *system dynamics is not the right tool for every situation*. The 1.02 brief states it bluntly via a quote attributed to a former Changing Systems Fellow: "If you are out on a hike and you break your ankle, call an ambulance. You don't need to make a system dynamics model about it!" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+CONSIDERATIONS"). The 1.07 brief warns against "jumping in to use archetypes when facilitating a group model building workshop or community session" because doing so "has the potential to reinforce a dynamic of expert vs. novice" where the modeller's diagnostic overshadows participants' lived experience [V] (Brief 1.07, "+ CONSIDERATIONS"). The series consistently positions itself against expertise-as-imposition.

## Coverage note on field-agnosticism and worked examples

Every brief uses K-12 education-equity worked examples (achievement gap, exclusionary school discipline, teacher burnout, school social-work limits, parent-teacher trust, mental-health waitlists). The series' *About the Series* footer states explicitly that the series "focuses on introducing core concepts of systems thinking and system dynamics as they relate to issues of education equity. This series draws from community-based modeling work with educators and students over the last ten years" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+ ABOUT THE SERIES"; identical text in every brief).

The deep reference treats the briefs as carrying *field-agnostic concept prose* — definitions, principles, structural explanations of systems thinking — together with *education-equity worked examples* that illustrate the concepts. The concept prose is the authors' claim; the worked examples are the authors' applied case material, supporting the claim but not defining it. This distinction is the load-bearing call that allowed the five included briefs into the present ingest while excluding 1.01 and 1.04 (where, per the operator's pre-ingest assessment, the education frame is structural to the concept's operational meaning rather than illustrative of it). Throughout this deep reference, education-example content is preserved where the source presented it; distillations downstream (Pass G) translate the concepts into the task's working vocabulary and reconstruct worked examples in that vocabulary.

## Part I: Foundation — characteristics of complex problems and the role of mental models

### Brief 1.02: (Some) characteristics of complex problems

The brief opens by framing social challenges as "messy, wicked problems" that share the feature of *complexity*, and offers five characteristics (with education examples) under the heading "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education" [AP] (Brief 1.02, opening; Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education"). The five characteristics:

1. **Changing over time (dynamic).** "The problems we are solving today look different than they did yesterday, and will look different tomorrow" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education"). The brief lists named components that change over time in education systems (students learn; staff are hired and leave; parents' priorities change; policies are enacted and revised; curriculum is introduced and replaced; communities grow and change), and concludes "all components of education systems change over time, so we must think about these factors as dynamic rather than static" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education") [AE].

2. **Multiple stakeholders with different goals whose decisions and actions impact each other.** "Administrators, teachers, students, parents, and teachers are experiencing systems differently, therefore have different incentives and motivations" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education"). The example given: "The district's goal in mandating a school-wide curriculum to improve test scores may conflict with the teacher's goal to meet the individual needs of each student" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education") [AE].

3. **Time delays between action and result.** "Despite our desire to see instant results, there is a lag time between our actions and their impacts" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education"). Example: "An investment in early childhood education will take years to improve graduation rates" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education") [AE]. The brief's framing of the failure mode: "Often funding or buy-in is gone long before an initiative can demonstrate impact" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education").

4. **Unintended consequences.** The brief's worked example is testing pressure: time "spent preparing for standardized tests. In the short term, test scores may improve. But there is an unintended consequence: with the added pressure on testing, teachers become burnt out and students become less engaged in learning. Ultimately, the test scores drop further" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education") [AE]. The pattern — short-term gain, long-term reversal driven by an unmodelled side effect — is the brief's introduction of the "fixes that fail" dynamic which Brief 1.07 names formally.

5. **Accumulations and history dependence.** "The behavior of a system is not only influenced by what is going on right at this moment, but the history of accumulated experiences of people in that system" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education"). The brief uses the parent outreach example: "A high school parent outreach coordinator may initiate large scale listening sessions and free brunches to build relationships with parents, but parents' perceptions and experience of the school may be influenced by the accumulated positive and negative experiences over years of interacting with the school -- both as a parent but also as a student themselves" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education") [AE]. This characteristic is the conceptual seed for Brief 1.06 (*Accumulations*).

### System dynamics as the named tool family

The brief positions *traditional program design tools* as inadequate for complex problems: "Traditional program design tools, such as logic models, help us develop, negotiate, and communicate our change efforts in a clear, linear way (i.e. Resources --> Activities --> Outputs --> Outcomes --> Impact). However, these tools can fail to account for the characteristics of complex problems, causing our best plans to fail or lead to surprising results" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+System dynamics offers tools for working with complex problems"). The contrast — linear logic models versus system-dynamics tools — is load-bearing and recurs across the series.

The brief's definition of system dynamics: "the use of causal maps and computer simulation models to engage with this complexity and understand system behavior from a feedback perspective. Visual tools for mapping and modeling such as causal loop diagrams and stock and flow diagrams help us create simplified representations of complex systems, allowing us to identify places to intervene, test out our ideas, and anticipate their consequences. System dynamics tools help us to consider the behavior of the 'whole' rather than fixating on the 'parts' when addressing complex problems" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+System dynamics offers tools for working with complex problems"). The whole-versus-parts framing is the series' core systems-thinking commitment.

### When system dynamics is not the right tool

The brief's *Considerations* section names the limit: "Because system dynamics is especially suited for gaining insight into complex problems, it is not the right method for every situation. Sometimes what's actually needed is a simple calculation (i.e. Which program is more cost effective?), a survey (i.e. What do people think about this new policy?), or even just a meeting between stakeholders. Sometimes the path forward is obvious, or a decision has to be made immediately" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+CONSIDERATIONS"). The illustrative quote attributed to Adam Raney, "former Changing Systems Fellow with the Social System Design Lab": "If you are out on a hike and you break your ankle, call an ambulance. You don't need to make a system dynamics model about it!" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+CONSIDERATIONS") [BT].

### Citation back to Sterman 2006

The brief's *Sources* note that "This presentation of (some) characteristics of complex problems was influenced by John Sterman's paper 'Learning from Evidence in a Complex World' from 2006" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+ ABOUT THE SERIES" / "+ SOURCES" — Sterman, J. D. (2006), *American Journal of Public Health*, 96(3), 505–514) [BT].

### Brief 1.03: Mental models

The brief's opening principle, in bold: "A central principle of the systems thinking perspective is that system structures are supported and held in place by our underlying beliefs, mindsets, and goals" [V] (Brief 1.03, opening header, cross-referencing the *Systems Thinking Iceberg* brief 1.01 which is not in the present ingest). "Therefore, in order to transform and redesign systems we must also transform our mindsets" [V] (Brief 1.03, opening). The brief's stated purpose is "to introduce the concept of mental models, and to discuss how system dynamics tools can be used to elicit, negotiate, and transform them" [V] (Brief 1.03, opening).

### The blindfolded-elephant parable

The brief opens its concept section by invoking "the famous parable of the blindfolded" — a phrase truncated in the converted markdown but contextually unambiguous as the blind-men-and-the-elephant parable; the brief's figure caption is: "Complex systems in which we work are like this elephant. Each of us has our own, valid mental picture of the parts, but our understanding of the whole is limited by our own unique vantage point" [V] (Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT", figure caption) [AE]. The brief is explicit that the elephant photograph is attributed in the sources to "Andrews, F. (2005). Elephant side-view Kruger [Photograph]. Wikimedia... CC BY-SA 3.0" [V] (Brief 1.03, "+ SOURCES").

### Definition of mental models

"Mental models are our internalized pictures that help us make sense of 'the elephant' – the messy, wicked problems that we encounter" [V] (Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT"). The brief lists education examples of "the elephant": "teacher turnover, school discipline, school mental health, chronic absenteeism, etc." [V] (Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT") [AE].

"Mental models are the cognitive 'maps' that help us each, individually, understand the world around us. They are how we imagine in our minds that complex systems work. Whether or not we are aware of them, mental models help us navigate the systems we encounter in everyday life. Our mental models influence how we define a problem, the language we use to describe it, and the actions we take to solve it" [V] (Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT") [AP]. The brief footnotes Johnson-Laird (1983) and Doyle & Ford (1998) as foundational citations for mental-models theory [BT] (Brief 1.03, "+ SOURCES").

### Mental models are incomplete

"Our mental models influence the actions we take to solve problems, but they are incomplete. None of us have complete knowledge of systems. Our understanding is informed by our lived experiences, values, assumptions, power, and biases" [V] (Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT"). The brief extends the elephant analogy: "Just like the blindfolded people in the parable, students, families, administrators, teachers, and policymakers each have distinct views of the nature of a problem and how to act to change the problem. Because our mental models are inherently incomplete, our efforts to solve problems risk being ineffective, counterproductive, or even harmful" [V] (Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT") [AR].

### Mental models can be improved through systems dynamics

"Luckily, Mental Models can be improved. The role of system dynamics is to improve our individual and collective mental models so our actions are more effective. When we create system dynamics maps and models, we get our mental models out of our minds and onto the paper. By making our mental models explicit, we can challenge them, negotiate them, and make them more complete" [V] (Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT") [AR]. This is the brief's load-bearing operational claim about system dynamics tooling.

### Group model building (GMB)

The brief introduces *Group Model Building* as the named participatory method: "Group model building (GMB) is a process for bringing people together to develop a model of the system, to create shared insights and common language, and to build consensus for implementing change. While such processes may incite difficult conversations and disagreement, they can help us to build a more complete picture of systems and, ultimately, create shared mental models for change. To build equitable and sustainable solutions to complex problems, we must uncover the whole 'elephant,' which requires amplifying the perspectives and voices of groups with differing vantage points" [V] (Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT"). The brief cites Hovmand (2014), *Community based system dynamics* [BT] (Brief 1.03, "+ SOURCES"), as the methodological source.

### The stickiness of mental models

The brief's *Considerations* section flags a critical caveat: "Mental models are very sticky. While it is valuable to unpack our mental models and the experiences, biases, or positions that may inform them, we cannot assume that just by sharing assumptions, alone, broadens one's mental model or set of values and beliefs. In fact, mental models are resistant to change, especially among individuals who aren't personally experiencing any negative system effects" [V] (Brief 1.03, "+CONSIDERATIONS") [AR]. The sticky-and-power-asymmetric framing — those not feeling system harm are more resistant to mental-model change — is one of the series' sharper claims.

### Meadows attribution

The brief closes its *Considerations* with a borrowed-through citation: "Mental models are a key concept of systems thinking and, as such, most effective when utilized alongside other system thinking tools and approaches. By bringing our mental models 'into the light of day,' as Donella Meadows explains, through the iterative practice of systems thinking, we are able to test them against other evidence" [V] (Brief 1.03, "+CONSIDERATIONS"). The Meadows citation in *Sources* is *Thinking in systems: A primer* (Chelsea Green, 2008) [BT] (Brief 1.03, "+ SOURCES").

### Acknowledgements: lineage of the elephant visual

The brief's acknowledgements section is explicit about lineage: "The elephant visual in *Figure 2: System dynamics to improve mental models* was developed by Ellis Ballard, adapting visuals presented by Dr. Peter Hovmand, who built on concepts described by Philip Johnson-Laird" [V] (Brief 1.03, "+ACKNOWLEDGEMENTS"). The visual lineage — Johnson-Laird (1983) → Hovmand → Ballard — is itself a worked instance of the borrowed-through pattern [BT].

## Part II: Mechanism — feedback thinking and accumulations

### Brief 1.05: Understanding systems from a feedback perspective

The brief opens with a definitional claim about systems thinking: "Systems perspectives encourage us to see the world as an interconnected system of causal relationships" [V] (Brief 1.05, opening). "A fundamental idea of system dynamics perspective the focus on feedback thinking – looking at cause and effect as not just moving in one direction" [V] (Brief 1.05, opening) [AP] (sentence is grammatically truncated in the source).

The brief's stated purpose is to "introduce the concept of feedback thinking through the lens of challenges in K-12 education, and present Causal Loop Diagrams as tools to describe feedback relationships between components of a system" [V] (Brief 1.05, opening).

### Linear thinking versus feedback thinking

"In many fields, we are conditioned to approach problems through linear thinking, which we could also call 'event' or 'cause-effect' thinking. When looking at problems, we look for causal relationships in one direction: 'X' causes 'Y'. If we take action on 'X', logic suggests we will expect a direct effect on 'Y'. We list all the factors that influence the outcome we want to change, and launch interventions to address each one in order to create a comprehensive strategy" [V] (Brief 1.05, "+Linear vs. Feedback Perspective") [AP].

"In contrast, the feedback perspective complicates this idea of 'X' causes 'Y'. Feedback thinking recognizes relationships and interdependencies between the parts. Using feedback thinking, we look for causal relationships in both directions. Rather than addressing factors in isolation, we look to feedback structure to understand why problems persist despite our best efforts – why we might push on one part of the system and see a response in another, in sometimes unexpected ways. When designing interventions using feedback thinking, we seek to change the structure of the system by adding or removing feedback loops; strengthening or weakening loops, and adding or removing links" [V] (Brief 1.05, "+Linear vs. Feedback Perspective") [AR].

The intervention-design move — change structure by adding/removing/strengthening/weakening loops and links — is the brief's operational claim about what a feedback-thinking practitioner does.

### Worked example: the school dropout case

The brief presents a paired example contrasting linear and feedback approaches to a school district's goal "to reduce the dropout rate by 5% in the next three years" [V] (Brief 1.05, "+AN EXAMPLE OF LINEAR VS FEEDBACK IN EDUCATION") [AE]:

*Approach 1 (Linear Thinking):* "The leadership team discusses the factors they believe are associated with the rise in dropouts: low student engagement, poor teacher performance, low academic performance, and high levels of student trauma. To solve the problem, they decide to introduce a new, intensive, trauma-informed district-wide curriculum. The idea is that the new curriculum will directly address student trauma, and intensive training will strengthen teacher performance, resulting in more student engagement and academic performance. The curriculum seems to be working at first, decreasing the dropout rate in Year 1. However, by the end of the three years, the dropout rate is even higher than where it started" [V] (Brief 1.05, "+AN EXAMPLE OF LINEAR VS FEEDBACK IN EDUCATION") [AE].

*Approach 2 (Feedback Thinking):* "The leadership team takes a step back to examine the intervention more holistically, through a feedback lens. They convene a group of administrators, students, staff and parents to build a **Causal Loop Diagram** to describe the multiple consequences of the new curriculum strategy" [V] (Brief 1.05, "+AN EXAMPLE OF LINEAR VS FEEDBACK IN EDUCATION") [AE].

The resulting CLD "tells a story" with three named loops [V] (Brief 1.05, "+AN EXAMPLE OF LINEAR VS FEEDBACK IN EDUCATION"):

- *"Short-term fix"* — the district introducing the curriculum with intent to improve teaching quality, increase engagement, and reduce dropouts.
- *"Unintended Consequence on Instruction"* — the curriculum significantly increasing teacher workload, leading to burnout and stress, undermining teaching quality, and exacerbating the dropout rate.
- A third loop in which "as teachers become more stressed, they are unable to build and maintain strong relationships with students at-risk of dropping out. Consequently, students became less engaged and the dropout rate increases even further" [V] (Brief 1.05, "+AN EXAMPLE OF LINEAR VS FEEDBACK IN EDUCATION") [AE].

The example is the brief's worked instance of the unintended-consequences characteristic from Brief 1.02 and the *fixes-that-fail* archetype that Brief 1.07 names formally.

### Conclusion of the brief

"Feedback lens helps us think through why interventions may have undesirable results even in situations where we are highly motivated and have the right technical knowledge" [V] (Brief 1.05, "+AN EXAMPLE OF LINEAR VS FEEDBACK IN EDUCATION", concluding paragraph, with citation to Sterman 2000, *Business Dynamics*) [BT] (Brief 1.05, "+ SOURCES").

### CLDs as hypotheses, not answers

The brief's *Considerations* section makes a load-bearing epistemic claim about CLDs:

> "A causal loop diagram (CLD) is a systems thinking tool to help us approach problems from a feedback perspective. Remember, a CLD is a hypothesis – there is not one single 'right' answer to the variables that are included or the relationships that exist between them. The model we produce is informed by each of our individual mental models, which come from our unique vantage point and lived experiences" [V] (Brief 1.05, "+ CONSIDERATIONS").

The brief also names the iterative discipline: "Causal loop diagramming takes practice! Often we start developing a diagram and have variables that don't actually vary, and causal links that are more like associations or correlations. The goal of using these tools is to fine tune your thinking, rather than get it 'right' on the first time" [V] (Brief 1.05, "+ CONSIDERATIONS") [AR]. The contrast with conventional "build the right model" framing is the brief's contrarian move on CLD epistemics.

### Getting started prompts

The brief closes with four diagnostic prompts for practitioners [V] (Brief 1.05, "+ GETTING STARTED"):

1. "Think of a complex problem you want to address in your work or life. What are some of the causes of that problem?"
2. "Where can you identify potential feedback effects, where an initial causal link feeds back to form a loop? Try to draw a feedback loop using the conventions above."
3. "How does feedback propose different ways of looking at potential solutions than linear or cause-effect thinking?"
4. "How do you see this concept fitting into your teaching/facilitation/management/leadership?"

The fourth prompt's list — *teaching/facilitation/management/leadership* — is the brief's most explicit signal that the concept is meant to apply beyond education to general practice.

### Brief 1.06: Accumulations

The brief opens by naming three features of complex systems that make change difficult in K-12 education: "delays between the intervention and outcome, unexpected or confusing responses to interventions, and differences in how individuals and groups experience the same systems" [V] (Brief 1.06, opening) [AP]. *Accumulation* "provides important insight into how misperceptions, delays, and counter-intuitive system responses occur in education change efforts" [V] (Brief 1.06, opening). Stock and Flow Diagrams are introduced as the "visual tools to identify and visually describe key accumulations in the systems and facilitate conversations to understand systems better" [V] (Brief 1.06, opening).

### What accumulation means: the bathtub

The brief's definition is via Meadows' canonical bathtub example: "Accumulation is one of the core concepts of system dynamics. An accumulation is something that builds up or drains over time, like a bathtub. So, let's use Donella Meadows' example of the bathtub. If you want the stock of water to increase, you add water from the faucet. If you want it to decrease, you remove water through the drain. The rate at which you add water from the faucet matters. If you had a gushing faucet and a clogged drain, of course, the water in the tub would rise. If you unplug the drain to allow a bit of a trickle out but the faucet is still gushing, the water in the tub continues to increase" [V] (Brief 1.06, "+ WHAT DO WE MEAN BY ACCUMULATION?") [BT] (citing Meadows 2008, *Thinking in Systems*) (Brief 1.06, "+ SOURCES").

The brief extends the bathtub: "You've started to solve the problem by opening the drain, but the overall behavior of the system still continues, just at a slower rate than before. If you turn off the faucet, the water in the stock will not magically go away. The only way to decrease the level of the tub is to have water flowing out of the drain faster than the water coming in" [V] (Brief 1.06, "+ WHAT DO WE MEAN BY ACCUMULATION?") [AP]. The operational insight: stock changes only when net flow (inflow minus outflow) is non-zero, and the direction of stock change is the sign of the net flow.

### Tangible versus intangible accumulations

The brief lists examples to make the concept concrete: "Accumulations can also be intangible – or hard to see or touch but nevertheless real – such as traumatic experiences of a child or a family's trust in a school district. For example, when it is time to pay your rent or mortgage, you want to have enough money accumulated in your bank account! When your dirty clothes accumulate too much, you do laundry. Or maybe you are a person who does laundry every week to prevent an accumulation of dirty clothing" [V] (Brief 1.06, "+ WHAT ARE EXAMPLES OF ACCUMULATION?") [AE]. The everyday-life and education examples mix deliberately to show the concept's general applicability.

### Stock-and-flow diagrams: terminology

"Stocks and flows are a way of diagramming accumulations. A stock is just another word for an accumulation. A flow is an action that changes the level of a stock. Flows either build up a stock or deplete it" [V] (Brief 1.06, "+ REPRESENTING ACCUMULATION WITH STOCK AND FLOW DIAGRAMS"). The diagrammatic convention is illustrated by Figure 1 (*The Stock and Flows of a Bathtub*) [V] (Brief 1.06, figure caption); the figure itself was not OCR-extractable from the converted markdown.

### Why accumulation matters: history dependence

The brief presents three examples of accumulation as the lens for thinking about history dependence [V] (Brief 1.06, "+ WHY DOES THIS ACCUMULATION CONCEPT MATTER?"):

1. "We can teach a math lesson today to prepare a student for tomorrow's test. But the number of math concepts the student has accumulated from the past three years will have a far greater impact than the time spent studying today" [V] [AE].

2. "When meeting a new parent for a parent-teacher conference, it can be easy to assume that are coming to you with a blank slate – an empty tub. But that parent might have had years or generations of distrust that has built over time, and you are meeting that parent as an accumulation of their history, not just the short interaction of that moment" [V] [AE]. The framing — *meeting that parent as an accumulation of their history* — is one of the briefs' sharper restatements of history-dependence.

3. "When managing school mental health services, it does not tell you much to know that three students signed up for therapy today. It matters a lot more to know that those three students joined a group of 20 students that have accumulated on a waiting list for therapy. The system will react to the accumulation, not only the input" [V] [AE]. The flow-versus-stock distinction (signups today = flow; waitlist size = stock) is the brief's operational point.

### Worked example: teacher attrition stocks and flows

The brief presents a teacher-attrition stock-and-flow example (with the diagram itself not preserved in OCR): "If you ignore the outflow of 'Teachers leaving', you won't solve the problem – you will just speed up how quickly teachers are moving through the system. You can slow down the outflow of 'Teachers Leaving' by investigating what is causing teachers to leave and addressing those causes" [V] (Brief 1.06, "+ WHY DOES THIS ACCUMULATION CONCEPT MATTER?", section continuing into the diagram caption) [AE]. The example illustrates that hiring (inflow) without attention to attrition (outflow) does not change the stock of teachers in the long run.

### Considerations: when intuition fails

The brief offers four named cautions for applying stock-and-flow thinking [V] (Brief 1.06, "+ CONSIDERATIONS WHEN APPLYING THE CONCEPT OF ACCUMULATIONS"):

1. *Intuition is unreliable.* "Using intuition alone can lead to incorrect assumptions about accumulations. Accumulations occur naturally in the world around us but understanding them is not always intuitive. Studies show that even students with strong mathematical training make persistent mistakes understanding even simple dynamic systems that include accumulations. Understanding dynamic systems gets even harder once you add feedback loops and time delays. Visual stock and flow diagrams and mathematical models of accumulations can help you think more clearly and challenge your intuition" [V] [BT] (citing Cronin, Gonzalez & Sterman 2009, *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*).

2. *Quantification of intangibles is hard.* "Accurately quantifying stocks and flows for intangible variables or situations can be difficult. Though it can be done, there is often less certainty relative to models of tangible and measurable variables" [V] [AR].

3. *Cognitive accessibility.* "Accumulations and stock and flow diagrams can be harder for some people to conceptualize than causal loop diagrams. As with all system dynamics and group model building work, jargon and academic language can create a barrier to entry and an expert/novice dynamic that fails to recognize the value of people's lived experiences and understanding of problems" [V] [AR].

4. *Illusion of accuracy.* "Stock and flow diagrams can lead to the illusion of accuracy. These diagrams may look formal and mathematical, but it is important to remember that the quality of output of a model depends on the mental models, stories, and experiences of the people who informed it!" [V] [AR].

The fourth caution closes the loop back to Brief 1.03 (*Mental Models*) — the formality of the diagram does not exempt the diagram from inheriting the mental-models limitations of its builders.

### Getting started: three practice sequences

The brief offers three named practice sequences (*See accumulations around you*, *Practice Diagramming*, *Share Out*) with explicit verbs (*Explore*, *Consider*, *Reflect*, *Draw*, *Brainstorm*, *Share*) [V] (Brief 1.06, "+ GETTING STARTED"). The third sequence's prompt — "How do you see this concept fitting into your teaching/facilitation/management/leadership?" — repeats the cross-domain framing from Brief 1.05.

## Part III: Pattern recognition — system archetypes

### Brief 1.07: System archetypes

The brief opens by framing the pattern-recognition problem: "K-12 education presents an incredibly complex system that makes solving problems challenging. Often, we implement changes with the best of intentions, only to see problems get worse rather than better. Many of the structures that cause these patterns can be found all around us - in schools, in businesses, in non-profit organizations, in health systems, and in communities. By learning about these common structures or *system archetypes*, we can start to identify and anticipate them" [V] (Brief 1.07, opening) [AP]. The brief is explicit about the cross-domain applicability — *schools, businesses, non-profits, health systems, communities* — and the structural source of the pattern: "common structures."

The brief's stated purpose is "to describe how education stakeholders can use system archetypes as a tool for recognizing, anticipating, and addressing the system dynamics driving the common patterns of problems in their school communities" [V] (Brief 1.07, opening).

### Definition of system archetype

"System archetypes are visual descriptions of generic, recurring system structures in the form of Causal Loop Diagrams" [V] (Brief 1.07, "+ CONCEPT"). "Using system dynamics, we can start to identify and visually describe general patterns that are repeated in widely different contexts. System dynamicists and systems thinkers have observed some of these common dynamics and defined a set of 'system archetypes' that can be applied to multiple different scenarios" [V] (Brief 1.07, "+ CONCEPT") [AP].

### How many archetypes exist

The brief is explicit that the canonical list is not fixed: "There is no definitive list of system archetypes; there is a broad list of eight to twelve core archetypes, but some even argue that four archetypes can explain all the other archetypes" [V] (Brief 1.07, "+ CONCEPT"). The brief selects five "from the great resource 'Systems archetypes I: Diagnosing systemic issues and designing high-leverage interventions'" by Kim (1993) [V] [BT] (Brief 1.07, "+ CONCEPT"; Brief 1.07, "+ SOURCES" reference 4).

### Archetype 1: Fixes that fail

**Template definition:** "The 'fixes that fail' archetype describes attempts to address a problem through a short-term fix. The fix might solve the problem in the short run, but it also generates unintended consequences that actually make the original problem worse" [V] (Brief 1.07, "Archetype #1: Fixes that fail", *Template Archetype #1*).

**Worked example (exclusionary discipline):** "An example of the 'fixes that fail' archetype tells the story of exclusionary discipline in schools. Imagine a child who had a difficult morning at home, did not eat breakfast and arrived late to school. She is disruptive in class. A short-term solution for the teacher would be to send the child to time-out, suspension, or the principal's office for the rest of the day. The teacher is employing a short-term fix; they are removing the disruption from the classroom. However, over time the child becomes even more disengaged from school, feeling that there are no adults" [V] (Brief 1.07, "Archetype #1: Fixes that fail", *Example Archetype #1*) [AE]. The example continues into a truncated final line — "class." — which appears to be the tail of "[no adults] in this school who care about her, and her behavior gets worse the next time she is in class" or similar; the truncation is a conversion artefact, not a content gap.

### Archetype 2: Success to the successful

**Template definition:** "The 'success to the successful' archetype shows competition for resources between two groups (Group A and B). Group A, the group that starts out as more successful, is rewarded with more resources and becomes even more successful. Group B, the group that is less successful to begin with, gets trapped in a downward spiral" [V] (Brief 1.07, "Archetype #2: Success to the successful", *Template Archetype #2*).

**Worked example (educational tracking):** "The 'success to the successful' archetype can be used to describe many different educational disparities. In this example, imagine that student A starts kindergarten already knowing how to read and cooperate with other children ('High Achieving Student'). Student B does not recognize the alphabet and has not been in a classroom setting before ('Struggling Student'). Once the teacher gets to know both these children, the prepared Student A might get bumped up to a higher-level reading group, nominated for a special science program, and eventually tracked into advanced placement classes with teachers who have higher expectations. As student A succeeds, opportunities continue to come their way. Student B struggles in class. They do not get the opportunity to participate in the special science program, so student B is not excited about science. They become less interested in school and begin to withdraw. When the teacher passes them up to the next grade, they start a math class without having the foundations they need. You can see how initial disparities can become quickly reinforced" [V] (Brief 1.07, "Archetype #2: Success to the successful", *Example Archetype #2*) [AE].

The structural mechanism — initial disparities reinforced by resource allocation that favours those already succeeding — is the brief's core claim about success-to-the-successful.

### Archetype 3: Shifting the burden

**Template definition:** "The 'shifting the burden' archetype shows what happens when we address the symptoms of a problem with a quick-fix solution. This solution is easier and faster than creating an upstream solution to the problem, and we are often meeting a very real, urgent need for a student. However, the quick-fix to the symptoms may actually impede our ability to invest time and energy to the fundamental solution. This makes it even harder to apply a fundamental solution and even more tempting to rely on quick fixes. Thus, the problem cannot be completely solved" [V] (Brief 1.07, "Archetype #3: Shifting the burden", *Template Archetype #3*).

**Worked example (discipline as quick-fix for trauma):** "Archetype describes disciplinary responses to behavioral challenges as a quick-fix solution. Children who display behavioral challenges at school are often exhibiting symptoms of unaddressed trauma. A fundamental solution would be to invest in mental health services for children with behavioral challenges. However, trauma treatment takes time, so there is a delay between receiving mental health services and healing from past trauma. A more immediate 'solution' is to discipline children with behavioral challenges in order to put a stop to their disruptive behavior" [V] (Brief 1.07, "Archetype #3: Shifting the burden", *Example Archetype #3*) [AE].

The completion of the example documents the reinforcing trap: "Unfortunately, a child who is disciplined may feel disconnected or emotionally unsafe at school, making them less likely to trust the adults around them. Without trusted adults, the child is less likely to share their mental health concerns or be referred to mental health services. Behavioral problems are likely to persist without mental health treatment for the trauma. Overburdened schools in a constant state of crisis begin to rely on discipline rather than investing in the mental health services that could address the fundamental problem of trauma. The behavioral challenges persist" [V] (Brief 1.07, "Archetype #3: Shifting the burden", *Example Archetype #3*) [AE]. The structural lock-in — symptomatic fix erodes the trust required to access the fundamental solution — is the brief's account of how shifting the burden makes itself permanent.

### Archetype 4: Drifting goals

**Template definition:** "The 'Drifting Goals' archetype helps us describe the impact of lowering expectations. The difference between the desired outcome ('Goal') and actual performance ('Actual') is called a 'gap'. One way to close this gap is to take corrective action, which, after time, improves actual performance. Another way to close this gap is to lower the goal by settling for less. When the goal is lowered, the gap shrinks, leading to less need for corrective action" [V] (Brief 1.07, "Archetype #4: Drifting goals", *Template Archetype #4*).

**Worked example (lowered teacher expectations):** "We see the 'Drifting Goals' archetype in the educational system. When there is a gap between a [standard and a struggling student's performance, the corrective action would be to provide additional support] and the gap would close. Unfortunately, an overburdened teacher may not have time to take this action with all children who are struggling. Instead, the teacher may simply lower their expectations, recognizing that it is 'unrealistic' to get all these 3rd graders up to the standards given that they have been 'behind' since kindergarten. With a lowered goal comes a smaller gap and less incentive to take corrective action" [V] (Brief 1.07, "Archetype #4: Drifting goals", *Example Archetype #4*) [AE]. The bracketed reconstruction in the middle is to bridge a converted-markdown line gap; the surrounding prose is faithful to the source.

The structural insight is that goal-as-the-thing-that-flexes-under-stress is a hidden failure mode — the goal looks like a constant but is actually an endogenous variable that the system can lower instead of taking action.

### Archetype 5: Limits to growth

**Template definition:** "The 'Limits to Growth' archetype illustrates a reinforcing growth loop: as more effort is applied, performance improves and more effort is applied. As the growth continues, a balancing loop kicks in, driven by an external limiting condition or resource constraint. This creates a limiting action which decreases performance. Despite efforts to keep increasing growth, the balancing loop counteracts it and limits the amount of growth possible" [V] (Brief 1.07, "Archetype #5: Limits to growth", *Template Archetype #5*).

**Worked example (school social workers):** "An example of the 'limits to growth' archetype can be found in school social work. Imagine that a school has a budget for two social workers. As the students get to know the social workers and feel supported by them, word spreads that there is help available for kids who need it. Students seek out the social workers, who in turn provide support. Unfortunately, there is a resource constraint on the amount of time a social worker has. At first, the social workers can see more and more students who drop by their offices throughout the day. But at some point, they become too busy to provide quality individualized support. The social workers are now providing less support. Word begins to spread among students that the social workers are too busy and do not really care about the students, so students stop seeking support from the social workers. Without an increase in the number of social workers, there is a limit to the growth of the school mental health program" [V] (Brief 1.07, "Archetype #5: Limits to growth", *Example Archetype #5*) [AE]. The structural mechanism — a reinforcing loop driving growth into a balancing-loop ceiling created by a resource constraint — matches the *Limits to Growth* archetype template as the brief adapts it from Kim (1993) [BT] (Brief 1.07, "+ SOURCES" reference 4).

### How to use system archetypes in practice

The brief offers five named uses [V] (Brief 1.07, "+ HOW TO USE SYSTEM ARCHETYPES IN PRACTICE"):

1. *To avoid repeatedly falling into traps.* "System archetypes can show relatively simple underlying dynamics that help explain and communicate problems in multiple different domains. This can help us anticipate a problem or avoid common traps in our lives, organizations, and communities" [V] [AR].

2. *To find different solutions.* The truncated text in the source ("System archetypes [help us see] solutions" with intervening words lost in OCR) supports the inferred claim, but the relevant passage in the source is preserved well enough to confirm the intent: archetypes generate alternative intervention hypotheses by surfacing the structural mechanism.

3. *As a communication tool.* "System archetypes can serve as a simple, visual representation of a problem to facilitate communication among a team about solutions" [V] [AR].

4. *As a foundation for building more complex models.* "System archetypes can be combined with other archetypes and balancing or reinforcing loops to represent more specific problems" [V] [AR].

5. *As a tool to introduce system dynamics.* "System archetypes can be useful to introduce basic concepts of system dynamics to others because they may provide language for and visual representation of problems that people have experienced and intuitively understand" [V] [AR].

### Considerations: archetypes are not prescriptive

The brief's *Considerations* section is one of the series' sharper contrarian moves:

> "*System archetypes should not be used prescriptively:* System archetypes are not meant to erase the uniqueness of a situation. Oversimplifying a problem or jumping to the conclusion that it is best represented by an archetype can impede the creative and iterative process that makes system dynamics modeling so useful for thinking and dialogue" [V] (Brief 1.07, "+ CONSIDERATIONS").

> "*System archetypes may not be necessary:* Jumping in to use archetypes when facilitating a group model building workshop or community session has the potential to reinforce a dynamic of expert vs. novice. The modeler draws on their modeling 'expertise' to diagnose an archetype in a way that overshadows the lived experience of the participants, who actually understand their situation more deeply. These individuals or groups may not want or need intellectualized external labels to be placed on their model" [V] (Brief 1.07, "+ CONSIDERATIONS").

The two cautions — *not prescriptive*, *may not be necessary* — are explicit pushback against the systems-thinking tradition's tendency to treat archetypes as diagnostic taxonomies. The brief preserves archetypes as hypothesis-generators rather than as labels.

### Acknowledgements: Senge lineage

The brief's acknowledgements section names the canonical Senge lineage explicitly: "The system archetypes described here are inspired from and adapted from resources available on systems thinking. Some recommended resources include work by Peter Senge, including the book 'The Fifth Discipline' and 'Schools that Learn'. The Systems Thinker and Pegasus communications has useful resources, and the Waters Center for Systems Change has great resources on adapting systems archetypes to educational settings" [V] (Brief 1.07, "+ ACKNOWLEDGEMENTS") [BT].

The Senge attribution is critical for corpus integration — the SSDL briefs are the Senge-tradition translation under a clean open licence, which the corpus's earlier triage identified as a load-bearing gap.

## Positions the authors explicitly frame against

1. **Linear (cause-effect, "event") thinking as the default frame for complex problems.** "In many fields, we are conditioned to approach problems through linear thinking, which we could also call 'event' or 'cause-effect' thinking" [V] (Brief 1.05, "+Linear vs. Feedback Perspective"). The series' core contrarian move: linear thinking does not work on complex problems despite being the disciplinary default in many fields.

2. **Logic models as adequate for complex-problem design.** "Traditional program design tools, such as logic models, help us develop, negotiate, and communicate our change efforts in a clear, linear way (i.e. Resources --> Activities --> Outputs --> Outcomes --> Impact). However, these tools can fail to account for the characteristics of complex problems, causing our best plans to fail or lead to surprising results" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+System dynamics offers tools for working with complex problems"). Logic models are explicitly framed as insufficient for systems-thinking work.

3. **Sharing mental models as sufficient to broaden them.** "We cannot assume that just by sharing assumptions, alone, broadens one's mental model or set of values and beliefs. In fact, mental models are resistant to change, especially among individuals who aren't personally experiencing any negative system effects" [V] (Brief 1.03, "+CONSIDERATIONS"). The brief is explicit that bringing mental models out into discussion is necessary but not sufficient for transformation; power asymmetry shapes openness to change.

4. **Intuition as adequate for reasoning about accumulations.** "Using intuition alone can lead to incorrect assumptions about accumulations. Accumulations occur naturally in the world around us but understanding them is not always intuitive. Studies show that even students with strong mathematical training make persistent mistakes understanding even simple dynamic systems that include accumulations" [V] (Brief 1.06, "+ CONSIDERATIONS WHEN APPLYING THE CONCEPT OF ACCUMULATIONS"). Stock-and-flow thinking is required because untrained intuition is unreliable.

5. **Stock-and-flow diagrams as offering precision.** "Stock and flow diagrams can lead to the illusion of accuracy. These diagrams may look formal and mathematical, but it is important to remember that the quality of output of a model depends on the mental models, stories, and experiences of the people who informed it!" [V] (Brief 1.06, "+ CONSIDERATIONS WHEN APPLYING THE CONCEPT OF ACCUMULATIONS"). The formality of stock-and-flow visualisation does not exempt the model from mental-model limitations.

6. **CLDs as "right" or "wrong".** "A CLD is a hypothesis – there is not one single 'right' answer to the variables that are included or the relationships that exist between them" [V] (Brief 1.05, "+ CONSIDERATIONS"). The brief is explicit that CLDs are hypotheses to be refined through iteration, not models to be solved correctly on the first try.

7. **System archetypes as prescriptive diagnostic labels.** "System archetypes should not be used prescriptively: System archetypes are not meant to erase the uniqueness of a situation. Oversimplifying a problem or jumping to the conclusion that it is best represented by an archetype can impede the creative and iterative process that makes system dynamics modeling so useful for thinking and dialogue" [V] (Brief 1.07, "+ CONSIDERATIONS"). Archetypes are hypothesis-generators, not labels to be applied.

8. **Modeller-as-expert vs. participant-as-novice power dynamics.** "Jumping in to use archetypes when facilitating a group model building workshop or community session has the potential to reinforce a dynamic of expert vs. novice. The modeler draws on their modeling 'expertise' to diagnose an archetype in a way that overshadows the lived experience of the participants, who actually understand their situation more deeply. These individuals or groups may not want or need intellectualized external labels to be placed on their model" [V] (Brief 1.07, "+ CONSIDERATIONS"). The series' consistent position against expert-overshadowing-of-lived-experience.

9. **System dynamics as universally applicable.** "Because system dynamics is especially suited for gaining insight into complex problems, it is not the right method for every situation. Sometimes what's actually needed is a simple calculation... a survey... or even just a meeting between stakeholders. Sometimes the path forward is obvious, or a decision has to be made immediately" [V] (Brief 1.02, "+CONSIDERATIONS"). System dynamics is the right tool for complex problems, not the right tool for every problem.

10. **Academic language and jargon as natural.** "As with all system dynamics and group model building work, jargon and academic language can create a barrier to entry and an expert/novice dynamic that fails to recognize the value of people's lived experiences and understanding of problems" [V] (Brief 1.06, "+ CONSIDERATIONS WHEN APPLYING THE CONCEPT OF ACCUMULATIONS"). Plain language is a methodological commitment, not a stylistic preference.

## The curriculum arc: foundation, mechanism, pattern recognition

The five included briefs form a curriculum with a deliberate pedagogical sequence, though the briefs do not name themselves as such. The arc is reconstructed here from the structural reading:

**Foundation (Briefs 1.02 and 1.03).** What complex problems look like, and why one cannot reason about them without first surfacing the mental models that shape how one sees them. Brief 1.02 establishes the *target* of systems thinking — complex problems with the five characteristics — and motivates the need for new tools. Brief 1.03 establishes the *medium* of systems thinking — internal mental models that must be made explicit before they can be improved.

**Mechanism (Briefs 1.05 and 1.06).** Once the practitioner accepts that complex problems require non-linear reasoning and mental-model surfacing, the next layer is the structural mechanism: feedback (1.05) and accumulation (1.06). Feedback explains why effects loop back to causes and why interventions on isolated parts often fail. Accumulation explains why stocks of states (trust, skills, fatigue, money, students-on-waitlists) drive system behaviour as much as moment-to-moment flows do. The two together are sufficient for most system-dynamics analysis at the conceptual level.

**Pattern recognition (Brief 1.07).** Archetypes synthesise the prior briefs into named patterns. *Fixes that fail* is the unintended-consequence characteristic from 1.02 made explicit through a CLD (1.05); *Shifting the burden* requires understanding the *delay-between-fundamental-solution-and-result* characteristic from 1.02 plus the *trust accumulation* concept from 1.06; *Limits to growth* requires understanding the *reinforcing-then-balancing-loop* structure that 1.05 enables; *Drifting goals* requires understanding *goal as endogenous variable*. Archetypes are pattern-recognition shortcuts that the earlier briefs equip the practitioner to apply.

The two briefs excluded from this ingest (1.01 *Systems Thinking Iceberg*, 1.04 *Framing Dynamic Problems*) would sit at the start of the foundation layer (1.01) and at the boundary between foundation and mechanism (1.04). Their absence does not break the curriculum arc for the field-agnostic concept material; it omits the structural-equity framings that the operator's pre-ingest assessment identified as load-bearing in those two specific briefs.

## Notes on what this deep reference deliberately does *not* contain

- **Education-equity framing as a structural claim.** The series' education-equity context is the *applied domain* in which the SSDL's community-based modeling work happens; the briefs' concept prose is field-agnostic systems-thinking content (foundationally Sterman, Meadows, Senge, Johnson-Laird, Kim, Hovmand). The deep reference treats the education examples as worked illustrations (per the [AE] marker) and does not extend the briefs' claims to "archetypes apply uniquely or primarily to education systems" — a position the source does not take (Brief 1.07 is explicit that archetypes apply "in schools, in businesses, in non-profit organizations, in health systems, and in communities" [V]).

- **Cross-references to authors the source does not cite.** The deep reference preserves only authors the briefs themselves cite. Authors associated with systems-thinking tradition more broadly but not cited by the SSDL briefs (Forrester, Goodman, Argyris, Schein, the broader Lipton-tradition) do not appear here even where the corpus carries them or could carry them.

- **Operational distillation guidance.** Per the protocol's tier separation, diagnostic questions, anti-pattern lists, and task-projection guidance are not in the deep reference; they appear in the distillations downstream.
