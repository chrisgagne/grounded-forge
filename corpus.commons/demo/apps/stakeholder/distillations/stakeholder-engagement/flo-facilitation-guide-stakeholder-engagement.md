# Currie, Riessner, Bennett & Cougler Blom, FLO Facilitation Guide — Stakeholder-Engagement Distillation

**Source:** Bennett, G., Cougler Blom, B., Riessner, S., & Currie, S. (2019). *FLO Facilitation Guide*. Victoria, B.C.: BCcampus. **Licence:** CC BY 4.0. Open scope; attribution required on derivative works.

## Stakeholder-Engagement Relevance

The FLO Facilitation Guide is the corpus's most directly applicable source on the craft of engaging stakeholders in an ongoing facilitated conversation. Where TC 25-20 and the LFUO Learning Review Guide cover the event-shape of structured group dialogue, and the Scrum Guide carries the cadence of a development team, FLO supplies the move-by-move, week-by-week practice of holding a learning group over a multi-week arc. It is unusually concrete on the discipline of presence: when to step in, when to step back, what to model, what to ask, how to tone the room, when to fade, when to close.

The Guide's load-bearing stakeholder-engagement contributions are: (a) the scaffolding-and-fading presence allocation that shapes engagement intensity across the arc; (b) the modelling-as-mechanism through-line that frames facilitator authenticity as part of the curriculum; (c) the climate-as-operational stance that names trust, safety, and Vegas-rules as design choices rather than soft skills; (d) the open-ended-invitational-question discipline as the dominant question form; (e) the resist-thoroughness feedback rule that protects peer-to-peer engagement; (f) the develop-vs-grade default that maintains candour; (g) the track architecture as a worked example of designing varied stretch into one engagement; (h) the counsel-out move that names when to help a struggling participant defer; (i) the engagement-equity tracking matrix that surfaces under-attended-to participants; and (j) the four core skill categories (Support Diverse Learners; Build/Sustain Community; Manage Course; Model Effective Facilitation) that taxonomise the work.

## Key Concepts for Stakeholder-Engagement

1.  **Scaffolding-and-fading presence allocation.** "During the first week of each course, facilitator presence is high as the facilitators take on the roles of a community builder and a guide. … As the course moves on, … FLO facilitators take on the roles of coach and mentor. They step back and encourage learning through the use of different questioning strategies and proven facilitation techniques. They participate in discussions but don't lead." Engagement-design principle: facilitator presence is a budgeted resource — high when the group needs scaffolding, lighter once the group builds capacity. (Source: Currie et al., *FLO Facilitation Guide*, Ch 3, "Roles and functions of FLO facilitators")

2.  **Modelling as the central mechanism.** "In addition to talking about online facilitation skills with our participants throughout the course, we also model those same skills. We want our participants to see us continually working on enhancing the skills in ourselves — to model reflective practice as an online facilitator and to bring an authentic presence to our courses." The facilitator's authentic engagement with the practice is part of the curriculum. (Source: Ch 3, "Developing FLO facilitation skills")

3.  **Climate is operational, not soft.** Two of four core skill categories (Build/Sustain Online Community; Model Effective Online Facilitation) are explicitly climate-and-community focused. Privacy-and-confidentiality with Vegas rules is treated as a foundation-of-trust design choice: "Our learning relies on the exchange of honest, constructive feedback, and we need to agree on rules that support this foundation of trust." (Source: Ch 3, "Developing FLO facilitation skills"; Ch 4, "Privacy and Confidentiality")

4.  **Open-ended, invitational, probing questions.** Across all four courses, the Guide's question form is invitational — "post in their planning forum to ask some open-ended questions about how they think things are going"; the MicroCourse facilitator's "curious voice" is an explicit tone. Engagement-design principle: closed and directive questions extract specific data; open and invitational questions surface what the participant brought. (Source: Ch 4, "Example of video feedback with transcript"; Ch 7, "Monitor the sharing of drafts and prototypes"; Ch 3, "Accessible design, inclusive facilitation")

5.  **Resist-thoroughness: protect peer-to-peer engagement.** "Resist the urge to be thorough, but be clear that you're only commenting on one or two aspects of the artifact. Invite participants to jump in and elaborate. … Jumping in too quickly to offer your advice or feedback, or to respond to questions, might discourage others from engaging." Facilitator silence creates space for peer engagement; facilitator over-presence crowds it out. (Source: Ch 7, "Monitor the sharing of drafts and prototypes")

6.  **Develop-vs-grade default protects candour.** "This course is designed to be developmental; it is not a graded course in the traditional sense." The self-assessment rubric (three-level developmental: Beginning / Developing / Accomplished) is the default mode. Engagement-design principle: grading drops candour; self-assessment maintains it. (Source: Ch 4, "Course assessment strategies")

7.  **Track architecture for varied stretch.** FLO Synchronous offers two tracks (Reviewing Participant vs Practicing Facilitator) with different requirements and rubric items. When an engagement includes participants with varied readiness, designing two explicit tracks is better than a single default with implicit opt-out. (Source: Ch 6, "Help participants choose")

8.  **Counsel-out as a respectful move.** "If you find out a participant is deeply struggling, perhaps from an overwhelming schedule, your role is to help them decide if now is the right time to take the course. We strongly advise against allowing participants to audit FLO courses." A struggling participant continuing in degraded form serves neither the participant nor the group. (Source: Ch 6, "Be in touch with participants who haven't logged in")

9.  **Engagement-equity tracking matrix.** "One idea to help you track interactions with your participants to ensure you engage with them fairly equally throughout the course is to use a tracking matrix. … Every time you respond to a participant, make a mark in a column next to their name." Facilitator attention tends to flow toward already-engaged participants; surfacing the distribution makes the inequity visible. (Source: Ch 6, "One idea to help you track interactions")

10.  **Welcome post as first move and first-impression artefact.** "This is your first impression. … Make sure your post sets a good impression: establishes instructor presence, presents a clear message without being too long or too short. Be sure your introductory post ends with an invitation for your participants to share their introductions in a similar way." The convening communication carries the tone, the climate, and the invitation; it is not a logistics document. (Source: Ch 4, "Your Welcome post doesn't have to be just text…")

11.  **Be pro-active in first-week overwhelm.** "Mention that the first week of an online course is often overwhelming (for both participants and facilitators), that it will get better, and that you will do what you can to mitigate start-up stresses." Name predictable participant experiences before participants experience them as personal failings. (Source: Ch 4, "Be pro-active in the first week")

12.  **No-pressuring-tone discipline.** "Avoid pressuring participants to comply with course deadlines, or suggesting they are only welcome if they are committed to following the activity plan. This is an informal, adult learning environment; expect that individuals will have their own agenda." Tone that polices rather than supports degrades engagement quality. (Source: Ch 7, "Write a welcome post")

13.  **Closing-and-leaving design.** "An important facilitator role is to draw the course to a close and celebrate learning, connections and accomplishments." The closing is designed, not improvised; it includes celebration, reflection, and a forward-look. (Source: Ch 5, "Closing the course")

14.  **Co-facilitation on complementarity criteria.** Co-conveners chosen for complementarity — complementary skills (technical vs facilitation), complementary working hours (night owl vs early riser) — sustain longer engagement coverage than co-conveners chosen for seniority or convenience. (Source: Ch 7, "Two heads are better than one")

15.  **Accessibility as ongoing inclusive facilitation.** "Ensuring accessibility of learning is an ongoing quest, as technologies change and we gain experience in online facilitation of FLO courses." Four diagnostic questions for repeat use. Inclusion is a continuing practice, not a state. (Source: Ch 3, "Accessible design, inclusive facilitation")

16.  **Harvest the gems — collective sense-making.** "Throughout the course, it's useful to create a routine that brings together the resources that the participants are sharing." The convener has a role in surfacing collective output back to the group, making the group's work visible to itself. (Source: Ch 7, "Harvest the gems!")

17.  **Reflective practice as scheduled, structured, modelled, shared discipline.** "Evidence has shown that learning is improved when participants become more aware (metacognition) of how and what they are learning." Reflection that is scheduled, structured, modelled by the facilitator, and shared in the group produces metacognitive learning that unstructured reflection does not. (Source: Ch 5, sidebar on reflective practice)

## Questions to Ask During Stakeholder-Engagement

### Phase 1: Mapping

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify the participant population | Who is in this engagement? What is their prior experience with the practice being engaged? Where are they in their development? |
| Identify the readiness-for-stretch distribution | Are all participants ready for the same level of stretch, or does the population include people who could only manage a Reviewing-Participant-equivalent track? |
| Identify the institution or sponsor stakeholders | Who is paying for, sponsoring, or authorising this engagement? What do they expect as evidence it produced what they paid for? The developmental-by-default posture sits in tension with sponsors who want grading; surfacing the tension early prevents late-stage conflict. |
| Identify the co-facilitator landscape | Who could co-facilitate? On what complementarity dimensions — skill mix, working hours, life rhythm? |

### Phase 2: Framing the engagement

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Is this engagement facilitative or transmissive? | If the learning is in the conversation and the co-construction, this is facilitative. If the learning is in content delivery, FLO's frame does not apply. |
| What is our climate-and-trust design? | Privacy posture, Vegas-rules-equivalent, content-sharing permission. Have we stated these at the start of the engagement, or left them implicit? |
| What is the developmental-vs-graded default? | Are we defaulting to self-assessment via rubric with explicit opt-in to grading, or the other way around? The default shapes candour. |
| Have we picked a question-form discipline? | Open-ended, invitational, probing — is that what we are practising? |
| Have we set the engagement scale and time budget? | Class size, weekly time commitment, total duration. |

### Phase 3: Convening

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Have we crafted the welcome post or convening message? | "This is your first impression." Establish presence; clear without being too long or too short; ends with invitation. |
| Have we stated the climate rules out loud? | Vegas rules, privacy, recording consent, what happens if people miss a session. The rules are stated, not assumed. |
| Have we set up the engagement-equity tracking? | Tracking matrix or equivalent — participant names and many blank columns; mark when responding; review periodically. |
| Have we designed accessibility into the engagement? | UDL multiple-means lens; alternatives to new tools; awareness of how diverse learners perceive the environment. |

### Phase 4: Holding the engagement

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Are we calibrating our presence to the phase? | Heavy in the opening weeks (community building, scaffolding); lighter in middle weeks (coach, mentor). Where are we on the arc, and where should we be? |
| Are we modelling what we are asking participants to do? | If we are asking them to share reflective practice, are we sharing our own? |
| Are we using open-ended invitational questions? | "How do you think things are going?" Is the question-form discipline holding, or drifting into closed questions? |
| Are we resisting thoroughness in our feedback? | Comment on one or two aspects of the artefact; invite participants to elaborate. |
| Are we surfacing predictable participant experiences structurally? | First-week overwhelm, Week-3 anxiety, end-of-course let-down. Naming these structurally rather than personally is part of the work. |
| Are we noticing the Anxious-Annie pattern in ourselves? | When we feel the urge to chase, over-respond, intervene early — is this responding to a participant problem, or to our own facilitator anxiety? |
| Are we counselling-out participants who cannot meet participation requirements? | Allowing degraded participation serves neither them nor the group. |
| Are we checking the engagement-equity tracking matrix? | Whose names have few marks next to them? |

### Phase 5: Reflecting and closing

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Have we designed the closing? | Synchronous celebration, final forum post — the closing is a designed move, not an improvised end. What is ours? |
| Have we run a structured reflective practice across the engagement? | Learning Journal equivalent, weekly nuggets equivalent, Looking-Back/Looking-Forward closing. |
| Have we harvested the gems? | What did this group surface that is worth bringing back to the group? |
| Have we scheduled the co-facilitator debrief? | "Don't part company with your co-facilitators without scheduling a debrief." The debrief is a closure move, not optional. |

### Phase 6: Post-engagement

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Have we responded to participant self-assessments? | Especially where the participant's self-assessment and our perception differ — reach out directly to invite discussion. |
| Have we reflected on our own facilitation? | "Make notes of things that you'd like to enhance or change for next time — especially while it's fresh in your memory!" |
| Have we kept in touch with participants where appropriate? | Continued connection extends the engagement value beyond the formal close. |

## What to Look For

- **Method chosen before engagement purpose is clear.** If the team reached for a facilitated format out of habit rather than fit, the engagement will underserve the participant group. Signal: the design choices feel generic, not calibrated to who is in the room. Diagnosis: return to the population mapping phase. Follow-up: confirm whether facilitative or transmissive mode is actually wanted.
- **Facilitator over-presence crowding out peer-to-peer engagement.** When the facilitator is providing comprehensive feedback and answering every question, peer engagement collapses. Signal: one voice dominates asynchronous forums. Diagnosis: scaffolding-and-fading arc has not activated. Follow-up: explicitly invite peers to elaborate; invoke the resist-thoroughness rule.
- **Trust and safety treated as soft skills.** When climate design is skipped, candour is sacrificed. Signal: participants provide superficial, polished contributions only. Diagnosis: Vegas-rules or developmental-default framing was not established. Follow-up: name the climate norms explicitly and invite recommitment.
- **Grading frame suppressing developmental engagement.** When participants know they will be graded, they optimise for grade rather than for learning. Signal: contributions are defensive or highly polished rather than exploratory. Diagnosis: grading default is operating where developmental default would serve better. Follow-up: revisit assessment framing; make the self-assessment rubric the primary instrument.
- **Facilitator anxiety driving premature intervention.** When the Anxious-Annie pattern activates, the facilitator chases engagement rather than enabling it. Signal: the facilitator is intervening frequently before participants have had time to respond. Diagnosis: locating the anxiety as the facilitator's own pattern rather than a participant problem. Follow-up: invoke the Trust the Process principle; check engagement-equity matrix before intervening.

## When to Use This Reference

- A multi-week facilitated engagement is being designed and the convener needs a move-by-move, week-by-week practice framework rather than a single-event protocol.
- A facilitated engagement is running and the convener needs diagnostic questions about presence calibration, peer-to-peer engagement quality, and equity of facilitator attention.
- The convener is choosing between a graded and a developmental assessment default and needs a framework for the tradeoff.
- Co-facilitation is being designed and the partnership needs to be structured on complementarity rather than seniority.
- A closing or post-engagement design is needed and the convener wants to treat it as a designed move rather than an improvised end.
- The convener suspects facilitator anxiety is driving over-intervention and needs a framework for noticing and naming that pattern.

## Worked Example

A regional learning consortium is running a six-week online cohort for 18 mid-career professionals who are new to facilitated group learning. Two co-facilitators take on complementary roles: one with a facilitation background, one with technical platform expertise (different working hours as a bonus).

**Framing.** Before the cohort opens, the facilitators design the climate explicitly: a Vegas-rules agreement on candour, a developmental-not-graded assessment default with self-assessment rubrics, and a no-pressuring-tone commitment in all communications. These are stated in the welcome post, not assumed. (Source: Ch 4, "Privacy and Confidentiality"; Ch 4, "Course assessment strategies")

**Opening.** The welcome post is crafted as a first-impression artefact: it establishes facilitator presence, is appropriately brief, and ends with an explicit invitation for participants to share their own introductions. The first week carries high facilitator presence — welcoming, clarifying, troubleshooting, naming first-week overwhelm as structural rather than personal. (Source: Ch 3, "Roles and functions of FLO facilitators"; Ch 4, "Be pro-active in the first week")

**Fading.** By Week 3, the facilitators step back into coach-and-mentor mode, posting summaries of participant ideas rather than leading discussion, using open-ended invitational questions ("what surprised you in this week's exchange?"), and resisting the urge to provide comprehensive feedback when two participants have different interpretations. A third participant's elaboration is invited instead. (Source: Ch 3, "Roles and functions of FLO facilitators"; Ch 7, "Monitor the sharing of drafts and prototypes")

**Equity check.** At the end of Week 4, the engagement-equity tracking matrix shows three participants have received very few facilitator responses. One is a quiet contributor; one is highly competent and not needing prompting; one is struggling. The facilitators design a targeted reach-out to the third and support a defer-decision conversation. (Source: Ch 6, "One idea to help you track interactions"; Ch 6, "Be in touch with participants who haven't logged in")

**Closing.** The final week includes a structured Looking-Back/Looking-Forward reflective activity, a group harvest of the gems, and a synchronous celebration. The co-facilitators schedule a debrief before the cohort formally closes. (Source: Ch 5, "Closing the course"; Ch 7, "Harvest the gems!")

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Maintaining heavy facilitator presence throughout the engagement when the design called for fading after the opening weeks.
- Fading too early, before participants have built capacity to manage the discussion.
- Treating modelling as facilitator-style preference rather than as a designed-in mechanism the engagement depends on.
- Treating climate, safety, and trust as soft skills handled-if-time-permits rather than as operational design choices.
- Asking closed and directive questions when open-ended invitational questions would surface participant perspectives.
- Providing thorough comprehensive feedback in a peer-review engagement, crowding out peer-to-peer engagement.
- Accepting superficial peer feedback rather than raising the bar.
- Defaulting to grading in a developmental engagement, then being surprised when candour drops.
- Designing a single-track engagement when the participant population has varied readiness for stretch.
- Allowing a deeply-struggling participant to continue in degraded form rather than supporting a defer-decision.
- Letting facilitator attention flow only toward the already-engaged; failing to set up engagement-equity tracking.
- Sending a logistics-only welcome post that fails to set tone, establish presence, or invite participant introduction.
- Failing to name predictable participant experiences (first-week overwhelm, Week-3 anxiety) structurally; letting participants experience them as personal failings.
- Acting on Anxious-Annie anxiety without first locating the anxiety as the facilitator's own pattern.
- Using pressuring tone in communications when participants are adults with their own agendas.
- Under-designing the closing — letting the engagement just end rather than designing a celebration, a reflection, and a forward-look.
- Failing to model reflective practice yourself when asking participants to engage in it.
- Skipping the post-engagement co-facilitator debrief, then repeating avoidable problems in the next iteration.
- Choosing a co-facilitator on seniority or convenience rather than on complementarity.

**Named limits of the source.** The text explicitly acknowledges or implies these scope boundaries:
- The Guide is scoped to online facilitation in multi-week cohort courses; it does not address in-person facilitation, single-event workshops, or large-group event design.
- Organisational-level or community-level stakeholder engagement (Mitchell-Agle-Wood salience, governance structures) is outside scope; the Guide's stakeholder set is the participant cohort and the co-facilitation team.
- The Guide does not develop negotiation theory, conflict-resolution methodology, or cross-cultural facilitation; its cultural awareness note is limited to the accessibility diagnostic questions.
- MicroCourse facilitation is explicitly lighter-touch than Fundamentals/Design/Synchronous; the Guide acknowledges that the equity-tracking and counsel-out moves do not apply in the same way to MicroCourses.
