# U.S. Marine Corps, MCDP-1 Warfighting — Stakeholder Engagement Distillation

**Source:** U.S. Marine Corps (1997). *MCDP 1: Warfighting*. Headquarters United States Marine Corps, Department of the Navy, 20 June 1997. PCN 142 000006 00. Supersedes FMFM 1 Warfighting (1989). **Licence:** Public domain (US Government work product). **Scope:** open.

## Stakeholder Engagement Relevance

MCDP-1 is doctrinally about combat — adversarial engagement between two hostile wills. Most stakeholder-engagement contexts in civilian organisations are not combat: stakeholders are usually negotiable parties whose interests partly align with your own. The *Zweikampf* framing of war does not transfer directly to most stakeholder work.

That said, three threads from MCDP-1 do transfer cleanly to civilian stakeholder engagement.

### 1. Commander's intent must be understood two levels up (Ch 4)

The disciplinary requirement *intent must be understood two levels up* is the doctrinal version of what stakeholder-engagement practice calls *shared understanding of the bigger picture*. For stakeholder engagement at any scale — multi-team programme management, vendor-and-customer coordination, cross-organisation initiatives, public-stakeholder consultation — the operative test is *do the stakeholders know the intent two levels above the work they are doing?*

If they do not, the engagement is brittle. Each local-level decision will be made in isolation, sometimes correctly, sometimes incorrectly. When they conflict, no shared frame exists to adjudicate. The engagement consumes increasing coordination overhead.

If they do, the engagement is resilient. Local-level decisions can be made independently with high confidence that they will align across the engagement.

The stakeholder-engagement application: communicate intent more than you communicate process. The intent travels; the process does not.

### 2. Main effort / Schwerpunkt (Ch 4)

The discipline of one main effort applies to stakeholder engagement in a specific way: *when stakeholder interests conflict, which one yields?* If every stakeholder is treated as equally important, the engagement is unanchored and prioritisation decisions are unresolvable.

The doctrinal answer is *name the main effort; every other interest is supporting effort that yields when in conflict*. In practice, this requires some stakeholders to be explicitly subordinated — which is uncomfortable but operationally necessary.

The stakeholder-engagement application: facilitators / programme managers / engagement leads need to be willing to name which interest is the main effort and which interests are supporting. The discomfort of naming this is the cost of avoiding it later when prioritisation becomes urgent.

### 3. Surfaces and gaps (Ch 2) and reconnaissance pull (Ch 4 Notes 11)

The pamphlet's heuristic — *avoid enemy strength; exploit weakness; reconnaissance pull over command push* (citing Lind 1985) — transfers to stakeholder work as *engage where stakeholder receptivity is high; do not push against entrenched positions; let the encountered openings shape the engagement's main effort*.

For stakeholder engagement, this means: do not engage stakeholders on their positions of strongest resistance first. Engage where there is curiosity, openness, latent agreement, or shared interest. The engagement's main effort should follow the reconnaissance pull from the receptive parts of the stakeholder network, not push against the resistant parts in the order they appear on the stakeholder map.

### 4. Doctrine as a way of thinking, not a checklist (Ch 3)

Stakeholder-engagement methods (stakeholder analysis, mapping, the Mendelow matrix, RACI, the various engagement-level frameworks) are useful insofar as they develop judgment; they are corrosive insofar as they substitute for judgment. The doctrinal frame is the same: the method is *a way of thinking*. A stakeholder-engagement practitioner who has internalised the thinking can adapt the method to the situation; one who applies the method mechanically will produce theatrical engagement without substantive alignment.

## Stakeholder Engagement Cautions

The *Zweikampf* framing of war does not fit most civilian stakeholder contexts. Stakeholder engagement is rarely combat; framing it that way produces unnecessary adversariality and forecloses cooperation. The OODA / Schwerpunkt / mission-tactics / surfaces-and-gaps machinery transfers; the *Zweikampf* framing usually does not.

The doctrine's emphasis on *tempo as decisive* should be calibrated for stakeholder contexts. Sometimes tempo wins (a faster decision cycle than the stakeholder's opposition can respond to). Sometimes patience wins (waiting for the stakeholder's positions to evolve). The doctrinal frame defaults to tempo; many stakeholder situations reward patience instead.

For high-conflict stakeholder situations (regulatory enforcement, contract disputes, public-policy battles, hostile takeovers), the *Zweikampf* framing becomes more applicable. MCDP-1 is most useful as a reach-for-when-adversarial-engagement-is-actually-operative reference, not as the default frame for cooperative stakeholder work.
