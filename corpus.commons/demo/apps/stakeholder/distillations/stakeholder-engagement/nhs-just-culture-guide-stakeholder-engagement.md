# NHS Just Culture Guide, Stakeholder-Engagement Distillation

**Source:** NHS Improvement (2018). *A Just Culture Guide*. A3 landscape poster, single page. Open Government Licence v3.0 (CC BY 4.0-equivalent). Based on the work of Professor James Reason and the National Patient Safety Agency's Incident Decision Tree. https://cpcw.org.uk/wp-content/uploads/sites/19/2018/03/180316-NHSi_just_culture_guide_A3.pdf.

## Stakeholder-Engagement Relevance

The NHS Just Culture Guide is primarily a decision aid, but it is positioned in the source as a *communication tool* with four distinct stakeholder audiences — managers, staff, patients, and families. The source explicitly describes the guide as "a reference point for organisational HR and incident reporting policies, and as a communication tool to help staff, patients and families understand how the appropriate response to a member of staff involved in an incident can and should differ according to the circumstances in which an error was made" (Source: Preamble, "Purpose and use"). This dual purpose — internal decision aid and external communication tool — is what makes the guide a stakeholder-engagement artefact alongside its decision-making function.

The stakeholder-engagement relevance is therefore narrower than the decision-making relevance, but real: the source addresses *how* a just-culture response gets explained, to whom, and what role each stakeholder plays in the conversation. Three distinct engagement contexts run through the source. First, the *manager-to-manager conversation* about whether a staff member requires individual support or intervention — the guide's primary frame, described as "a conversation between managers." Second, the *manager-to-staff conversation* about how the organisation will respond if an incident occurs — "an important part of a just culture is being able to explain the approach that will be taken if an incident occurs." Third, the *organisation-to-patients-and-families conversation* — the guide as a communication tool for the public-facing explanation of why the response to staff involved in an incident differs by circumstances rather than by outcome.

A fourth implicit engagement context is the *manager-to-individual staff member* conversation — the conversation the guide is structuring the manager *for*. The source does not script this conversation, but the structure of the tree shapes what the manager is positioning the conversation around: not "you caused this outcome" but "given these circumstances, what is the appropriate response, and what wider investigation will also be done?" The just-culture frame is itself an engagement frame, characterised by the dual goal of "protecting staff from unfair targeting" and protecting patients "by removing the tendency to treat wider patient safety issues as individual issues."

This distillation projects the source onto the stakeholder-engagement task by surfacing the questions a manager asks at each of the four engagement contexts the source supports. The decision-tree mechanics live in the decision-making distillation; this distillation foregrounds the *conversation* the tree is held inside.

## Key Concepts for Stakeholder-Engagement

1.  **The guide as conversation aid.** The source's primary frame is that the guide "supports a conversation between managers about whether a staff member involved in a patient safety incident requires specific individual support or intervention to work safely." The artefact is positioned as a discussion-structuring tool, not as a one-way determination. (Source: Preamble, "Purpose and use")

2.  **Four stakeholder audiences.** The guide names four distinct audiences: managers (the primary user), staff (the audience whose treatment the guide governs), patients (the audience whose safety the guide is in service of), and families (the audience to whom the response must be explainable). (Source: Preamble, "Purpose and use")

3.  **Making the approach explainable as a just-culture practice.** "An important part of a just culture is being able to explain the approach that will be taken if an incident occurs." Explainability is not a downstream consequence of the decision — it is part of the just-culture practice itself. (Source: Preamble, "Purpose and use")

4.  **The dual purpose: protect staff, protect patients.** The guide's stated end is dual: "protecting staff from unfair targeting" and protecting patients "by removing the tendency to treat wider patient safety issues as individual issues." The two goals are presented as aligned, not in tension. (Source: Preamble, "Purpose and use")

5.  **The wider-investigation parallel as engagement obligation.** Every recommendation end-state pairs its operational guidance with a directive about wider investigation. The wider investigation is itself a stakeholder engagement — the corrective work that addresses future patients depends on the investigation surfacing the underlying causes and the organisation acting on them. (Source: Q1-Q5 recommendations)

6.  **Reference point for organisational HR and incident reporting policies.** The guide is positioned for use *by all parties* as a reference point — a shared artefact that organisational HR policy, incident reporting policy, and individual conversations can all anchor to. The artefact's value is partly in being shared. (Source: Preamble, "Purpose and use")

7.  **Communication tool for public-facing explanation.** The guide helps "staff, patients and families understand how the appropriate response to a member of staff involved in an incident can and should differ according to the circumstances in which an error was made." The artefact is designed to make a counter-intuitive principle communicable. (Source: Preamble, "Purpose and use")

8.  **Sequential testing as engagement frame.** The decision tree's five-test structure (deliberate harm → health → foresight → substitution → mitigation) is itself an engagement frame: it surfaces the questions in an order that lets each stakeholder see why a particular response was reached, rather than presenting the response as a determination. (Source: Q1-Q5)

9.  **Default to system, not to individual.** "Action singling out an individual is rarely appropriate — most patient safety issues have deeper causes and require wider action." This default is part of how the guide engages staff: it pre-commits the organisation to a system-first stance, reducing the perception that the individual is the default target of corrective action. (Source: Preamble, "Purpose and use")

10.  **Health-branch engagement (Recommendations B and C).** The substance-abuse and ill-health branches route to organisational substance-abuse-at-work guidance and occupational-health referral respectively. The engagement frame is procedural-not-punitive: the staff member is treated as a person requiring support, not as an individual to be sanctioned. (Source: Q2, "Recommendation B"; Q2, "Recommendation C")

11.  **HR coupling: senior HR advice on the degree of mitigation.** The mitigating-circumstances branch (Recommendation F) explicitly routes to "senior HR advice on what degree of mitigation applies." HR is a distinct internal stakeholder whose engagement is required where individual-management action is contemplated. (Source: Q5, "Recommendation F")

12.  **Regulatory bodies as external stakeholders.** Recommendation A (deliberate harm) and Recommendation G (residual) both name regulatory bodies as potential stakeholders to be contacted under organisational guidance. (Source: Q1, "Recommendation A"; Q5, "Recommendation G")

13.  **Police and disciplinary processes as gravest engagement.** Recommendation A also names "referral to police and disciplinary processes" as part of the response to deliberate harm. The guide signals the gravest stakeholder engagement only on that branch. (Source: Q1, "Recommendation A")

14.  **Re-entrant engagement: returning to staff when facts change.** The guide is re-entrant: "may need to be revisited as more information becomes available." This implies a stakeholder-engagement obligation to return to the affected staff member (and to the patient and family conversation) when the path through the tree changes. (Source: Preamble, "Please note", bullet 2)

## Questions to Ask at Each Engagement Context

### Phase 1: Manager-to-manager conversation (the primary frame)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Establish that the guide applies | Has the investigation surfaced a concern about an individual action that needs separate evaluation, beyond the systemic investigation? |
| Confirm decomposition | Is this conversation about one action (or failure to act), or do we need to walk the tree multiple times for distinct actions? |
| Agree the procedural anchor | Are we both using the guide as a shared reference point so the conversation is structured rather than ad hoc? |
| Surface disagreement | Where do the two of us answer the sub-questions differently, and what evidence resolves the difference? |
| Preserve the HR coupling | Is senior HR being engaged in parallel — particularly if we are approaching Q5 (mitigation) or Recommendation A or G? |

### Phase 2: Manager-to-individual-staff-member conversation

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Frame the conversation | Can I explain to the staff member that the goal of this conversation is to evaluate the circumstances, not the outcome, and that the guide structures how that is done? |
| Acknowledge the parallel obligation | Have I communicated that a wider investigation is also under way, independent of any individual response? |
| Make the routing visible | At each of Q1-Q5, can I explain to the staff member which question we are asking, why, and how the answer affects what comes next? |
| Protect from unfair targeting | Have I signalled that the guide's default is *not* to single out the individual, and that the burden of evidence runs against that default? |
| Distinguish support from sanction | Where the response is occupational-health (Recommendation C) or substance-abuse-at-work (Recommendation B), can I frame the response as support, not sanction? |

### Phase 3: Organisation-to-patients-and-families conversation

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Explain the differentiation principle | Can I explain why the appropriate response to a member of staff involved in an incident can and should differ according to the circumstances in which the error was made, even when the outcome is the same? |
| Preserve patient-safety priority | Have I shown that the wider investigation — the work that addresses future patient safety — proceeds in parallel with the individual response, and is not displaced by it? |
| Be honest about the branch | Can I describe which branch of the tree the case followed, and the recommendation that resulted, without obscuring the routing? |
| Acknowledge re-entrancy | If new facts emerge, have I prepared the patient and family for the possibility that the guide will be revisited and the response may change? |

### Phase 4: External-stakeholder engagement (regulators, professional bodies, police)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Confirm the trigger | Is the case on Recommendation A (deliberate harm) or Recommendation G (residual management action), where regulatory or external engagement is signalled? |
| Apply organisational guidance | Has organisational guidance for appropriate management action been followed in selecting which external bodies to engage? |
| Coordinate with HR | Has senior HR advised on the regulatory or disciplinary referral? The guide does not replace HR advice. |
| Document the routing | Has the path through the tree been documented so that the external-stakeholder engagement is traceable to the case's circumstances? |

### Phase 5: Communication-tool use across audiences (the shared artefact)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Anchor to the artefact | Can I refer to the published guide as the shared procedural anchor when explaining the organisation's response across audiences? |
| Reference HR policy | Have I cited the organisational HR and incident reporting policies that the guide is a reference point for? |
| Signal the prior | Have I made the default-to-system prior explicit — that singling out the individual is rarely appropriate, by design? |
| Show consistency | Can I show that the same procedural anchor would be used for any case of similar circumstances, independent of which staff member is involved? |

## What to Look For

**Signal:** A manager presents the outcome of the just-culture process to staff as a determination — "the investigation found X, and the decision is Y" — without explaining the routing.
**Diagnosis:** The guide is being used as a verdict-delivery tool rather than a conversation-structuring tool. Staff will experience the outcome as unexplained and potentially arbitrary.
**Follow-up:** Reframe the conversation: walk through each of Q1-Q5 with the relevant party, naming which question is being asked and how the answer shapes the recommendation. The sequential-testing structure is the engagement frame.

**Signal:** Patients and families are told that a staff member "has been dealt with" without understanding how the process differs from blame-assignment.
**Diagnosis:** The differentiation principle has not been communicated: the guide's response depends on circumstances, not on the outcome's severity.
**Follow-up:** Use the guide itself as a communication tool. Explain the dual purpose: "protecting staff from unfair targeting" and protecting patients "by removing the tendency to treat wider patient safety issues as individual issues." Show that the wider investigation runs in parallel with any individual response.

**Signal:** The same case is walked through the tree multiple times in separate management conversations, reaching different conclusions.
**Diagnosis:** The decomposition discipline has not been applied — either the case involves multiple distinct actions that should be walked through the tree separately, or the two conversations are using different factual inputs.
**Follow-up:** Apply the guide's caveat: "only one action (or failure to act) is taken through the guide at a time, and multiple actions in one incident must be considered separately." Agree the factual basis before walking the tree again.

**Signal:** New evidence emerges after the initial conversation and the staff member hears about the revised routing through informal channels.
**Diagnosis:** The re-entrant property of the guide was not communicated at the start, and no mechanism for returning to the staff member with updates was designed.
**Follow-up:** Return to the staff member formally. The guide may "need to be revisited as more information becomes available" — this is design, not failure.

## When to Use This Reference

- A patient safety incident has occurred and the organisation needs a structured framework for evaluating whether individual-directed action is appropriate alongside the systemic investigation.
- A manager needs to explain the organisation's just-culture approach to a staff member before an incident occurs — as a communication of what the organisation is committed to.
- Patients or families need to understand why the response to a staff member involved in an incident differs by circumstances rather than by outcome severity.
- A regulatory referral is being considered and the manager needs to confirm that the decision tree has been properly walked before engaging external bodies.
- HR is being brought into a case at the mitigating-circumstances stage and needs the documented routing through Q1-Q5 to advise appropriately.
- An earlier just-culture assessment needs to be revisited because new facts have emerged.

## Worked Example

A ward nurse administers the wrong dosage of an anticoagulant to a post-surgical patient. The patient is not harmed because the error is caught during routine monitoring. The ward manager initiates the incident-reporting process and, recognising that the investigation is beginning to surface a concern about the nurse's individual action, raises the just-culture guide with the clinical governance lead.

The two managers walk the tree together (Phase 1: manager-to-manager conversation). Q1: was the harm deliberate? No — the nurse was following a verbal instruction from the prescribing doctor, which she understood to be the correct dosage. Q2: were health factors involved? No evidence. Q3 (foresight): was there an agreed protocol for double-checking high-risk medication dosages with a second nurse? Yes — but the protocol was under revision at the time of the incident, and the revised version had not been circulated to all ward staff. The nurse did not knowingly depart from a workable agreed protocol; the protocol was in flux. Q3 routes to Recommendation D: action singling out the individual is not appropriate; the wider investigation should focus on the protocol-revision and communication process.

The clinical governance lead meets with the nurse (Phase 2: manager-to-staff conversation). She explains each test in sequence, names which question exonerated the individual contribution, and confirms that the investigation will focus on the protocol-revision process — not on the nurse. She also names the parallel obligation: the wider investigation will look at how the revised protocol should have been circulated and will produce a recommendation for the medication-safety governance structure.

The trust's patient experience team notifies the patient (Phase 3: organisation-to-patients-and-families). They explain that the dosage error was caught, that no harm resulted, and that the response focuses on the system conditions that allowed the error to occur — not on individual punishment.

The scenario is operator-authored. The manager-to-manager conversation framing, the sequential-test structure (Q1-Q5), the Recommendation D routing (not appropriate to single out — foresight branch), the manager-to-staff explainability obligation ("an important part of a just culture is being able to explain the approach"), and the dual-purpose framing (protecting staff and patients simultaneously) are all `[V]`- and `[AR]`-marked passages in the deep reference. ## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Treating the conversation as a determination rather than a structured discussion.** The source explicitly frames the guide as a conversation aid. Using it to deliver a verdict in one direction recasts it.
- **Engaging the staff member without the wider-investigation parallel.** Every recommendation preserves the parallel obligation; failing to communicate the parallel obligation positions the individual response as the whole response.
- **Outcome-led engagement with patients and families.** The source's differentiation principle is that response differs by circumstances, not by outcome. Engagements that frame the response around the severity of the outcome bypass the principle.
- **Conflating the guide's use with HR action.** The guide does not replace HR advice; positioning the guide as the HR decision misrepresents the source.
- **Treating the guide as a one-shot artefact in stakeholder conversations.** The re-entrant property is part of the design; closing the conversation prematurely fails to honour the source's recognition that new facts may change the path.
- **Using the guide to manage perception rather than to structure response.** The source positions the guide as supporting consistent, constructive, and fair evaluation; using it as reputational management distorts that.
- **Skipping the manager-to-staff conversation about which branch the case followed.** The source's framing of the guide as a communication tool implies the routing should be communicable to the staff member, not just to managers.
