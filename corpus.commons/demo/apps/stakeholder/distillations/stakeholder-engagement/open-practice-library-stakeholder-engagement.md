## Stakeholder-Engagement Relevance

The Open Practice Library is built around facilitated, multi-participant work. A high proportion of the 266 practices are *stakeholder-engagement* practices in the strict sense — they presume someone with skin in the game who is not on the immediate team, and they specify how to bring that person's perspective into the work. The library's Discovery phase (86 practices) is the densest stakeholder-engagement cluster: opportunity framing, problem-space exploration, customer/user research, stakeholder identification, alignment work. Foundation (111 practices) carries the facilitation primitives that *enable* multi-stakeholder sessions to run productively (1-2-4-All, Affinity Mapping, Confidence Voting, Lean Coffee, Check-ins, Social Contract). Delivery and Options carry the engagement practices that surround a build cycle (Showcase / Sprint Review, Retrospectives, Stakeholder RACI, Dot Voting).

The stakeholder frame the library carries is explicit on three points. First, **stakeholders are plural and structured**, not undifferentiated — the library distinguishes primary stakeholders (the end user), secondary stakeholders (those the end user interacts with), and other stakeholders (those the secondary interact with) in *Stakeholder Mapping (Mobius Outcome Delivery)*; it also distinguishes Responsible / Accountable / Consulted / Informed roles in *Stakeholder RACI Map*. Second, **stakeholder engagement is craft, not transaction** — the library treats engagement as an ongoing relationship requiring named practices for opening sessions safely (Check-ins, Social Contract), surfacing unspoken issues (Elephant in the Room), and closing with action commitments (Action Steps). Third, **the practice is the bridge between stakeholders and the team's vocabulary** — the library's facilitation primitives are designed to translate stakeholder voice into team-shareable artefacts (empathy maps, journey maps, stakeholder maps, impact maps, RACI tables) that survive the session and inform downstream work.

This distillation projects OPL practices onto the stakeholder-engagement task by phase: identify stakeholders, prepare engagement, run the session, capture and synthesise, close and follow up.

## Key Concepts for Stakeholder-Engagement

1. **Stakeholder identification as its own practice.** The library treats "who has a stake?" as a question to ask explicitly, not to answer by default. *Stakeholder Map* uses Power × Interest (or Power × Sentiment) quadrants and is preceded by a brainstorming round considering "people or groups that are impacted, can support, can delay if involved too late, are decision makers, are in the operations of the new process/tool when deployed, have been involved with a similar project in the past" (Source: Practice "Stakeholder Map", "Round 1"). *Stakeholder Mapping (Mobius Outcome Delivery)* uses concentric circles (Primary / Secondary / Other) and emphasises naming the primary stakeholder concretely rather than as "customer" or "user" (Source: Practice "Stakeholder Mapping (Mobius Outcome Delivery)", "How to do it?"). *Stakeholder RACI Map* assigns Responsible / Accountable / Consulted / Informed roles per activity.

2. **Stakeholder interviews as structured engagement, not transactional.** *Stakeholders Interview* is "a one on one conversation to help better understand what stakeholders want and need from the product" with three primary information areas: user needs, business goals, technical limitations (Source: Practice "Stakeholders Interview", "What is it?"). Recommended duration is 45 minutes to 1 hour. Tips include establishing psychological safety, pair-interviewing for validation across technical/business/marketing vocabularies, allowing silence, and using open-ended rather than direct questions.

3. **Empathy as the entry artefact.** *Empathy Mapping* (Dave Gray of XPLANE [BT]) "is a collaborative tool used to develop insight into customers, users, etc." (Source: Practice "Empathy Mapping", "What is it?"). Senses captured: "what the person is seeing, thinking, doing and feeling." The practice's discipline: it is run with the stakeholder present where possible, or with research data feeding into it where not — the gap between empathy-mapped assumptions and validated empathy maps is itself diagnostic.

4. **The customer journey as a multi-touchpoint artefact.** *Experience Mapping*, *Emotional Journey*, *Service Blueprint*, *Domain Storytelling*, and *Event Storming* (Brandolini, 2013 [BT]) are all multi-stakeholder process-modelling practices. *Event Storming* explicitly produces "a common and shared language between Business & IT" (Source: Practice "Event Storming", "Why do it?") — it is a stakeholder-bridging artefact, not just a process diagram. *Emotional Journey* maps user feelings step-by-step as a complement to the activity-level journey map.

5. **Stakeholder participation in option-generation.** *Example Mapping* puts stakeholders in the room when acceptance criteria are written, with a four-colour sticky structure (yellow story / blue rules / green examples / red questions). The practice "should be used when you have the right people in the room to be able to explain business rules and example scenarios, and a technical team that can ask the right questions about those rules" (Source: Practice "Example Mapping", "Why do it?").

6. **Inclusive facilitation primitives.** The library's facilitation defaults are explicitly anti-HiPPO (Highest Paid Person's Opinion). *1-2-4-All*: "All voices are heard, incorporating 'silent' conversations and expanding input diversity — No more HiPPOs (Highest Paid Person's Opinion)!" (Source: Practice "1-2-4-All", "Why do it?"). *Dot Voting*, *$100 Prioritisation*, and *Confidence Voting* (attributed to Jean Tabaka [BT]) equalise voice in convergence. *Dissent Cards* (Marquet [BT]) institutionalises productive disagreement when stakeholders default to deference.

7. **Stakeholders as Showcase / Sprint Review audience.** *Showcase* (the OPL framing of Sprint Review) is "an event where stakeholders and interested parties are given a demonstration of recent work performed by a team" (Source: Practice "Showcase", "What is it?"). The advice: "Where possible avoid scripting the showcase — give stakeholders/end users the ability to play around with the new product. Showcases should be as interactive as possible, aiding the team in collecting real world feedback" (Source: Practice "Showcase", "Advice").

8. **Stakeholder communication plan as artefact.** *Stakeholder Map* explicitly produces "a stakeholder map and a communication plan" as the target outcome (Source: Practice "Stakeholder Map", "What is it?"). The map is not the end; the per-quadrant engagement strategy (who is informed, who is consulted, who is responsible for engaging) is the practice's load-bearing output. Periodic review during retrospectives is recommended.

9. **Engagement across organisational silos.** *5 Elements Canvas* surfaces "areas of mutual interest and benefit between teams/silos that are trying to solve similar problems independently to each other" (Source: Practice "5 Elements Canvas", "Why do it?") — Development / Operations / Leadership / Product / Architecture as five default stakeholder groups. *6 Dimensions of Discovery* extends the framing to People & culture / Tools & technologies / Process / Communication / Work environment / Governance. *Collaborative Pathways* and *Inter-Team Agreement* operationalise cross-team engagement.

10. **Formal relational contracts for ecosystem engagement.** *Formal Relational Contract* is the library's contrarian on transactional contracting: "Transactional (legal) contracts are still relevant but do not cover all required parts which are essential for collaboration based on transparency and trust... we believe collaboration, building an ecosystem as we do more and more does not fit in a transactional contract but actually needs a formal (written) relational contract" (Source: Practice "Formal Relational Contract", "What is it?") [AR]. The seven-step process: Social Contract → trust foundation → joint vision and goals → guiding principles → terms and conditions → governance mechanisms → canvas consolidation.

11. **Discovery work as multi-stakeholder framing.** *Start With Why* (Sinek [BT]), *Vision Statement*, *OKRs*, *Impact Mapping* (Adzic [BT]), *North Star Framework* (Ellis [BT]), *Lean Canvas* (Maurya [BT]) — these practices all *produce a shared frame* between organisational stakeholders and the team. Impact Mapping is framed explicitly as engagement: "It is an excellent way to allow leadership and autonomous teams to have a conversation around strategic priorities and how they translate into the work of the team" (Source: Practice "Impact Mapping", "Why do it?").

12. **Inclusive design as engagement of voiceless stakeholders.** *Empathy Mapping*, *User Persona*, *Proto-Persona*, and the structural-engagement practices (*Stakeholder Mapping* with concentric circles, *6 Dimensions of Discovery* governance dimension) all surface the question of whose voice is not in the room. Implicit but consistent across the library.

13. **Closing engagement with action commitments.** *Action steps* is a closing practice that "invites everyone to commit to taking a first step towards action… Inspired by the idea that 1000 mile journey begins with one step, action steps asks people to think about the first step they will take to continue the work begun together, and commit to action" (Source: Practice "Action steps", "What is it?"). Each commitment carries Who / What / When / With.

14. **Retrospectives as stakeholder feedback loop.** *Retrospectives* and its variants (4Ls, Rose Thorn Bud, Plus Minus Interesting, Realtime Retrospective) include external-stakeholder versions where the team retrospects with the Product Owner or other stakeholders, not just internally. The 4Ls Retrospective explicitly lists "Sprint or Project Team, Product Owner" as participants (Source: Practice "4Ls Retrospective", frontmatter).

## Questions to ask during stakeholder engagement

### Phase 0: Identify and map stakeholders

| Need | Question | OPL practice |
|---|---|---|
| Brainstorm the list | Who is impacted? Who can support? Who can delay if involved too late? Who decides? Who operates the result? Who has historical context? | *Stakeholder Map*, "Round 1" |
| Narrow to specific people | Have we named individuals rather than generic roles like "the customer"? | *Stakeholder Mapping (Mobius Outcome Delivery)* |
| Position on power vs interest | Where does each stakeholder sit on Power × Interest (or Power × Sentiment)? | *Stakeholder Map*, "Round 3" |
| Surface concentric structure | Who is the primary stakeholder? Who do they interact with (secondary)? Who do the secondary interact with (other)? | *Stakeholder Mapping (Mobius Outcome Delivery)* |
| Define engagement responsibility | For each activity, who is Responsible / Accountable / Consulted / Informed? | *Stakeholder RACI Map* |
| Spot voiceless stakeholders | Is there a stakeholder group affected by the work who is not in the room (future generations, regulators, non-users impacted by externalities)? | *6 Dimensions of Discovery* (governance dimension); *Stakeholder Mapping* third ring |

### Phase 1: Prepare for stakeholder engagement

| Need | Question | OPL practice |
|---|---|---|
| Establish safety | Have we set up a social contract for this engagement? | *Social Contract* |
| Open the session | How are we doing a check-in to set tone? | *Check-ins* |
| Surface latent issues | Is there an elephant in the room with this stakeholder that needs to be named first? | *An Elephant in the Room* |
| Choose facilitation primitive | Will this be silent-generation-first to avoid HiPPO dominance? | *1-2-4-All*; *Silent Brainstorming* |
| Plan participation explicitly | Have we listed the stakeholders by name and confirmed attendance? | *Group Launch Facilitation*; *Stakeholders Interview* |

### Phase 2: Conduct the engagement session

| Need | Question | OPL practice |
|---|---|---|
| One-on-one stakeholder conversation | Have we structured a stakeholder interview with user-needs / business-goals / technical-limitations areas? | *Stakeholders Interview* |
| Build empathy artefact | What is the stakeholder seeing, thinking, doing, feeling? | *Empathy Mapping* |
| Map the stakeholder's journey | What are their actions step-by-step? Where do they feel pain or delight? | *Emotional Journey*; *Experience Mapping*; *Service Blueprint* |
| Map the business process with stakeholders | How does business and IT share a language for what happens? | *Event Storming* (Brandolini); *Domain Storytelling* |
| Generate aligned options | How are we collaboratively brainstorming with stakeholders in the room? | *1-2-4-All*; *10-for-10*; *6 Dimensions of Discovery* |
| Test understanding of business rules | Are we writing acceptance criteria with examples, with stakeholders contributing? | *Example Mapping* |
| Surface dissent productively | Are we using cards or structured questions to make disagreement safe? | *Dissent Cards* |
| Prioritise with stakeholders | How are we converging on priority — dots, dollars, MoSCoW, RICE? | *Dot Voting*; *$100 Prioritisation*; *MoSCoW Method*; *RICE Scoring Model* |
| Check stakeholder confidence | How confident are stakeholders in the proposed direction (0-5 fingers)? | *Confidence Voting* |

### Phase 3: Frame and align (Discovery work)

| Need | Question | OPL practice |
|---|---|---|
| Find the shared purpose | Why does this work matter? Why does the organisation exist? | *Start With Why*; *Vision Statement* |
| Translate strategy to team work | What are the objectives and key results? | *Objectives & Key Results (OKRs)* |
| Map strategy to behavioural change | What human behaviour change does success require for each stakeholder? | *Impact Mapping* (Adzic) |
| Define the success metric | What is the single most important metric for this product? | *North Star Framework* (Ellis) |
| Frame the business model | What is the problem, target customer, UVP, solution, channels, revenue, cost, key metrics, unfair advantage? | *Lean Canvas* (Maurya) |
| Frame jobs the stakeholder hires the product for | What job is the customer hiring this product to do? | *Jobs to be Done* (Ulwick [BT]) |
| Imagine the end state | What does success look like at 12 months? What assumptions must hold? | *Start At The End* |

### Phase 4: Run the stakeholder-facing delivery cadence

| Need | Question | OPL practice |
|---|---|---|
| Demo working software | Is the showcase interactive, with stakeholders touching the product? | *Showcase* / *Sprint Review* |
| Retrospect with stakeholders | Are we including the Product Owner / key stakeholders in the retro where appropriate? | *4Ls Retrospective*; *Retrospectives* |
| Track decisions transparently | Are we recording architectural decisions in version-control-tracked records? | *Architectural Decision Records (ADR)* |
| Reduce stakeholder cognitive load | Is the work visualised in a way stakeholders can see at a glance? | *Visualisation of Work*; *Burnup Chart*; *Burndown* |

### Phase 5: Close and follow up

| Need | Question | OPL practice |
|---|---|---|
| Commit to action | What is each person's first step, by when, with whom? | *Action steps* |
| Translate to communication plan | How will we keep each stakeholder informed proportionate to their position on the map? | *Stakeholder Map* communication-plan output |
| Document the engagement | Have we shared the empathy map / journey map / stakeholder map / RACI? | *Docs As Code* (and the artefacts above) |
| Review engagement periodically | Are we revisiting the stakeholder map during retros? | *Retrospectives*; *Stakeholder Map* "Post-follow up" |

### Phase 6: Engage at ecosystem scale

| Need | Question | OPL practice |
|---|---|---|
| Set up formal cross-party agreement | Are we treating this multi-party engagement as a relational contract, not a transactional one? | *Formal Relational Contract* |
| Build inter-team agreement | What is our agreement with the other teams we depend on or that depend on us? | *Inter-Team Agreement*; *Collaborative Pathways* |
| Establish a launch | Is the new team or cross-team initiative being launched with structured alignment? | *Group Launch Facilitation*; *Leadership Launch* |
| Map cross-silo collaboration | Are we mapping bridges between teams and committing to repair? | *Build The Bridges* |

## What to Look For

**Signal:** The team repeatedly refers to "the customer" or "the user" in planning conversations without naming a specific person or group.
**Diagnosis:** Stakeholder identification has not happened. Generic stakeholder identities produce generic engagement. *Stakeholder Mapping (Mobius Outcome Delivery)* warns explicitly: "We don't want generic phrases like 'customer' or 'user'."
**Follow-up:** Run a stakeholder-identification practice (*Stakeholder Map*, *Stakeholder Mapping (Mobius Outcome Delivery)*) before the next planning session. Name specific individuals or named groups at minimum.

**Signal:** Post-workshop energy is high but two weeks later nothing has changed.
**Diagnosis:** The session closed without per-person action commitments (Who / What / When / With).
**Follow-up:** Return to the *Action steps* practice for the next session. Add a review of the previous session's commitments as the opening of the next one.

**Signal:** The empathy map built in the planning session looks complete and confident, but stakeholders have not been in the room.
**Diagnosis:** The empathy map was built from team assumptions, not validated with actual stakeholders. "A sparse empathy map indicates that more research needs to be done" — but a falsely-full one is more dangerous.
**Follow-up:** Mark the empathy map explicitly as "assumption-based." Schedule a *Stakeholders Interview* or direct observation session to validate or revise.

**Signal:** The senior leader in the room speaks first in brainstorms and other participants' ideas cluster around the leader's framing.
**Diagnosis:** HiPPO dominance. No structural protection against the Highest Paid Person's Opinion.
**Follow-up:** Open the next brainstorm with silent individual generation (*Silent Brainstorming* or *1-2-4-All*) before any verbal sharing. The silent phase is the structural protection.

**Signal:** The stakeholder map has not been revised since the project began, even though two key stakeholders have changed roles.
**Diagnosis:** The stakeholder map is being treated as a project-launch artefact rather than a living document. *Stakeholder Map* explicitly recommends "periodic review during retrospectives."
**Follow-up:** Add the stakeholder map to the Sprint Retrospective agenda. Treat changes to the map as work, not as a sign of prior error.

## When to Use This Reference

- An *engagement is being designed* and the practitioner needs a practice-level vocabulary for each engagement phase: identify, prepare, conduct, frame, deliver, close.
- A *stakeholder has not been named yet* — only a generic role like "customer" or "user" is in use.
- A *facilitation session* needs to be structured to suppress HiPPO dominance and surface quieter voices.
- A *showcase / Sprint Review* is collapsing into a scripted presentation — the *Showcase* practice's advice applies.
- A *multi-party ecosystem engagement* (across organisations, across vendor-client lines, across silos) needs a relational rather than transactional contract.
- A *discovery phase* needs to produce shared purpose, aligned goals, and stakeholder-to-behaviour mappings before a build cycle begins.
- A *retrospective* needs to include external stakeholders (Product Owner, key users) rather than being team-only.
- An *empathy map or user journey map* needs to be validated against actual stakeholders rather than team assumptions.

## Worked Example

A six-person product team in a healthcare SaaS company is tasked with redesigning the onboarding flow for a new tier of enterprise customers. The team's first instinct is to prototype three options and present them to the enterprise account managers in a showcase. The product manager recognises this is a showcase-as-presentation pattern and stops the process.

Instead the team runs a stakeholder-identification session (*Stakeholder Map*). Round 1 surfaces six groups: enterprise account managers (AMs), IT security leads at the customer organisations, end-users (clinicians), the compliance team, the sales engineers who run the actual onboarding, and the customer success team. Round 2 names two individuals per group. Round 3 positions them: AMs are high power/high interest; compliance is high power/low interest; clinicians are low power/high interest (most impacted, least organisational pull).

The team schedules three *Stakeholders Interviews* — one AM, one clinician, one sales engineer. Each is 50 minutes, pair-interviewed (one asks, one notes). The interviews surface a clinician need the team had not anticipated: the current onboarding flow requires a security-clearance step that clinicians cannot complete without IT involvement, creating a two-day delay before first use.

The team runs an *Empathy Mapping* session with the clinician interview data and validates it against a second clinician in a 20-minute follow-up call. The empathy map is marked "validated — two clinicians, May 2026."

The showcase, when it runs, is interactive: each account manager and sales engineer navigates the redesigned flow themselves while a team member uses the think-aloud equivalent (asking "what are you expecting here?"). The compliance team receives a written summary — their engagement need is information, not interaction.

The session closes with *Action steps*: each AM names one change they will request from their IT contact to reduce the security-clearance delay on the customer side. The product team names one sprint-backlog item it will prioritise for the technical fix on the product side.

The scenario is operator-authored. The stakeholder-identification practice (Round 1 brainstorm, Round 2 naming, Round 3 positioning), the *Stakeholders Interview* structure, the *Empathy Mapping* validation discipline, the *Showcase* interactivity advice, and the *Action steps* closing discipline are all `[V]`-marked passages in the deep reference. No verbatim source blockquotes appear in this distillation.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Default to "the customer" or "the user" as a stakeholder.** *Stakeholder Mapping (Mobius Outcome Delivery)* warns explicitly: "We don't want generic phrases like 'customer' or 'user'." Generic stakeholder identities produce generic engagement.
- **HiPPO-dominated stakeholder sessions.** The Highest Paid Person's Opinion failure mode is the canonical anti-pattern across the library's facilitation practices; design the session to surface every voice before the senior stakeholder commits a position.
- **Confusing showcases with status presentations.** *Showcase*: "Where possible avoid scripting the showcase — give stakeholders/end users the ability to play around with the new product. Showcases should be as interactive as possible." Scripted demos teach stakeholders to be passive.
- **Status-update standups in stakeholder sessions.** Drift into status reporting loses the engagement value.
- **Treating engagement as a one-off discovery activity.** *Stakeholder Map* explicitly recommends "periodic review during retrospectives, to see if the communication plan and level of involvement of stakeholders is working or needs to be refreshed."
- **Coerced consensus from stakeholders.** *Disagree and Commit*: pushing stakeholders to consensus produces compliance, not commitment, and compliance erodes during execution.
- **Empathy-without-validation.** Empathy maps built from team assumptions, never validated with the actual stakeholder, become confident-looking false priors.
- **Skipping the elephant.** Engagement that proceeds without naming the unspoken issue produces polite agreement that breaks under pressure. *An Elephant in the Room* exists to be named before progressing.
- **Generic RACIs.** RACI matrices that name "the team" instead of named individuals or that do not distinguish Accountable (one person, final say) from Responsible (those doing the work) produce confusion rather than alignment.
- **Engagement without action commitments.** Sessions that end with "we'll synthesise and circulate" without per-person Who/What/When commitments lose energy and rarely lead to follow-through. *Action steps* is the closing discipline.
- **Treating the stakeholder list as static.** Reorganisations, role changes, departures, and new entrants change the map. The communication plan should be reviewed on retrospective cadence.
- **Practices without principles in stakeholder work.** *Establish Shared Principles*: copying *what* a successful team does for stakeholder engagement without the underlying principles (transparency, equitable voice, shared purpose) produces the visible shape without the value.
