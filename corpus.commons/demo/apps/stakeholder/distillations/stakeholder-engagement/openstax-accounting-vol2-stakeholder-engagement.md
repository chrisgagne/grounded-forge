# OpenStax Principles of Accounting Volume 2, Stakeholder-Engagement Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2019). *Principles of Accounting, Volume 2: Managerial Accounting*. Rice University. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/principles-managerial-accounting. Senior contributing authors Mitchell Franklin, Patty Graybeal, Dixon Cooper; 28 contributing authors.

## Stakeholder-Engagement Relevance

A managerial-accounting textbook is an unusual but informative source for stakeholder engagement. Most of the volume is about producing internal information for managers — a focus that would seem to leave external stakeholders out of frame. Yet the volume develops substantial stakeholder-engagement material in three places. First, Ch 13 ("Sustainability Reporting") is dedicated to the question of who beyond shareholders has standing in corporate accountability and how organisations should communicate with that wider stakeholder set. Second, Ch 12.4 (the balanced scorecard) frames stakeholder perspective as a corrective to stockholder-only evaluation. Third, scattered Ethical Considerations sidebars across Ch 1, Ch 8, Ch 9, and elsewhere develop the stakeholder dimension of internal decisions whose effects spill outward.

The volume's stakeholder-engagement contribution is distinctive: it grounds engagement in the *information* dimension. Where other stakeholder frameworks emphasise dialogue, mapping, and relationship management, this volume emphasises what information stakeholders need, how that information is produced, and through what reporting frameworks it is communicated. Ch 13.2 catalogues six stakeholder groups (shareholders, lenders, employees, customers, governments and regulators, community) and analyses each group's information needs in turn. Ch 13.3 develops three reporting frameworks (GRI, SASB, Integrated Reporting) each with a distinctive theory of materiality and a distinctive stakeholder audience.

The volume also provides an unusually concrete cost analysis for stakeholder claims. Three foundational corporate cases — Union Carbide / Bhopal, Nestlé / baby formula, Johnson & Johnson / Tylenol — are developed at length, with quantified consequences (deaths, injured, recall costs, share-price effects, decades-long reputational damage). The cases anchor the volume's claim that decisions ignoring stakeholder consequences can be ruinous on financial criteria as well as ethical ones. The Ford Pinto case (Ch 10.6) reinforces this with a quantitative cost-benefit analysis where the calculated savings were dwarfed by litigation costs and reputational damage from a known product defect.

This distillation synthesises these threads into a working pattern: how to identify stakeholders and their information needs; how to choose a reporting framework; how to communicate financial and non-financial information to different stakeholder groups; how to address stakeholder consequences of internal management decisions; and how to maintain accountability over time.

## Key Concepts for Stakeholder Engagement

1. **Stakeholders defined.** "Anyone directly or indirectly affected by the organization, including employees, customers, government entities, regulators, creditors, and the local community" (Source: OpenStax, *Principles of Accounting Vol 2*, Ch 13.2, "Identify User Needs for Information"). The list expands by context. Primary users of corporate information are typically shareholders and lenders; secondary users are customers, suppliers, governments, regulators, employees, and the community.

2. **Six stakeholder groups with distinct information needs.** Ch 13.2 develops user-by-user analysis: shareholders (focus on viability and value retention; use ratios like P/E, EPS, ROI; ethical-investment movement growing), lenders (going-concern risk, geographic concentration), employees (value alignment — 76 per cent of millennials weigh CSR in employment choices), customers (value-aligned products — 33 per cent buy from socially-responsible brands per 2016 Unilever study), governments and regulators (responsible behaviour reduces need for restrictive legislation), and community (the social contract — "the expectation that companies will hold to an unwritten contract with society as a whole"). (Source: Ch 13.2)

3. **The triple bottom line and the People-Planet-Profit framing.** John Elkington's 1997 framing "expand[ed] the traditional financial reporting framework so as to capture a firm's social and environmental performance" (Ch 13.1, "Initial Sustainability Reports"). Triple bottom line accountability is the conceptual foundation for the stakeholder-wider reporting that anchors contemporary engagement.

4. **Sustainability and CSR.** Sustainability is "meeting the needs of the present generation without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs"; CSR is "the set of actions that firms take to assume responsibility for their impact on the environment and social well-being" (Ch 13.1). Both expand the criteria of corporate decisions beyond financial returns to environmental and social effects.

5. **Three reporting frameworks with distinct materiality theories.** GRI (Global Reporting Initiative, 1997) is broadly stakeholder-oriented; materiality covers significant economic, environmental, and social impacts that influence stakeholder decisions. SASB (Sustainability Accounting Standards Board, 2011) is investor-focused; materiality follows the U.S. Supreme Court's "total mix" standard. Integrated Reporting (IIRC, 2010) brings together six capitals — financial, manufactured, intellectual, human, social-and-relationship, natural — into one report. Each framework matches different stakeholder audiences. (Source: Ch 13.3)

6. **The public benefit corporation as legal expression of stakeholder accountability.** The benefit-corporation model "broadens the perspective of traditional corporate law by incorporating concepts of purpose, accountability and transparency with respect to all corporate stakeholders, not just stockholders" (Ch 13.2). Patagonia became "one of California's first B corporations" in 2012, reflecting "more than 30 years" of a 1-per-cent-of-sales (or 10-per-cent-of-pre-tax-profits) giving commitment.

7. **The social contract framing.** The community's claim on corporate behaviour rests on "an unwritten contract with society as a whole" (Ch 13.2, "Community"). When companies undertake actions that harm society or contradict its general values, "community backlash may cost the firm dearly" — illustrated by the Union Carbide, Nestlé, and Johnson & Johnson cases.

8. **The balanced scorecard's stakeholder perspective.** The BSC enables "a stakeholder perspective as compared to a stockholder perspective" (Ch 12.4). Stockholders are owners concerned mainly with profitability; stakeholders include "investors, creditors, managers, regulators, employees, customers, suppliers, and even laypeople who are concerned about whether or not the company is a good world citizen". The BSC's customer perspective and learning-and-growth perspective embed stakeholder considerations into routine performance evaluation.

9. **Three foundational corporate cases that drove sustainability reporting.** Union Carbide's Bhopal disaster (1984) — "highlighted the power disparity between corporations and poor individuals" with more than 600,000 exposed and an official toll of 3,598 dead. Nestlé's baby-formula campaign — "one of the longest-running consumer boycotts in modern history" with ongoing effects since the 1970s. Johnson & Johnson's Tylenol response (1982) — recalled 31 million bottles and spent more than $100 million; share price recovered in six weeks; "the company's response has been lauded in business case studies and has formed the basis of crisis communications strategies developed by researchers" (Ch 13.1).

10. **Quantitative evidence that stakeholder-aligned firms outperform.** A Harvard Business Review study found that "socially responsible companies post higher profits and stock performance than those that were not focused on social responsibility"; a Deutsche Bank meta-analysis of more than 2,000 studies dating to the 1970s reported "90% of which suggested that socially responsible investing gives better returns than passive investing" (Ch 13.2, "Shareholders"). Ethical investing in 2016 totalled $8.7 trillion, up 33 per cent from 2014, accounting for 20 per cent of all investment under professional management.

11. **The ethical Considerations frame across decisions.** Most chapters include ethical sidebars connecting internal management decisions to stakeholder consequences. Examples: variance manipulation that hides degraded supplier quality (Ch 8.1); transfer pricing that benefits one segment at company-wide cost (Ch 9.4); responsibility-centre evaluation that excludes uncontrollable factors but stakeholder demands transparency anyway (Ch 12.1); cost models that should reflect organisational structure and business reality rather than gameability (Ch 6.1, citing IFAC).

12. **Stakeholder ethics in pro-stakeholder decision-making.** Hitt and Collins are cited approvingly: companies with "a pro-stakeholder culture should better understand the multiple ethical demands of those stakeholders" and gain "an advantage in recognizing economic opportunities associated with such concerns". Stakeholder consideration is positioned as both ethical responsibility and strategic opportunity (Ch 9.4, citing Hitt and Collins, *Business Horizons*).

13. **Multiple stakeholder claims on the same decision.** The IMA's ethical reasoning case (Ch 1.4, "Institute of Management Accountants Ethical Standards") frames a managerial accountant as obligated to "many stakeholders such as investors, creditors, employees and the community". Different stakeholders have different obligations: "the primary obligation to a creditor may be to make timely payments, the obligation to the community may be to minimize negative environmental impact". Stakeholder-engagement design must hold these multiple claims simultaneously.

14. **Future-issues framing extends stakeholder engagement.** Ch 13.4 projects future stakeholder issues including cybersecurity (consumer-confidence dimension), shifting political winds in globalisation, and AI's effect on employment (raising "ethical questions, such as whether corporations have a greater responsibility to society than to shareholders"). The Bill Gates "robot tax" / Universal Basic Income framing and Yanis Varoufakis's Universal Basic Dividend concept are referenced as emerging stakeholder-economic ideas.

## Questions to Ask During Stakeholder Engagement

### Phase 1: Mapping (Identifying who has standing)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify primary information users | Who has the most direct claim on the information being produced? Shareholders and lenders are the typical primary users for corporate financial information; the analysis may differ for sustainability reporting. |
| Identify secondary users | Who is affected indirectly? Customers, employees, suppliers, governments, regulators, the community? |
| Surface latent stakeholders | Who is affected but not yet engaged? Whose voice is missing because they lack the power, knowledge, or organisational resources to assert themselves? Future generations? Communities outside the immediate operational footprint? |
| Map information dependencies | What does each stakeholder need to know to make their decisions about the organisation — purchase, employment, regulation, investment, partnership? |
| Identify the social-contract dimension | What unwritten expectations does the community hold? Where does corporate behaviour risk falling outside community values? |

### Phase 2: Framing the Engagement (Choosing a reporting framework)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Choose a framework | Is the audience primarily investors (SASB framework appropriate), broadly stakeholder-oriented (GRI framework appropriate), or seeking integrated financial-and-non-financial reporting (IIRC Integrated Reporting framework appropriate)? |
| Define materiality | What level of impact warrants disclosure? GRI uses stakeholder-impact standard; SASB uses total-mix-investor standard; IIRC uses value-creation standard. The choice depends on audience. |
| Identify the six capitals (if Integrated Reporting) | How does the organisation create or use financial, manufactured, intellectual, human, social-and-relationship, and natural capital? Which are most material to value creation? |
| Establish the triple-bottom-line frame | What are the people, planet, and profit dimensions of the decision or report? Where do they reinforce one another? Where do they conflict? |
| Ensure procedural justice | Even when distributive outcomes will disappoint some stakeholders, is the process for making and communicating decisions transparent, consistent, and respectful? |

### Phase 3: Engaging different stakeholder groups

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| For shareholders | Do they receive the information needed to assess long-term viability, including non-financial indicators of risk? Have ethical-investment criteria been addressed for the growing share of investors using ESG screens? |
| For lenders | Have going-concern risks been disclosed including non-financial dimensions such as geographic concentration, regulatory exposure, and reputational risk? |
| For employees | Are commitments to workplace conditions, equity, ethical behaviour, and corporate social responsibility communicated in ways that allow employees to align their work with the organisation's values? Are matching-gift, paid-volunteer-time, and similar programs explained accessibly? |
| For customers | Are CSR commitments and their evidence communicated in ways customers can act on? Are claims supported with verifiable data rather than vague assertions? |
| For governments and regulators | Are best-practice commitments documented and verifiable? Are industry-alliance commitments supported with progress reports? |
| For the community | Are the social-contract dimensions of operational decisions communicated proactively rather than after community concern emerges? |
| For all groups | Have you avoided "greenwashing" — claims of social or environmental responsibility unsupported by underlying practice? |

### Phase 4: Surfacing stakeholder consequences of internal decisions

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| For pricing decisions | Will price increases or special-order discounts disadvantage specific customer groups? Will the Robinson-Patman Act constrain pricing options? |
| For sourcing decisions | Does outsourcing affect employee groups (displacement), supplier communities (sudden contract loss), or downstream customers (quality variance)? |
| For investment decisions | Do new capital projects affect community stakeholders (pollution, employment, traffic) in ways that warrant their input? |
| For variance and standard-cost decisions | Are favourable variances actually sustainable, or do they reflect supplier-quality compromises that will hurt customer stakeholders later? |
| For transfer pricing | Are intra-organisational transfer prices producing decisions that align with company-wide goals, or are they distorting goal congruence in ways that may harm external stakeholders through inferior products? |
| For ethical considerations | Have explicit ethical dimensions been surfaced — competence, confidentiality, integrity, credibility — using the IMA framework? Have stakeholders without voice been considered? |

### Phase 5: Reporting and disclosure

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Reporting frequency | Are sustainability reports produced regularly enough to support stakeholder decisions? Annual is typical for sustainability reports; more frequent communication may be warranted for crisis-level events. |
| Reporting comparability | Has the same framework been applied year-over-year so trends can be assessed? Changes in framework or methodology should be disclosed. |
| Reporting verification | Is third-party assurance provided for sustainability claims? Independent verification builds credibility analogous to financial-statement audits. |
| Reporting narrative | Does the report explain not only what was done but why — connecting sustainability initiatives to organisational strategy and stakeholder value? |
| Reporting integration | Is sustainability information presented alongside financial information in a way that allows stakeholders to see the full picture, or are the reports siloed? |

### Phase 6: Crisis response

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Crisis priority | When a crisis emerges (product defect, environmental incident, ethics breach), is the priority "How do we protect the people?" before "How do we save the product?" — the J&J Tylenol pattern? |
| Stakeholder communication speed | Are stakeholders informed quickly through credible channels, or is information being managed for legal protection at the cost of stakeholder trust? |
| Action depth | Is the response remedial (cleaning up) or systemic (preventing recurrence)? Stakeholders increasingly read responses for evidence of systemic learning. |
| Long-term reputation | Are short-term costs being weighed against long-term reputation? J&J spent $100 million on the recall and recovered share price in six weeks; Union Carbide's CEO fled and the case continues to shape corporate responsibility 30+ years later. |
| Stakeholder follow-through | Have those affected received the support promised, or is the crisis being treated as concluded once media attention fades? Bhopal victims continued seeking compensation decades later. |

### Phase 7: Sustaining the relationship

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Reporting continuity | Is sustainability reporting maintained year-over-year as a sustained commitment rather than a one-time exercise? |
| Stakeholder feedback loops | Are channels open for stakeholders to provide feedback that shapes future decisions, or is reporting one-way? |
| Trend disclosure | Are stakeholders shown trends rather than just current snapshots? Year-over-year improvement (or deterioration) is more informative than a single point. |
| Strategic integration | Is sustainability integrated into strategic decisions, or is it a separate report disconnected from operations? |
| Longitudinal commitments | Are long-horizon commitments (Walmart's Project Gigaton, for example, by 2030) tracked transparently with interim milestones? |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: A sustainability report exists but operations contradict it.** Signal: claims about CSR or environmental responsibility appear in the report but employee or community testimony contradicts them. Diagnosis: greenwashing — sustainability communication unsupported by practice. Follow-up: align operations with reporting; reduce reporting claims to what is verifiable; commit to verification by third parties.

- **Pattern: A crisis develops without stakeholder communication infrastructure in place.** Signal: a product defect, environmental incident, or ethics breach emerges and the organisation lacks established channels to communicate. Diagnosis: stakeholder engagement has been treated as event-driven rather than sustained. Follow-up: build communication infrastructure independent of crisis; the J&J Tylenol response was effective because the company already had relationships with health authorities and could mobilise quickly.

- **Pattern: Stakeholder concerns dismissed as costs.** Signal: management treats stakeholder claims as cost categories to be minimised rather than legitimate interests to be addressed. Diagnosis: stockholder-only mindset that misses the long-run financial costs of stakeholder antagonism. Follow-up: reframe stakeholder relationships as opportunities (Hitt-Collins framing); evidence is that "socially responsible companies post higher profits and stock performance than those that were not focused on social responsibility".

- **Pattern: Materiality defined narrowly to exclude non-financial impacts.** Signal: sustainability report excludes major environmental or social impacts because they are deemed financially immaterial. Diagnosis: SASB-style investor-only materiality applied where GRI-style stakeholder-broader materiality is warranted. Follow-up: reconsider the audience; if community and employees are stakeholders, their materiality threshold is different from investors'.

- **Pattern: A pure cost-benefit analysis recommending an ethically questionable decision.** Signal: spreadsheet supports a cheap-but-risky alternative without consideration of consequences for affected stakeholders. Diagnosis: Ford Pinto pattern — quantitative analysis missing ethical dimension. Follow-up: apply the IMA's eight-step ethics framework; consider stakeholders without voice; test against the news-test.

- **Pattern: ESG investors moving away from a holding.** Signal: ethical-investment funds divest; share price weakens as a result. Diagnosis: missing or weak CSR practice is now financially material. Follow-up: investigate the specific ESG concerns; address them substantively rather than communicatively; recognise that 20 per cent of professional-investment management now applies ESG criteria.

- **Pattern: Employees leaving for value-aligned competitors.** Signal: turnover concentrated in younger employees who cite values misalignment in exit interviews. Diagnosis: gap between organisational stated values and employee experience. Follow-up: 76 per cent of millennials weigh CSR in employment choices; recruiting and retention costs may justify CSR investments that look like pure cost.

- **Pattern: Customers boycotting on social media.** Signal: rapid customer attrition driven by viral concern about an organisational practice. Diagnosis: information asymmetry has narrowed; "investors and consumers alike can make financial decisions about firms that align with their own values" (Ch 13.1). Follow-up: address the underlying practice substantively; reactive crisis communication without underlying change rarely repairs the relationship.

- **Pattern: A reporting framework chosen for ease rather than fit.** Signal: the organisation reports under one framework because it is convenient or familiar, not because it matches stakeholder needs. Diagnosis: framework chosen for the producer rather than the audience. Follow-up: assess whether GRI, SASB, or Integrated Reporting best serves the actual stakeholders; consider reporting under multiple frameworks if the audiences are diverse.

- **Pattern: Long-horizon commitments with no interim accountability.** Signal: a 2030 commitment with no annual progress reports or milestones. Diagnosis: long-horizon commitments without interim accountability are not credible. Follow-up: structure annual milestones; report progress transparently; adjust if necessary rather than abandoning.

- **Pattern: Stakeholder voices absent from board and committee structures.** Signal: governance does not include representation or formal voice for non-shareholder stakeholders. Diagnosis: structural under-representation that may produce systematic decisions against stakeholder interests. Follow-up: consider public benefit corporation conversion; consider stakeholder advisory boards; consider explicit stakeholder-representation criteria in board composition.

## When to Use This Reference

- An organisation is producing its first sustainability report and choosing among GRI, SASB, and Integrated Reporting frameworks.
- A crisis (product defect, environmental incident, ethics breach) requires rapid stakeholder communication and the team needs a framework for prioritising responses.
- A capital-investment decision has community impacts that warrant stakeholder consultation before commitment.
- A keep-or-discontinue decision affects employees, suppliers, and local communities and the team needs to map consequences before deciding.
- Performance evaluation is being redesigned and the team is considering balanced-scorecard approaches that incorporate stakeholder dimensions.
- An organisation is considering public benefit corporation status or other stakeholder-accountability legal structures.
- ESG investor pressure is building and the organisation needs to assess and communicate its sustainability posture.
- Multiple stakeholder claims are conflicting and the team needs an analytical framework for weighing them.

## Worked Example

A mid-sized chemical manufacturer, EastBay Industries, faces a multi-stakeholder situation arising from a planned facility expansion. The new production line would add capacity for a high-margin specialty chemical, but the manufacturing process produces moderate air emissions within current regulatory limits. EastBay must engage multiple stakeholders before committing.

**Stakeholder mapping.** Primary information users: shareholders (interested in growth and ROI), lenders (interested in viability and concentration risk), and the regional environmental regulator (interested in emissions and compliance trajectory). Secondary users: facility employees (interested in job stability, working conditions, health), the local community (interested in air quality and community-and-corporate relationships), neighbouring residents (interested in property values and quality of life), customers (interested in supply continuity and product quality), and downstream supply-chain partners. Latent stakeholders: future generations who will inherit cumulative environmental effects; community members not yet aware of the proposal who may organise once they learn.

**Materiality analysis.** Under SASB-style investor materiality, the emissions level is currently below regulatory thresholds and arguably immaterial. Under GRI-style stakeholder materiality, the community impact is material because it falls within community values and concerns even if regulatory thresholds are met. Under Integrated Reporting's six-capitals framing, the project consumes natural capital (air quality), creates manufactured and financial capital (the new line and its returns), draws on social-and-relationship capital (community trust), and depends on intellectual capital (specialty-chemical expertise) and human capital (employees). The decision affects all six, and the report should reflect that.

**Reporting framework choice.** EastBay decides to use the GRI framework for stakeholder-broad communication and to supplement with SASB-style disclosures for investors. Integrated Reporting is rejected as too complex for current organisational capacity but flagged for future adoption.

**Stakeholder engagement design.** EastBay distinguishes among engagement levels:
- *For shareholders and lenders*: a financial-impact analysis showing growth potential, capital costs, and projected ROI.
- *For employees*: a town-hall meeting communicating job creation, health-and-safety standards, and the company's CSR commitments.
- *For the regulator*: a formal pre-application consultation including the proposed emissions-monitoring regime and trajectory toward eventual reduction.
- *For the local community*: a public meeting with a presentation, Q&A, and an honest acknowledgement that the emissions, though below regulatory thresholds, fall within stakeholder concern. EastBay commits to investing in community air-quality monitoring (placing four sensors with public-readable data), to a 20 per cent emissions-reduction plan over five years, and to an annual community accountability meeting.
- *For downstream customers*: technical documentation about supply continuity and quality.

**Surfacing internal-decision consequences.** The decision-making process surfaces tensions between shareholder financial returns and community stakeholder concerns. The CFO's initial cost-benefit analysis treated the emissions as costs of compliance only; the broader stakeholder engagement reveals that uninvested community trust will erode in ways that could affect future regulatory permissions, employee recruitment, and even share-price ESG ratings. The expanded analysis includes these dimensions.

**Crisis preparation.** Recognising that even well-managed projects can produce unexpected events, EastBay establishes a crisis-communication protocol modelled on the J&J Tylenol response: a strategy team prepared to ask "How do we protect the people?" before "How do we protect the project?" with pre-approved community communication channels.

**Reporting commitment.** EastBay commits to annual GRI-aligned sustainability reporting that includes the new line's emissions data, community-meeting outcomes, and progress on the five-year reduction commitment. The reporting will be third-party-verified to build credibility.

**Long-term integration.** The decision establishes a precedent within EastBay for treating sustainability and stakeholder engagement as inputs to all major capital decisions. Future projects will use the same materiality analysis, the same engagement levels, and the same reporting commitments.

This worked example illustrates several volume concepts: stakeholder mapping by type and information need; materiality analysis under different reporting frameworks; communication tailored to stakeholder type; surfacing internal-decision consequences for external stakeholders; crisis preparation drawing on J&J's case as a template; and the integration of sustainability into routine corporate decisions rather than treating it as a separate report. ## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Stockholder-only accountability in a stakeholder-affecting decision.** The Bhopal, Nestlé, and Tylenol cases illustrate that decisions made on stockholder criteria alone can produce stakeholder consequences that ultimately damage shareholder value as well.
- **Greenwashing.** Sustainability claims unsupported by practice erode rather than build trust and increase scrutiny of all subsequent claims. Third-party verification and operational alignment are the remedies.
- **Reporting as compliance only.** Treating sustainability reporting as a regulatory compliance task rather than a stakeholder-communication tool produces formulaic reports that fail to engage stakeholders and miss the strategic opportunities Hitt-Collins identifies.
- **Crisis communication without underlying change.** Reactive communication about an emerging crisis, without substantive change to the practice that caused it, rarely repairs stakeholder trust. Substantive change is the durable response.
- **Materiality defined to exclude inconvenient impacts.** Narrow investor-materiality applied to broadly stakeholder-oriented audiences misses what those audiences need to make decisions. The framework should match the audience.
- **One-time reports without longitudinal commitment.** Sustainability reports produced once or sporadically are less credible than sustained year-over-year reporting with comparable methodology and trend disclosure.
- **Long-horizon commitments without interim milestones.** A 2030 commitment without annual progress reports is not credible. Interim milestones with transparent progress tracking are required.
- **Excluding latent stakeholders.** Future generations, communities outside the immediate operational footprint, and community members not yet organised all have standing in some decisions. Excluding them produces decisions that face later opposition once those stakeholders organise.
- **Treating CSR as cost rather than investment.** The HBR study and Deutsche Bank meta-analysis evidence that socially responsible companies outperform suggests CSR is a strategic investment, not a cost. Treating it as cost incentivises minimisation rather than effective deployment.
- **Failing to integrate stakeholder consequences into internal decision-making.** Ch 9.4's treatment of transfer pricing, Ch 8's treatment of standard-cost variances, Ch 10's treatment of make-or-buy and segment-elimination — each has stakeholder consequences that responsibility-centre framing can obscure.
