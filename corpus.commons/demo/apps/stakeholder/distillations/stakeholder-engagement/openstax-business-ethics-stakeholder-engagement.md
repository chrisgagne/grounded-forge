# OpenStax Business Ethics, Stakeholder-Engagement Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2018). *Business Ethics*. Rice University. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/business-ethics. Senior contributing authors Stephen M. Byars and Kurt Stanberry; seven contributing authors.

## Stakeholder-Engagement Relevance

OpenStax's *Business Ethics* is fundamentally a stakeholder-pluralist text. Chapter 3, "Defining and Prioritising Stakeholders", is dedicated to the central engagement question — who counts, why, and in what order. Chapter 4 extends the analysis to three "special" stakeholders: society at large (corporate-law and CSR debates), the environment (rights-of-nature and earth-jurisprudence frames), and government (revolving doors, regulation, lobbying). Chapter 5 examines how culture, time, geography, and religion shape the stakeholder field. Chapters 6 and 8 treat employees as primary stakeholders with distinct rights (safety, fair wages, accommodation, anti-harassment, equal pay, privacy). Chapter 7 inverts the relationship — what employees, in turn, owe employers as stakeholders. Chapter 9 examines stakeholders inside professional contexts (entrepreneurship, advertising, insurance, health care). Chapter 11 closes with the lead author's stakeholder credo: "all stakeholders are significant".

This distillation extracts the stakeholder-engagement vocabulary, taxonomies, and decision tools from across the book and projects them onto the engagement task. The key claims the book brings to engagement work are: (1) stakeholders are ends in themselves, not means to bottom-line outcomes; (2) prioritisation is a managerial competence requiring power × interest analysis combined with exigency; (3) the moral minimum requires honouring obligations to non-shareholder stakeholders; (4) the single ethical standard applies across cultures and so anchors international engagement; (5) ethical maximums protect long-term goodwill more reliably than ethical minimums.

## Key Concepts for Stakeholder Engagement

1. **Stakeholder definition.** Stakeholders are individuals and entities affected by a business's decisions: "clients, customers, suppliers, investors, retailers, employees, the media, the government, members of the surrounding community, competitors, and even the environment". The book stresses that stakeholders may play multiple roles (an employee is also a customer, a community member, perhaps a stockholder). (Source: *Business Ethics*, Ch 1.1, "Acting with Integrity"; Ch 3.1, "Stakeholder Relationships")

2. **Three approaches to stakeholder claims (Donaldson and Preston).** *Descriptive* — the company is composed of stakeholder groups, each with interests; develop the most accurate model and balance interests fairly. *Instrumental* — managing stakeholders well improves the bottom line. *Normative* — stakeholders are ends in themselves; "the interests of all stakeholders are of intrinsic value". The book endorses the normative approach as foundational. (Source: Ch 3.2, "The Ethical Basis of Stakeholders' Claims")

3. **Stakeholder linkage categories (Grunig and Hunt).** *Enabling* (regulators, board, stockholders — they "permit the firm to function"); *normative* (industry peers, competitors — they shape industry norms); *functional* (suppliers, employees, customers, distributors — they handle inputs and outputs); *diffused* (NGOs, voters, media — less direct but potentially impactful relationships). (Source: Ch 3.2, "Defining Stakeholder Categories")

4. **Four publics by awareness and capacity.** *Nonpublic* (no problem recognised), *latent* (problem unrecognised), *aware* (problem recognised), *active* (organised in response). The Tylenol crisis demonstrated Johnson & Johnson modelling enlightened communication across publics; Volkswagen's emissions cover-up the failure mode. (Source: Ch 3.2, "Grunig and Hunt's Theory")

5. **Stakeholder prioritisation: power, interest, exigency.** Power × interest matrix — high-power-high-interest = key stakeholder; low-power-low-interest = monitor only; high-power-low-interest = keep satisfied; low-power-high-interest = communicate / inform but their priority is low unless exigency rises. *Exigency* (urgency of a stakeholder claim) combines with inherent priority. (Source: Ch 3.3, "Stakeholder Prioritization")

6. **MITRE five-step stakeholder management.** Identify stakeholders (and reidentify periodically); gather data on concerns through surveys, focus groups, social-media monitoring, product-usage data; analyse and present to decision-makers (or decide directly); inform stakeholders that their concerns were considered. The text frames this as standard practice and recommends explicit communication that closes the loop. (Source: Ch 3.3, "Managing Stakeholder Expectations")

7. **Triple bottom line (Elkington, John).** People, planet, profit. Reframes the bottom line as three measures: economic, social, environmental. Forces stakeholder-engagement work to consider externalities and indirect stakeholders (the environment, future generations, communities). (Source: Ch 3.4, "People, Planet, Profit: The Triple Bottom Line")

8. **Greenwashing as engagement failure.** "Carrying out superficial CSR efforts that merely cover up systemic ethics problems for the sake of public relations". The Coca-Cola water-footprint example and the Ben & Jerry's CAFO supplier example illustrate the failure mode: communication without substantive practice change. The signal that engagement has degraded into PR is when stakeholder claims are answered with messaging rather than behaviour change. (Source: Ch 3.4, "CSR as Public Relations Tool")

9. **Special stakeholders — society, environment, government.** Society's claim is grounded in the social contract: society permits incorporation, so corporations owe a *quid pro quo*. The environment's claim is debated under earth jurisprudence — in Ecuador (2008 constitution) the environment has legal standing; in the US, the *tragedy of the commons* frames the engagement problem. Government is both regulator and lobbying target; the revolving door creates engagement-integrity questions. (Source: Ch 4)

10. **Cross-cultural stakeholder engagement.** *Localisation* — adapting products, content, and engagement protocols to local norms — is required. Time and personal-space conventions vary widely; *contact* versus *noncontact* cultures shape engagement (handshakes, eye contact, proximity). The single ethical standard applies even when local norms permit conduct (bribery, sweatshops) the firm's standards prohibit. (Source: Ch 5.3, "Business as Global Relationships")

11. **Employees as primary stakeholders.** Ethical employer obligations: safe workplace (OSHA), harassment-free environment (EEOC / Title VII), fair wages, transparency, advance notice of mass layoffs (WARN Act, sixty days), reasonable accommodation (ADA), pay equity (Equal Pay Act, Bostock), privacy. The text takes a strong position that compensation should narrow the CEO-to-worker pay gap (current ~300× US ratio). (Source: Ch 6, Ch 8)

12. **Diversity and inclusion as engagement work.** *Diversity* is the mix; *inclusion* is the engagement of all employees in the corporate culture. Howard Ross: "Diversity is being invited to the party; inclusion is being asked to dance." McKinsey's diversity-dividend research links top-quartile diversity to top-quartile financial performance. (Source: Ch 8.1, "Workplace Diversity")

13. **Reasonable accommodation; the Verizon example.** ADA (1990) requires accommodation unless it imposes undue hardship. Verizon's $20 million EEOC settlement (the largest single ADA settlement) for an inflexible no-fault attendance policy illustrates the engagement failure when stakeholder needs are subordinated to administrative simplicity. (Source: Ch 8.2, "Protections for People with Disabilities")

14. **Stakeholder engagement under crisis.** The Samsung washer recall (full refunds for ~3 million units) is paradigmatic ethical-maximum engagement; Volkswagen's emissions cover-up is the failure mode. The Equifax data-breach response (executive stock sales weeks before public announcement) exemplifies the betrayal of customer-stakeholder trust. (Source: Ch 1.2, "The Equifax Data Breach"; Ch 3.1)

15. **Whistleblowing as stakeholder voice.** Whistleblowers are stakeholders surfacing harm to other stakeholders (taxpayers, investors, customers, the public). The False Claims Act qui tam framework rewards relators 15-30% of recovery. The text frames whistleblowing as "a steward of the public interest" when motives are appropriate. (Source: Ch 7.5, "Whistleblowing: Risks and Rewards")

16. **The 1-1-1 model and integrated philanthropy (Salesforce / Benioff).** Donating 1% of equity, 1% of employee time, and 1% of products to nonprofit organisations. The lead author's Appendix C credo: ethical entrepreneurs "engage in both private and corporate philanthropy" and are "good stewards of the social and physical environments where they do business". (Source: App B, "Marc Benioff"; App C)

17. **The moral minimum and the social contract.** Honouring obligations to all stakeholders, including those who own no shares, is "the moral minimum a firm must undertake to satisfy the base threshold for acting ethically". Engagement work that meets only the legal minimum is below the moral minimum if it neglects non-shareholder stakeholders. (Source: Ch 4.1, "The Two Sides of the Corporate Responsibility Debate")

## Questions to Ask During Stakeholder Engagement

### Phase 1: Mapping (identifying stakeholders)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify all stakeholders | Who is affected by this decision, project, or business? Customers, employees, suppliers, communities, the environment, regulators, future generations, indirect stakeholders without voice? |
| Categorise by linkage type | Are these stakeholders enabling (regulators, board, stockholders), normative (peers, competitors), functional (suppliers, employees, customers, distributors), or diffused (NGOs, voters, media)? Different types warrant different engagement protocols. |
| Surface stakeholders without voice | Who is affected but has no formal channel to express concern? Future generations? The environment itself? Indirect stakeholders (children of employees in regions of operation; communities downstream)? |
| Detect multi-role stakeholders | Are some stakeholders playing more than one role (employee + customer + community member)? Their interests may conflict; how do they reconcile internally? |
| Acknowledge the social contract | Does this engagement reflect the *quid pro quo* — the firm enjoys society's permission to incorporate, so what does it owe society in return? |

### Phase 2: Categorising and prioritising

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Map power and interest | For each stakeholder, what is their level of power over the firm and interest in the outcome? Where does each fall in the power × interest matrix? |
| Detect low-power-high-interest stakeholders | Who has high interest but low power — most likely to be deprioritised? The book is explicit that ethical engagement requires that low-power-high-interest stakeholders not be ignored. |
| Assess exigency | Is a stakeholder claim becoming urgent? Has a low-priority stakeholder moved into a high-priority position by force of events? |
| Distinguish descriptive, instrumental, normative framings | Are we mapping stakeholders to understand them (descriptive), to manage them for our benefit (instrumental), or to honour them as ends in themselves (normative)? The text treats normative as foundational. |
| Identify the four publics | For this issue, are stakeholders nonpublic, latent, aware, or active? Different communication strategies apply to each. |

### Phase 3: Engaging (stakeholder communication and consultation)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Engage early and substantively | Have we gathered stakeholder input before a decision is taken, not as ratification afterward? |
| Choose the appropriate channel | Survey, focus group, public meeting, informal consultation, social media monitoring, product-usage analytics? Different stakeholder types respond to different channels. |
| Avoid greenwashing | Are our communications about stakeholder concerns matched by behaviour change? Or are we issuing statements that signal CSR without substance? |
| Honour cultural variation | Are we adapting engagement to local norms — time, personal space, communication style, religious observance, dress, language? |
| Maintain reciprocity | Are we informing stakeholders that their concerns were considered? The MITRE five-step process insists on closing the loop. |
| Resist the corporate-personhood asymmetry | Citizens United gave corporations free-speech rights to spend without limit on political advocacy. In engagements, are we using this asymmetry to overpower stakeholders (lobbying against environmental regulation) or to amplify stakeholder voice? |

### Phase 4: Conflict and disagreement among stakeholders

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Use the moral-minimum test | When stakeholder claims conflict, what response would honour the basic rights of all stakeholders affected, even if not all can be fully satisfied? |
| Apply ethical-maximum / ethical-minimum framing | Are we evaluating responses across the spectrum from the legally minimum to the strongest available stakeholder-honouring action? Where on the spectrum will we land, and why? |
| Apply the veil-of-ignorance test | If we did not know our own role in the affected population, would we accept this resolution? |
| Test for short-term-profit bias against long-term goodwill | Is the choice optimising quarterly results at the expense of stakeholder relationships that compound over years? |
| Detect coalition shifts | Have stakeholders moved from latent to aware to active publics? Has a coalition formed that did not exist when the issue was framed? |
| Identify when an external advocate (NGO, regulator, whistleblower) is reframing the engagement | Volkswagen and Wells Fargo discovered the engagement landscape after the EPA / regulators announced; ethical engagement gets ahead of this by surfacing concerns proactively. |

### Phase 5: Sustaining engagement over time

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Re-identify stakeholders periodically | Stakeholder cohorts change in size and significance over time. Who has emerged since the last engagement cycle? Who has receded? |
| Build feedback channels | Are stakeholders empowered to surface unanticipated consequences? |
| Monitor goodwill | Goodwill is built slowly and eroded quickly. What signals — Net Promoter Score, retention, brand metrics, employee engagement scores — are tracking the relationship over time? |
| Test the *tone at the top* | Are senior leaders modelling the engagement values we have committed to externally, or only externally claiming them? |
| Maintain whistleblower protection | Are internal channels protected in fact, not just in policy? Reporters of wrongdoing should not face career retaliation; the Sallie Krawcheck and Sherron Watkins cases illustrate the cost when this fails. |
| Honour the stewardship relationship for nonprofits | Where the stakeholder is a donor or beneficiary of charity, the obligation is stewardship — donations are gifts intended for others very much in need (Lindahl). |

### Phase 6: Cross-cultural and international engagement

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply the single ethical standard | Are we engaging differently in cultures that permit bribery, sweatshop labour, environmental dumping, or corruption? The text takes a strong position: the same standard applies. |
| Localise without compromising | Are we adapting to local norms (time, personal space, language) without compromising on absolute values (honesty, dignity, anti-corruption, anti-harassment)? |
| Test against the FCPA / OECD framework | If a payment, gift, or action would violate the FCPA in the US, does it become acceptable abroad? The text's position: no. |
| Recognise the imperialism risk | Are we imposing Western norms on local stakeholders in ways that override their voice? Or are we requiring norms (worker safety, anti-child-labour) that are absolute? Distinguish localising preferences from absolute values. |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: Stakeholder list is being assembled by distribution-list habit.** Signal: the same names appear in every engagement, regardless of issue. Diagnosis (in source vocabulary): stakeholder identification is descriptive without normative testing — *who has standing on this issue* is being conflated with *who is on the convening list*. Follow-up: re-identify per issue; surface low-power-high-interest stakeholders.
- **Pattern: A high-power stakeholder is dominating the engagement.** Signal: discussion centres on the regulator's or the largest customer's concerns; smaller voices are heard symbolically. Diagnosis: power is being weighted exclusively without normative consideration of stakeholders as ends. Follow-up: apply the veil-of-ignorance test; ask whether the resolution would be acceptable from the position of the lowest-power-high-interest stakeholder.
- **Pattern: Stakeholder concerns are being answered with messaging.** Signal: a sustainability statement is published; a CSR page is updated; an apology is issued; underlying behaviour is unchanged. Diagnosis: greenwashing (or the analogous PR-substitute for substantive change in any ethical domain). Follow-up: measure whether the underlying practice has changed; if not, the engagement is performative.
- **Pattern: A latent stakeholder coalition is forming around an issue the firm has not addressed.** Signal: social-media activity, NGO reports, regulatory inquiry, journalism. Diagnosis: an aware public is becoming active; the firm has missed the latent-public window when communication could have been guided. Follow-up: engage immediately with the substantive concern (not just the messaging); the Tylenol-Johnson&Johnson playbook is more effective than the Volkswagen-cover-up playbook.
- **Pattern: The engagement is structurally one-way.** Signal: the firm communicates positions to stakeholders; stakeholders have no protected channel back. Diagnosis: the MITRE step five (informing stakeholders that their concerns were considered) is being skipped, or stakeholder consultation is ratification not consultation. Follow-up: build feedback channels with protection (whistleblower-style anonymity for sensitive issues).
- **Pattern: A whistleblower is being treated as disloyal.** Signal: known reporters of wrongdoing are isolated, "reorganised out", or quietly blocked from promotion. Diagnosis: stakeholder voice is being suppressed; the firm is privileging internal political stability over substantive concern resolution. Follow-up: review the whistleblowing protection in fact; protect the reporter; investigate the substantive claim.
- **Pattern: Stakeholders without voice are being silently externalised.** Signal: environmental costs, costs to communities, costs to future generations, costs to children of workers — none appear in the engagement frame. Diagnosis: tragedy-of-the-commons dynamic; no stakeholder represents the common resource. Follow-up: invoke triple-bottom-line / earth-jurisprudence framings to surface these stakeholders explicitly.
- **Pattern: A multinational is operating to local norm in a low-standards jurisdiction.** Signal: factory conditions, environmental practices, or anti-corruption posture differs from the firm's home-country posture. Diagnosis: ethical relativism / cultural-accommodation failure; the single-standard rule is being violated. Follow-up: apply the categorical-imperative test; review FCPA and OECD framework; the worker / community stakeholders abroad are not lower-priority than home-country stakeholders.
- **Pattern: The stakeholder map omits the environment.** Signal: discussions of decisions affecting natural resources, emissions, or land use proceed without an environmental stakeholder voice. Diagnosis: the corporate-personhood asymmetry — corporations have legal standing, the environment generally does not. Follow-up: invoke earth-jurisprudence framing (Cullinan, Berry, Ecuador); represent the environment explicitly in the stakeholder map.
- **Pattern: An employee accommodation request is being denied for administrative simplicity.** Signal: an inflexible attendance policy, a one-size-fits-all dress code, a uniform schedule. Diagnosis: the Verizon pattern — administrative simplicity at the cost of stakeholder accommodation. Follow-up: evaluate against ADA / Title VII reasonable-accommodation standard; the moral minimum exceeds the legally minimum.

## When to Use This Reference

- A new project, product, or strategic move is being scoped and the question is who has standing.
- Stakeholder claims are conflicting and the question is how to weigh and prioritise them.
- A scandal or crisis has emerged and the firm is choosing between minimum response and ethical-maximum response.
- A multinational is entering a new jurisdiction with different cultural and regulatory norms.
- An employee group, NGO, or community coalition is forming around an issue the firm has not yet addressed.
- A decision affects stakeholders without voice (environment, future generations, indirect stakeholders, children, voiceless beneficiaries).
- The firm is reviewing CSR or sustainability claims and the question is whether they are substantive or PR.
- A whistleblower has surfaced; the engagement question is how to respond ethically and respond to the substantive claim.
- The firm's compensation, accommodation, harassment, or diversity practices need ethical review.
- The firm is evaluating its tone-at-the-top — is leadership modelling the values claimed externally?

## Worked Example

A regional grocery chain (50 stores, 3,000 employees) is considering closing five underperforming stores in lower-income neighbourhoods. The CFO frames this as a routine portfolio decision: the five stores have negative contribution margin, freeing capital to invest in growth markets. The CEO asks the head of community engagement to review the proposal before the board meeting.

**Mapping.** The community engagement head identifies stakeholders. Enabling: shareholders, board, lenders. Functional: employees of the five stores (~250), suppliers (especially small local producers who depend on these stores for shelf space), customers (especially older customers and those without cars). Diffused: community organisations, local press, regulators (the closures may affect food access in two neighbourhoods qualifying as "food deserts" under USDA criteria). Stakeholders without voice: children whose families' food access depends on the stores; elderly residents; future residents.

**Categorising.** Power × interest: shareholders high-high; the 250 employees low-power-high-interest; the affected customers low-power-high-interest; the small local suppliers low-power-medium-interest; community groups variable power but high interest. The CFO's framing maps shareholders to the dominant engagement; the moral-minimum test asks whether low-power-high-interest stakeholders are being honoured as ends or treated as means to capital reallocation.

**Triple-bottom-line analysis.** *People*: closing the stores eliminates 250 jobs; eliminates food access for two neighbourhoods that already qualify as food deserts; eliminates an aggregator for ~30 small local producers. *Planet*: less direct, but increased driving for groceries from displaced customers. *Profit*: capital release of ~$8M annually after store-closing costs; ~$15M one-time from real estate sales.

**Engaging.** The community engagement head proposes a multi-stakeholder consultation: meetings in each of the five neighbourhoods with affected customers; conversations with each of the 30 small suppliers; review with the employee union representing the 250 store employees; consultation with city governments and food-access nonprofits.

**Conflict and ethical-maximum framing.** A pure utilitarian calculation favours closure (capital reallocation produces stronger long-term return; affected populations are smaller than the customer base of the surviving stores). A deontological frame surfaces the duty to community stakeholders who have been served by the firm for decades; closure treats them merely as means to the firm's capital reallocation. A Rawlsian veil-of-ignorance test: if I did not know my role (CEO, shareholder, customer, employee, supplier), would I accept this resolution? The lowest-position stakeholders bear the greatest cost.

**Ethical-maximum option development.** The community engagement head, working with operations, develops a hybrid: keep two of the five stores open with reduced footprint (cutting fixed costs ~40% per store) under a new neighbourhood-store format with smaller staff and tightened SKU mix; close three; partner with a regional food-access nonprofit for the three closed neighbourhoods to support a delivery / mobile market substitute, with the firm contributing $2M/year for three years. Total capital release: ~$5M/year (smaller than CFO's proposal) but stakeholder welfare is substantively honoured.

**Tone at the top.** The CEO presents both options to the board, framing them as "ethical-minimum" (close all five) versus "ethical-maximum" (the hybrid). The board chooses the hybrid; the CEO communicates the decision to the affected employees (extending advance notice beyond the WARN minimum where feasible) and to the affected communities.

**Sustaining.** Eighteen months later: store sales in the surviving smaller-format locations are recovering; the food-access partnership has been recognised by regional press and one regulatory agency; goodwill among the firm's broader customer base has registered measurably in employee engagement scores and in retention. The CFO concedes that the long-term financial outcome is comparable to the closure-only option, with substantially greater stakeholder welfare and reputation gain.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Treating stakeholders as means to bottom-line outcomes.** The instrumental approach is permitted but not foundational; the normative approach treats stakeholders as ends in themselves.
- **Distribution-list stakeholder identification.** Re-identifying per issue is essential.
- **Power-only weighting.** Low-power-high-interest stakeholders are most likely to be ignored, but their claims often carry the greatest moral weight.
- **Greenwashing.** CSR statements that substitute for behaviour change.
- **Compliance-as-checkbox engagement.** Meeting the legal minimum without honouring the moral minimum.
- **One-way engagement (consultation as ratification).** Real consultation gathers input *before* the decision and informs stakeholders that their concerns were considered.
- **Cultural-relativism defence for low-standards jurisdictions.** The single ethical standard applies internationally.
- **Tone-at-the-top mismatch.** Leadership behaviour at odds with publicly claimed engagement values.
- **Whistleblower retaliation.** Treating ethical voice as disloyalty.
- **Externalising costs to stakeholders without voice** (environment, future generations, indirect stakeholders).
