# OpenStax Principles of Economics 3e, Stakeholder-Engagement Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2022). *Principles of Economics 3e*. Rice University. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/principles-economics-3e. Lead author Steven A. Greenlaw; nine contributing authors.

## Stakeholder-Engagement Relevance

OpenStax's *Principles of Economics 3e* is primarily a system-level book: most of its analysis is about how markets, prices, and aggregate variables behave. It is therefore tempting to treat it as orthogonal to stakeholder engagement, which is intrinsically about people and relationships. That would miss its contribution. Several chapters address situations where stakeholder analysis is unavoidable and the book provides the structural frame for understanding why one party can sustain a position that another cannot. Labour markets and unions (Ch 14) frame the bargaining structure between employers and workers, including monopsony, bilateral monopoly, and the changing institutional landscape of organised labour. Negative and positive externalities (Chs 12, 13) name the third parties affected by transactions they are not party to — the textbook home of "stakeholders without voice." Public goods and common resources (Ch 13) frame the free-rider problem and the institutional responses (Ostrom's local self-governance among them). Poverty and inequality (Ch 15) examine distributional consequences and the trade-offs of redistributive policy. Public economy (Ch 18) treats the political-process stakeholders — voters, special interests, regulators, lobbyists — and the failures of representative democracy. Antitrust and natural-monopoly regulation (Ch 11) treat producers, consumers, and regulators as a stakeholder triangle. Immigration (Ch 14.6) and globalisation/protectionism (Chs 33-34) make the distributional consequences of trade explicit and identify the consumer / displaced-worker / export-sector triangle that defines trade-policy stakeholder engagement.

The book's distinctive contribution to stakeholder engagement, then, is **structural**: it explains *why* certain stakeholder relationships have the bargaining shape they do (elasticity, market power, externality, asymmetric information, free-riding), and what institutional responses match what structural problem. It is not a guide to interpersonal engagement; it is a guide to recognising the systemic forces that shape any specific engagement.

This distillation projects that structural framework onto the seven-phase engagement framework used across the corpus: mapping, framing, convening, surfacing conflict, reaching agreement, ratifying, and post-engagement.

## Key Concepts for Stakeholder Engagement

1. **Stakeholders defined by externality, not by participation.** An externality is "a market exchange that affects a third party who is outside or 'external' to the exchange." Stakeholders, in this framing, are exactly the parties affected without being party to the transaction. Pollution affects downstream communities; education affects neighbours, employers, and democratic stability. Engagement requires identifying these externally-affected parties first. (Source: OpenStax, *Principles of Economics 3e*, Ch 12.1)

2. **Public goods, common resources, and the free-rider problem.** Some goods are nonexcludable (cannot exclude users) and non-rival (one person's use doesn't reduce another's). These create systematic free-riding: rational individual non-contribution when collective contribution is needed. Engagement design must account for whether the goods at issue are private (rivalrous, excludable), public (nonexcludable, non-rival), common-pool (rivalrous, nonexcludable), or club (excludable, non-rival). The institutional response differs by type. (Source: Ch 13.3)

3. **Monopsony and one-employer towns.** When a single employer dominates a labour market, the employer faces an upward-sloping labour supply curve and hires fewer workers at lower wages than competitive markets would yield. Engagement with workers in monopsony settings differs structurally from engagement in competitive labour markets; the worker has fewer outside options and bargaining is asymmetric. (Source: Ch 14.2)

4. **Unions as supply-side monopolies; bilateral monopoly.** Collective bargaining shifts the power balance through coordinated supply control. Union and monopsony together produce bilateral monopoly: employment is unambiguously lower than competitive, wage is indeterminate between bargaining limits. The engagement question becomes "who has what BATNA," not "what is the market wage." (Source: Ch 14.2, Ch 14.3, Ch 14.4)

5. **Discrimination and earnings gaps.** Earnings gaps by gender (~82% of male earnings) and race (~75% White-Black for men) persist after controlling for observable factors. Becker's argument that competitive markets penalise discrimination is challenged by Darity and others. Lisa D. Cook estimates GDP could be ~4.4% higher with full participation. Engagement on workforce equity must take seriously that markets alone have not closed these gaps. (Source: Ch 14.5)

6. **Immigration: aggregate gains, distributional costs.** Immigration raises U.S. economic output but produces small wage reductions (~1%) for low-skill domestic workers competing with low-skill immigrants. Net fiscal effect is positive federally but can be negative at state and local levels where immigration concentrates. Engagement on immigration must distinguish aggregate from distributional framing. (Source: Ch 14.6)

7. **Inequality and the safety net.** U.S. income inequality has risen since the late 1970s, driven by household-composition changes and "winner-take-all" labour markets. The safety net (TANF, EITC, SNAP, Medicaid, SSI) reduces poverty but creates poverty-trap effects that policy design must address. Engagement on redistributive policy faces a real efficiency-equity trade-off, but well-designed programmes (especially education-as-investment) can improve both. (Source: Ch 15.1, Ch 15.3, Ch 15.4, Ch 15.5)

8. **Information asymmetry and signalling.** Akerlof's lemons problem: where one party has better information, the better-informed can drive out the trustworthy. Mechanisms reduce asymmetry: warranties, brands, occupational licences, reputation, cosigners, collateral. For stakeholder engagement, the question is "who knows what they don't know they don't know?" and "what mechanism can credibly communicate the missing information?" (Source: Ch 16.1)

9. **Insurance, moral hazard, and adverse selection.** Insurance pools risk but generates two information problems. Moral hazard: insured behave more riskily. Adverse selection: high-risk individuals seek insurance, driving out low-risk customers, in a death spiral. The mitigation tools (deductibles, copayments, mandates, group coverage, risk-pool design) are all engagement-design tools when the stakeholder context involves shared risk. (Source: Ch 16.2)

10. **Public-choice realism: rational ignorance, special interests, pork-barrel.** Anthony Downs's rational ignorance: voters lack incentives to become well-informed. Special-interest groups exploit concentrated benefits and dispersed costs. Pork-barrel and logrolling produce legislative outcomes that do not maximise social welfare. Engagement with political stakeholders must acknowledge these structural features rather than assume an idealised democratic process. (Source: Ch 18)

11. **Coase, property rights, and transaction costs.** With clear property rights and low transaction costs, parties can negotiate efficient outcomes regardless of who has the rights. Where transaction costs are high (many parties, ambiguous causation, dispersed harms), private negotiation fails and regulation or market-based interventions (pollution charges, marketable permits) become necessary. (Source: Ch 12.3)

12. **Trade and the consumer / worker / industry triangle.** Free trade increases consumer surplus and aggregate welfare; protectionism produces concentrated benefits to the protected industry at the cost of consumers and downstream firms. Cost-per-protected-job estimates (textile ~$199K, sugar ~$826K, dairy ~$685K) make the trade-off explicit. The engagement question is whether displaced workers are compensated through retraining and adjustment assistance, not whether the displacement is permitted. (Source: Ch 34.2)

13. **Antitrust and the regulator-firm-consumer triangle.** Mergers and restrictive practices implicate three stakeholder groups. The four-firm concentration ratio and Herfindahl-Hirschman Index measure structural concentration; modern analysis uses competitive-effects studies. Regulatory capture is the persistent risk: industry can capture the regulator and turn protection-of-consumers into protection-of-incumbents. (Source: Ch 11)

## Questions to Ask During Stakeholder Engagement

### Phase 1: Mapping (Identifying who has standing)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify externally-affected parties | Who is affected by this decision but not party to it? Where are the externalities? Who bears costs without consenting; who receives benefits without contributing? |
| Identify the "voiceless" stakeholders | Are there future generations, the natural environment, distant communities, or weak counterparties who are affected but have no formal seat? How will their interests be represented? |
| Identify the structural shape of any labour engagement | Is the labour market in this engagement competitive, monopsonistic, unionised, or bilateral monopoly? The engagement design depends on the structure. |
| Map information asymmetries | Who knows what others don't know? Are there lemons-problem dynamics, where the better-informed are driving out the trustworthy? |
| Identify the kind of good at issue | Is the matter at the heart of the engagement private, public, common-pool, or club? Free-rider risk varies with the answer. |

### Phase 2: Framing the Engagement (Before convening)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Distinguish aggregate from distributional framing | Is this engagement about whether something is good for the aggregate (efficiency), or about how its costs and benefits are distributed? Both questions matter; conflating them produces argument at cross purposes. |
| Surface elasticity-driven power asymmetry | Whose options are inelastic and whose are elastic? The party with elastic options can walk away; the party with inelastic options is exposed to price-and-condition pressure. |
| Frame the BATNA | What is each party's best alternative to a negotiated agreement? In bilateral-monopoly settings, the wage / price / quantity range is determined by relative bargaining position, not by an external benchmark. |
| Test for free-rider risk | Will participants benefit even if they don't contribute? If yes, the engagement design must use mandate, taxation, or social-pressure mechanisms — not voluntary contribution. |
| Acknowledge regulatory capture risk | If a regulator is part of this engagement, what protects against capture? Whose interests are most likely to dominate the rule-making process? |

### Phase 3: Convening (Choosing format and participants)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Match topology to good-type | For public goods and common resources, the convening must include all affected parties or design enforcement that does so. Local self-governance (Ostrom) succeeds in some cases; broader enforcement (regulation, taxation) is needed where local solidarity is absent. |
| Address voiceless stakeholders structurally | If future generations or the natural environment are stakeholders, who is delegated to represent their interest? Is the delegation credible — does the representative bear consequences for getting it wrong? |
| Counter information asymmetry | Have we set up signalling devices (warranties, audits, transparency mandates, third-party certification) so that stakeholders engage with verified rather than asserted information? |
| Address concentrated-benefits / dispersed-costs | If the convening is structurally biased — small organised group versus large unorganised group — what corrective mechanisms (mandatory representation, public hearings, opportunity for written submission) reduce the bias? |

### Phase 4: Surfacing Conflict (When disagreement emerges)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Distinguish efficiency disagreement from distribution disagreement | Are parties disagreeing about whether something works (efficiency), or about who gets what (distribution)? Both can be resolved, but the conversation differs. |
| Trace incidence | When a tax, fee, wage, or burden is being assigned to one party, who actually bears the cost? Elasticity determines incidence; the legal target is often not the economic bearer. |
| Surface externality framing | Is one party effectively asking another to bear an external cost the first does not internalise? The fix may be Coasean (assign property rights and let parties bargain) or regulatory (cap, tax, or mandate). |
| Test for moral hazard / adverse selection | Is one party's exposure to risk creating perverse incentives in the other? Insurance-market dynamics (deductibles, mandates, risk-pool design) provide the structural toolkit. |

### Phase 5: Reaching Agreement (Closing the engagement)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply Pareto-improvement and compensation tests | Does the proposed agreement leave at least one party better off without making any worse off (Pareto improvement)? If not, who is being made worse off, and is compensation included? |
| Internalise externalities | Does the agreement bring third-party costs and benefits into the transaction (via tax, charge, permit, or property-rights assignment)? If not, the agreement may be locally efficient but socially deficient. |
| Address the marginal incentive | What is each party's marginal incentive once the agreement is in force? Will compliance happen because parties want to comply, or only because monitoring catches them? |
| Document and credibly commit | Is the commitment structure credible — backed by enforcement, reputation, or repeated interaction? Without credibility, agreements unravel under future pressure. |

### Phase 6: Ratifying (Making the agreement stick)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Address downstream stakeholders | Have parties whose acceptance is needed for execution been engaged in the ratification, even if not in the negotiation? |
| Plan for political-economy pushback | If the agreement creates concentrated losers and dispersed winners, the losers will organise. What political-economy resilience is built into the design? |
| Anticipate adjustment costs | If the agreement triggers reallocation (workers displaced, firms exiting, capital redeployed), what transition support and adjustment assistance is built in? |

### Phase 7: Post-engagement (Maintaining the relationship)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Monitor incidence drift | The economic incidence of an agreement (who actually bears its cost) can shift over time as elasticities, alternatives, and institutions change. Are we re-checking? |
| Track externality changes | Is the third-party impact behaving as expected, or have second-order effects emerged that warrant adjustment? |
| Test for capture | If a regulator was created or empowered, is it operating in the public interest, or is it serving the regulated industry? Independent review and rotation of personnel are standard correctives. |
| Maintain the institutional frame | For long-running engagements (collective bargaining, environmental cap-and-trade, public-good provision), is the institutional frame still fit for purpose, or has the underlying structure shifted? |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: A decision is being made between two parties when a third party bears the cost.** Signal: third parties absent from the conversation; harms or benefits to them mentioned only as side effects. Diagnosis: externality not internalised. Follow-up: surface the externality explicitly; consider Coasean (property-rights) or regulatory (cap, tax, permit) responses; bring affected third parties into the engagement or design proxy representation.
- **Pattern: A voluntary-contribution scheme is failing to fund a public good.** Signal: free-riding observed; contributors feel resentful; the scheme persistently under-funds. Diagnosis: structural — public goods cannot be reliably funded by voluntary contribution. Follow-up: shift to mandate, taxation, advertising-supported provision, or local-self-governance with enforcement; do not rely on appeals to civic virtue.
- **Pattern: Workers have weak bargaining position in a one-employer town.** Signal: wages low relative to productivity; turnover low because alternatives are scarce; workforce engagement initiatives not landing. Diagnosis: monopsony. Follow-up: engagement must compensate for the structural asymmetry — this is where union recognition, transparent compensation studies, and minimum-wage / labour-standard floors land.
- **Pattern: Aggregate framing is dominating distributional concerns.** Signal: "this is good for the country / industry / firm" without examining who wins and who loses. Diagnosis: distributional analysis missing. Follow-up: identify winners and losers explicitly; ask whether compensation or adjustment assistance is part of the design; do not treat aggregate gain as a sufficient condition for engagement consent.
- **Pattern: A regulatory body has begun to advocate for the regulated industry.** Signal: rule-making increasingly favours incumbent firms; consumer voice fading from the record. Diagnosis: regulatory capture. Follow-up: independent review; rotation of personnel; structural separation of consumer-protection and industry-promotion functions.
- **Pattern: An insurance, warranty, or risk-sharing arrangement is producing perverse behaviour.** Signal: behaviour shifts after the arrangement is in place; the protected party takes more risk. Diagnosis: moral hazard. Follow-up: introduce deductibles, copayments, or partial coverage; align incentives so the protected party retains some skin in the game.
- **Pattern: A trade or globalisation debate is framing tariffs as protecting jobs.** Signal: discussion focuses on the protected industry; consumer cost and downstream-firm cost absent. Diagnosis: cost-per-protected-job not surfaced. Follow-up: estimate cost-per-protected-job; compare to direct adjustment-assistance costs; ask whether retraining, wage insurance, or relocation support would address the underlying distributional concern at lower aggregate cost.
- **Pattern: A common resource is being overused.** Signal: depletion accelerating; users complain but nobody is willing to limit their own use. Diagnosis: tragedy of the commons; nonexcludability without rival-use accounting. Follow-up: assign property rights (where feasible), set marketable permits (cap-and-trade), or build local-governance institutions with monitoring and graduated sanctions (Ostrom's design principles).

## When to Use This Reference

- A decision implicates third parties who are not at the table.
- A workforce engagement is happening in a context with significant labour-market asymmetry.
- A collective-action situation is failing because of free-riding.
- A trade, immigration, or globalisation question requires distributional analysis.
- A regulatory decision needs scrutiny for capture risk.
- A risk-sharing arrangement is producing moral-hazard or adverse-selection effects.
- A public-good or common-resource setting requires institutional design beyond voluntary cooperation.

## Worked Example

A regional municipality is consulting on a watershed plan covering ten upstream and downstream towns, three large industrial users, an agricultural cooperative, a recreational-fishing association, an environmental NGO, and a state regulator. The watershed has historically been used as a free pollution sink by upstream industry; downstream towns and the fishing association have absorbed the cost; the agricultural cooperative both contributes runoff (negative externality) and depends on water quality (positive interest); the NGO advocates for the watershed itself.

**Mapping.** The water is a common-pool resource (rivalrous, nonexcludable in current institutional design). Externalities run from upstream industry and agriculture to downstream towns, the fishing association, and the environment. The voiceless stakeholders are the watershed itself and future users; the NGO offers proxy representation. The regulatory body has potential for capture by industry given the scale-asymmetry of opposing interests.

**Framing.** This is not just a technical-engineering decision; it is a stakeholder-allocation decision with both efficiency (how much pollution should be permitted?) and distributional (who bears the cost?) dimensions. Industry has elastic alternatives (relocate, change process); downstream towns have inelastic alternatives (cannot move the water). The bargaining asymmetry is structural.

**Convening.** A three-tier engagement: industry representatives, downstream-town representatives, and the regulator at the centre; agricultural cooperative, fishing association, and NGO at a parallel tier with formal speaking rights; voiceless stakeholders represented by NGO and an independent ecological assessment commissioned for the process. Concentrated-benefits / dispersed-costs bias is countered by mandatory public hearings and a written-submission window.

**Surfacing conflict.** The disagreement is partly efficiency (can the watershed sustain current load?) and partly distributional (who bears the abatement cost?). Industry will frame the question as "competitiveness"; downstream towns will frame it as "public health"; the NGO will frame it as "watershed integrity." The municipal facilitator separates the two questions explicitly.

**Reaching agreement.** A cap-and-trade design is proposed with the cap set at a level the ecological assessment endorses, permits initially allocated based on historical use (with transitional support), and revenue from permit auctions earmarked for downstream-impact mitigation. Industry receives a multi-year phase-in; downstream towns receive water-quality monitoring access; the agricultural cooperative receives technical assistance to reduce runoff. The fishing association receives a monitored quota of permits to retire to support recovery.

**Ratifying.** The state regulator's authority is structurally separated from industry-promotion functions. An independent review is scheduled at year three. Sunset clauses on any transitional support require explicit re-authorisation.

**Post-engagement.** Annual public reporting on water quality, permit prices, and abatement compliance. Periodic re-mapping of stakeholder interests, especially as industries enter or exit the watershed. The cap is automatically reviewed every five years; capture-risk indicators (regulator personnel, industry-funded research dominance, lobbying intensity) are tracked.

This worked example illustrates several key concepts: externality framing, common-pool-resource governance, elasticity-driven bargaining asymmetry, voiceless-stakeholder representation, cap-and-trade as a market-based response, regulatory-capture mitigation, and adjustment assistance as part of the deal. ## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Engaging only the parties at the table.** Externally-affected parties have standing whether or not they are in the room; design must surface them.
- **Voluntary funding for public goods.** Predictable under-funding follows; the design must shift to mandate, taxation, or social-pressure mechanisms.
- **Treating aggregate gain as sufficient consent.** Distributional consequences require their own analysis and their own engagement.
- **Treating elasticity-driven asymmetry as moral failing.** Workers in monopsony settings are not "lacking ambition"; they have fewer outside options. The structural framing matters; engagement should compensate, not blame.
- **Trusting an unaccountable regulator indefinitely.** Capture is the predictable failure mode; rotation, structural separation, and independent review are the corrections.
- **Ignoring moral-hazard and adverse-selection effects.** Insurance, warranty, and risk-sharing arrangements need design features (deductibles, copayments, mandates) that internalise behavioural responses.
- **Trade-policy framing that names jobs but not consumer costs.** Cost-per-protected-job is the load-bearing comparison; without it, the political-economy bias toward concentrated benefits dominates.
- **Local solutions without enforcement.** Local self-governance of common resources can succeed (Ostrom's evidence) but only with monitoring, graduated sanctions, and conflict-resolution mechanisms; the appeal-to-civic-virtue version fails.
