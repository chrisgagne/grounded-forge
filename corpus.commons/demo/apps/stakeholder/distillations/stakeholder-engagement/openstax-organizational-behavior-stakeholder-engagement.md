# OpenStax Organizational Behavior, Stakeholder-Engagement Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2019). *Organizational Behavior*. Rice University. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/organizational-behavior. Lead author J. Stewart Black; 11 contributing authors.

## Stakeholder-Engagement Relevance

OpenStax's Organizational Behavior treats stakeholder engagement as a recurring concern across multiple chapters rather than confining it to one. The decision-making chapter (Ch 6) names stakeholders explicitly as "all the individuals or groups that are affected by an organization (such as customers, employees, shareholders, etc.)" and frames stakeholder consideration as central to ethical decision-making. The group and intergroup chapter (Ch 9) provides the foundational vocabulary for understanding how groups form, develop norms, and interact across boundaries. The teams chapter (Ch 10) addresses managing team boundaries — the interface with external stakeholders. The communication chapter (Ch 11) frames Mintzberg's liaison role, where managers spend 47 per cent of their time with peers across organisational boundaries. The leadership chapter (Ch 12) treats followers as active participants whose acceptance shapes leadership. The power and politics chapter (Ch 13) provides the analytical apparatus for understanding stakeholder coalitions, counterpower, and resource dependencies. The conflict and negotiation chapter (Ch 14) provides explicit techniques for engaging stakeholders whose interests diverge.

This distillation synthesises these threads into a working pattern: how to map stakeholders by power and standing; how to frame the engagement before convening; how to convene with the right mode of conflict resolution; how to manage cultural and demographic differences in engagement; and how to maintain stakeholder relationships over time.

## Key Concepts for Stakeholder Engagement

1. **Stakeholders defined.** All individuals or groups affected by an organisation, including customers, employees, shareholders, suppliers, regulators, and communities. The list expands by context. (Source: OpenStax, *Organizational Behavior*, Ch 6.1)

2. **Three types of group interdependence.** Pooled (groups largely independent but contributing to a larger whole), sequential (one group's outputs are another's inputs), and reciprocal (groups depend on each other for inputs). The type of interdependence determines how much interaction is needed; high reciprocal interdependence requires high integration. (Source: Ch 9.4, "Interdependence Requirements")

3. **Five integration techniques.** Rules and procedures, member exchange, linking roles, task forces, and decoupling. Each fits a different intergroup configuration; decoupling separates groups whose conflict cannot be productively resolved. (Source: Ch 9.4, "Managing Intergroup Behavior and Performance")

4. **French and Raven's five power bases.** Reward (control of desired outcomes), coercive (capacity to punish), referent (interpersonal attraction or charisma), expert (knowledge or expertise), legitimate (right to influence based on position). Stakeholders draw on different bases; recognising which base is in play shapes engagement strategy. (Source: Ch 12.3 and Ch 13.1)

5. **Counterpower and power dependencies.** A target's response to influence depends on the target's values (whether the offered outcomes matter), the relationship nature, and counterpower (other power sources the target can draw on). Stakeholders are not merely on the receiving end of organisational influence; they have their own power bases. (Source: Ch 13.1, "Power Dependencies")

6. **Salancik-Pfeffer resource dependence.** Subunits and stakeholders gain power through controlling critical resources others need. Stakeholder maps should track who controls what and how substitutable each contribution is. (Source: Ch 13.3)

7. **Hickson strategic contingencies.** Three sources of subunit power: ability to cope with uncertainty, non-substitutability, and centrality (workflow pervasiveness and immediacy). Stakeholder importance fluctuates as organisational contingencies shift. (Source: Ch 13.3)

8. **Five conflict-resolution modes (Thomas).** On assertiveness × cooperativeness axes: competing, collaborating, compromising, avoiding, accommodating. Each fits different stakeholder situations. Collaboration suits high-stakes integrative conflicts; compromise suits time-pressured situations of equal power; accommodation suits situations where the issue matters more to the other side. (Source: Ch 14.2)

9. **Distributive versus integrative bargaining.** Distributive (win-lose, fixed pie, short-term) versus integrative (win-win, expanded pie, long-term). Most stakeholder relationships warrant integrative framing; specific transactions may require distributive framing. (Source: Ch 14.4)

10. **BATNA and principled negotiation.** The Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement is the measure of bargaining stance. Strong BATNA holders need not concede; weak BATNA holders should examine whether reaching an agreement is in their interest. (Source: Ch 14.4)

11. **Four cultural negotiation differences.** Direct versus indirect communication, attitudes toward hierarchy, decision-making norms, and accent and fluency. Multicultural stakeholder engagement requires attention to all four. (Source: Ch 10.6 and Ch 14.4)

12. **Mintzberg's liaison role.** Managers spend the majority of their time with peers across organisational boundaries (47 per cent in Stewart's 1967 study); the liaison role establishes and maintains connections that produce the information and trust needed for stakeholder work. (Source: Ch 11.3)

13. **Open systems and external alignment.** Organisations are open systems exchanging resources with their environment through feedback loops. Stakeholder engagement is the human side of those resource exchanges; closed-system thinking misses the resource flows that determine organisational viability. (Source: Ch 15.4)

## Questions to Ask During Stakeholder Engagement

### Phase 1: Mapping (Identifying who has standing)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify standing | Who is affected by this decision or initiative — employees, customers, suppliers, regulators, communities, future generations? Whose interests should be considered even if they have not yet asserted themselves? |
| Map power bases | Which stakeholders hold reward power? Coercive? Referent? Expert? Legitimate? Whose power is most relevant to this engagement? |
| Map resource dependencies | Who controls critical resources we need? What resources do we control that they need? Where is the asymmetry? |
| Identify counterpower | If we exercise influence, what counterpower can stakeholders draw on? Where would push-back come from? |
| Surface latent stakeholders | Who is affected but not yet engaged? Whose voice is missing because they lack the power to assert themselves? |

### Phase 2: Framing the Engagement (Before convening)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Determine the question shape | Is this a ratification (decision largely made; stakeholder consent needed)? A consultation (input shapes a decision still open)? A genuine co-design (no decision pre-made)? |
| Identify the type of interdependence | Pooled, sequential, or reciprocal? The type determines how integrated the engagement needs to be. |
| Choose the conflict-resolution mode | Given the stakes, time, power balance, and relationship importance, which Thomas mode is appropriate — competing, collaborating, compromising, avoiding, accommodating? |
| Choose the bargaining frame | Is this a distributive negotiation (zero-sum, short-term) or an integrative one (positive-sum, long-term)? Most stakeholder relationships warrant integrative framing. |
| Surface BATNA | What is our BATNA? What do we estimate the stakeholder's BATNA to be? Are we genuinely able to walk away, and would we want to? |
| Surface ethical implications | Whose interests would be harmed by the most expedient outcome? Are we accounting for them? |

### Phase 3: Convening (Choosing format and participants)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Match topology to question | Many stakeholders for ratification with one decision-maker, or fewer with deeper input for synthesis, or small group for exploratory work? |
| Manage hierarchical attitudes | Are stakeholders from different cultural backgrounds with different hierarchy expectations? Is the format respectful of those differences? |
| Account for communication-style differences | Direct versus indirect, fluency-and-accent dynamics. Are we using formats that level the playing field? |
| Avoid groupthink | Is the room genuinely diverse? Will we hear dissent or only consensus? |
| Set ground rules | Are participants clear on what they are being asked to do — generate options, evaluate, decide, ratify, advise? |

### Phase 4: Surfacing Conflict (When disagreement emerges)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Distinguish process from relationship conflict | Is this disagreement about the substance of the issue (productive) or about the parties (corrosive)? Surface productive process conflict; quell relationship conflict. |
| Apply the Thomas mode | Competing (when speed and authority warrant), collaborating (high-stakes integrative), compromising (time-pressured equal-power), avoiding (trivial or symptoms-not-causes), accommodating (issue matters more to others). |
| Test for fairness | Are we applying procedural justice? Even when distributive outcomes disappoint, fair process sustains commitment. |
| Use information exchange | Are we exchanging genuine information about interests and constraints, or staking positions? Integrative bargaining requires the former. |
| Engage cultural intelligence | Are we sensing how the disagreement reads in each party's cultural frame? Same words may carry different weights. |

### Phase 5: Reaching Agreement (Closing the engagement)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Test agreement understanding | Do all parties understand the same agreement? In some cultures, a signed contract begins rather than closes negotiation; we should not assume universal interpretation. |
| Document obligations and accountabilities | Who is responsible for what, by when? Are accountability structures clear? |
| Address ratification before closing | Has the agreement been ratified by parties whose acceptance is needed for execution? |
| Provide for change | What happens if circumstances change? Are exit provisions and revision procedures defined? |

### Phase 6: Ratifying (Making the agreement stick)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Communicate to non-attending stakeholders | Have we communicated the outcome to stakeholders who were not in the room but whose acceptance matters? |
| Address conflict from the outcome | Some agreements create unhappy stakeholders. Are we prepared for the conflict productively rather than avoiding it? |
| Manage stakeholder expectations | Did the engagement raise expectations that the eventual outcome cannot meet? Have we addressed the gap? |

### Phase 7: Post-engagement (Maintaining the relationship)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Sustain liaison | Are we maintaining the connections that produced the engagement, or letting them lapse until the next negotiation? |
| Detect drift | Are stakeholder positions, resources, or contingencies shifting in ways that require re-engagement? Strategic contingencies change over time. |
| Build durable trust | Are we building trust through follow-through on commitments, or eroding it through inattention? |
| Capture learnings | What did the engagement teach us about this stakeholder, this issue type, our own engagement habits? |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: A stakeholder list grows at every meeting.** Signal: each session reveals new stakeholders who should have been included. Diagnosis: incomplete initial mapping; missing latent stakeholders. Follow-up: pause to remap before continuing; identify standing systematically rather than reactively.
- **Pattern: Late-stage objections threaten an apparently closed agreement.** Signal: a stakeholder who appeared satisfied raises new concerns at ratification. Diagnosis: the engagement was treated as ratification when it was actually consultation; or a stakeholder with standing was inadequately represented. Follow-up: revisit the framing; identify what was missed; re-engage rather than override.
- **Pattern: Conflict has descended into personal attack.** Signal: arguments about people rather than issues; reactive-system responses dominate. Diagnosis: process conflict has shifted into relationship conflict. Follow-up: pause; reframe to specific behavioural and substantive disagreements; consider third-party consultation.
- **Pattern: Same agreement, different expectations across cultures.** Signal: parties believe they have agreed but the implementation reveals divergent understandings. Diagnosis: cultural differences in what an agreement means (contract-as-end versus contract-as-beginning, for example). Follow-up: explicitly verify shared understanding; document expectations in concrete terms; build in checkpoints.
- **Pattern: Influence attempts produce alienation rather than commitment.** Signal: stakeholders comply but resentfully; subsequent engagement is harder. Diagnosis: coercive or pure-reward power being used where referent or expert power would have produced commitment. Follow-up: shift to influence based on rationality, expertise, or trust; rebuild relationships before next engagement.
- **Pattern: Stakeholder coalitions form to oppose an initiative.** Signal: coalition emerges with shared interests opposing the proposal. Diagnosis: stakeholders have aggregated their power as counterpower; resource dependencies have been triggered. Follow-up: engage the coalition's actual interests; consider whether the proposal needs revision rather than harder push.
- **Pattern: Engagement happens only when there is a problem.** Signal: stakeholder relationships are reactive, episodic, transactional. Diagnosis: liaison-role investment is inadequate; trust is being depleted rather than accumulated. Follow-up: build sustained engagement habits independent of immediate need.
- **Pattern: Persuasion runs through pure rationality, with stakeholders unmoved.** Signal: well-argued cases fail to land. Diagnosis: rationality is one influence path; emotion, identity, and values may be operating; cultural intelligence (head, body, heart) may be needed. Follow-up: sense the emotional and value dimensions; engage the heart as well as the head.

## When to Use This Reference

- A multi-stakeholder decision is approaching and the convener is choosing format and participants.
- A previously cooperative stakeholder relationship is showing strain.
- A change initiative requires alignment across organisational subunits with different time and goal orientations.
- A negotiation is approaching and the team needs to choose distributive versus integrative framing.
- A multicultural team is engaging stakeholders across linguistic and cultural boundaries.
- A coalition has formed in opposition to a proposal and the response needs to be calibrated.

## Worked Example

A regional health-system director must close a struggling clinic. Stakeholders include clinic staff, patients, the local community board, the regional medical society, payers, the regional press, and the broader system's operations team.

**Mapping.** The source defines stakeholders as "all the individuals or groups that are affected by an organization (such as customers, employees, shareholders, etc.)" and positions stakeholder consideration as central to ethical decision-making (Source: OpenStax, *Organizational Behavior*, Ch 6.1). Applying French and Raven's five power bases (Ch 12.3, Ch 13.1): clinic staff hold expert power; patients hold referent power; the community board holds legitimate power; the medical society holds expert and referent power; payers hold reward power; the press holds reward and coercive power; operations holds resource power. Latent stakeholders — families of patients, neighbouring clinics that may absorb caseload — surface through the Salancik-Pfeffer resource-dependence lens: who controls critical resources, and who depends on resources we control (Ch 13.3).

**Framing.** This is consultation about how to close, not ratification or co-design. The director chooses the bargaining frame: integrative with the medical society (long-term relationship; positive-sum options to explore), distributive only on the core closure decision itself (Ch 14.4). BATNA analysis — the director's BATNA is to proceed without the medical society's cooperation; the medical society's BATNA is to oppose publicly — confirms that both parties are better off with integrative engagement (Ch 14.4).

**Convening and conflict resolution.** The director applies Thomas's five modes situationally (Ch 14.2): collaborating with the medical society (high-stakes integrative); compromising with payers (time-pressured, equal-power); accommodating with the community board on timing where possible; competing on the core closure decision itself (non-negotiable). Multicultural engagement with the patient community uses indirect communication and hierarchy-sensitive formats, applying all four cultural negotiation differences the source names (Ch 10.6, Ch 14.4).

**Conflict surfacing.** The medical society raises substantive regional-capacity concerns. The director treats these as productive process conflict — surfacing constraints and exploring integrative options — rather than allowing them to shift into relationship conflict, the pattern the source distinguishes in Ch 6.4. The community board raises emotional concerns; the director engages through head, body, and heart (Ch 10.6).

**Post-engagement.** The director sustains the Mintzberg liaison role past the closure date (Ch 11.3), building trust for future engagements rather than depleting it. The source notes managers spend 47 per cent of their time with peers across organisational boundaries (Ch 11.3); the director treats this as an investment, not a sunk cost.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Treating standing as a function of seniority or formal role.** Standing is determined by who is affected, not by hierarchy. Missing stakeholders produce late-stage opposition.
- **Convening one large meeting to do mapping, framing, deciding, and ratifying simultaneously.** The work is different at each phase and warrants different topology, format, and participants.
- **Pure-rationality engagement with culturally diverse stakeholders.** The head alone misses the body and heart sources of cultural intelligence; engagements that exclude emotion and values fail in cultures where these are central.
- **Distributive framing of an integrative relationship.** Treating a long-term stakeholder as a one-off transaction depletes trust and forfeits future cooperation.
- **Coercive power as default influence.** It produces compliance and alienation, not commitment; subsequent engagement is harder.
- **Closed-system thinking about stakeholder relationships.** Treating stakeholder engagement as transactional rather than as a continuing exchange of resources misses the ongoing nature of organisational viability.
- **Pay/reward systems disconnected from engagement quality.** Equity theory predicts disengagement when the link between effort, outcome, and reward is unclear; this applies as much to internal stakeholders (employees) as to external ones.
- **Avoiding necessary process conflict.** Avoiding conflict appears to maintain relationships in the short term but allows substantive disagreements to fester and re-emerge as relationship conflict.
