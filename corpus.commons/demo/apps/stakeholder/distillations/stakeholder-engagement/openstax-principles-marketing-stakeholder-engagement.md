# OpenStax, Principles of Marketing, Stakeholder-Engagement Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2023). *Principles of Marketing*. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0.

## Stakeholder-Engagement Relevance

*Principles of Marketing* is, at one level, a stakeholder-engagement primer. The text uses the phrase "interested parties" — explicitly chosen as a synonym for "stakeholders" (Ch 1.1) — and partitions them into *internal* (employees, owners, managers, investors, shareholders) and *external* (customers, creditors, suppliers, distributors, society) categories that map cleanly onto stakeholder taxonomies. Across all 19 chapters, the book treats engagement as multidirectional: customers (B2C and B2B); employees as internal customers (Ch 11.2 service-profit chain; Ch 13.5 internal marketing); suppliers and channel intermediaries (Ch 17 distribution); the public, regulators, communities, and the media (Ch 14.4 public relations; Ch 13.5 federal regulators); special-attention populations (Ch 8 multicultural and sociodemographic groups; Ch 5.7 children, the elderly, low-income consumers); and — in the closing chapter — the planet itself, treated as a stakeholder via the ESG framework (Ch 19).

The book is also strongly *engagement-centric* rather than transaction-centric. Customer-relationship management (Ch 1.6) is presented not as a transaction-tracker but as a two-way street that "allows customers to define and consequently shape offerings in terms of their requirements." The Service Marketing Triangle (Ch 11.2) names three legs of engagement explicitly: external (making promises), internal (enabling promises through employees), interactive (keeping promises in the service encounter). The Gap Model (Ch 11.3) catalogues the five places engagement breaks down. And the seven-step personal-selling process (Ch 15.3) is a direct, named template for relationship-building (with "follow-up" — the post-sale relationship-management step — explicitly called out as where the "real work begins").

The book is most useful for *commercial* stakeholder engagement — engagement instrumented in service of value creation and exchange. It is less directly useful for stakeholder engagement that is purely civic or governance-focused (board engagement, regulatory rulemaking participation, corporate citizenship beyond the marketing function). For internal-organisational stakeholder engagement (employee engagement *as workforce engagement*, not as service-delivery enabler), pair this reference with an organisational-behaviour text.

## Verbatim anchors from the source

The exchange process — the structural definition of a stakeholder transaction — is "the act of obtaining a desired product or service from an individual or business by providing in return something of value" [V] (OpenStax, *Principles of Marketing*, Ch 1.5, "The Exchange Process").

Customer relationship management — the two-way engagement infrastructure — is "the means through which companies track, manage, and analyze customer interactions" [V] (Ch 1.6, "The Impact of CRM").

The Kotler definition the book adopts for stakeholder-aware sustainability: "an organization should meet the needs of its present consumers without compromising the ability of future generations to fulfill their own needs" [V] (Ch 19.1, "Environmental Sustainability").

Costco's stakeholder-balance formulation, cited approvingly: "Sustainability to us is remaining a profitable business while doing the right thing" [V] (Ch 19.2).

A marketing channel — the multi-party engagement system through intermediaries — is "a system of people, organizations, and activities that work together to make goods and services available to consumers to purchase" [V] (Ch 17.1, "Marketing Channels Defined").

## Key Concepts for Stakeholder Engagement

1.  **Internal and external interested parties.** The text's foundational distinction: internal (employees, owners, managers, investors) and external (customers, creditors, suppliers, distributors, society) — both sets must be engaged. (Source: OpenStax, *Principles of Marketing*, Ch 1.1, "How Marketing Benefits the Organization, Its Interested Parties, and Society")
2.  **The microenvironment as engagement landscape.** Five external parties to engage with directly: suppliers, market intermediaries, customers, competitors, the general public. (Source: Ch 1.3, "Components of the Microenvironment")
3.  **Internal marketing.** Promoting the objectives, products, and services of a company to *its own employees* — recognising employees as the first stakeholder group. (Source: Ch 1.1; Ch 13.5)
4.  **The exchange process.** Engagement is a value-for-value exchange — buyer, seller, desired object, value — with both parties' interests honoured. (Source: Ch 1.5)
5.  **CRM as two-way communication.** "It's a two-way street that allows customers to define and consequently shape offerings in terms of their requirements." (Source: Ch 1.6, "The Role of CRM in Building Customer Equity")
6.  **The service-profit chain.** Internal service quality → employee satisfaction → loyalty/productivity → external value proposition → customer satisfaction → customer loyalty → profit. The chain is broken if any link fails. (Source: Ch 11.2)
7.  **Service Marketing Triangle.** Three engagement legs: external marketing (company → customer, making promises), internal marketing (company → employees, enabling promises), interactive marketing (employees ↔ customers in service encounters, keeping promises). (Source: Ch 11.2)
8.  **Five gaps in service-quality engagement.** Knowledge gap (between management's understanding and customer expectation), policy gap (between understanding and standards), delivery gap (between standards and execution), communication gap (between delivery and what's communicated), customer gap (between expectation and perceived experience). (Source: Ch 11.3)
9.  **Buying centre as a multi-stakeholder unit.** In B2B, engagement is with seven roles: initiator, influencer, gatekeeper, buyer, decider, user. (Source: Ch 4.2)
10.  **Reference groups and influencers.** Engagement isn't just with the customer — it's with the formal and informal groups that shape their preferences and purchases. (Source: Ch 3.2, "Social Factors")
11.  **Public relations.** Unpaid, earned-media engagement with the press, public officials, communities. PR is the most credible promotional form. (Source: Ch 14.4)
12.  **Multicultural and sociodemographic engagement.** Diversity marketing as a *culturally-responsive* engagement strategy — acknowledging differences and validating individuals' importance. (Source: Ch 8.2)
13.  **The 5A customer journey.** Aware, Appeal, Ask, Act, Advocacy — engagement deepens at each stage. (Source: Ch 13.4)
14.  **Stakeholder approach to sustainability.** "Sustainable marketing takes a 'stakeholder approach' and considers customers, shareholders, employees, vendors, interest groups, media, and the general public." (Source: Ch 19.2)
15.  **Three pillars / ESG.** Environmental, social, economic/governance — the three groups of stakeholders sustainability serves. (Source: Ch 19.1)
16.  **Authentic vs inauthentic engagement.** The Walmart Juneteenth ice cream backlash exemplifies inauthentic engagement; Bombas's give-with-purchase model exemplifies authentic. (Source: Ch 19.5)

## Questions to Ask During Stakeholder Engagement

### Phase 1: Map the stakeholders

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify internal stakeholders | Who are our employees, owners, managers, investors, shareholders — and who among them is affected by or influences this engagement effort? |
| Identify external stakeholders | Who are our customers (current, prospective, lost), creditors, suppliers, distributors, intermediaries, regulators, community members, media, competitors, the general public? |
| Identify the buying centre (B2B) | If we're engaging with a business buyer, who is the initiator, influencer, gatekeeper, buyer, decider, user — and which of those roles do we currently have access to? |
| Identify reference groups | Whose opinions do our target audience consult — formal groups (clubs, churches, professional bodies) and informal ones (friends, family, online communities, influencers)? |
| Test for marginalised voices | Have we surfaced multicultural and sociodemographic groups (Hispanic, Black, Asian, Native American/Alaska Native; LGBTQIA+; generational cohorts; socioeconomically disadvantaged; consumers with disabilities) that mainstream engagement often overlooks? |
| Identify nonhuman stakeholders | What about the environment — what's the impact on planet, communities, future generations? |

### Phase 2: Choose engagement intent

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Frame the exchange | What value does our company offer to *each* stakeholder, and what value do we hope to receive in return? |
| Frame the relationship horizon | Are we engaging for a transaction or for a relationship — and is our follow-up plan consistent with the answer? |
| Test stakeholder centricity | Are we approaching this *consumer-centric* (what they need first) or *company-centric* (what we want first)? |
| Test engagement authenticity | Does the engagement integrate with our company's organic purpose, or is it bolted-on (the Walmart Juneteenth pattern)? |
| Position the engagement | What experience do we want this stakeholder to have with our brand — and what one-line positioning statement captures it? |

### Phase 3: Choose engagement channels and message

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Channel mix | Which of the six promotion-mix elements carry the engagement — advertising, sales promotion, personal selling, public relations, direct marketing, digital/Internet? |
| Match channel to stakeholder | For B2B buyers — personal selling. For mass consumers — advertising, sales promotion, digital. For investors and the public — public relations and the annual report. For loyal customers — direct marketing and CRM-driven communications. |
| Test for IMC | Are all channels carrying a *consistent* message, or is engagement fragmented across channels? |
| Match message appeal | Rational, emotional, or moral appeal — and is the appeal compatible with the stakeholder's values? |
| Test for cultural responsiveness | Does the message respect language differences, symbol meanings, cultural values (Hofstede dimensions for international; multicultural insights for domestic)? |
| Choose social media platform | Which platform fits this stakeholder group — Facebook (broad), Instagram (under 30), LinkedIn (B2B/professional), Pinterest (visual/product discovery), Twitter (B2B/public dialogue)? |

### Phase 4: Engage employees as stakeholders

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Internal service quality | Are frontline employees supported by effective policies and back-of-house service so they can deliver on customer-facing promises? |
| Employee satisfaction | Do company policies empower employees to deliver quality? Is satisfaction high enough to drive loyalty and productivity? |
| Empowerment | Can employees deviate from the script when serving the customer (the Zappos call-centre model)? |
| Feedback loops | Per Yoshida's "Iceberg of Ignorance" (4% of frontline problems known by top management, 100% by employees), have we built channels for frontline knowledge to surface? |
| Internal marketing | Are employees informed about company objectives, products, services, and marketing campaigns *before* customers are? |
| Diversity in workforce | Does our workforce reflect the markets we serve? |

### Phase 5: Engage customers across the journey

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Aware stage | How are first-time prospects becoming aware of us — paid search, paid social, content, PR, word-of-mouth from advocates? |
| Appeal stage | What's our value-proposition for this segment — and is it tested in concept research before we commit to it? |
| Ask stage | How easy is it for a prospect to ask us a question — chatbot, contact form, phone, in-app message? Are we responsive within the 30–60-minute window consumers expect? |
| Act stage | Have we removed friction from the actual purchase — pricing transparent, options clear, channels convenient, post-sale support visible? |
| Advocacy stage | Do we have a follow-up programme that turns satisfied customers into referrers — loyalty programmes, review prompts, ambassador programmes, social-media celebration of customer stories? |
| Customer feedback | Are we collecting CSAT, CES, complaint data, and verbatims — and is feedback flowing back into product, pricing, channel, and promotion decisions? |
| Service-encounter design | At each interactive touchpoint, are we delivering on Reliability, Assurance, Tangibles, Empathy, Responsiveness (RATER)? |

### Phase 6: Engage suppliers and intermediaries

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Supplier engagement | Are we treating suppliers as partners with shared interest in customer value (Dave's Killer Bread / certified-organic supplier model), or as cost-minimisation targets? |
| Channel-member relationships | Are we building collaborative, transparent, cohesive relationships with intermediaries — or is each member maximising its own profit at the expense of channel value? |
| Channel conflict surfacing | Are we surfacing vertical conflicts (between levels) and horizontal conflicts (within levels) early enough to resolve them? |
| Channel partner motivation | Are we using bonuses, incentives, sales materials, samples, training, dealer conferences appropriately to support channel members? |
| Channel evaluation | Are we measuring channel partners on inventory turnover, order accuracy, time to ship, percentage on-time — and sharing those metrics back to them? |

### Phase 7: Engage the public, regulators, community, the planet

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Public relations | Are we using press relations, public affairs, lobbying (transparently and ethically), events, digital/social media to maintain a positive public image? |
| Crisis preparedness | Do we have a crisis communication plan tested against the Tylenol-1982 standard — direct, transparent, costly when needed? |
| Compliance | Are we meeting FTC, FCC, FDA, CAN-SPAM Act, GDPR, FCPA, and other regulatory requirements — and going beyond minimum compliance into ESG-aligned operations? |
| Community engagement | Are we sponsoring or hosting events that serve the community and reinforce shared values? |
| Diversity engagement | Are our marketing efforts inclusive of multicultural and sociodemographic groups, with cultural responsiveness as a baseline standard? |
| Sustainability commitments | Do we have measurable environmental commitments (emissions, recycling, sustainable packaging, sustainable sourcing) and report on them annually? |
| Stakeholder-vs-shareholder balance | Are we creating value for *all* stakeholders, or letting shareholder return crowd out other obligations? |

### Phase 8: Resolve engagement breakdowns

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Knowledge gap | Are we *sure* we know what each stakeholder wants — or are we substituting management's view for stakeholder voice? |
| Policy gap | When we know what stakeholders want, do our delivery policies and standards translate that understanding (specificity over vague aspiration)? |
| Delivery gap | When we have the policies, are frontline staff actually delivering — and if not, is the issue training, capability, willingness, or staffing? |
| Communication gap | What we *delivered* — is it what we *communicated*? Did marketing claims overpromise? |
| Customer gap | What expectation gaps remain — and how do we close them through expectation-setting up front? |
| Channel-conflict resolution | When channel conflict surfaces, are we facilitating dialogue and aligning incentives, or letting it fester? |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: Customer-defined value drifting from company-defined value.** Signal: rising complaint rates despite stable CSAT, or rising returns despite stable revenue. Diagnosis: a knowledge gap between what customers want and what we think they want. Follow-up: refresh marketing research; surface frontline employee insights; close the customer-feedback loop.
- **Pattern: Employees as a hidden stakeholder cost.** Signal: high turnover, low employee NPS, declining service quality, increasing time-to-fill. Diagnosis: internal service quality has degraded; the service-profit chain is broken. Follow-up: invest in internal marketing, training, empowerment; map the chain and find the broken link.
- **Pattern: Stakeholder fragmentation by channel.** Signal: customers reporting different prices or service levels depending on whether they engaged via web, phone, store, or partner. Diagnosis: lack of omnichannel integration. Follow-up: align pricing, service standards, and information across channels.
- **Pattern: Bolted-on diversity or sustainability marketing.** Signal: campaigns that signal commitment without operational backing. Diagnosis: inauthentic engagement; backlash risk. Follow-up: integrate the commitment into supply chain, hiring, governance — then communicate.
- **Pattern: Channel-partner extraction without channel-partner support.** Signal: distributors burning out, retailers stocking competitors more aggressively, dealer-conference attendance falling. Diagnosis: relationship has slipped from partnership to exploitation. Follow-up: re-establish vertical-marketing-system collaboration; share data and incentives.
- **Pattern: Public-relations crisis denial.** Signal: company defending an obviously offensive campaign, or hiding evidence of a defect. Diagnosis: Toyota-unintended-acceleration pattern. Follow-up: Tylenol-1982-style direct, transparent response — even at cost.
- **Pattern: Selective engagement.** Signal: heavy engagement with most-profitable customers, minimal engagement with marginalised ones. Diagnosis: missed-revenue and reputation risks; legal exposure (HUD-Facebook-style discrimination claims). Follow-up: ADAMS test of segments; explicitly test for accessibility and substantiality before excluding.

## When to Use This Reference

- *Customer-engagement strategy* design — building or refreshing CRM, loyalty programmes, customer experience.
- *Channel-partner relationship* renegotiation — when conflict surfaces, when adding partners, when consolidating.
- *Employee-engagement* programme design — particularly for service-led businesses where the service-profit chain is load-bearing.
- *Diversity-marketing* and *multicultural-marketing* programme design.
- *Stakeholder-mapping* exercises — when stakeholder analysis is part of strategic planning, M&A, or sustainability reporting.
- *Crisis-communication* preparedness — building PR-response capability before crisis.
- *Sustainability/ESG-engagement strategy* — integrating environmental, social, governance commitments with operations and communications.
- *B2B account engagement plans* — building buying-centre maps for major accounts.

## Worked Example

A regional health-insurance plan is preparing to expand from one state to three. Members include working families, small-business owners, and Medicare-eligible seniors. The plan's CEO wants engagement strategy advice for the expansion.

Map the stakeholders (Ch 1.1, Ch 1.3, Ch 4.2):

- *Internal*: employees (especially member-service representatives, brokers, claims processors), board, investors.
- *External direct*: members across three life-cycle stages; employer customers (small-business owners are buyers in their own buying centres — initiator HR, influencer benefits broker, gatekeeper finance, decider owner, users employees); brokers and agents as channel intermediaries; healthcare providers (suppliers); state regulators in each new state; the broader public via press and community.
- *Marginalised consideration*: low-income consumers (Ch 5.7 — explicitly cautioned against exploitative marketing); seniors (Ch 5.7 — flagged as vulnerable to "cure-all" claims); multicultural members (Ch 8.4 — Hispanic, Black, Asian populations growing).

Use the service-profit chain (Ch 11.2): expansion will stress internal service quality. New staff in the new states need training before they touch members. Internal marketing must precede external marketing.

Use the Service Marketing Triangle (Ch 11.2): make explicit promises in external marketing (renewal-time consistency); enable promises through internal marketing (broker training, member-services scripts, claim-processor capacity); honour promises through interactive marketing (the call-centre encounter — every RATER element matters).

Use buying-centre mapping (Ch 4.2) for the small-business segment: build named-account plans that engage broker, HR, finance, owner, and employees with fit-for-role messaging.

Use multicultural-marketing principles (Ch 8.4): in markets with significant Hispanic populations, deliver Spanish-language member services (acculturation-spectrum: most second-generation members will be bicultural; first-generation more likely to need Spanish primary). Engage with Black and Asian community organisations as part of a cultural-responsiveness strategy. Avoid stereotypes (the Burger King Texican Whopper failure mode).

Use IMC (Ch 13.3): align messages across employer-direct sales, broker-channel, direct-mail, digital, PR. The same value proposition — "predictable benefits, local service, competitive pricing" — appears with the same supporting evidence everywhere.

Use the 5A customer journey (Ch 13.4) for the consumer segment: paid search and content marketing for Aware; comparison tools and broker referrals for Appeal; chatbot and broker call for Ask; clear enrolment flow for Act; member-portal engagement, satisfaction surveys, and renewal incentives for Advocacy.

Use ESG-stakeholder principles (Ch 19.2): engage state regulators with transparency (financial disclosures, compliance commitments). Engage providers with collaborative quality programmes. Engage communities with health-education sponsorships that align with the plan's actual operations rather than as PR overlay.

Crisis preparedness (Ch 14.4): pre-build a communication plan for a possible IT-outage or claim-processing failure during expansion. Tylenol-style: transparent, direct, costly when needed.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- *Only engaging stakeholders who pay you* — ignoring intermediaries, employees, communities, regulators until they create problems.
- *Engaging stakeholders symmetrically* — assuming each group needs the same channels, message, and frequency.
- *Asymmetric promises* — making promises in advertising and breaking them in interactive service encounters (Gap 4 communication-gap pattern).
- *Bolted-on diversity engagement* — campaigns that don't reflect the operations.
- *PR-crisis denial* — defending the indefensible (Toyota acceleration; Pepsi Kendall Jenner; Nivea "White Is Purity").
- *Ignoring the buying centre* — selling only to the buyer in B2B without engaging influencers, gatekeepers, deciders, users.
- *Letting channel conflict fester* — assuming intermediaries will tolerate worse terms because they have nowhere else to go.
- *Selective inclusion in research* — research that only samples the easiest-to-reach, then claims to represent the whole market.
- *Performative inclusion* — campaigns featuring diversity in casting without operational diversity in hiring, supply chain, governance.
- *Treating sustainability as marketing only* — the inverse of the Patagonia model, where operations have to deliver before marketing communicates.
