# OpenStax Psychology 2e, Stakeholder-Engagement Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2020). *Psychology 2e*. Rice University. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/psychology-2e. Senior contributing authors Rose M. Spielman, William J. Jenkins, Marilyn D. Lovett; 10 contributing authors.

## Stakeholder-Engagement Relevance

OpenStax *Psychology 2e* is an introductory survey, not a stakeholder-engagement manual; nevertheless its social-psychology, motivation, learning, and industrial-organisational chapters supply load-bearing material for engaging others. Chapter 12 (Social Psychology) is the densest contribution: roles and norms; attitudes and persuasion (Yale framework, elaboration likelihood model, foot-in-the-door); conformity (Asch), compliance, and obedience (Milgram); group dynamics (groupthink, polarisation, social loafing, deindividuation); prejudice, stereotyping, and discrimination, including the dual attitudes model and the implicit / explicit distinction; the bystander effect and prosocial behaviour; relationship formation through proximity, similarity, and reciprocity. Chapter 6 (Learning) supplies behavioural-change mechanics (reinforcement, modelling) relevant to influencing stakeholder behaviour over time. Chapter 10 (Motivation and Emotion) supplies arousal, emotion, and motivation theory relevant to how stakeholders experience the engagement. Chapter 13 (Industrial-Organisational) carries explicit organisational-stakeholder content: leadership styles, organisational culture, sexual harassment, workplace violence, procedural justice. Chapter 14 (Stress and Health) carries social-support research, with implications for sustaining stakeholders through demanding engagements.

This distillation draws on this material — *Psychology 2e*'s *cognitive*, *social*, *motivational*, and *organisational* psychology — to support stakeholder-engagement practice. Therapy and clinical content from Chapters 15 and 16 is excluded; the framework here is for organisational and interpersonal engagement, not for diagnosing or treating psychological disorders.

The book's central engagement-relevant insights: people interpret others' behaviour through systematically biased lenses (fundamental attribution error, self-serving bias, in-group bias); group dynamics shape what people will say even when they disagree (Asch, Milgram, groupthink); persuasion runs through two routes whose appropriate use depends on the audience and stakes (central versus peripheral, ELM); prejudice operates explicitly and implicitly, and superordinate goals plus contact under specific conditions reduce it; behaviour change is shaped by reinforcement and modelling, not just by exhortation; and social support is a primary buffer against the stress of difficult engagements.

## Key Concepts for Stakeholder Engagement

1. **Roles, norms, and scripts.** A social role is a pattern of behaviour expected of a person in a given setting; norms are group expectations of appropriate behaviour; scripts are knowledge of expected event sequences. Stakeholders bring role expectations into engagements, and the convener's own role in the room is part of what is read. (Source: OpenStax, *Psychology 2e*, Ch 12.2)

2. **Cognitive dissonance and the justification of effort.** Festinger's cognitive dissonance: psychological discomfort arising from inconsistent attitudes, behaviours, or cognitions. Reduction may involve changing the behaviour, changing the cognition, or adding a new cognition. Justification of effort: difficult initiations increase liking for groups, including for stakeholder coalitions whose members have invested. (Source: Ch 12.3)

3. **Yale persuasion model.** Source credibility and attractiveness; message subtlety, sidedness, and timing; audience attention, intelligence, self-esteem, and age. The persuasive impact of a message is the combined product of all three sets of factors. (Source: Ch 12.3)

4. **Elaboration likelihood model (Petty and Cacioppo).** The central route uses logic and data; it requires an analytical and motivated audience and produces durable attitude change. The peripheral route uses cues like credibility, emotion, and association; it requires less effort but produces less durable change. Stakeholder engagements that rely solely on the peripheral route should not expect durable commitment. (Source: Ch 12.3)

5. **Foot-in-the-door and consistency-based compliance.** Agreement to a small request increases the likelihood of agreement to a larger request, partly because compliance becomes consistent with the actor's self-perception (Freedman and Fraser). The converse — door-in-the-face — works through reciprocal concession. (Source: Ch 12.3)

6. **Asch conformity.** 76% of Asch's participants conformed at least once on a clearly incorrect line judgement; conformity is moderated by majority size (up to about seven), the presence of even a single dissenter (drops conformity to near zero), and public versus private response. The implication: structuring for dissent and for private response is a design choice that shapes what stakeholders will say. (Source: Ch 12.4)

7. **Milgram obedience.** 65% of Milgram's participants administered what they believed to be lethal shocks under instruction from an authority. Obedience varies with proximity, the perceived legitimacy of the authority, and the physical setting. Conveners of stakeholder engagements carry authority effects that shape what participants feel licensed to say. (Source: Ch 12.4)

8. **Groupthink and group polarisation.** Groupthink occurs in cohesive groups under pressure to maintain unanimity; symptoms include perceived invulnerability, moral certainty, self-censorship, illusion of unanimity, and stereotyping out-groups. Group polarisation: discussion strengthens initial group leanings. Counters: external opinions, private voting, leaders withholding initial positions, contingency planning. (Source: Ch 12.4)

9. **Social loafing and deindividuation.** Effort declines in pooled tasks; mitigated by individual evaluation, smaller groups, and challenging tasks. Collectivistic cultures show less social loafing. Anonymity in groups reduces accountability (deindividuation). (Source: Ch 12.4)

10. **Prejudice, stereotype, discrimination — explicit and implicit.** Prejudice (negative attitude), stereotype (cognitive belief about group members), discrimination (negative action). The dual attitudes model distinguishes explicit (conscious) from implicit (unconscious) attitudes; modern measures can reveal implicit bias even where explicit bias is suppressed. Self-fulfilling prophecy and confirmation bias maintain stereotypes. (Source: Ch 12.5)

11. **Superordinate goals and contact theory.** Sherif's Robbers Cave and Aronson's jigsaw classroom show that cooperation toward goals neither group can achieve alone reduces inter-group prejudice. Pettigrew and Tropp's meta-analysis (cited in Ch 13) confirms that contact between groups reduces prejudice when contact occurs under conditions of equal status, common goals, cooperation, and institutional support. (Source: Ch 12.6 and Ch 13.3)

12. **In-group bias, scapegoating, just-world hypothesis.** In-group bias favours one's own group; scapegoating displaces frustration onto out-groups; the just-world hypothesis (people get what they deserve) underwrites victim-blaming. Each operates against fair stakeholder engagement when in-groups and out-groups are activated. (Source: Ch 12.5 and Ch 12.1)

13. **Bystander effect and diffusion of responsibility.** Helping declines as the number of witnesses increases. The implication for stakeholder engagement: when many stakeholders share responsibility for a problem, no single stakeholder may move; explicit accountability and small-group structures counter this. (Source: Ch 12.6)

14. **Relationship formation: proximity, similarity, reciprocity.** Proximity and similarity are the strongest predictors of relationship formation. Homophily (preference for similar others) limits diversity exposure. Reciprocity and self-disclosure maintain relationships. Engagement strategies that ignore proximity and similarity are working against the underlying social mechanics. (Source: Ch 12.7)

15. **Observational learning and modelling.** Bandura's social learning theory: behaviour is acquired through attention, retention, reproduction, and motivation, and is shaped by vicarious reinforcement. Stakeholders learn what is acceptable partly by watching what happens to others; what the convener models is part of the engagement. (Source: Ch 6.4)

16. **Theory X versus Theory Y management.** Theory X assumes workers dislike work and need control; Theory Y assumes they seek satisfaction and respond to participation. The text presents Theory Y as more effective for modern organisations. The implication for stakeholder engagement: assumptions about stakeholders shape what kinds of engagement are even attempted. (Source: Ch 13.3)

17. **Procedural justice (Greenberg).** Fairness of the processes determining outcomes predicts retaliation, aggression, and commitment. Adequate explanations of decisions reduced theft compared to curt explanations in Greenberg's experiments. Process matters as much as outcome for sustaining stakeholder relationships. (Source: Ch 13.3)

18. **Social support and stress buffering.** Holt-Lunstad et al.'s meta-analysis (148 studies, 300,000+ participants): 50% greater survival likelihood among individuals with stronger social relationships. Social support buffers immune function and blood pressure during stressful tasks. Sustaining stakeholders through demanding engagements is itself a health intervention. (Source: Ch 14.4)

## Questions to Ask During Stakeholder Engagement

### Phase 1: Mapping (Identifying who has standing)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify standing | Who is affected by this initiative — employees, customers, suppliers, regulators, communities, future stakeholders? Whose interests should be considered even if they have not yet asserted themselves? |
| Surface implicit hierarchies | Who has standing by formal role versus who has standing by impact? Are we conflating the two? |
| Detect in-group / out-group framing | Are we treating some stakeholders as "us" and others as "them" in ways that will shape what we hear from each? |
| Map roles and scripts | What roles do participants expect to play in this engagement? What scripts do they bring? Are our chosen formats compatible with those expectations? |
| Identify likely obedience effects | Is our authority position likely to suppress what some stakeholders feel licensed to say? |

### Phase 2: Framing (Before convening)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Determine the question shape | Is this ratification (decision largely made), consultation (input shapes a decision still open), or co-design (no decision pre-made)? Are we honest about which? |
| Choose central versus peripheral persuasion | Is the engagement designed to produce durable commitment (central route, requires logic, data, motivated audience) or short-term agreement (peripheral)? Are we using the route appropriate to the goal? |
| Plan for cognitive dissonance | Will the engagement ask stakeholders to act in ways inconsistent with prior attitudes? How will the dissonance be resolved — through changed attitude, changed cognition, or added cognition? |
| Surface implicit-bias risk | Are decisions about people part of this engagement? What structures will counter implicit bias (structured criteria, blind evaluation, counter-stereotypic exposure)? |
| Plan for procedural justice | Is the process itself fair, and will stakeholders perceive it as fair? Adequate explanation of the why matters as much as the what. |

### Phase 3: Convening (Format and participants)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Structure for dissent | Will stakeholders feel safe expressing minority views? Is private input available alongside public discussion? Is at least one voice cued to dissent? |
| Counter conformity pressure | Are we seeking consensus too quickly? Have we structured for the Asch effect — at least one dissenter dramatically reduces conformity, so a designated devil's advocate matters? |
| Counter authority effects | Does our role suppress what others will say? Should we withhold our preferred view until others have spoken? Should a more peer-like facilitator run the engagement? |
| Counter group polarisation | Is the room composed of similar-leaning stakeholders, who will polarise toward their shared lean during discussion? Should we deliberately diversify? |
| Counter social loafing | Is each participant individually accountable for input, or are contributions pooled? Smaller groups and individual evaluation counter loafing. |

### Phase 4: Surfacing Conflict

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Distinguish dispositional from situational attribution | When a stakeholder raises a concern, are we explaining their reaction dispositionally (they're difficult, they're emotional) or situationally (their position carries this concern naturally)? |
| Counter actor-observer asymmetry | Are we explaining their behaviour by who they are and our own by what we faced? Inverting the framing as a check is useful. |
| Surface implicit attitudes | Are reactions patterning with group membership in ways the conscious values do not predict? Does this signal implicit bias operating? |
| Apply contact and superordinate-goal principles | Where intergroup conflict has surfaced, is there a superordinate goal that can structure cooperation? Are the contact conditions (equal status, common goals, cooperation, institutional support) in place? |
| Manage the cognitive dissonance the conflict triggers | When the conflict reveals something inconsistent with the convener's prior views, how is the convener resolving the dissonance — through learning or through dismissing the stakeholder? |

### Phase 5: Reaching Agreement

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Test for premature consensus | Has agreement formed too smoothly? Has every voice been heard or only the majority? Private follow-up may surface what public discussion suppressed. |
| Apply procedural justice | Even when distributive outcomes disappoint, is the process being explained adequately? Curt explanations corrode commitment. |
| Plan for behaviour change, not just attitude change | If implementation requires stakeholders to act differently, are we structuring for the reinforcement and modelling that will sustain the change, not just the persuasion that announces it? |
| Address procedural fairness across cultures | Are we applying culturally specific assumptions about what fair process looks like? Different cultures weight participation, hierarchy, and consensus differently. |

### Phase 6: Sustaining (Post-engagement)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Sustain reinforcement | Is the new behaviour being reinforced? Variable-ratio reinforcement is the most extinction-resistant; intermittent acknowledgement sustains better than constant praise that can become invisible. |
| Sustain modelling | Is leadership modelling the behaviour the engagement asked stakeholders to adopt? Observational learning tracks what happens to whom, not just what was said. |
| Sustain social support | Are stakeholders carrying difficult demands receiving the social support that buffers stress? Holt-Lunstad-scale evidence makes this a health intervention, not just an HR nicety. |
| Detect drift | Are stakeholders drifting back toward prior patterns? What reinforcement contingencies sustain the drift, and what would sustain the new pattern instead? |
| Maintain proximity | Relationships sustain through proximity, similarity, and reciprocity. Are we creating occasions for these mechanisms to operate, or letting the relationship lapse until the next demand? |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: A "difficult" stakeholder is being attributed bad faith.** Signal: their concerns are explained by who they are rather than what their position carries. Diagnosis (in source vocabulary): fundamental attribution error; possibly out-group othering. Follow-up: invert to a situational frame; ask what their position actually requires them to attend to; engage their concerns rather than their character.
- **Pattern: A unanimous group decision feels too clean.** Signal: no dissent voiced; shared certainty after brief discussion. Diagnosis: groupthink, possibly amplified by Asch-style conformity pressure or Milgram-style authority effect. Follow-up: structure for private input; designated devil's advocate; revisit with explicit invitation to challenge.
- **Pattern: A stakeholder publicly agrees but privately undermines.** Signal: nodding in the room, resistance in implementation. Diagnosis: conformity pressure produced public compliance without private acceptance; central-route persuasion did not occur. Follow-up: re-engage with logic and data; create private channels for dissent; check whether the original engagement was central- or peripheral-route.
- **Pattern: Persuasion runs through pure rationality, with stakeholders unmoved.** Signal: well-argued cases fail to land. Diagnosis: peripheral cues (credibility, emotion, identity) are operating where central-route assumptions are being made. Follow-up: attend to the source factors (credibility, attractiveness) and the audience factors (motivation, attention) of the Yale framework, not just the message.
- **Pattern: Engagement decisions pattern with group membership independent of stated criteria.** Signal: the same kinds of stakeholders are heard and the same kinds are missed. Diagnosis: implicit bias; possibly homophily (preference for similar others). Follow-up: structured criteria; counter-stereotypic exemplar exposure; deliberate inclusion of low-similarity voices.
- **Pattern: An inter-group dispute is framed as a values clash.** Signal: each side's identity is being defended rather than the substantive disagreement examined. Diagnosis: in-group bias and stereotyping have activated. Follow-up: superordinate goals; contact under the four conditions (equal status, common goals, cooperation, institutional support); reframe to specific behavioural and substantive disagreements.
- **Pattern: Stakeholders share responsibility but no one acts.** Signal: shared concern, no action. Diagnosis: bystander effect, diffusion of responsibility. Follow-up: explicit accountability; smaller groups; named individuals rather than collective roles for action items.
- **Pattern: Fair process is being skipped because the outcome is "obvious".** Signal: leaders skip explanation, consultation, or appeal procedures. Diagnosis: procedural-justice violation; the cost is retaliation, aggression, and erosion of commitment regardless of how good the outcome is. Follow-up: restore procedure even where the outcome will not change.
- **Pattern: The convener's own arousal is shaping the engagement.** Signal: the convener is leading from anxiety, urgency, or anger. Diagnosis: emotional state is driving format and tone choices. Follow-up: structurally lower arousal before convening; defer where possible; bring a partner to share the convening load.

## When to Use This Reference

- A multi-stakeholder engagement is approaching and the convener is structuring against conformity, authority, and groupthink effects.
- A coalition has formed in opposition and the response needs to address inter-group dynamics rather than just the substantive issue.
- A persuasion campaign is being designed and the convener is choosing between central-route and peripheral-route approaches.
- A decision about people (hiring, promotion, allocation) is being made and the convener wants to structure against implicit bias.
- A retrospective is examining a stakeholder breakdown and the convener wants to disentangle dispositional and situational accounts.
- A behaviour-change initiative is being launched and the convener wants to plan for reinforcement and modelling, not just attitude change.

## Worked Example

A regional university president must engage faculty, staff, students, alumni, and a regional employer coalition about a major curriculum revision. The faculty are sceptical (they bear the implementation cost); employers are enthusiastic (they want graduates with different skills); students are mixed (some welcome new options, some worry about credit transfer); alumni are protective of the existing programme.

Mapping: the president maps stakeholders by standing (impact) rather than just by formal role. Latent stakeholders include feeder secondary schools and regional community-college transfer applicants. The president notes an emerging in-group / out-group framing within the university (faculty as "us," employers as "them") and works to counter it.

Framing: the engagement is consultation (the change will happen; how is open). The president decides on central-route persuasion for the faculty audience (logic, data, multi-year analysis) and a hybrid for student and alumni audiences. Procedural justice is built in: clear explanation of why, structured input opportunities, transparent decision criteria.

Convening: separate engagements for each stakeholder group (different roles, different scripts), with a final integrative session. Within each, structure for dissent: anonymous input alongside public discussion; private polling before group convergence; a designated devil's advocate for each substantive proposal. The president withholds preferred views until others have spoken, to avoid authority anchoring.

Surfacing conflict: when faculty raise concerns about implementation cost, the president avoids a dispositional read ("they're protecting turf") and engages the situational frame (their teaching load, tenure cases, and curricular expertise are all genuinely affected). When two faculty members are explaining the regional employer coalition dispositionally ("they don't understand what universities are for"), the president introduces direct contact between faculty and employer representatives under the four contact conditions: equal status (both as expert stakeholders in their domains), common goals (graduate employability and educational depth, framed as joint not competing), cooperation (a working group designs specific curriculum elements together), and institutional support (the engagement is sponsored, not improvised).

Reaching agreement: agreement comes through a compromise architecture (some new programmes, some preserved). Procedural justice is honoured by explaining the reasoning behind each decision, including the decisions that disappoint specific stakeholders. The president is honest that the alumni voice was heard but did not determine the outcome, and explains why.

Sustaining: post-engagement, the president sustains liaison with all stakeholder groups through quarterly updates and standing meetings. Faculty implementing new courses are reinforced through both formal acknowledgement and informal support; employers are kept in regular contact through the working group rather than only consulted at moments of need. Social support for faculty bearing implementation cost is structured (course-release time, peer support, administrative buffering).

This worked example illustrates several key concepts in the source: standing-not-role mapping, in-group / out-group activation, central-route persuasion, contact theory's four conditions, procedural justice, and the sustained reinforcement and modelling that converts agreement into durable behaviour change. ## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Treating a difficult stakeholder dispositionally.** The fundamental attribution error makes character explanations feel definitive; situational variables go unexamined and the engagement breaks down.
- **Forcing consensus quickly.** Asch effects, groupthink, and authority pressure produce public agreement without private acceptance; implementation reveals the gap.
- **Pure-rationality persuasion when peripheral cues dominate.** The Yale framework and ELM predict that rationality alone fails when source credibility, audience motivation, or affective context are not aligned.
- **Decisions about people without structure.** Implicit bias plus homophily plus the team halo effect produce decisions that pattern with demographics rather than merit.
- **Skipping procedural fairness because the outcome is obvious.** Greenberg's work shows that perceived process fairness predicts retaliation and aggression independent of outcome quality.
- **One-shot persuasion instead of sustained reinforcement and modelling.** Behaviour change requires Bandura's four elements (attention, retention, reproduction, motivation) and ongoing reinforcement, not just an effective one-time pitch.
- **Treating intergroup conflict as a values clash.** Without superordinate goals and contact under the four conditions, identity defence overtakes substantive engagement.
- **Sustaining stakeholders only when there is a problem.** Proximity-and-similarity mechanics demand ongoing engagement, not transactional appearance only at moments of need.
