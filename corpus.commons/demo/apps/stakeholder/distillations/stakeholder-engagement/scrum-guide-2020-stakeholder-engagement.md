# Schwaber & Sutherland, The Scrum Guide — Stakeholder-Engagement Distillation

**Source:** Schwaber, K. & Sutherland, J. (2020). *The Scrum Guide: The Definitive Guide to Scrum: The Rules of the Game*. November 2020 edition. scrumguides.org. Licence: CC BY-SA 4.0. https://scrumguides.org/docs/scrumguide/v2020/2020-Scrum-Guide-US.pdf.

## Stakeholder-Engagement Relevance

The Scrum Guide treats stakeholder engagement as a structural feature of the framework, not as a separate concern. The Product Owner role exists in part to represent stakeholder interests in the Product Backlog. The Scrum Master serves three distinct principals — Scrum Team, Product Owner, and organisation — and "facilitating stakeholder collaboration as requested or needed" is named explicitly. The Sprint Review is the dedicated stakeholder-engagement ceremony: "The Scrum Team presents the results of their work to key stakeholders and progress toward the Product Goal is discussed." And the Scrum Team itself is responsible for "all product-related activities from stakeholder collaboration, verification, maintenance, operation, experimentation, research and development."

Three distinct engagement modes are encoded in the framework:

**External-stakeholder engagement.** The Product Owner is the structural conduit. "The Product Owner may represent the needs of many stakeholders in the Product Backlog. Those wanting to change the Product Backlog can do so by trying to convince the Product Owner." This is a deliberate single-throat-to-talk-to design: stakeholders engage through the Product Owner rather than each lobbying the Developers directly. The Sprint Review is where stakeholders engage directly with the whole team in a working session, not a presentation, and "Based on this information, attendees collaborate on what to do next. The Product Backlog may also be adjusted to meet new opportunities."

**Internal-team engagement.** The Scrum Team itself is the most intimate engagement: one Scrum Master, one Product Owner, and Developers, with "no sub-teams or hierarchies" — "a cohesive unit of professionals focused on one objective at a time." Sprint Planning is collaborative across the whole team; the Sprint Retrospective is the team-internal engagement ceremony where "the Scrum Team inspects how the last Sprint went with regards to individuals, interactions, processes, tools, and their Definition of Done." The five Scrum Values — Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, Courage — explicitly include "the Scrum Team and its stakeholders are open about the work and the challenges."

**Organisational engagement.** The Scrum Master is the conduit. "The Scrum Master serves the organization in several ways, including: Leading, training, and coaching the organization in its Scrum adoption; Planning and advising Scrum implementations within the organization; Helping employees and stakeholders understand and enact an empirical approach for complex work; and, Removing barriers between stakeholders and Scrum Teams." Note the last point: removing barriers *between* stakeholders and Scrum Teams is a Scrum Master responsibility, not a Product Owner one.

A unifying theme: stakeholder engagement in Scrum is *cadenced*. The Sprint Review is the regular, structured engagement moment with external stakeholders; the Daily Scrum is the daily team-internal engagement; the Sprint Retrospective is the per-Sprint team improvement engagement; the Product Backlog refinement is the ongoing conversation between Product Owner and Developers about what stakeholders need. Engagement is not an event organised when a problem surfaces; it is a structural rhythm.

## Key Concepts for Stakeholder-Engagement

1. **Stakeholders in Scrum (named in the Guide).** The Guide names stakeholders without enumerating a complete typology. Surfaced stakeholders include: customers/users (implicit in "value", "the Increment must be usable"); key stakeholders at the Sprint Review; "those wanting to change the Product Backlog"; the organisation; and the team itself. For a fuller stakeholder typology, the Guide is not the source; for *how engagement is structured*, it is. (Source: Schwaber & Sutherland, *Scrum Guide* 2020, throughout)

2. **Product Owner as single-throat-to-talk-to.** "The Product Owner is one person, not a committee. The Product Owner may represent the needs of many stakeholders in the Product Backlog. Those wanting to change the Product Backlog can do so by trying to convince the Product Owner." Stakeholders engage *through* the Product Owner, not around them. ("Scrum Team", "Product Owner")

3. **Sprint Review as the dedicated stakeholder ceremony.** "The purpose of the Sprint Review is to inspect the outcome of the Sprint and determine future adaptations. The Scrum Team presents the results of their work to key stakeholders and progress toward the Product Goal is discussed... The Sprint Review is a working session and the Scrum Team should avoid limiting it to a presentation." 4-hour maximum timebox for a one-month Sprint. ("Scrum Events", "Sprint Review")

4. **Sprint Retrospective as team-internal engagement.** "The Sprint Retrospective is to plan ways to increase quality and effectiveness. The Scrum Team inspects how the last Sprint went with regards to individuals, interactions, processes, tools, and their Definition of Done." 3-hour maximum timebox for a one-month Sprint. ("Scrum Events", "Sprint Retrospective")

5. **Daily Scrum as Developer-to-Developer engagement.** A 15-minute event for the Developers (Product Owner and Scrum Master participate only if working on Sprint Backlog items). "Daily Scrums improve communications, identify impediments, promote quick decision-making, and consequently eliminate the need for other meetings." Structure is at Developers' discretion. ("Scrum Events", "Daily Scrum")

6. **Scrum Master serving three principals.** The Scrum Master serves: the **Scrum Team** (coaching, focus on Increments, impediment removal, event facilitation); the **Product Owner** (Product Goal techniques, Product Backlog item clarity, "facilitating stakeholder collaboration as requested or needed"); and the **organisation** (Scrum adoption coaching, advising implementations, "removing barriers between stakeholders and Scrum Teams"). ("Scrum Team", "Scrum Master")

7. **Cross-functional, self-managing team as the engagement core.** "Scrum Teams are cross-functional, meaning the members have all the skills necessary to create value each Sprint. They are also self-managing, meaning they internally decide who does what, when, and how." No sub-teams or hierarchies. Engagement begins with the team's own coherence. ("Scrum Team")

8. **The five Scrum Values as engagement conditions.** Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, Courage. Openness is named explicitly for the stakeholder relation: "The Scrum Team and its stakeholders are open about the work and the challenges." Respect is named for team members and their counterparts: "Scrum Team members respect each other to be capable, independent people, and are respected as such by the people with whom they work." ("Scrum Values")

9. **The Product Goal as long-term engagement anchor.** "The Product Goal describes a future state of the product which can serve as a target for the Scrum Team to plan against... The Product Goal is the long-term objective for the Scrum Team. They must fulfill (or abandon) one objective before taking on the next." Stakeholders engage around what the team is collectively trying to achieve. ("Scrum Artifacts", "Product Backlog", "Commitment: Product Goal")

10. **Stakeholders may attend Sprint Planning.** "The Scrum Team may also invite other people to attend Sprint Planning to provide advice." Stakeholder voice is welcome upstream of the commitment, by invitation. ("Scrum Events", "Sprint Planning")

11. **The Increment may be released to stakeholders mid-Sprint.** "Multiple Increments may be created within a Sprint... an Increment may be delivered to stakeholders prior to the end of the Sprint. The Sprint Review should never be considered a gate to releasing value." Stakeholder value delivery is decoupled from the inspection cadence. ("Scrum Artifacts", "Increment")

12. **The Definition of Done as a shared stakeholder commitment.** "The Definition of Done creates transparency by providing everyone a shared understanding of what work was completed as part of the Increment." The DoD is the artefact stakeholders and the team share for distinguishing "done" from "claimed done." ("Scrum Artifacts", "Increment", "Commitment: Definition of Done")

13. **Beyond software: Scrum applies to "essentially complex work."** "We are humbled to see Scrum being adopted in many domains holding essentially complex work, beyond software product development where Scrum has its roots. As Scrum's use spreads, developers, researchers, analysts, scientists, and other specialists do the work." Engagement patterns transfer. ("Purpose of the Scrum Guide")

14. **Whole-team accountability for stakeholder activities.** "The Scrum Team is responsible for all product-related activities from stakeholder collaboration, verification, maintenance, operation, experimentation, research and development, and anything else that might be required." Stakeholder collaboration is not Product-Owner-only or Scrum-Master-only — it is whole-team. ("Scrum Team")

## Questions to Ask During Stakeholder Engagement

### Phase 1: Mapping (Identifying who has standing)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify the stakeholder layers | Who are the stakeholders for this product — customers/users, organisational leaders, regulators, the team itself? Are there voiceless stakeholders the Product Owner needs to represent (future users, downstream operations, affected non-customers)? |
| Surface "those wanting to change the Product Backlog" | Who is currently trying to influence what the team builds? Are they engaging through the Product Owner (correct), or pressuring Developers directly (wrong path)? |
| Identify the Sprint Review attendees | Who are the "key stakeholders" who need to be in the Sprint Review? Have we invited the right people for the upcoming Sprint Review? |
| Identify organisational barriers to surface | What organisational barriers between stakeholders and the Scrum Team need the Scrum Master's attention? (Removing these is a named Scrum Master responsibility.) |
| Test the Product Owner's stakeholder model | Does the Product Owner have a clear, defensible model of which stakeholders matter most for the next Sprint, the next Product Goal, the product overall? |

### Phase 2: Framing (Bounding the engagement before convening)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Frame the Sprint Review purpose | The Sprint Review is "to inspect the outcome of the Sprint and determine future adaptations" — not a status report, not a demo, not a sign-off ceremony. Is the team going into the Sprint Review with that framing? |
| Frame the Sprint Retrospective purpose | The Sprint Retrospective is "to plan ways to increase quality and effectiveness" — covering individuals, interactions, processes, tools, and Definition of Done. Is the team framing it as an honest inspection or as a perfunctory check-the-box? |
| Set Sprint Review attendees | Who needs to be in the room for the Sprint Review to be a working session, not a presentation? The wrong attendees (too many, too few, wrong roles) will collapse the engagement quality. |
| Set Sprint Planning advisor invitations | "The Scrum Team may also invite other people to attend Sprint Planning to provide advice." Do we need an advisor for the upcoming planning — a customer representative, a domain expert, an operations lead? |
| Anchor on the Product Goal | Is the Product Goal current and explicit enough to anchor stakeholder conversations? "The rest of the Product Backlog emerges to define 'what' will fulfill the Product Goal." |
| Frame engagement around the values | Is the team prepared to be open about challenges (Openness), courageous about hard truths (Courage), respectful of stakeholder perspectives (Respect)? |

### Phase 3: Convening (How to engage in the moment)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Run the Sprint Review as a working session | Are we collaborating on what to do next, or have we slipped into presentation mode? The Guide is explicit: "The Sprint Review is a working session and the Scrum Team should avoid limiting it to a presentation." |
| Adjust the Product Backlog in the room | "The Product Backlog may also be adjusted to meet new opportunities" during the Sprint Review. Are we treating the Product Backlog as a living artefact in stakeholder conversations? |
| Use the Daily Scrum for impediment surfacing | The 15-minute Daily Scrum is where impediments to the Scrum Team's progress get surfaced. Is the conversation focused on Sprint Goal progress and the next day's plan? |
| Engage the team in the Retrospective fully | In the Sprint Retrospective, are we inspecting individuals, interactions, processes, tools, *and* Definition of Done? Are we identifying "assumptions that led them astray" and exploring "their origins"? |
| Apply the values in engagement | Is the engagement embodying Openness, Respect, Courage? The values "give direction to the Scrum Team with regard to their work, actions, and behavior." |

### Phase 4: Capturing (Recording what was learned)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Update the Product Backlog after Sprint Review | Has the Product Backlog been adjusted based on what stakeholders said? "Attendees collaborate on what to do next. The Product Backlog may also be adjusted to meet new opportunities." |
| Capture Retrospective decisions in commitments | What concrete change did the Retrospective produce? "The most impactful improvements are addressed as soon as possible. They may even be added to the Sprint Backlog for the next Sprint." |
| Update the Definition of Done if needed | If the Sprint Review or Retrospective surfaced quality expectations the team is not meeting, is the Definition of Done being updated to reflect the new bar? |
| Translate stakeholder needs to Product Backlog items | When stakeholders express new needs, is the Product Owner translating them into clearly-communicated Product Backlog items, ordered appropriately, transparent and understood? |
| Communicate decisions to those not in the room | Sprint Review attendees may not include all stakeholders. How is the Product Owner communicating decisions to the broader stakeholder set? |

### Phase 5: Following through (Maintaining engagement over time)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Maintain the Sprint cadence | Are we holding each event as prescribed, or have we let any of them lapse? "Failure to operate any events as prescribed results in lost opportunities to inspect and adapt." |
| Honour Product Owner decisions | "For Product Owners to succeed, the entire organization must respect their decisions." Is the organisation honouring Product Owner decisions about Product Backlog ordering, or working around them? |
| Refine the Product Backlog continuously | Product Backlog refinement "is an ongoing activity to add details, such as a description, order, and size." Is the Product Owner conducting ongoing refinement with the Developers and incorporating stakeholder feedback? |
| Deliver value between Sprint Reviews | "An Increment may be delivered to stakeholders prior to the end of the Sprint. The Sprint Review should never be considered a gate to releasing value." |
| Remove barriers as the Scrum Master | What barriers between stakeholders and the Scrum Team has the Scrum Master surfaced this Sprint? What is being done to remove them? |
| Track progress toward the Product Goal | Is the Product Goal being made visible, and is progress against it being inspected at each Sprint Review? |

### Phase 6: Closing (Adjourning well)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Close the Product Goal explicitly | "They must fulfill (or abandon) one objective before taking on the next." Has the Product Goal been fulfilled, or is it being abandoned? Either decision needs an explicit acknowledgement with stakeholders. |
| Handle Sprint cancellation as a stakeholder event | "Only the Product Owner has the authority to cancel the Sprint." If a Sprint is cancelled, how is the Product Owner communicating with stakeholders about why and what happens next? |
| Hand over outstanding Product Backlog items | If a product moves to a new team or is sunset, what is the disposition of remaining Product Backlog items? Is the Definition of Done documented for future maintainers? |
| Capture the value delivered | Across the product's life, has the team delivered Increments that meet the Definition of Done and advance the Product Goal? The Sprint Review history is the running record of stakeholder-validated value. |

## What to Look For

**Signal:** Stakeholders in the Sprint Review listen politely but do not adjust anything in the backlog.
**Diagnosis:** The Sprint Review has slipped into a presentation format. "The Sprint Review is a working session and the Scrum Team should avoid limiting it to a presentation." Stakeholders are not collaborating on what to do next; they are watching a demo.
**Follow-up:** Restructure the Sprint Review: demo the working Increment, then ask stakeholders to try it themselves where possible. Treat the backlog as a working artefact in the room — bring it up, show the ordering, invite adjustment.

**Signal:** Multiple stakeholders are approaching Developers directly with feature requests.
**Diagnosis:** The Product Owner is not operating as the single-throat-to-talk-to. "Those wanting to change the Product Backlog can do so by trying to convince the Product Owner." Direct stakeholder-to-developer pressure bypasses the Product Owner and fragments priorities.
**Follow-up:** The Scrum Master surfaces this as an organisational barrier. Redirect stakeholders to the Product Owner. If the Product Owner is not accessible enough to handle the volume, that is a capacity problem the Scrum Master addresses with the organisation.

**Signal:** The Sprint Retrospective produces the same three improvement items every sprint without change.
**Diagnosis:** The Retrospective is not producing concrete change commitments. "The most impactful improvements are addressed as soon as possible. They may even be added to the Sprint Backlog for the next Sprint."
**Follow-up:** Add at least one improvement item from the Retrospective to the Sprint Backlog. The improvement that stays in a "action items" list outside the backlog will not be pulled.

**Signal:** The Product Goal is vague or frequently revised.
**Diagnosis:** The long-term engagement anchor for stakeholder conversations is unstable. "The rest of the Product Backlog emerges to define 'what' will fulfill the Product Goal" — without a clear Product Goal, backlog ordering is arbitrary and stakeholder conversations have no shared reference.
**Follow-up:** The Product Owner works with the Scrum Team to articulate a Product Goal specific enough to anchor decisions. The Scrum Master coaches both the Product Owner and the organisation on what a Product Goal is for.

## When to Use This Reference

- A *Sprint Review* is collapsing into a presentation rather than a working stakeholder-engagement session.
- A *Product Backlog* is being shaped by a committee of stakeholders rather than by a single Product Owner.
- A *Product Owner* is being treated as a proxy or scribe rather than the single accountable person for value maximisation.
- A *Sprint Retrospective* is being skipped or perfunctory rather than producing concrete improvements.
- An *organisational barrier* between stakeholders and a Scrum Team needs surfacing (Scrum Master responsibility).
- The *Daily Scrum* is being used as a status report to managers rather than as Developers-only Sprint Goal coordination.
- A *new stakeholder* is trying to engage the team directly rather than through the Product Owner.
- The *Product Goal* is unclear or missing, leaving stakeholder conversations without a shared anchor.
- The *Definition of Done* is contested between team and stakeholders — is it the organisation's, the team's, or in dispute?

## Worked Example

A software team at a regional insurance company is building a digital claims portal. After three Sprints, the claims operations manager (a key stakeholder) stops attending Sprint Reviews. When asked why, she says the reviews feel like "another meeting to sit through" — the team demos features, she nods, and nothing changes.

The Scrum Master recognises the pattern: the Sprint Review has become a presentation, not a working session. The Product Owner has been ordering the backlog independently between reviews, and the manager's input rarely changes anything visibly.

For Sprint 4's review, the Scrum Master restructures the event. The first 10 minutes: the Product Owner presents progress toward the Product Goal — explicitly, with a visual — and shows how the current Sprint's Increment contributes. The next 25 minutes: the claims operations manager and two claims adjusters navigate the portal themselves. The Product Owner runs the backlog on a screen; when the manager says "this field layout will slow my team down," the Product Owner moves the UX-redesign item from Sprint 7 to Sprint 5 in the room.

The manager leaves having seen her input change the ordering. At the next review, she brings two colleagues.

The scenario is operator-authored. The Sprint Review as working session (not a presentation), the Product Backlog as living artefact in the room, the Product Goal as the anchor for progress reporting, the Product Owner as the single accountable person for backlog ordering, and the Scrum Master's barrier-removal responsibility are all `[V]`-marked passages in the deep reference. No verbatim source blockquotes appear in this distillation.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Sprint Review as a presentation.** The Guide is explicit: "The Sprint Review is a working session and the Scrum Team should avoid limiting it to a presentation." Scripted demos teach stakeholders to be passive.
- **Product Backlog shaped by a committee of stakeholders.** "The Product Owner is one person, not a committee." Shared ownership of backlog ordering produces a backlog that satisfies no one.
- **Stakeholders bypassing the Product Owner.** "Those wanting to change the Product Backlog can do so by trying to convince the Product Owner." Direct stakeholder-to-developer pressure fragments priorities and undermines the Product Owner's accountability.
- **Sprint Retrospective that produces no concrete change.** "The most impactful improvements are addressed as soon as possible. They may even be added to the Sprint Backlog." A retrospective whose outputs live only in a separate action-item list will not be pulled.
- **Product Goal that is vague or absent.** Without a Product Goal, stakeholder conversations have no shared anchor and backlog ordering is arbitrary.
- **Sprint events treated as optional.** "Failure to operate any events as prescribed results in lost opportunities to inspect and adapt." A dropped Retrospective is a dropped engagement loop.
- **Treating Sprint Review as the gate for releasing value.** "The Sprint Review should never be considered a gate to releasing value." Decoupling delivery from the inspection cadence gives stakeholders value when they need it.
- **Organisation not respecting Product Owner decisions.** "For Product Owners to succeed, the entire organization must respect their decisions." An organisation that routes around the Product Owner has undermined the engagement structure Scrum depends on.
- **Scrum Master not surfacing barriers.** "Removing barriers between stakeholders and Scrum Teams" is a named Scrum Master responsibility; a Scrum Master who does not actively surface and address these barriers has left the engagement structure incomplete.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The Scrum Guide 2020 is a self-contained framework definition. Within the text, the only explicit external pointer is the licence reference to Creative Commons. The provenance section names early collaborators by name without external citation. There are no `[BT]` items in the deep reference — the Guide does not cite external authors or frameworks within its text.

**Named limits of the source.** The text explicitly acknowledges or implies these scope boundaries:
- Detailed stakeholder-mapping frameworks (Mitchell-Agle-Wood salience, Freeman stakeholder theory, Donaldson-Preston three approaches) are not developed. The Scrum Guide names stakeholders without offering a typology.
- Customer development methods (interviews, focus groups, lab studies) are not prescribed. The Guide does not prescribe how the Product Owner gathers stakeholder input.
- UI design and usability engagement (paper prototyping, think-aloud protocol, Inclusivity Heuristics) are not developed.
- Specific Sprint Review facilitation techniques — how to run a demo well, how to elicit honest stakeholder feedback, how to handle conflicting stakeholder voices — are not prescribed. The Guide says it is a working session; it does not say how to run one.
- Specific Sprint Retrospective facilitation techniques (Start/Stop/Continue, 4Ls, Sailboat, Mad-Sad-Glad) are not prescribed.
- Power dynamics (French and Raven, resource dependence, coalition politics) are not analysed.
- Negotiation theory is not developed.
