
# Barbrook-Johnson & Penn, Systems Mapping — seven methods for causal models of systems (light reference)

**Source:** Barbrook-Johnson, P., & Penn, A. S. (2022). *Systems Mapping: How to build and use causal models of systems*. Palgrave Macmillan / Springer Nature. ISBN 978-3-031-01833-6 (print) / 978-3-031-01919-7 (eBook). DOI 10.1007/978-3-031-01919-7. 197 pages. Licence: **CC BY 4.0** (Open Access).
**Structure:** 12 chapters. Ch 1 *Introduction* (framing and three positioning figures). Chs 2–8 are method chapters: Rich Pictures, Theory of Change, Causal Loop Diagrams, Participatory Systems Mapping, Fuzzy Cognitive Mapping, Bayesian Belief Networks, System Dynamics — each with a shared structure (what is it / how to do it / common issues / good and bad at / history / getting started). Chs 9–11 are cross-cutting (data and evidence; workshops; comparing/choosing/combining). Ch 12 *Conclusion* with five take-home messages.

## Author's Thesis (condensed)

Systems mapping is a useful, intuitive, and increasingly demanded family of methods for building causal models of complex systems — but the landscape is muddled, terminology is inconsistent, and large differences between methods are often missed because systems mapping is subsumed under broader systems-thinking framing. The book deliberately scopes to seven methods that "represent causality and influence in a system" and can be used in a participatory way, explicitly excluding non-causal approaches (mind mapping, social network analysis, viable systems model, etc.) and broader schools of practice (participatory action research, design thinking, permaculture).

Five other load-bearing claims thread through the book. (i) The value of systems mapping is concentrated across five general-purpose modes: helping us think, orient ourselves, synthesise and connect information, communicate, and extrapolate from assumptions to implications — not primarily forecasting. (ii) The value is often in the *process* rather than the product (the map itself), particularly in participatory modes. (iii) *Appropriateness* — a three-way triangulation between project purpose, system characteristics, and method nature — is the right frame for choosing between methods; bias toward familiar methods is the most common interference. (iv) *Combining* methods (sequentially or as hybrids) is normally valuable when time permits, and is a productive source of methodological innovation. (v) The appropriate stance for engaging with complex adaptive systems is participatory, holistic, systemic, and humble — "we cannot force or control [them], but we can work with them to steer and nurture the change we want."

## The seven methods at a glance

- **Rich Pictures** — free-form drawing of a situation or system in groups, with minimal facilitation; the most flexible/qualitative method; emerged from Soft Systems Methodology (Checkland, Lancaster, 1970s). Strengths: openness, low barrier, captures perspectives. Weaknesses: will not formalise knowledge precisely; cannot seed more formal methods directly.

- **Theory of Change diagrams** — networks of inputs → activities → outputs → outcomes → impacts, built around an intervention; coined by Carol Weiss at the 1995 Aspen Institute roundtable; an entire approach to intervention design and evaluation, not just a mapping method. Strengths: structures evaluation and design; surfaces assumptions. Weaknesses: prevents whole-system view; reinforces optimism; excludes those not invested in the intervention.

- **Causal Loop Diagrams (CLDs)** — networks of variables with positive/negative causal arrows, focused on feedback loops around a *core system engine*; emerged from System Dynamics practice in the 1970s. Eight named system archetypes from Kim (2000): drifting goals, escalation, fixes that fail, growth and under-investment, limits to success, shifting the burden, success to the successful, tragedy of the commons. Strengths: visually appealing; hints at dynamics; stepping-stone to System Dynamics. Weaknesses: requires feedbacks to be present; no quantitative analysis.

- **Participatory Systems Mapping (PSM)** — directed cyclic graphs of factors and their causal relations, annotated with stakeholder information (importance, vulnerability, controllability); developed by the authors over the last decade; large maps (20–100+ nodes) analysed via submap extraction using network-analysis rules (upstream/downstream steps, ego networks, paths). Strengths: middle ground between flexible-qualitative and formal-quantitative; works in data-poor contexts. Weaknesses: relatively static — does not explore dynamics.

- **Fuzzy Cognitive Mapping (FCM)** — networks of factors with weighted edges in [-1, +1]; "semi-quantitative"; introduced by Bart Kosko (1986) as an extension to Axelrod's (1976) cognitive maps. Two analysis schools (following Helfgott et al. 2015): *causal* (link strength = certainty of causation; thresholding/squashing function required) and *dynamical* (link strength = magnitude of effect; no thresholding). Strengths: intuitive; quick; ideal for workshops; "a form of quantitative storytelling". Weaknesses: easy to misapply or misunderstand; output sensitivity to threshold function form can reverse results.

- **Bayesian Belief Networks (BBNs)** — directed acyclic graphs of variables with discrete states, encoding conditional probability tables; coined by Judea Pearl (1988) with Neapolitan (1989) as foundational texts. Strict constraints: no feedbacks (workarounds via *dynamic BBNs*); typically max two–three parents per node; max ~20 nodes; bounded set of discrete states. Strengths: transparency (no "hidden" decisions like FCM threshold or System Dynamics parameters); probabilistic representation; captures wider context through non-zero conditional probabilities. Weaknesses: not whole-system; awkward for feedbacks; time-consuming to update.

- **System Dynamics** — networks of *stocks* (accumulating/depleting numerical entities) and *flows* (differential equations governing rates of change), allowing simulation; developed by Jay Forrester at MIT in the 1950s; raised to high profile by the 1972 *Limits to Growth* report. Despite the name, "models tend to be focused on specific dynamical problems rather than whole systems". Active prediction-vs-pattern debate in the community. Strengths: high-level strategic insights on dynamical questions; teaches dynamic thinking. Weaknesses: time-consuming; difficult for soft factors; not useful for operational/micro questions.

## Three conceptual spaces (Ch 1) for relating the methods

- **System focus ↔ intervention focus × qualitative ↔ quantitative** (Fig 1.1). PSM, CLD, Rich Pictures cluster on the qualitative side; FCM, System Dynamics, BBN on the quantitative side; Theory of Change is intervention-focused.
- **Emphasis on participation × intuitive-easy-to-start ↔ formal-harder-to-start** (Fig 1.2). PSM and FCM emphasise participation; BBN is neutral on participation; System Dynamics is on the formal end.
- **Types of outputs and analysis** (Fig 1.3, Venn diagram). Visual maps; network-analysis outputs; dynamic/simulation outputs; probabilistic outputs.

## Five uses of systems mapping (Ch 1)

1. **Helping us think.** Forces specificity about assumptions, beliefs, understanding. "You basically cannot avoid having it do this to you."
2. **Helping us orient ourselves.** Reveals positions in the system and who is affected.
3. **Helping us synthesise and connect information.** Flexible methods bring together different evidence types.
4. **Helping us communicate.** Both as process (group mapping surfaces hidden assumptions) and as product (the map as repository). Watch for the *horrendogram* failure mode in unfiltered complexity displays.
5. **Helping us extrapolate from assumptions to implications.** Simulation-capable methods (System Dynamics) look forward; BBNs follow conditional dependencies; FCM computes combined effects; PSM visualises causal pathways.

## Sources of data and evidence (Ch 9)

Four overlapping input types. (i) **Participatory** processes — defended at length as the right tool in data-poor complex-adaptive-system contexts; produces maps as *boundary objects* (Star & Griesemer 1989) around which stakeholders learn. (ii) **Qualitative data** from interviews/focus groups — heavy researcher judgement to combine accounts; the synthesised map "no individual actually described" is a known artefact. (iii) **Existing evidence** (peer-reviewed studies, grey literature) — strong evidential support but coverage gaps and systematic bias risk; treat as a focused evidence review. (iv) **Quantitative data** — either pairwise statistical/causal-inference, or whole-network *causal discovery* / *network estimation*; national-scale data-rich systems are most amenable. The chapter closes with a "plea for creativity" — any method-and-data combination is valid if gaps are acknowledged.

## Running workshops (Ch 10)

A practical runbook: clear purpose, careful invite, detailed time-plan in 15–60 minute units, accessible venue (go to them not them to you), flexible furniture, low-tech mapping materials (post-its, whiteboard paper), dedicated note-taker (audio recording costs more than it gives back — transcription "five or six times the length of the recording"), photos throughout, *give away power* as facilitator, work in pairs not crowds. Online workshops: attendance is easier, interaction is harder, shorter-and-more-frequent works, software choice matters, burnout is more subtle.

## Comparing, choosing, combining methods (Ch 11)

The *appropriateness* triangle: project purpose / system characteristics / method nature. Bias toward familiar methods is the most common interference; data-availability constraints are often overstated. Two combination modes — *sequential* (one method's outputs feed another) and *hybridised* (one map combines elements of two or more methods). Combining is "normally useful or valuable" when time permits.

## Final take-home messages (Ch 12)

(i) See system maps as *living documents* with maintenance plans. (ii) Be clear about whether the value lies in process, outputs, or both — stakeholders are often surprised how much value the *process* delivers compared to the *answer*. (iii) Climb the *ladder of using methods*: from hammer-everything-is-a-nail, to awareness of alternatives, to mastery of nuanced method choice. (iv) Decide between prioritising participation and prioritising conceptual rigour — both at once is rarely possible. (v) Advocate for *participatory steering of complex adaptive systems* — work with systems, not against them.

## Signature Contrarian Positions

1. *Against "black-box" highly technical modelling that does not lead to action* — named as one of three historical failures of complexity-science applications.
2. *Against the "horrendogram" failure mode* — unfiltered complexity displays that paralyse rather than guide action. PSM's submap-based analysis is the authors' deliberate response.
3. *Against treating ToC diagrams as products rather than processes* — the value is in development and iteration with stakeholders.
4. *Against researcher-assumption-driven FCM analysis* — the threshold function can have more impact on results than the map structure (the analytical concern that drove the authors' move to PSM).
5. *Against the validation-as-quantitative-truth view of model legitimacy* — in genuine complex adaptive systems we will rarely have the data needed to validate every node and edge.
6. *Against prediction as the sole purpose of System Dynamics* — pluralist position: purpose drives the model; some models predict, others reveal patterns.
7. *Against the System-Dynamics-as-whole-system view* — System Dynamics is best at *dynamical sub-parts* of systems.
8. *Against the Maslow's-hammer failure mode* — climb the ladder of using methods rather than applying one method to everything.
9. *Against bias toward familiar methods in method selection* — appropriateness, not familiarity, is the right criterion.
