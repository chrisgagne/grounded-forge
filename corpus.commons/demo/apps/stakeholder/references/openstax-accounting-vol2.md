## Author's Thesis (condensed)

Managerial accounting is the process of producing decision-relevant cost and performance information for internal users — managers responsible for planning, controlling, and evaluating business operations. Where financial accounting reports historical results to external users under GAAP, managerial accounting produces detailed, timely, often forward-looking information custom-shaped to the decision at hand. The volume's organising frame is the planning-controlling-evaluating cycle.

Five load-bearing claims structure the volume. First, cost classification is decision-relative: the same cost can be fixed, variable, mixed, direct, indirect, product, or period depending on how it will be used. Second, short-term and long-term decisions warrant different analytical frames — relevant cost analysis ignores the time value of money and applies to short-term operational decisions; discounted cash flow analysis applies to long-term capital decisions. Third, financial measures alone are insufficient for performance evaluation; responsibility-accounting frameworks should align manager incentives with corporate strategy through quantitative-and-qualitative measures. Fourth, allocation of overhead matters for both pricing and decision-making, with activity-based costing correcting distortions traditional allocation creates as overhead's share of total cost rises. Fifth, sustainability reporting is now-mainstream corporate accountability extending the audience of reporting from stockholders to a wider stakeholder set.

## Managerial Accounting Fundamentals (Ch 1)

Managerial accounting differs from financial accounting on seven dimensions: users (internal versus external), governance (no GAAP versus GAAP), reports (custom versus financial statements), frequency (as-needed versus quarterly/annual), purpose (planning, controlling, evaluating versus external decision-making), focus (segments versus whole company), and nature (monetary plus non-monetary versus monetary only). The Institute of Management Accountants frames the discipline as partnering in management decision-making, devising planning and performance management systems, and providing financial-reporting-and-control expertise to assist strategy implementation. The IMA's four ethical standards — competence, confidentiality, integrity, credibility — anchor the profession's ethics. The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 and the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act provide the legislative backdrop. Five environmental trends reshape contemporary practice: technology (ERP systems, automation, RFID, intangible-asset growth), lean practices (TQM, JIT, Lean Six Sigma, kaizen, Goldratt's Theory of Constraints), the balanced scorecard, globalisation, and corporate social responsibility/sustainability.

## Cost Behaviour and Building Blocks (Ch 2)

Three organisational types — manufacturing, merchandising, service — incur different cost categories. The volume's central decision-relative claim: cost classification depends on the use of the cost data. Three behavioural categories: fixed (constant in total within a relevant range), variable (constant per unit, varying in total with activity), and mixed (combining both via Y = a + bx). Stepped costs remain constant within a relevant range and step to a new plateau when the range boundary is crossed. Manufacturing product costs split into three categories: direct materials, direct labour, manufacturing overhead. Combined: prime costs (direct materials plus direct labour) and conversion costs (direct labour plus manufacturing overhead). Period costs (selling and administrative) are expensed in the period incurred. Three estimation methods (scatter graph, high-low, least-squares regression) separate mixed costs into fixed and variable components.

## Cost-Volume-Profit Analysis (Ch 3)

Contribution margin is the bridge between cost behaviour and CVP analysis: selling price minus variable cost per unit, expressed per unit, as a ratio, or in total. Contribution margin first covers fixed costs, then becomes profit. The break-even point is where total revenue equals total costs: fixed costs divided by contribution margin per unit (in units) or contribution margin ratio (in dollars). Target profit treats the desired profit as if it were a fixed cost. After-tax target profit requires conversion to pre-tax via division by (1 - tax rate). Sensitivity analysis examines effects of changes in price, volume, variable cost, or fixed cost. In multi-product environments, the sales mix is treated as a composite unit with a composite contribution margin. Margin of safety is current sales minus break-even sales. Operating leverage measures sensitivity of net operating income to sales changes; high-fixed-cost structures produce higher leverage and higher risk-and-return.

## Job-Order Costing (Ch 4)

Job-order costing traces direct costs to individual jobs and is used for custom or differentiated products. Costs flow through three inventory accounts: raw materials, work-in-process, finished goods. Cost of goods manufactured aggregates the period's completed-job costs. Materials requisition forms, time tickets, and job cost sheets are the primary documents. Indirect labour and indirect materials feed the manufacturing overhead pool. The expense recognition principle drives inventoried treatment of product costs and immediate expensing of period costs. Predetermined overhead rates are computed at year-start (estimated overhead divided by estimated cost-driver activity) because actual overhead is not known in real time and varies seasonally. End-of-period differences between actual and applied overhead are typically closed to cost of goods sold.

## Process Costing (Ch 5)

Process costing is used for continuous, batch-based production where unit-level cost tracing is impractical (cereal, soft drinks, paint, drumsticks, cookies). The central concept is equivalent units: the number of whole units that would have been produced if production were sequential. Equivalent units are calculated separately for direct materials (typically added at process start) and conversion costs (added evenly throughout). The four-step procedure: determine units; compute equivalent units; determine cost per equivalent unit; allocate costs to units transferred and ending WIP. Two valuation methods: weighted-average and FIFO. Costs flow from one department's WIP to the next; transferred-in costs become the basis for the next stage's processing.

## Activity-Based, Variable, and Absorption Costing (Ch 6)

Activity-based costing assigns overhead based on the activities that drive overhead costs, contrasting with traditional allocation's single-cost-driver approach. ABC's value rises as overhead's share of total cost rises and direct labour's share falls — a consequence of automation. The five-stage ABC process: identify activities; determine cost pools; calculate activity rates; allocate to products; calculate unit product costs. Costs are gathered at four levels: unit, batch, product, factory. Variable costing assigns only variable manufacturing costs to products, treating fixed manufacturing overhead as a period cost; absorption costing assigns all manufacturing costs (variable and fixed) to products. Absorption costing is GAAP-compliant; variable costing is not. The volume takes the position that variable costing produces better information for internal decision-making — variable costing prevents the income-manipulation-via-overproduction that absorption costing enables.

## Budgeting (Ch 7)

A budget is a quantitative plan estimating timing and amounts of resource flows. The master budget has two halves: the operating budget (sales, production, materials, labour, overhead, S&A, budgeted income statement) and the financial budget (capital asset, cash, budgeted balance sheet). Operating budgets cascade from the sales budget through production to the budgeted income statement. Financial budgets begin with capital expenditures and feed the cash budget through cash collections and cash payments schedules. Four budgeting approaches: top-down, bottom-up (participative), combination, zero-based. Static budgets fix one production level; flexible budgets adjust budgeted costs to actual production. Rolling budgets continuously add a period as old ones expire. Variances comparing actual to budgeted may be favourable or unfavourable; flexible-budget variances isolate cost-control effects from volume effects.

## Standard Costs and Variances (Ch 8)

Standards are expectations for components of production: standard cost is what the company expects to pay; standard quantity is what it expects to use. Ideal standards are demotivating because unattainable; attainable standards are recommended. Six variances structure the analysis: direct materials price variance (computed at purchase), direct materials quantity variance, direct labour rate variance, direct labour time variance, variable overhead rate variance, variable overhead efficiency variance. Each is favourable or unfavourable. The total cost variance combines price/rate and quantity/time variances. Variances often interact (e.g., a favourable price variance from cheap materials produces an unfavourable quantity variance from waste). Companies use variances to identify causes; the recommended approach explains both favourable and unfavourable variances. Failure modes: budget slack (inflated standards) and managing-to-the-variance (short-term decisions that meet thresholds at the cost of long-term goals).

## Responsibility Accounting and Decentralisation (Ch 9)

Centralised organisations concentrate decision authority at the top; decentralised organisations distribute it. Most organisations are mixed. Responsibility accounting structures financial accountability into five centre types: cost centre (manager controls costs only), discretionary cost centre (no clear cost-output link, e.g., HR), revenue centre (manager controls revenues only), profit centre (manager controls revenues and costs), investment centre (manager also controls invested capital). Each warrants different evaluation measures. Three quantitative tools for investment centres: profit margin percentage, return on investment (ROI = profit / investment base), and residual income (RI = income - investment × cost-of-capital). ROI alone creates goal-congruence problems — a high-ROI segment manager rejects projects below segment ROI even when they benefit the company; RI accepts any project with positive residual income. Controllable, uncontrollable, and allocated costs are distinguished, with the prescription that managers should be evaluated only on controllable costs. Transfer pricing affects goal congruence between selling and buying segments.

## Short-Term Decision-Making (Ch 10)

Short-term decisions turn on relevant costs and revenues — those that differ between alternatives — and ignore the time value of money. Five canonical short-term decision categories: special orders, make-or-buy, keep-or-discontinue, sell-or-process-further, constrained-resource allocation. Cost categories that recur: avoidable (eliminable by choice), unavoidable (fixed by prior commitment), sunk (already incurred, never relevant), opportunity (forgone alternative). Decisions should distinguish quantitative factors (measurable cost and revenue effects) from qualitative factors (morale, customer relationships, reputation, ethics). For special orders, capacity availability determines whether fixed costs become relevant. For make-or-buy, only avoidable internal costs count; allocated unavoidable fixed costs do not. For keep-or-discontinue, the contribution-margin approach avoids misleading allocation effects. For sell-or-process-further, joint costs are sunk and irrelevant; only future incremental costs matter. For constrained resources, rank products by contribution margin per unit of the constraining resource, not by unit contribution margin alone. The Ford Pinto case anchors the volume's ethical caution against pure cost-minimisation analysis.

## Capital Budgeting (Ch 11)

Capital investment is allocation of funds to long-lived assets for growth, distinct from operating expenses for day-to-day operations. The capital decision process: determine needs, identify resource limits, establish baseline criteria, evaluate alternatives via screening and preference decisions, decide. Two non-time-value methods serve as screens: payback period (initial investment divided by annual net cash flow; uneven flows require partial-year computation) and accounting rate of return (incremental net income divided by initial investment less salvage). The time value of money asserts a dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow. Two cash-flow types: lump sums and annuities; ordinary annuities pay at end of period, annuities due at start. Compounding earns interest on interest; discounting reverses the process. Two discounted-cash-flow methods are the volume's substantive evaluation tools: net present value (positive NPV is investment-worthy at the required rate of return) and internal rate of return (the discount rate at which NPV equals zero; accept if it exceeds the required rate). The profitability index (present value of cash flows divided by initial investment) facilitates comparison of mutually exclusive projects of different sizes.

## Balanced Scorecard and Performance Measures (Ch 12)

Performance measurement should produce goal congruence between manager and corporate goals. Good measures are based on controllable factors, are measurable, timely, consistent, comparable to a baseline, and not biased toward the manager over the company. Traditional accounting-based measures have known limitations: gameable (LIFO timing), affected by uncontrollable economic conditions, biased toward short-term focus, and miss long-term-value-creating actions like training and customer-base development. The DuPont decomposition splits ROI into sales margin times asset turnover, revealing different paths to high return (jewellery's high-margin / low-turnover versus grocery's low-margin / high-turnover). Economic value added (EVA = after-tax income - invested capital × WACC) is RI's market-aligned cousin: WACC reflects actual financing cost; EVA uses after-tax income. The balanced scorecard, originated by Schneiderman (1987) and developed by Kaplan and Norton, evaluates across four perspectives: financial, internal business, customer, learning and growth. Quantitative-plus-qualitative across four perspectives produces more durable evaluation than financial-only approaches. BSC at the corporate, divisional, and individual employee level uses the same four perspectives with progressively more specific goals and measures.

## Sustainability Reporting (Ch 13)

Sustainability is meeting present needs without compromising future generations'; CSR is corporate action accepting responsibility for environmental and social impact. The Brundtland Commission Report (1987) established the foundational definition; the Paris Climate Agreement (2015, 196 nations) anchors contemporary international consensus. Three foundational corporate cases drove the field: Union Carbide (Bhopal 1984, more than 600,000 exposed, official toll 3,598 dead), Nestlé (baby formula in developing countries; UNICEF reported 14× higher diarrhoea-death risk for formula-fed infants), and Johnson & Johnson (Tylenol cyanide poisoning 1982; recall cost more than $100 million; share price recovered in six weeks). The triple bottom line (Elkington 1997, "people, planet, profit") expanded reporting from shareholders to all stakeholders. User-by-user analysis distinguishes shareholders' viability concerns from lenders' going-concern concerns from employees' value-alignment concerns from customers' value-alignment concerns from regulators' best-practice concerns from community's social-contract expectation. Three major reporting frameworks: GRI (1997, 92 per cent of Global 250 in 2016), SASB (2011, investor-focused, 79 industries across 10 sectors), Integrated Reporting (IIRC 2010, six capitals: financial, manufactured, intellectual, human, social-and-relationship, natural). Each defines materiality differently. Public benefit corporations are an emerging legal expression of stakeholder accountability. KPMG reported in 2017 that 93 per cent of the world's 250 largest companies by revenue produced corporate responsibility reports.

## Appendix A: Financial Statement Analysis

Three primary tools: horizontal analysis (trend across periods, dollar and percent change), vertical analysis (line-item proportions within a period, common-size statements), and four ratio categories. Liquidity ratios (working capital, current ratio, quick ratio) show short-term obligation capacity. Solvency ratios (debt-to-equity, times interest earned) show long-term obligation capacity. Efficiency ratios (accounts receivable turnover, total asset turnover, inventory turnover, days' sales in inventory) show asset management. Profitability ratios (profit margin, return on total assets, return on equity) show returns on operations. Past performance does not always dictate future performance; economic influences, accounting policy changes, and reporting practice changes are common limitations.

## Signature Contrarian Positions

1. **Cost classification is decision-relative.** The same cost can be fixed, variable, mixed, direct, indirect, product, period — depending on its use. There is no intrinsic classification.
2. **Variable costing is more useful for internal decisions than absorption costing**, even though only absorption costing is GAAP-compliant. Absorption costing's overhead-allocation-to-inventory mechanic enables income manipulation through overproduction.
3. **Equal allocation of common costs misleads segment evaluation.** A segment can appear unprofitable purely because of allocation choices; the contribution-margin approach should be used for keep-or-discontinue decisions.
4. **Sole reliance on ROI produces goal incongruence.** A high-ROI segment manager will reject a positive-NPV project that benefits the company because it lowers segment ROI; residual income and EVA correct the incentive.
5. **Financial measures alone are insufficient for performance evaluation.** They lag, can be gamed, and miss long-term-value-creating actions; the balanced scorecard's quantitative-plus-qualitative across four perspectives is required.
6. **Stockholder-only accountability is incomplete.** Sustainability reporting and the public benefit corporation model express stakeholder-wider accountability that financial reporting alone misses.
7. **Pure cost-minimisation produces ethical disasters.** The Ford Pinto case demonstrates that quantitative cost-benefit analysis excluding ethical and reputational dimensions can produce decisions that are profitable in the short term and disastrous in the long term.
8. **Ideal standards are demotivating; attainable standards are productive.** The middle is more useful than either extreme.
9. **Past sunk costs are never relevant to current decisions.** Despite human reluctance to ignore them, including sunk costs in decision analysis distorts the choice between alternatives.
10. **In multi-constraint production, the relevant ranking is contribution margin per unit of the constraining resource**, not unit contribution margin. The highest-margin product may not be the highest contribution-per-machine-hour product.
