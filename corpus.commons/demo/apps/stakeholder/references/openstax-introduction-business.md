## Author's thesis (condensed)

Introduction to Business presents the discipline as an applied integration across functional areas (economics, ethics, global trade, ownership forms, entrepreneurship, management, organisational design, HR, motivation, operations, marketing, distribution, IT, accounting, finance, careers, legal/tax environment). The book's organising premise is that survivor businesses are those that pay attention to a continuously changing external environment composed of seven sub-environments (economic, political-and-legal, demographic, social, competitive, global, technological), and that managers are "primarily adapters to, rather than agents of, change."

Four load-bearing claims structure the survey. First, businesses operate within external environments largely beyond direct managerial control, requiring continuous environmental scanning and adaptation. Second, stakeholder consideration is integral to business success — Carroll's CSR pyramid (economic, legal, ethical, philanthropic responsibilities) frames the social-responsibility argument and treats employees, customers, society, and investors as stakeholder groups. Third, effective management is a process built around four functions — planning, organising, leading, controlling — performed across three management levels (top, middle, supervisory) using three skill types (technical, human-relations, conceptual), with leadership style varying along an autocratic-participative-free-rein continuum. Fourth, motivation, performance, and reward must be linked through expectancy and equity mechanisms (also explored in Ch 9 alongside historical motivation theories from Taylor through Maslow, McGregor, Herzberg, and reinforcement theory).

## Economic systems and the business environment

A *business* is an organisation that strives for profit by providing goods and services. Four traditional factors of production (natural resources, labor, capital, entrepreneurship) plus a fifth (knowledge) are common inputs. Seven environmental sub-environments shape business decisions: economic, political-and-legal, demographic, social, competitive, global, technological. Managers control internal operations but adapt to external environments. Three economic systems exist: capitalism (private ownership and market-set prices), communism (government ownership and centralised planning), socialism (basic industries owned or strongly regulated). Most economies are mixed. Three macroeconomic goals: economic growth (measured by GDP), full employment (94-96% target), price stability (CPI/PPI). Government uses monetary policy (Federal Reserve, federal funds rate) and fiscal policy (taxation and spending). Four market structures: perfect competition, pure monopoly, monopolistic competition, oligopoly.

## Ethics, social responsibility, and stakeholders

Ethics is moral standards for judging right or wrong. Four philosophical frames: justice, utilitarianism, deontology, individual rights. Eleven categories of unethical activity (Brigham Young University) catalogue common violations. Three organisational tools encourage ethical conduct: leading by example, ethics training programs, formal codes of ethics. Two decision tests: the feelings test and the newspaper/social-media test. Carroll's pyramid of corporate social responsibility (CSR) stacks four components — economic responsibility (foundation), legal responsibility, ethical responsibility, philanthropic responsibility. Stakeholders include employees, customers, society (including environmental protection and corporate philanthropy of >$19 billion annually in the U.S.), and investors. Strategic giving ties philanthropy to mission/goals. The new social contract between employer and employee covers compensation, management, culture, and learning/development.

## Global marketplace

Comparative advantage anchors trade theory: each country specialises in what it produces most readily and trades for goods foreign countries produce most readily. Trade barriers include natural barriers (distance, language), tariff barriers (taxes on imports), and non-tariff barriers (import quotas, embargoes, buy-national regulations, exchange controls). The WTO has emerged as the world's most powerful institution for reducing trade barriers. The IMF and World Bank support global financial stability and development. Major regional agreements: NAFTA (US, Canada, Mexico), Mercosur (South America), CAFTA (Central America), EU (28 European nations as of 2018, with Brexit pending). Six entry modes ranked by risk: exporting, licensing, franchising, contract manufacturing, joint ventures, direct foreign investment. Cultural differences matter (the Mitsubishi Pajero name and Coca-Cola Chinese-character translation are cautionary examples).

## Forms of business ownership

Three primary forms: sole proprietorship (72% of US businesses, 4% of revenue, 15% of profit), partnership (10%/15%/27%), corporation (18%/81%/58%). Sole proprietorships offer ease of formation and direct control but unlimited liability. Partnerships add resources and skills but unlimited liability for general partners (LLPs limit this). Corporations offer limited liability, transferability, unlimited life, and access to financing but face double taxation and complexity. Three corporate variants: C corporation (conventional), S corporation (hybrid; up to 100 shareholders; pass-through taxation), LLC (hybrid; treated as partnership for tax purposes; flexible). Cooperatives serve member-owners for shared purposes (Ace Hardware, Sunkist, Land O'Lakes); two types — buyer cooperatives and seller cooperatives. Joint ventures allow two firms to combine resources for specific projects. Franchising involves a franchisor and franchisees; provides ~8.9 million U.S. direct jobs and $890 billion economic output.

## Entrepreneurship and small business

Entrepreneurs come in three types: classic entrepreneurs (further split into micropreneurs and growth-oriented), multipreneurs (serial founders), intrapreneurs (entrepreneurial within a large corporation). Nine entrepreneurial traits: ambitious, independent, self-confident, risk-takers (preferring moderate risk), visionary, creative, energetic, passionate, committed. Small businesses employ ~half the U.S. private workforce and represent ~half U.S. economic output. The SBA checklist for starting a business runs from self-assessment through business plan to financing. Two financing modes: debt (interest-bearing loans) and equity (stock sale, including angel investors and venture capital). Five-year survival rate is 48.7%; ~50% of businesses fail within five years. Common failure causes: economic factors, financial inadequacies, lack of experience, personal reasons.

## Management and leadership

Management is the process of guiding development, maintenance, and resource allocation to attain organisational goals. Four management functions: planning, organising, leading, controlling. Four planning types ordered by horizon: strategic (1-5 years, top management), tactical (less than 1 year, middle management), operational (current, supervisory), contingency (alternative paths for crises). The managerial pyramid has three levels (top, middle, supervisory) requiring three skill types in different proportions (technical, human-relations, conceptual). Power has five primary sources (legitimate, reward, coercive, expert, referent). Leadership styles span a continuum: autocratic (directive), participative (democratic, consensual, consultative), free-rein (laissez-faire). Situational leadership selects styles based on maturity and competency. Empowerment delegates authority and resources. Corporate culture is the set of attitudes, values, and behaviour standards distinguishing one organisation from another. Mintzberg's three managerial role categories: informational (monitor, disseminator, spokesperson), interpersonal (figurehead, leader, liaison), decisional (entrepreneur, disturbance handler, resource allocator, negotiator). Decisions are programmed (routine, rule-amenable) or non-programmed (novel, requires the five-step decision process). Trends include crisis management, outside directors, IT proliferation, and global management skills.

## Organisational structure

Organisation structures along division of labor, departmentalization (functional, product, process, customer, geographic — five traditional types), and delegation. Many large organisations combine multiple types (P&G's "four pillars" combine GBU/SMO/GBS/Corporate Functions). Line, line-and-staff, matrix, and committee structures span structural options. Matrix structures combine functional and product, but violate unity of command and can complicate accountability. Three types of work teams: problem-solving, self-managed (in ~80% of Fortune 1000 companies), cross-functional. Authority and unity-of-command frame the chain of command; span of control determines hierarchy depth. Five factors determine optimal span: task complexity, location, delegation ability, interaction needs, worker skill/motivation. Mechanistic vs organic structures: mechanistic suits stable simple environments and cost-control strategies; organic suits dynamic complex environments and innovation strategies. The informal organisation supplements formal structure through grapevines that 47% of employees believe over senior leadership messages. Trends include reengineering, virtual corporations, virtual teams, outsourcing, and global mergers.

## Human resource management

The HR management process is sequential: job analysis and design, planning and forecasting, recruitment, selection, training and development, performance planning and evaluation, compensation and benefits. Two recruitment sources: internal labor market (existing employees) and external labor market. Six-step selection process: initial screening, employment testing, selection interview, background and reference check, physical/drug testing, decision to hire. Ritz-Carlton's recruitment process exemplifies treating applicants as customers. Training is on-the-job (orientation, job rotation, apprenticeship, mentoring) or off-the-job (classroom, programmed instruction, simulation). Performance appraisal compares actual to expected performance; companies have moved away from annual reviews toward continuous feedback. Compensation has direct and indirect components; incentive pay includes commissions, bonuses, profit-sharing. Labor unions represent ~10.7% of U.S. workers (down from 20.1% in 1983). Collective bargaining produces the labor agreement; grievances flow through a multi-step procedure to arbitration. Pressure tactics for contract settlement include strikes, lockouts, and replacement workers. Federal laws govern minimum wage, equal pay, and discrimination in hiring/promotion/compensation.

## Motivating employees

Ten motivation theories developed chronologically. Scientific management (Taylor): four principles emphasising economic incentives. Hawthorne studies (Mayo): the Hawthorne effect — employees perform better when feeling singled out. Maslow's hierarchy of needs: five levels (physiological, safety, social, esteem, self-actualisation), with the strict hierarchy not strongly supported by research. McGregor's Theory X (pessimistic) and Theory Y (optimistic) management style assumptions. Theory Z (Ouchi): hybrid combining U.S. and Japanese elements. Herzberg's motivator-hygiene theory: motivators (achievement, recognition, work itself, responsibility) are intrinsic; hygiene factors (company policy, supervision, working conditions) are extrinsic. Expectancy theory (Vroom-Lawler): motivation depends on effort-performance, performance-outcome, and outcome-needs links plus valence. Equity theory (Adams): perceived fairness of outcome-input ratios versus referents shapes worker satisfaction. Goal-setting theory (Locke): specific, difficult, accepted goals improve performance. Reinforcement theory: behaviour is a function of consequences (reward, punishment, none). Cross-cultural variation in motivation differs across English-speaking, French-speaking, Northern European, Latin American/Southern European, German, and Japanese contexts. Motivational job design: job enlargement, job enrichment, job rotation. Work-scheduling options: flextime (57% of US companies), compressed workweek (29%), four-day workweek (13%), telecommuting, job sharing. Trends: education and training, employee ownership (~6,717 ESOPs), work-life benefits (sabbaticals), nurturing knowledge workers as "associates" not "subordinates."

## Operations management

Production turns inputs into outputs through operations management. Three production-decision stages: planning (location, layout, resources), control (quality, costs, scheduling), improvement (more efficient methods). Three production types: mass production (uniform, low-cost), mass customization (mass production then per-customer features), customization (one-at-a-time per customer). Two conversion processes: process manufacturing (input broken into outputs), assembly process (inputs combined or transformed). Two timing patterns: continuous (long runs, high volume) and intermittent (short runs, high variety). Four facility layouts: process layout (workflow around tasks), product/assembly-line layout (workstations in line), fixed-position layout (product stays put), cellular manufacturing (small self-contained team-based units). Resource planning starts with bill of material; make-or-buy decisions consider cost, secrecy, supplier reliability. Computerised systems escalate scope: MRP (materials), MRP II (cross-functional), ERP (full enterprise plus suppliers/customers). Supply-chain management is end-to-end customer-driven coordination. Routing and scheduling tools: Gantt charts, Critical Path Method (CPM), PERT (with three time estimates per task). Quality management: Total Quality Management (Deming), continuous improvement, Six Sigma (3.4 defects per million using DMAIC), Malcolm Baldrige Award, ISO 9000 (>500K orgs registered) and ISO 14000. Lean manufacturing eliminates non-value-added steps; just-in-time (JIT) reduces inventory at risk of supply-chain disruption. Manufacturing technology includes CAD/CAM, robotics, FMS, CIM. Trends: skills-gap workforce crisis, U.S. innovation leadership at risk, business process management as integrator of scattered global operations.

## Marketing — products and pricing

Marketing finds the needs and wants of buyers and provides products meeting or exceeding expectations using the "right principle" — right product to right person at right place, time, price, with right promotion and people. The marketing concept uses customer needs as the starting point for product development. Customer value is the ratio of benefits to sacrifice. Customer satisfaction shapes loyalty; relationship marketing focuses on long-term partnerships (5% retention increase = 25-95% profit increase). The 5Ps marketing mix: product, price, place, promotion, people. Six environmental forces shape strategy: cultural/social, demographic, economic, technological, political/legal, competitive. Four competitive advantage types: cost (subject to erosion), product differentiation (more durable), service differentiation (especially in social-media age, >80% of US GDP), niche (often the only option for small firms facing giants). Five-step consumer decision-making process: need recognition, information search, alternative evaluation, purchase decision, post-purchase satisfaction. B2B vs consumer markets differ in volume, customer count, location, and direct distribution. Five forms of consumer market segmentation: demographic, geographic, psychographic, benefit, volume. Business markets segment differently (use of product, characteristics of purchasing function, size of client, industry). Products are goods, services, or ideas. Product life cycle has four stages: introduction, growth, maturity, decline. Pricing strategy balances demand and cost; introductory penetration or skimming pricing tactics support different positions.

## Distribution and promotion

Marketing intermediaries (agents/brokers, industrial distributors, wholesalers, retailers) move products from producer to user. Channels reduce transactions and ease flow through sorting (sorting out, accumulating, allocating including breaking bulk). Two main wholesale types: merchant wholesalers (80%; take title) and agents/brokers (commission-based; do not take title). Retail divides into in-store retailing (Walmart, Target, Macy's, Neiman Marcus) and nonstore retailing (vending, direct selling, direct-response, home shopping, internet). Atmospherics shape retail image. Supply-chain management coordinates entities into a seamless customer-driven process. Promotion has six goals: create awareness, get consumers to try, provide information, retain loyal customers, increase use frequency, identify target customers, teach the customer (especially in services co-creation). The promotional mix: traditional advertising ($206B U.S. spend annually), personal selling (~6.5M U.S. workers), sales promotion ($321B in coupons distributed annually), public relations (PR), social media, e-commerce. Integrated marketing communications (IMC) coordinates all six for consistent messaging. Six-step personal selling process: prospecting/qualifying, approaching, presenting/demonstrating, handling objections, closing, following up. Modern selling favours unstructured relationship-driven presentations over canned scripts.

## Information technology

Technology transforms business through information systems, computer networks (LAN, WAN, internet, intranets, extranets), management information systems, technology management/planning, computer protection, and ongoing trends. Cloud computing, mobile technology, robotics, and emerging blockchain are reshaping business operations. Companies face make-vs-buy and centralised-vs-decentralised IT governance choices.

## Accounting

Accounting is the process of collecting, recording, classifying, summarising, reporting, and analysing financial activities. Two report types: managerial accounting (internal) and financial accounting (external). GAAP (generally accepted accounting principles) governs U.S. financial reporting via FASB; the IASB publishes international IFRS but FASB-IASB convergence has stalled. Public accountants (Big Four: Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG) audit; CPAs are credentialed; CMAs serve managerial-accounting roles. The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (SOX) reshaped the post-Enron environment: redefined auditor-corporation relationship, restricted consulting work, required CEO/CFO financial-statement certification with criminal penalties, mandated internal controls, and created the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB). The accounting equation: Assets = Liabilities + Owners' equity. Double-entry bookkeeping keeps the equation balanced. The accounting cycle: analyse → journalise → post to ledger → trial balance → financial statements → analyse and decide. Three financial statements: balance sheet (financial position at a point), income statement (revenues minus expenses over a period), statement of cash flows (operating/investment/financing). Four ratio categories assess financial health: liquidity (current ratio, acid-test, net working capital), profitability (net profit margin, ROE, EPS), activity (inventory turnover), debt (debt-to-equity). Industry norms vary widely (grocery <1% net margin; jewelry >10%). Trends: cloud-based accounting services, automation in auditing, staffing-profile shifts.

## Money and financial institutions

Money has three functions: medium of exchange, store of value, measure of value. The Federal Reserve System (the Fed) is the central banking system with three monetary tools: open market operations, the discount rate, reserve requirements. U.S. financial institutions divide into depository (commercial banks, savings institutions, credit unions) and nondepository (insurance, pension funds, brokerage firms, finance companies). The FDIC and NCUSIF insure deposits up to $250,000. International banking, online banking, mobile banking, and emerging blockchain/cryptocurrency are catalogued as ongoing trends.

## Financial management and securities markets

Financial management is the art and science of managing a firm's money. Three key activities: planning, investment, financing. The main goal is to maximize firm value to owners. The risk-return trade-off requires higher return for higher risk. Two expense categories: short-term (operating, requiring cash management, accounts receivable management, inventory management) and long-term (capital expenditures, requiring capital budgeting). Two short-term financing types: unsecured (trade credit, bank lines of credit, commercial paper) and secured (collateralised, often by accounts receivable or inventory; factoring sells receivables at a discount). Long-term debt forms: term loans, bonds (par value typically $1,000; 10-30 year maturities), mortgage loans. Debt vs equity differ in voting, residual claim, and maturity. Equity financing comes from common stock (initial public offerings or new issues), retained earnings, preferred stock, and venture capital (1-5% of applying companies). Securities markets divide into primary (new issues; issuer gets proceeds) and secondary (existing securities traded among investors). Investment bankers underwrite new issues; stockbrokers execute transactions. Online brokerages (Schwab, Fidelity, TD Ameritrade, E*Trade) account for >80% of trading volume. Bond ratings (Moody's Aaa to D; S&P AAA to D) signal default risk. Mutual funds (~$40T globally), ETFs (~$3.5T), futures contracts, and options expand investment options. ~52% of U.S. adults own stocks (vs 25% in 1981).

## Career planning

Career success rests on transferable interpersonal skills: people skills, persuasion, thinking on one's feet, self-empowerment, political savvy, team building, K-I-N-D conflict handling. The six-step planning process (set goal, acquire knowledge, compare alternatives, choose strategy, make commitment, stay flexible) applies to careers and life. Three job-relevant skill categories: job-specific (particular tools/equipment), transferable career competencies (planning, research, communication, human relations, critical thinking, management, project management), and work values/personal qualities (positive attitude, enthusiasm, working with people). Modern job search uses ATS-optimised résumés keyed to job postings; major platforms include Indeed, Monster, Glassdoor, CareerBuilder, LinkedIn. Interview discipline: arrive early, focus on being likable, project confidence, demonstrate enthusiasm, demonstrate knowledge of and interest in the employer, avoid disqualifiers. Job offer evaluation uses FACTS: Fit, Advancement, Compensation, Training, Site.

## Legal and tax environment (Appendix A)

U.S. law has four primary sources: common law (judicial-decision-derived), statutory law (legislative; main source of new laws), administrative law (regulatory rulings), business law (governs commercial dealings; UCC standardises across 49 states). The court system has trial courts (district courts), appellate courts (12 circuit courts), and the Supreme Court. Arbitration (binding) and mediation (non-binding) provide nonjudicial dispute settlement. Contracts require mutual assent, capacity, consideration, legal purpose, legal form. Three breach remedies: damages, specific performance, restitution. Express and implied warranties accompany sales. Intellectual property rights include patents (20 years), copyrights (life plus 70 years), trademarks, servicemarks. Torts (civil wrongs) cover medical malpractice, slander, libel, product liability, fraud; product liability includes strict liability. Bankruptcy provides Chapter 7 (liquidation; ~70% of cases) and Chapter 11 (reorganisation); the 2005 Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act tightened access. Antitrust regulation includes the Sherman Antitrust Act 1890, Clayton Act 1914, Celler-Kefauver Act 1950, and FTC Act 1914. The Robinson-Patman Act 1936 tightened price discrimination. Twenty consumer protection laws span 1872 to 2004. Four main tax types: income (progressive), property, payroll, sales, excise.

## Signature contrarian positions

1. **Strict Maslow hierarchy is not supported by research.** People do not move through five levels in fixed sequence.
2. **Single-minded focus on profit ignores stakeholders.** Drucker's framing positions stakeholder consideration as integral to business success, not as an afterthought.
3. **Manager-as-agent-of-change overstates managerial control.** Managers are "primarily adapters to, rather than agents of, change" — most external environmental forces are beyond direct control.
4. **Autocratic leadership is on its way out.** The trend in modern organisations is away from directive controlling styles.
5. **Free-rein leadership is not always preferred.** Without clear expectations and feedback, the laissez-faire style frustrates employees.
6. **Canned sales presentations are ineffective in modern relationship-driven selling.** Unstructured presentations build the bonds that long-term sales require.
7. **Cost-only competitive advantage erodes.** Competitors adopt the same lower-cost technology or suppliers; differentiation, service, and niche advantages are more durable.
8. **Matrix structures can hinder accountability.** Unilever moved from matrix to product departmentalization for this reason.
9. **Knowledge work requires different management.** Knowledge workers are "associates" who are supposed to do the telling within their area of knowledge — argues against authority-based management of specialised expertise.
10. **The 2005 Bankruptcy Reform tightens consumer accountability.** The means-based test ensures Americans with heavy debt cannot easily walk away from obligations.
