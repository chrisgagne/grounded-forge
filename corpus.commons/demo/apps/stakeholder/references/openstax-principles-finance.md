## Author's thesis (condensed)

*Principles of Finance* presents finance as the management, movement, and raising of money, organised around three areas: business finance, investments, and financial markets and institutions. The book's framing claim is that "business finance is about developing and understanding the tools that help people make consistently good and repeatable decisions." The text positions finance as a decision-making toolkit grounded in five recurring principles.

First, the **risk-return tradeoff**: investors are risk-averse, so higher expected returns are required to bear greater uncertainty. Second, the **time value of money**: a dollar today is worth more than a dollar in the future because money invested earns interest, future cash carries default risk, and consumers prefer present consumption. Third, **firm value depends on cash flows, not accounting earnings** — the text repeatedly distinguishes accrual-based earnings from cash flows and treats the latter as the load-bearing measure. Fourth, **the goal of management is to maximise shareholder wealth subject to ethical and stakeholder constraints**, with agency theory providing the analytical apparatus for principal-agent conflicts. Fifth, **decisions are evaluated against the firm's cost of capital**, formalised as the weighted average cost of capital (WACC).

## Three areas of finance and the firm's three financial-decision domains

Finance is divided into business finance, investments, and financial markets and institutions. Within business finance, three decision domains anchor most of the book: working capital management (short-term assets and liabilities), capital budgeting (long-term/fixed assets), and capital structure (long-term debt and equity). Capital budgeting "adds the greatest value to a firm" and is "one of the most important financial functions within a firm." The CFO is responsible for setting working-capital policy, optimal capital structure, and final capital-budgeting decisions; the controller faces inward (reporting), the treasurer outward (financing relationships).

## Investment risk components

The text catalogues five risk components: default risk (issuer fails to pay), inflation risk (purchasing power erosion), diversifiable/unsystematic risk (eliminable through diversification), non-diversifiable/systematic risk (measured by beta), and political risk (governments changing rules). Beta measures systematic risk by regressing stock returns on market returns; the slope is beta. Bond risks add credit risk, liquidity risk, duration risk, call risk, reinvestment risk, and term risk. Bond ratings (Fitch, Moody's, S&P) classify default risk; investment-grade is BBB/Baa or better, junk is below.

## Corporate structure, governance, and stakeholders

The four common forms of business organisation are sole proprietorships, partnerships, corporations, and hybrids (LLCs, LLPs). More than 80 per cent of US business by sales is conducted by corporations. The corporate form's value rests on limited liability, growth-financing access, and stock liquidity, balanced against double taxation. S corporations and C corporations differ on shareholder limits and taxation flexibility.

Stakeholders include employees, customers, shareholders, suppliers, communities, and governments. Shareholders own; stakeholders have an interest in outcomes — overlapping but distinct categories. Boards of directors act as fiduciaries for shareholders and have responsibility for goal-setting, dividend policy, hiring and firing CEOs, and resource allocation. Independent boards (no material interest beyond directorship) are corporate-governance best practice; diversity is increasing but remains a priority area for development. Strong audit committees became central with the 2002 Sarbanes-Oxley Act after the Enron and similar scandals.

Agency problems arise when managers (agents) pursue interests divergent from shareholders' (principals'). Three forms: stockholders versus management, investors versus creditors, and stockholders versus other stakeholders. Mitigations include performance-based and stock-based compensation, the threat of hostile takeovers, and ESG ratings that increasingly inform investor decisions. The text frames Enron and Bernie Madoff as cautionary cases where governance failure enabled massive wealth destruction.

## Microeconomic, macroeconomic, and currency foundations

Microeconomic foundations: demand (downward-sloping in price), supply (upward-sloping in price), and market equilibrium. Macroeconomic foundations: inflation (measured by CPI, core CPI, PPI, and GDP deflator), unemployment (jobless, actively seeking, able to take a job), and GDP (consumption + investment + government spending + net exports). The business cycle alternates expansion and contraction, with peaks and troughs identified by the NBER. The 2009-2020 expansion was the longest in US history (128 months). The market for loanable funds determines equilibrium interest rates; risk premiums layer above the risk-free Treasury rate. Foreign exchange rates create transaction, translation, and economic risk for firms with international operations.

## Accrual accounting

Cash-basis accounting records transactions when cash flows; accrual-basis accounting records them when economic activity occurs. GAAP requires accrual accounting for public companies. The accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) anchors the double-entry system. Revenue and expense recognition follow the matching principle. Long-term assets are capitalised and depreciated over their useful lives via straight-line, units-of-production, or double-declining-balance methods. Profit and cash flow are not the same; the income statement and balance sheet alone do not show liquidity, which is why the statement of cash flows is necessary.

## Four financial statements and ratio analysis

The income statement shows performance over a period; the balance sheet shows financial state at a point in time; the statement of owner's equity tracks contributed and earned capital changes; the statement of cash flows reconciles net income to actual cash flow across operating, investing, and financing activities. EBITDA strips noncash items from net income to approximate operating cash flow. Free cash flow = operating cash flow − capital expenditures. Common-size statements express each line as a percentage (of net sales for the income statement, of total assets for the balance sheet) to enable cross-firm and cross-time comparisons.

Four ratio families:

- **Efficiency**: AR turnover, total asset turnover, inventory turnover, days' sales in inventory. Track use of assets to generate sales.
- **Liquidity**: current ratio, quick ratio (acid-test), cash ratio. Track ability to meet short-term obligations.
- **Solvency**: debt-to-assets, debt-to-equity, times interest earned. Track ability to meet long-term obligations.
- **Market value**: EPS (only ratio GAAP requires on the income statement), P/E, book value per share. Express market valuation.
- **Profitability**: profit margin, return on assets, return on equity. The DuPont method decomposes ROE into profit margin × asset turnover × equity multiplier (operating efficiency × asset usage × leverage).

## Time value of money

A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow because of (1) potential investment returns, (2) default risk, and (3) preference for present consumption. Present value (discounting) and future value (compounding) are inverses. Compounding frequency matters: more frequent compounding produces a higher effective annual rate than the stated rate. The Fisher effect connects nominal rates, real rates, and inflation: (1+i) = (1+R)(1+h).

A perpetuity is an infinite stream of payments; its present value is C/r (constant) or C/(r-g) (growing). An annuity is a finite stream of equal payments; the ordinary-annuity present value formula is PVa = PYMT × [(1 - (1+r)^-n) / r]. An annuity due (payments at the start of each period) has the same formula multiplied by (1+r). Loan amortisation applies the present-value-of-annuity formula in reverse to compute fixed periodic payments and the breakdown into interest and principal. Mixed (unequal) cash-flow streams cannot use the annuity shortcut and must be discounted period-by-period; Excel's NPV function does this but starts with year-1 cash flows, requiring time-zero cash flows to be subtracted separately.

## Bond and stock valuation

A bond's price is the sum of two present values: the annuity of coupon payments plus the lump-sum face value at maturity, both discounted at the YTM. Bond price and interest rate are inversely related. The yield curve plots yield against maturity; normal yield curves slope upward, inverted yield curves slope downward and historically precede recessions. Floating-rate bonds reset to a benchmark rate and remain near par.

For stock valuation, the dividend discount model values shares as the present value of future dividends. Variants include zero-growth (constant dividend), Gordon constant-growth, variable-growth, and two-stage models. The Gordon model is highly sensitive to assumptions about growth and required return; non-dividend-paying firms cannot be valued this way. The discounted cash flow (DCF) model values firms based on cash flows rather than dividends and is preferred for non-dividend-paying firms and by analysts who view cash as harder to manipulate than reported earnings. Preferred stock is valued as a perpetuity (D/r). Multiples-based valuation uses P/E, P/B, P/S, P/CF ratios for comparison; these reveal market expectations but should be used in concert with cash flow analysis.

The efficient market hypothesis comes in three forms: weak (price history irrelevant), semi-strong (public information already priced), and strong (even insider information already priced). Empirically, US markets are operationally efficient (orders executed quickly at best available prices) and exhibit substantial informational efficiency.

## Statistical methods in finance

Measures of centre: arithmetic mean, median, mode, geometric mean (used for compound returns), and weighted mean. Measures of spread: standard deviation (volatility, primary risk measure in finance), variance. Measures of position: z-scores, quartiles, percentiles, IQR. The normal distribution underpins most financial modelling assumptions; Excel's NORM.DIST function returns probability under the curve.

Correlation coefficient r ranges from -1 to +1; the threshold 2/√n approximates statistical significance at the 0.05 level. Linear regression uses the method of least squares to find a best-fit line minimising the sum of squared residuals. In finance, regression is used to compute beta (slope of stock returns regressed on market returns), build prediction intervals, and assess linear relationships. The text repeatedly emphasises that correlation does not imply causation.

## Risk and the CAPM

Investors are rewarded only for systematic (market) risk because firm-specific risk can be diversified away. Statman's research shows about 12 stocks suffice for a reasonably diversified portfolio. The capital asset pricing model gives expected return as Re = Rf + β(Rm - Rf), where β measures systematic risk relative to the market. Performance metrics build on this: Sharpe ratio (reward to total risk), Treynor ratio (reward to systematic risk), Jensen's alpha (abnormal return after CAPM adjustment). Sharpe and Treynor are relative measures; alpha is absolute.

## Capital budgeting

The decision: which projects should the firm undertake? The text catalogues five methods, each with strengths and weaknesses:

- **Payback period**: simple but ignores time value of money, has no theoretically grounded threshold, ignores post-breakeven cash flows, and makes no risk adjustment.
- **Net present value (NPV)**: theoretically correct. Accept positive-NPV projects, reject negative-NPV. The decision criterion accounts for time value of money and can be adjusted for project-specific risk via the discount rate.
- **Internal rate of return (IRR)**: discount rate that sets NPV to zero. Easy to interpret as an annual percentage but suffers from multiple-IRR problems, the reinvestment-rate assumption, and inability to compare scale across projects.
- **Profitability index (PI)**: PV of inflows / PV of outflows. Useful for ranking projects under capital constraints. PI > 1 means accept (consistent with positive NPV).
- **Discounted payback period and modified IRR (MIRR)**: address some IRR limitations but retain others.

For mutually exclusive projects, the highest-NPV project wins (not the highest-IRR). Projects of different lives are compared via replacement chain or equal annuity approaches. Resource-constrained capital budgeting ranks projects by PI to maximise total NPV from a fixed budget.

## Capital structure and the cost of capital

A firm's cost of capital is the weighted average of debt and equity costs: WACC = D% × Rd × (1-T) + P% × Rp + E% × Re. Weights are computed from market values, not book values. The after-tax cost of debt is the YTM × (1-T); interest is tax-deductible. Equity cost is estimated via CAPM or the constant dividend growth model; the two often give different estimates and require management judgement to reconcile.

In a perfect market with no taxes (Modigliani-Miller benchmark), capital structure is irrelevant — leverage raises equity cost proportionally to keep WACC constant. With corporate taxes, the interest tax shield (Rd × D × T) lowers WACC, encouraging debt use. The trade-off theory weighs the interest tax shield against costs of financial distress; the optimal debt level balances these. Industries vary widely: online retailers carry minimal debt, while rubber and tires carry over 60 per cent debt.

Alternative funding sources: preferred stock (Rp = D/P, after-tax payment), convertible bonds (lower interest rate compensates for the bondholder's option to convert), and new common stock (most expensive — bears flotation costs in addition to the underlying equity premium).

## Financial forecasting

Forecasts use historical data, financial-statement analysis, and contextual information about the business, competitors, and economy. Sales forecasts anchor the rest: variable costs scale with sales; fixed costs are forecast in dollar terms. Pro forma income statements and balance sheets are linked through net income → retained earnings, accounts receivable → sales, inventory → COGS, accounts payable → COGS, and similar relationships. Cash is typically the "plug" that balances the projected balance sheet.

The cash-flow forecast identifies surpluses and deficits month by month, allowing managers to plan short-term financing. Companies often hold a target minimum cash balance and trigger borrowing when forecasts dip below it. Scenario analysis varies multiple inputs simultaneously (recession vs normal vs expansion); sensitivity analysis varies one input at a time to identify which forecast inputs are most consequential.

## Working capital, trade credit, and cash management

Working capital management balances the operating cycle (inventory acquisition to cash receipt) against the payables deferral period. Three motives for holding cash: transactional, precautionary, speculative. Compensating balances are bank-mandated minimums in exchange for other corporate services.

Trade credit (accounts payable) is "spontaneous financing" — interest-free unless discounts are offered. The annual cost of forgoing a 2/10 net 30 discount is 36.73 per cent — substantial, and a hidden expense for firms that delay paying. Receivables management starts before the sale (creditworthiness assessment, terms, limits) and continues through aging schedules and bad-debt provisioning. Inventory management balances purchasing, carrying, ordering, and stockout costs; just-in-time and economic-order-quantity tools optimise this trade-off.

## Risk management for the financial manager

Three focal areas: cash flow level (more in, less out), cash flow speed (faster in, slower out), and cash flow risk (less uncertainty in both). Hedging reduces risk; speculating bets on direction. Three categories of risk and corresponding hedges:

- **Commodity price risk**: hedged by long-term contracts (counterparty risk, inflexibility), vertical integration (introduces other risks), or futures contracts (standardised, exchange-traded, marked to market daily).
- **Exchange rate risk**: transaction, translation, and economic exposures. Hedges include futures contracts (standardised), forward contracts (flexible but with counterparty risk), natural hedges (offsetting business operations), and options.
- **Interest rate risk**: rising rates lower bond prices and present values of future cash flows. Duration measures sensitivity. Interest rate swaps allow firms with comparative advantages in fixed and floating markets to exchange payment streams to mutual benefit.

Options are derivatives giving the holder the right (not obligation) to buy (call) or sell (put) at the strike price up to or on the expiration date. The premium paid for the option is the maximum loss to the holder; the writer's maximum gain is the premium and maximum loss is unlimited (for calls) or strike-minus-premium (for puts). Options contracts are zero-sum: holder gain = writer loss.

## Signature contrarian positions

1. **Cash flow, not earnings, is the true measure of business health.** "Cash flow is the axis upon which the world of business spins" (Irina Simmons interview). The text returns repeatedly to the distinction between accrual accounting earnings and actual cash flows, treating the latter as load-bearing.
2. **NPV beats payback period as a capital-budgeting criterion.** Payback ignores TVM, has no theoretically grounded threshold, ignores post-breakeven cash flows, and makes no risk adjustment.
3. **For mutually exclusive projects, NPV beats IRR for ranking.** IRR ignores scale and assumes intermediate cash flows reinvested at the IRR rate, which is often unrealistic.
4. **Stock returns historically exceed bond returns over long horizons.** Per Jeremy Siegel, "you have never lost money in stocks over any 20-year period" but bonds have lost half their portfolio value (after inflation) — making stocks the safer asset for long-term holders despite higher short-term volatility.
5. **Pay secrecy and dividend policy "stickiness" can mask cash-flow problems.** The text observes that companies sometimes maintain dividends through borrowing rather than from earnings, and that this is harmful to long-term financial health.
6. **Trade credit is not free.** The annualised opportunity cost of forgoing a 2/10 net 30 quick-payment discount is 36.73 per cent — far higher than most short-term financing costs.
7. **Effective interest rates exceed stated rates when sub-annual compounding occurs.** A 1.5% monthly credit-card rate is 19.56% effective annual, not 18%.
8. **PALs and RALs are usurious in effective terms.** A $14 fee on a $200 weekly loan is 7% per week — 364% annualised before compounding, ~470% with compounding.
9. **Excel's NPV function does not compute the conventional NPV.** It assumes the first cash flow is one period in the future; the time-zero cash flow must be subtracted separately. Many spreadsheet errors trace to this convention.
10. **SPACs underperform traditional IPOs from the public investor's perspective.** The 2015-2020 study cited shows SPAC mergers averaging -18.8% return vs +37.2% for traditional IPOs.
11. **Speculating on options or commodities is mathematically a zero-sum game.** Holder's gain equals writer's loss. Hedging reduces risk; speculating bets on direction.
12. **Warren Buffett's view**: dividends are a last resort, not a primary management tool. Reinvestment in operations, technology, or moats is preferred when ROE on retained earnings exceeds shareholders' alternative use of cash.
