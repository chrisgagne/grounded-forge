## Author's thesis (paraphrased from source)

Org Topologies (OT) is presented as "the first human-centric plus AI-friendly organizational change approach" [V] (p. 1, "Cover"). Its central claim is that organizational performance is shaped decisively by org design — by which the authors mean the configuration of structures, policies, processes, rewards, and the units that hold work — and that different business goals (rapid delivery, global adaptability, resource optimization, amplified innovation) require deliberately different org designs [AP] (p. 3, "Management Summary"). The authors frame this as **strategic org design**: leaders should not adopt a pre-existing framework hoping it will "solve your problems" but should align (1) business strategy, (2) organizational goals, and (3) the change process so they reinforce each other to be "fit for purpose" [AP] (p. 3, "Management Summary"; p. 10, "Strategic Org Design").

The mapping technique itself has four elements: "Two dimensions of org design; Four organizational characteristics; Sixteen archetypes within four groups; Three distinctive organizational topologies" [V] (p. 4, "Mapping Org Design with Org Topologies"). The two dimensions are the **scope of skills mandate** (horizontal axis, increasing from Functional → Multi-Skill → End-to-End → Expanding → Unbounded) and the **scope of work mandate** (vertical axis, increasing from Tasks → Capabilities → Partial-Business → Whole-Business → Unbounded) [AP] (pp. 6–7, "Horizontal/Vertical Dimension"). These axes generate a grid of named archetypes such as `CAPS-2` (a multi-skill unit working at the capability level) and `WHOLE-3` (an end-to-end unit working on whole-business problems) [AE] (p. 5, "The Org Topologies Map").

The second load-bearing claim is the **psychology-of-change discipline**: change initiatives fail at scale because "people, when not owning the change ideas, won't fully accept them and won't go the extra mile to make them work" [V] (p. 3, "Management Summary"). The Primer therefore positions OT against canned-solution consulting: "the market is full of canned change 'solutions.' OT offers a custom path by inviting you and your people to create your change, not consuming common yet often mediocre ideas" [V] (p. 3, "Management Summary"). This is a deliberate stance — people "have to own – not rent – their change" [V] (p. 1, "Cover").

The third load-bearing claim is the **MADE method**: a four-step change process (Map → Assess → Design → Elevate) that uses the OT map as a visual thinking tool for the redesign of organizations, supplemented by Elevating Katas™ ("a set of practices and principles that can be applied as thoughtful experiments to change the organizational structure, processes, policies, and so forth") [AP] (pp. 15–19, "Change MADE Real"; p. 19, "(4) ELEVATE"). The method is grounded in systems thinking — explicitly invoked in the MAP step as the discipline of focusing "on the interactions and influences between the parts" and recognising that "if each element (e.g. policies, structures) is optimized in isolation, the performance of the whole system may actually decline for the desired goal (flow can get worse!)" [V] (p. 16, "MADE Real: (1) MAP").

The fourth claim, distinctive to the 2025 Edition, is that **AI is a cornerstone of the future workforce** and that strategic org design must explicitly plan for it: "Specialization and expertise are vanishing as limited resources, due to intelligence as a service" [V] (p. 20, "Strategic AI Adoption"), so old assumptions like "we don't have enough experts, therefore…" should be discarded in favour of asking "where in the target org design does AI make an outsized impact" [AP] (p. 20, "Strategic AI Adoption").

## Part I: The two dimensions of org design

### Horizontal axis — Scope of Skills Mandate

The horizontal dimension "represents the scope of skills mandate that is given to an organizational unit. This corresponds to the level of autonomy a unit has in delivering items of work. The more rightward an organizational unit is on the map, the more that unit can quickly deliver without dependencies, and so the better the flow of value delivery" [V] (p. 6, "Horizontal Dimension: Skills Mandate"). The axis is also given an economic interpretation: it "represents the transaction costs (a.k.a. overhead costs) to deliver value (decreasing from left to right!), and with that, how much waste from concept to cash" [V] (p. 6, "Horizontal Dimension: Skills Mandate"). And a connection to Scrum is made explicit: "To make a connection to the popular software development approach Scrum, in that model, the horizontal axis is the increasing completeness of the Definition of Done" [V] (p. 6, "Horizontal Dimension: Skills Mandate") [BT].

Five levels from left to right [V, AE] (p. 6, "Five Horizontal Levels"):

1. **Functional Units** — "individuals and groups specializing in a functional area, often resulting in the creation of functional groups (or departments). Example: Movie Costume Department." Work must be specified and handed over; "the hand-offs and information scatter increase the need for coordination, resulting in slow delivery and high process waste".
2. **Multi-Skill Units** (cross-functional teams) — "provide more flow and responsiveness than Functional Units. Example: Surgical Team of doctors and nurses. Multi-skill Units are incomplete, meaning they do not contain all the skills required to deliver value end-to-end. They co-exist with functional units and other teams on whom they depend to deliver complete value." Example continued: "The Surgical Team relying on Post-Op Care Team, and both together needed for a complete service for the patient."
3. **End-to-End Units** — "independently do everything to deliver complete value to customers and thus have no dependencies. Example: Software Feature Team. This results in much faster flow, more responsiveness, shorter and stronger feedback cycles from customer to creator (since there are no middlemen), and less process waste. That usually leads to happier workers and customers."
4. **Expanding Units** — "end-to-end units with the mandate to acquire new skills when needed. Example: Special-Forces Military Team. Such teams need to continuously learn when preparing for new missions. Expanding Units keep learning whatever is needed to stay end-to-end capable over time."
5. **Unbounded Units** — "have the mandate to learn anything for unbounded exploration. This may seem unusual today, but with AI traditional limitations vanish, so who knows where future groups can go?"

The horizontal axis is necessary but not sufficient: "to start solving the real problem, progress along only the horizontal axis is not enough" [V] (p. 6, "Horizontal Dimension: Skills Mandate").

### Vertical axis — Scope of Work Mandate

The vertical dimension "represents the scope of work mandate given to organizational units. That determines how units collaborate, learn, and share work. The higher a unit is on the map, the bigger is its field of play. At the highest levels, the scope of work of different units overlaps, motivating them to learn to co-own and work as one super unit – 'Team-of-Teams'" [V] (p. 7, "Vertical Dimension: Work Mandate"). The economic interpretation parallels the horizontal: this axis "represents the switching costs to work on something different (decreasing from bottom to top), and, therefore how easy it is for the whole organization to switch direction, ideally driven by feedback, innovation, competition, or strategic changes" [V] (p. 7, "Vertical Dimension: Work Mandate").

Five levels from bottom to top [V, AE] (p. 7, "Five Vertical Levels"):

1. **Tasks Focus** — "Units work on the narrowest scope of work. Example: A Sous-Chef preparing ingredients, or a visual designer sketching a wireframe. As input, they receive fine-grained tasks that match their expertise ('chop onions', 'design graphic'). It's an org design decision to limit their mandate, even though they may be capable of learning and doing more."
2. **Capabilities Focus** — "Units work on a full 'capability' within a product or service. Example: Insurance Claims-Processing Group. For products, a capability is often called a feature or function. For services, it's also called a service element. The unit name usually signals the capability: Surgical Team, Engine Team, Web Search Team, and so on. Common consequences include silo thinking and local optimizations."
3. **Partial-Business Focus** — "Units work on a particular business vertical, clearly separated from the other areas. Example: Uber Ride versus Uber Eats, two separate profit centers within a single company. A key characteristic of partial-business units (e.g. all Teams within Uber Ride) is cross-team collaboration and learning because they are closely involved in business success or failure and so care about outcomes. The need for close collaboration between teams at this level usually leads to organizational simplification."
4. **Whole-Business Focus** — "Units work and collaborate on whole-business problems and customer value. Example: Two-person Instagram-like startup. There is no permanent separation inside the unit, such as business verticals, sub-products, or alike. At a larger scale, Teams may have a short-term focus but are not stuck there."
5. **Unbounded** — "refers to a state where units go where they want to explore new ideas to satisfy undiscovered customer needs, possibly from unknown markets. And they learn whatever they need. Example: A startup, such as a failing games company who kept pivoting until they found a product that succeeded, in their case, the collaboration tool Slack" [AE].

The two axes together generate the OT map and the named archetype grid (e.g. `CAPS-2`, `WHOLE-3`, `TASKS-1`, `PART-1`) that gives OT its working vocabulary [AP] (p. 5, "Language of Org Design").

### The named archetype language

The authors emphasise that the archetype names create a shared discourse: "An archetype is a common pattern for how an organizational unit is structured and operates, such as a cross-functional team that delivers end-to-end. Each of the 16 archetypes on the OT map has distinct characteristics. They have short names ('CAPS-1', 'WHOLE-3',..), which creates a common org design language, enabling change agents to accurately describe both the current state and the desired future state of their organization with shared understanding" [V] (p. 5, "Language of Org Design").

A worked sentence demonstrates the language in use: "Most of our teams are CAPS-2—multi-skilled yet incomplete, lacking certain capabilities. There are also functional TASKS-1 parties supporting our CAPS-2 teams. This creates dependencies and challenges that are managed by CAPS-1 and PART-1 groups…" [V] (p. 5, "Language of Org Design"). The authors comment: "These few sentences convey a wealth of information! They describe an ecosystem, allowing us to grasp its dynamics holistically, recognize existing challenges, and begin a meaningful conversation about organizational improvement" [V] (p. 5, "Language of Org Design").

This vocabulary is explicitly positioned against framework-specific jargon: "Org Topologies introduces this simple but powerful language to clarify organizational development without resorting to 'agile' or other framework-specific jargon. This makes it easier to see things as they are, bringing together specialists from different backgrounds and facilitating open, unambiguous discussions about the current situation and the next steps" [V] (p. 5, "Language of Org Design").

## Part II: Four organizational characteristics

The Primer introduces two binary characteristics that classify all archetypes [AR] (p. 8, "Four Organizational Characteristics").

### Incomplete versus Complete

"In certain organizational designs, individuals are viewed primarily as 'resource units' assigned to specific tasks. These archetypes create partial solutions and rely on 'thinkers and planners' in other archetypes that coordinate and prepare work to ensure full utilization of the 'resources'" [V] (p. 8, "Incomplete versus Complete Archetypes"). "These archetypes are incomplete because they require other archetypes to deliver customer value collectively. Incompleteness arises whenever an organizational unit holds only part of the skills or does not have the mandate to apply all the skills needed to serve customers" [V] (p. 8, "Incomplete versus Complete Archetypes"). "By contrast, complete archetypes can independently deliver customer value without depending on other archetypes. Members of these archetypes typically focus on 'value' and emphasize crucial concepts like 'outcome' and 'flow'" [V] (p. 8, "Incomplete versus Complete Archetypes").

The authors flag an honest tradeoff: "One consequence of an org design with complete archetypes is that they can become independently operating silos. Whether that's a virtue or vice depends" [V] (p. 8, "Incomplete versus Complete Archetypes").

### Output versus Outcome

"There is a well-known management principle of maximizing outcomes while minimizing outputs. Outputs—such as number of surgeries, amount of software features, number of completed design documents, or percentage of closed insurance claims—are relatively straightforward leading metrics. However, more output does not mean more outcomes. In fact, more output usually means more cost or investment, which can lower profit (an outcome). Investing to increase outcomes (healthy people, profit) is justified; 'investing' to increase output is suspect" [V] (p. 8, "Output versus Outcome Archetypes"). "So, an archetype can be output or outcome-oriented. For an organizational unit to deliver outcomes, it needs to work in a higher-level business context, as seen on the OT map" [V] (p. 8, "Output versus Outcome Archetypes").

## Part III: Four archetype groups

The OT map's four quadrants name **archetype groups** [AR, AE] (p. 9, "Four Archetype Groups"):

- **Directing Archetypes.** "This quadrant represents directing archetypes that oversee the higher scope of work and lack the capabilities to create customer value. They design, coordinate, and essentially speculate on the value by making plans and writing specs to be handed off to other archetypes that can turn concepts into cash. Example: The Project Manager is responsible for planning and allocating resources to meet a project's deadline but does not participate in the actual creation of value" [V] (p. 9, "Directing Archetypes").
- **Doing Archetypes.** "People working in this quadrant of doing archetypes cannot independently see 'the whole' and cannot create end-to-end customer value. They need to rely on external planners and coordinators (directing archetypes), and other doing-archetype units (other limited specialists) to deliver value. Example: The Sous-Chef in a restaurant who reports to the Head Chef and has many tactical responsibilities" [V] (p. 9, "Doing Archetypes").
- **Delivering Archetypes.** "This quadrant of delivering archetypes represents work units (people, groups, teams, AI agents) capable of delivering value but only within a narrow scope of work. Because of that, these archetypes become siloed and may deliver the 'wrong' value. They lack the big picture and flexibility to change direction dramatically and must rely on directive archetypes for value delivery. Example: The Search Team in an E-Commerce software platform" [V] (p. 9, "Delivering Archetypes").
- **Driving Archetypes.** "This is not 'driving' others. Imagine a car with passengers, any one of whom can drive, who together decide where they want to go, drive and arrive, and then go on. These driving archetypes are capable of understanding, creating, and delivering customer value in its broadest sense. That's where directing, doing, and delivering come together. These archetypes can grasp the concept of business objectives or end-to-end customer journeys and deliver upon them jointly thanks to an overlapping, shared scope of work and a broad skills mandate. Example: A three-person startup or five teams within a Team-of-Teams" [V] (p. 9, "Driving Archetypes").

The metaphor of the driving group as "a car with passengers, any one of whom can drive" [V] (p. 9, "Driving Archetypes") is the authors' framing for distributed authority within a shared mandate — the opposite of one archetype directing the others.

## Part IV: Strategic org design and the three topologies

### Fit for purpose

"Something important not every leader knows: (1) different org designs can have different goals (adaptiveness, flow, …), (2) organizational goal can help or hinder a business objective (market share, profit, …), (3) leaders can and should deliberately design an organization to be fit for purpose: that is, the org design goal (e.g. 'adaptive') should align with and influence the business objective (e.g. 'profit')" [V] (p. 10, "Strategic Org Design"). "This is strategic org design in which every organizational element—from structures to career paths to policies to processes—is intentionally decided and aligned so that each part reinforces the others to be fit for purpose ('the org goal is high adaptiveness to support profit growth')" [V] (p. 10, "Strategic Org Design").

This is explicitly framed against framework adoption and against the divide between strategy and execution: "This approach contrasts sharply with adopting a pre-existing framework that promises to 'solve your problems.' And it contrasts with the idea of senior management only defining business objectives; in strategic org design, leaders understand and design the details of their internal organization to be fit for purpose. They align the external (business objective) and internal (org design)" [V] (p. 10, "Strategic Org Design").

A worked example: "The map on the right is for an organization with the business objective to increase market share in a new market. What org goal will likely support this? Leadership decides that in this unfamiliar market they need lots of customer interaction, learning, and, most of all, adaptiveness (easy change) to discover opportunities and grow. They decide on an org design where teams work on the whole business challenge. This requires them to move vertically up the map by expanding their scope of work. And it requires them to expand their expertise, their scope of skill, moving horizontally to the right on the map" [V] (p. 10, "Horizontal & Vertical Progress on the Map") [AE].

### The three named topologies

The Primer names **three common org topologies**, each a stable region on the OT map [AR, AE] (pp. 11–13):

**Resource Topology** (Directing + Doing). "A topology characterized by frozen functional archetypes specialized in one domain. Its purpose is to ensure that each unit is 100% utilized (i.e. resource utilization). It is seen in 'resource management' approaches where 'resource managers' (or project managers) are responsible for planning and monitoring resource allocation and utilization" [V] (p. 11, "Resource Topology"). "The value-creation work takes place among the Doing Archetypes in the lower left quadrant. However, none of these can independently produce end-to-end customer value. They depend on each other due to narrow specialization. Additionally, they lack a clear and holistic understanding of customer value, as their work mandate focuses solely on specific tasks or capabilities" [V] (p. 11, "Resource Topology"). "Learning in this topology is largely confined to improving existing skills rather than discovering new ones" [V] (p. 11, "Resource Topology"). The authors specify when the topology fits: "such a topology is a management's choice and justified, for example, when the relationships are temporary between the Directing and Doing archetypes (e.g. a movie Producer hiring dancers for a scene)" [V] (p. 11, "Resource Topology") [AE].

**Delivery Topology** (Directing + Delivering). "Compared to the Resource Topology, this topology upgrades delivery units to have fast flow by removing dependencies. Structurally, this typically involves forming complete cross-functional teams, and facilitating a smoother path to 'completely done' work. In some domains, this topology is called a 'Feature Factory' since Delivery units can provide a near-endless stream of features. Note a strong focus on outputs and local efficiency" [V] (p. 12, "Delivery Topology"). The authors flag a structural limitation: "in order to streamline delivery, discovery must remain separated in space and time. Similar to the Resource Topology, Directing Archetypes emphasize up-front analysis to supply the work" [V] (p. 12, "Delivery Topology"). And a load-bearing warning: "Notice that there is no guarantee in this topology that the output effectively solves the real problem or creates outcomes people care about. Products and services with 'feature bloat' are a common consequence" [V] (p. 12, "Delivery Topology"). The fit: "well-suited for domains where the core challenge is not discovering what to deliver, but delivering new capabilities predictably with short lead times. For example, in a 'diner' restaurant with short-order cooks" [V] (p. 12, "Delivery Topology") [AE].

**Adaptive Topology** (Driving). "The Adaptive Topology merges 'directing, doing, and delivering' into one holistic unit within the Driving archetypes, where humans, AI agents, and robots collaborate on complex problems in an adaptive way, and the group explores and drives themselves in their 'car'. The goal of this topology is adaptiveness (easy, cheap change based on learning) and customer-centricity to discover and deliver customer delight" [V] (p. 13, "Adaptive Topology"). "The boundaries between teams are structurally removed and replaced by larger constructs—such as a Team-of-Teams—where organizational units work closely together across the entire business ('Uber') or a partial business domain ('Uber Eats')" [V] (p. 13, "Adaptive Topology") [AE]. The authors name an emergent property: "'Synchronicity of work' is a unique emergent quality of this topology: in contrast to the other two topologies where the Doing and Delivering archetypes own a narrow scope of work, here in the Adaptive Topology, the Driving archetype allows all parties to work in unison" [V] (p. 13, "Adaptive Topology"). The fit and the warning are explicit: "Don't underestimate the importance of making change cheap and easy! We have seen countless failures due to the opposite. The Adaptive Topology establishes the conditions to discover and deliver higher-impact customer outcomes, promoting long-term business resilience. It's fit for purpose for organizations where growth and learning are crucial, such as market disruptors and startups" [V] (p. 13, "Adaptive Topology").

The Primer calls the elevated archetypes within the Adaptive Topology "Elevated to distinguish them from the Resource and Delivery topologies" [V] (p. 13, "Adaptive Topology"). Three core features: "Broad scope of capabilities and work; Synchronous work, coordinated by those performing the work; Continuous learning both vertically (across the scope of work) and horizontally (across the scope of skills), making it cheap and easy to switch strategic direction" [V] (p. 13, "Adaptive Topology").

### Comparing the three topologies

The Primer provides a comparison table on p. 14 covering: Common Use Case, Goal/Focus, Value Creation, Dependencies, Group Structure, Empowerment of Individuals, Function of Individual Contributors, and Strategic AI Application [AR, AE] (p. 14, "Comparing the Three Topologies"). Selected rows (the comparison is matrix-shaped — full content present on p. 14):

- *Common Use Case:* Resource — "PMO resource management of specialists"; Delivery — "Fast delivery of proven worth with minimal change or learning"; Adaptive — "Fast delivery of uncertain results, to seek, change, discover, and adapt" [V] (p. 14, "Comparing the Three Topologies").
- *Goal/Focus:* Resource — "Maximizing resource use, specialization"; Delivery — "Maximizing output, predictability"; Adaptive — "Maximizing outcome, innovation" [V] (p. 14, "Comparing the Three Topologies").
- *Dependencies:* Resource — "High; units depend on coordination across functional silos"; Delivery — "Moderate; units coordinate across boundaries but less frequently"; Adaptive — "Low; units work autonomously with minimal dependency on external groups" [V] (p. 14, "Comparing the Three Topologies").
- *Empowerment of Individuals:* Resource — "Low; constrained to predefined tasks with little autonomy"; Delivery — "Moderate; empowered within a small, home unit (i.e., a team) but depends on a high-level inter-unit alignment and coordination managed by other archetypes"; Adaptive — "High; takes ownership of entire processes, including inter-unit coordination, and takes the initiative to define or fulfill organizational goals" [V] (p. 14, "Comparing the Three Topologies").

## Part V: The MADE method

"Org Topologies and the MADE method gets change going. It begins by visually mapping existing organizational units on the OT Map. Mapping the organizational elements will reveal the existing topology: Resource, Delivery, Adaptive, or something else. Next, assessing whether your current topology is fit for the purpose of your company's strategic objectives. If not, the Map easily helps with designing, encouraging people to imagine and visualize a better org design. Usually, this involves identifying which of the three common Org Topologies achieves the goal needed to deliver on the business objectives. With the target design envisioned, we elevate the design by crafting a path toward the target with the Elevating Katas™, a rich set of practices and principles for systemic organizational improvement" [V] (p. 15, "Change MADE Real: the Method").

The method "unfolds in four distinct steps: Map, Assess, Design, and Elevate" [V] (p. 15, "Change MADE Real: the Method"). Three further premises ground the method:

- "Every organization already has a design—whether it's purposeful or an adhoc accretion over the years. And each org design influences certain performance outcomes, such as adaptiveness, and also the culture, such as encouraging learning, or not" [V] (p. 15, "Strategic Org Design").
- "The org design is composed of elements – structures, policies, processes, rewards, and more. When these elements are thoughtfully aligned for some goal (e.g. adaptiveness) to support a business objective (e.g. new market growth), then an organization gains the capabilities it needs to succeed, and its org design is strategic" [V] (p. 15, "Strategic Org Design").

### (1) MAP

"The first step in an Org Topologies-driven transformation is to map organizational units on their corresponding archetypes. This activity requires practicing systems thinking, learning the Org Topologies language, and going to the hands-on workplace to understand how the units work: what kind of units are they, what is their input, what is their output, and which other archetypes are needed in the chain of value creation?" [V] (p. 16, "MADE Real: (1) MAP"). The Primer makes the systems-thinking discipline explicit and warns against the alternative: "Systems thinking teaches us to focus on the interactions and influences between the parts, and to improve all the major parts in a systemically reinforcing design that supports a common org goal (for instance, 'flow'). If each element (e.g. policies, structures) is optimized in isolation, the performance of the whole system may actually decline for the desired goal (flow can get worse!). By seeing the system as an integrated collection of interacting parts that need to be aligned, organizations can create change that is both holistic and sustainable" [V] (p. 16, "MADE Real: (1) MAP").

A worked mapping example: "The mapping on the right represents a topology that contains five archetypes: one WHOLE-1 unit: a human (product manager); one CAPS-1 unit: a functional group (a pool of project managers); one TASKS-1 unit: a functional group (a pool of e.g. back-end developers); two TASKS-2 units: incomplete multi-skill development teams (developers & testers). The connections between the units depict their relationships; a thicker line signifies a stronger dependency" [V] (p. 16, "MADE Real: (1) MAP") [AE].

### (2) ASSESS

"Each Org Topology optimizes for a specific goal. By comparing an organization's map to the three common Topologies, we can see (1) which goal the current organization is designed for, (2) how effectively it does that, and finally (3) how well that goal supports the business objective. This step is about testing the existing org design for strategic alignment" [V] (p. 17, "MADE Real: (2) ASSESS").

The Primer continues the worked example: "we see a Directing Archetype (the Product Manager at WHOLE-1), who oversees the whole business and passes product development ideas to the Doing Archetypes. This is where we see a functional department, where project managers understand product capabilities (CAPS-1). Their job is to break them into tasks for the developers. They decompose, plan, dispatch and monitor the work. Some of the developers work as cross-functional teams (TASKS-2), others are in functional resource pools (TASKS-1). This example is a Resource Topology, whose goal is maximizing resource utilization. It produces organizational capabilities like predictability and specialization, making it well-suited for environments with a low need for product discovery, and where slow delivery of value is not a problem" [V] (p. 17, "MADE Real: (2) ASSESS") [AE].

The authors then introduce the diagnostic mismatch: "To evaluate if this org design is fit for purpose (i.e. testing for strategic alignment) we need to examine the business strategy and objectives against the capabilities the current design creates. Suppose the company's strategy is to outpace competitors by being faster at releasing new product capabilities. In the mapping on the right, there's a mismatch: the strategic ambition requires an org design with the goal of speed rather than predictability and resource utilization. In this case, strategic alignment is weak and needs to be worked on (next two steps of MADE). Having this kind of mismatch is common because people don't see and understand there's a mismatch! Org Topologies makes it easy for people to see, understand, and then get change going" [V] (p. 17, "MADE Real: (2) ASSESS").

### (3) DESIGN

"Achieving a business objective (for instance, 'more profit') by a strategy involves external and internal factors. It is uncertain and probabilistic, since it is strongly influenced by factors external to – and outside the control of – the company. By contrast, org design is internal, under our control, and closer to being deterministic or predictable (for instance, 'design for fast flow'). This allows us to influence business success by at least gaining influence over the internal factors of the design" [V] (p. 18, "MADE Real: (3) DESIGN").

"Designing an organization is about selecting the org goal that will deliver the capabilities required by the strategy and its objective. Possible goals include output predictability, utilization, flow, customer delight, adaptiveness, and more. The key is picking the subset that is best fit for purpose. Note that if more than one goal is chosen together for an organization, issues arise: Is some goal primary over others? Do they conflict? Can the org design support them all? These issues are beyond the scope of this Primer" [V] (p. 18, "MADE Real: (3) DESIGN").

The Primer permits intra-organisational divergence: "Companies may also choose different org designs for different parts of the company. One division might focus on servicing existing customers on a legacy product, while another is experimenting with new offerings—each requiring its own goal and design" [V] (p. 18, "MADE Real: (3) DESIGN").

### (4) ELEVATE

"Elevating an organization means moving its org design closer to the upper right on the Org Topologies map. Such moves increase the scope of skills and the scope of work mandate of organizational elements. Note that AI now makes elevation much easier, inviting change as never before" [V] (p. 19, "MADE Real: (4) ELEVATE").

The named change-method is the **Elevating Katas™**: "A 'kata' is a studied, repeating, disciplined movement in Judo, adopted and adapted into business language. 'Kata' was popularized in management via Toyota, implying a disciplined and repeating pattern for improving" [V] (p. 19, "MADE Real: (4) ELEVATE") [BT]. "The Elevating Katas are a set of practices and principles that can be applied as thoughtful experiments to change the organizational structure, processes, policies, and so forth. We are working toward a collection of Elevating Katas that can be applied to get change going, helping to improve any organization in the thoughtful direction of any topology. This work will be ready with the release of the Org Topologies book. Read: orgtopologies.com/elevating-katas" [V] (p. 19, "MADE Real: (4) ELEVATE").

The Primer is explicit that elevation is incremental: "Elevation can be an incremental process that gradually improves the capabilities of an organization. Also, different parts of an organization (e.g. business divisions) can have their own development vector that is consistent with their business objectives. While the organization experiments with gradual change, periodic re-mapping with Org Topologies is an easy and fast way to complete a learning loop of feedback and adapting. We are never finished with the perfection journey" [V] (p. 19, "MADE Real: (4) ELEVATE").

## Part VI: Strategic AI adoption

"Any management approach that doesn't include AI as a central part of the future workforce, is of the past. We are rapidly entering a world of AI agents and humanoid robots playing a role at work and home" [V] (p. 20, "Strategic AI Adoption").

A load-bearing claim: "A key insight when designing with OT: Specialization and expertise are vanishing as limited resources, due to intelligence as a service. That makes it much easier to elevate an organization! So when you are deciding on new teams requiring new expert knowledge, shed the old assumptions, 'We don't have enough experts, therefore…'. Spin up another 10,000 agents" [V] (p. 20, "Strategic AI Adoption").

This claim has a structural consequence on the OT map: "Evolving rightward on the horizontal axis to broader skills mandate will become ever easier due to AI. And likewise upward on the vertical axis to a broader work mandate. So, top-right Driving archetypes will become more feasible" [V] (p. 20, "Strategic AI Adoption").

The authors offer three guiding questions for strategic AI adoption [V] (p. 20, "Strategic AI Adoption"):

1. "What parts of the organization are the focus of development with OT? Focus your AI investment in the area you are elevating, where competitive advantage may come from early adoption of new technology."
2. "What archetypes are part of your target, and what are the major bottlenecks or constraints limiting their rapid adoption? Find the early points where AI can make an outsized impact."
3. "Do the AIs need monitoring by humans? That implies new responsibilities and processes in your organization. We predict that monitoring will become an important responsibility for human workers."

A worked example follows on p. 21: "a pet-supply company creates e-commerce software in a Resource topology. It's a mature, uncomplicated market. Competition has delivered a better website and app that customers love. Leadership has decided on a business objective of retaining customers, with an org goal of fast flow to parity, by targeting a Delivery topology with CAPS-3 teams" [V] (p. 21, "Strategic AI Adoption") [AE]. The worked example walks the three guiding questions and arrives at: "Yes, because people have decided on AI agents that autonomously design and code front ends, independently creating and iterating on variations that they deploy and measure. The small number of existing front-end designers and developers joining the new CAPS-3 teams will be trained in agent monitoring and take on that responsibility" [V] (p. 21, "Strategic AI Adoption").

The p. 21 archetype-group table summarises strategic AI application across the four groups [V, AR] (p. 21, "Strategic AI Adoption in the Archetype Groups"):

- **Directing**: "Strategic assistance through tools like predictive analytics, resource optimization, and workflow automation. Simulate project outcomes, automate prioritization, and allocate resources effectively. Digital omnipresence within the work done by the 'Doing' archetypes to ensure control and compliance." Key benefit: "Reduces manual planning time. Provides data-driven insights for better strategic decisions."
- **Doing**: "Operational efficiency booster for tasks requiring routine expertise with intelligent automation, task-specific agents, and virtual assistants. Optimizes repetitive and tactical work. Automated and predictive guidance within task execution." Key benefit: "Enhances productivity by reducing the cognitive load. Ensures consistent execution quality."
- **Delivering**: "Collaboration and alignment enabler via real-time data integration, recommendation engines, and feedback loops. Aligns work with organizational objectives. Plus all above-mentioned applications of AI for the 'Doing' archetypes." Key benefit: "Reduces silos through integration. Ensures outputs align with strategic goals and avoid misaligned value delivery."
- **Driving**: "Strategic partner through adaptive machine learning, real-time dashboards, and customer journey analytics. Predict customer needs and optimize end-to-end processes. AI serves as a subject-matter expert assisting faster (expanding, unbounded) learning for the teams." Key benefit: "Amplifies autonomy and innovation. Enhances data-informed decision-making and customer alignment. Digital marketplaces for business objectives with AI-assisted prioritization mechanisms."

## Part VII: Strategic org design versus framework thinking

"Framework thinking assumes that implementing an industry-standard management framework is key to success. This is the pitch from consulting companies big and small, and naturally, it seems easier to follow the crowd over deeply understanding and designing your own organization" [V] (p. 22, "Strategic Org Design vs. Framework Thinking").

The Primer names three consequences of unconsidered framework adoption [V] (p. 22, "Strategic Org Design vs. Framework Thinking"):

1. "Not solving the root-cause problems motivating the change, since it's not designed for the specific case."
2. "Lack of enthusiastic understanding and support for the change, since the people didn't figure it out and create it themselves; it's pushed on to them. Don't underestimate this psychological dynamic!"
3. "And although scoffed at and ignored (at their peril) by managers new to change and social psychology, widespread and inadvertent promotion of a 'cargo-cult' mentality, leading to inappropriate and failed change, and Religious Wars" [BT — "cargo-cult" is a borrowed term, used here in the management-failure sense].

The Primer positions OT as an alternative path: "Org Topologies offers an alternative: strategic org design, guided by a path that connects business objectives, organizational goals, topologies, and, finally, frameworks. Following the Org Topologies approach will ensure: Strategy chosen to influence the organization's business objectives. Organizational goals and capabilities chosen to be fit for purpose for the strategy and objectives. Org Topologies—including but not limited to Resource, Delivery, or Adaptive—serve as a reference for assessing and designing a new target structure supporting the org design goals. And while the Elevating Katas provide one set of guides to get change going, of course people will also get guidance from existing frameworks, while aiming for the target topology" [V] (p. 22, "Strategic Org Design vs. Framework Thinking").

The Primer names frameworks that can be mapped onto the OT grid as illustrative examples: "SAFe; Team Topologies; Large-Scale Scrum (LeSS); FAST Agile; Spotify Model / McKinsey Product Operating model; Haier's RDHY; Bayer's DSO; and many more" [V] (p. 22, "Strategic Org Design vs. Framework Thinking") [BT]. Two specific framework mappings are illustrated on p. 22:

- *SAFe.* "The Scale Agile Framework (SAFe) is a framework used in software development. Saying 'We are doing SAFe' actually communicates little about what's really happening. But observe the OT map for SAFe; anyone can easily see that SAFe is mostly implemented with interdependent incomplete teams working on components at the Tasks level, and fast-flow teams at the Capabilities level" [V] (p. 22, "Mapping SAFe") [BT].
- *Rendanheyi.* "The Haier Rendanheyi model (RDHY) is an innovative management philosophy. It represents a radical departure from traditional hierarchical structures by aiming to align every employee's efforts directly with customer (or user) value creation. The OT map demonstrates how RDHY elevates its thousands of micro-enterprises (MEs) that directly interact with users, and optimizing for autonomy and customer-focused value creation" [V] (p. 22, "Mapping RDHY") [BT].

## Part VIII: Co-creation as the framework's reflexive principle

On the closing page, the authors offer a reflexive note about the Primer itself and the map's geometry: "This Primer is a product of co-creation. None of us would have been able to create it alone with such sharpness. Co-creation is a thing. And that's very much relevant to the map itself. As a team moves rightward on the map, its co-creation power increases. It's not just that the unit minimizes its inter-skill dependencies, but it elevates its intelligence. A team can co-create things that none of the individuals alone could. A co-creating end-to-end team is not just about speed and flow. The same also applies vertically. Co-creation can happen at the level of tasks, capabilities, or business. That altitude magnifies the power of co-creation" [V] (p. 27, "Closing note") [AR].

The closing line: "We hope you find value in this work to get change going!" [V] (p. 27, "Closing note"), signed by the three authors.

## Positions the author explicitly frames against

- **Framework thinking and canned-solution consulting.** The authors directly contrast OT with the consulting-firm pitch of "implementing an industry-standard management framework" as key to success; they characterise this as "the pitch from consulting companies big and small" and warn that frameworks "applied without understanding the implications" yield root-cause neglect, lack of ownership, "cargo-cult" mentality, and "Religious Wars" [V] (p. 22, "Strategic Org Design vs. Framework Thinking"). The Primer also notes the market "is full of canned change 'solutions'" and that OT instead "offers a custom path by inviting you and your people to create your change, not consuming common yet often mediocre ideas" [V] (p. 3, "Management Summary").
- **Strategy-execution split (senior management defining objectives only).** The authors frame against "the idea of senior management only defining business objectives" and assert that in strategic org design "leaders understand and design the details of their internal organization to be fit for purpose. They align the external (business objective) and internal (org design)" [V] (p. 10, "Strategic Org Design").
- **Resource-utilization framing as default org goal.** Where Resource Topology applies it is named as fit-for-purpose; the authors frame against treating it as the default. "The Directing Archetypes in the upper left quadrant treat the Doing Archetypes as 'resources,' matching skill demand with skill supply" [V] (p. 11, "Resource Topology") — the Primer's discussion clarifies this is one of three topologies, not the universal answer, and that learning in Resource Topology is "largely confined to improving existing skills rather than discovering new ones" [V] (p. 11, "Resource Topology").
- **Output-maximisation without outcome.** "More output usually means more cost or investment, which can lower profit (an outcome). Investing to increase outcomes (healthy people, profit) is justified; 'investing' to increase output is suspect" [V] (p. 8, "Output versus Outcome Archetypes"). The Primer also names "feature bloat" as a common consequence of Delivery-Topology output focus [V] (p. 12, "Delivery Topology").
- **Local optimisation of org-design elements in isolation.** "If each element (e.g. policies, structures) is optimized in isolation, the performance of the whole system may actually decline for the desired goal (flow can get worse!)" [V] (p. 16, "MADE Real: (1) MAP").
- **Single-team focus as a sufficient lens for org change.** "Many change initiatives focus too narrowly on individual teams while missing the bigger picture. A single high-performing team can't deliver its full potential if surrounding structures and processes remain unchanged. True organizational impact requires alignment across all levels—from the organization's strategic decisions to HR and Finance policies, from group structures to how teams collaborate daily" [V] (p. 4, "The Hidden Complexities of Org Change").
- **"We don't have enough experts" as a constraint on org design.** With AI: "shed the old assumptions, 'We don't have enough experts, therefore…'. Spin up another 10,000 agents" [V] (p. 20, "Strategic AI Adoption").

## What this Primer is and isn't

The Primer is "a 25-page Primer on Org Topologies" [V] (p. 1, "Cover") — explicitly a forerunner to a forthcoming book ("A man walks into a bar... > No, we'll save that for the book we're writing", p. 1, "Cover"). Several topics are named and deferred: the full Elevating Katas catalogue ("This work will be ready with the release of the Org Topologies book", p. 19); the handling of conflicting org goals ("Is some goal primary over others? Do they conflict? Can the org design support them all? These issues are beyond the scope of this Primer", p. 18). The Primer's purpose is to introduce the map, the four-step method, and the strategic-design stance — not to provide the full operational catalogue.

The 2025 Edition is explicitly an update over a previous version: "The updated Org Topologies map—represented and analyzed on the next page in detail—aims to enhance clarity, broaden applicability from product development to service organizations, and support modern organizational needs such as the adoption of AI. The terminology has been simplified, the visual design refined, and new archetypes for scalable capabilities have been introduced. The new version enables the support for multiple organizational optimizing goals (e.g. speed, adaptability, or innovation), providing a more versatile framework for strategic org design. It is also backward compatible with the previous version. One important change is the license. The Org Topologies Primer 2025 is made available under Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike 4.0 International also known as CC BY-NC-SA 4.0" [V] (p. 4, "What's new in the 2025 Edition").
