# Approach Perfect Field Guide to Scrum Events, AAR Distillation

**Source:** Chris Gagné (2020). *The Approach Perfect Field Guide to Scrum Events*. April 3, 2020 edition. Approach Perfect, Limited. CC BY 4.0 (author-relicensed for this corpus). Scope: open.

## AAR Relevance

This source is a Sprint Retrospective guide first; its AAR projection is by adaptation rather than by design. The load-bearing transfer points are Norm Kerth's Retrospective Prime Directive (the only verbatim blameless-by-default commitment the demo corpus carries from the retro-methodology tradition), the Vegas / Chatham House confidentiality rules (the mechanism for keeping AAR disclosures safe), 5-Whys with *surprise as the signal* (a discipline that maps directly to the AAR's contributory-factor phase), the one-or-two-improvement discipline (the AAR's action-design constraint), and the decisions-vs-actions split (which maps cleanly onto the AAR distinction between policy changes and tracked actions). The source also carries a quiet action-loop-closure pattern: the retro-close reads back commitments, names owners, names when each lands — exactly what Phase 5 of the AAR requires.

## Key Concepts for AAR

1. **Retrospective Prime Directive — verbatim.** "Regardless of what we discover, we understand and truly believe that everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time, their skills and abilities, the resources available, and the situation at hand." (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide*, Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda") Read at the opening of every AAR; makes local rationality the default frame before any contributory-factor work begins.

2. **Vegas Rule and Chatham House Rule as explicit confidentiality options.** The source names both: "Whatever happens in Vegas, stays in Vegas" (unanimous explicit consent before sharing) and "participants are free to use the information received, but neither the identity nor the affiliation of the speaker(s)... may be revealed." (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide*, Sprint Retrospective, "Tips") The AAR facilitator picks one explicitly at Phase 0 scoping.

3. **5-Whys with surprise as the signal.** "If you're rarely surprised by your root causes, you may not be digging deep enough." (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide*, Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda") Surprise is a diagnostic indicator that the analysis has reached a layer that wasn't already assumed. Applies in AAR Phase 2 contributory-factor work.

4. **One or two improvement items, not a list.** "It's better to have one or two improvement items for the next Sprint than to have too much to change at once." (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide*, Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda") The same discipline applies at AAR Phase 4: a long action list is a commitment that will not be honoured.

5. **Decisions vs actions split.** Improvements fall into two buckets: decisions (changes to Definition of Done, Definition of Ready, Working Agreement) and actions (new Backlog Items). (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide*, Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda") In AAR terms: policy or SOP changes are decisions; owned tasks with named dates are actions. The mechanisms are different.

6. **Capacity-adjusted velocity — the honesty discipline for commitments.** Forecasting uses "the average of the last 3–4 sprint's Capacity-adjusted Velocity adjusted for current and forecasted Capacity." (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide*, Sprint Planning, "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda") Translated to AAR action design: only commit to actions the team has actual capacity for, adjusted for what is already in flight.

7. **Don't demonstrate undone work.** "Work in process is a liability, not an asset." (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide*, Sprint Review, "Tips") In AAR context: don't present actions as complete when they are not. Incomplete work that is declared done in a follow-up review erodes the learning loop.

8. **Sprint Review is a working session, not a presentation.** "Less mature teams try to complete this in 30 minutes, but we've found that this becomes a rapid-fire, one-sided demo." (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide*, The Sprint, "Sample Sprint Agenda (Two-Week Sprint)") Translates to AAR Phase 1 and Phase 2: conversation that produces shared understanding, not a slide deck presenting one account.

9. **Retro close — read back commitments with names and dates.** The wrap-up segment reads back commitments, names owners, and names when each lands. (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide*, Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda") The AAR's Phase 5 learning-loop closure requires exactly this discipline.

10. **Strict participant-set control.** "No other may attend unless specifically invited to do so by the entire Scrum Team." (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide*, Sprint Retrospective, "Participants") In AAR context: the participant list is set in advance at Phase 0 scoping; uninvited participants change the psychological container.

11. **Manager-speaks-last convention.** Non-team participants "must understand that this is not their event and they may not interfere with the team's process, only support the team." (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide*, Sprint Planning, "Participants") In AAR context: rank should not pre-empt perspective; whoever holds the local account should speak before the person with authority over the outcome.

12. **Estimating defects — full-coverage discipline for commitments.** "Estimate all Backlog Items, even defects." (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide*, Backlog Refinement, "Tips") In AAR context: account for the actual cost of the incident, including the invisible maintenance tail, not just the acute event.

## Questions to Ask During AAR

### Phase 0 — Scoping

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Setting the confidentiality container | Will we use Vegas Rule (nothing shared without unanimous consent) or Chatham House Rule (information flows, identities stay in the room)? Pick one explicitly before the Prime Directive. |
| Setting the participant list | Who must be in the room for the account to be complete? Anyone whose attendance changes what people say should be named before the invite goes out. |

### Phase 1 — Timeline and local-rationality reconstruction

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Opening the review | Has the Prime Directive been read aloud? ("Regardless of what we discover, we understand and truly believe that everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time...") |
| Preventing rank from closing down accounts | Who in the room holds authority over the outcome being reviewed? Are they speaking last, not first? |

### Phase 2 — Contributory-factor analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Assessing 5-Whys depth | Are you surprised by what you're finding? If not, keep digging — surprise is the signal you've reached a layer that wasn't already assumed. |
| Preventing single-cause collapse | Have the contributing factors been separated from the one that was easiest to name first? |

### Phase 4 — Action design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Keeping the action list honest | Are we naming one or two actions the team can actually own, or are we listing seven and hoping? |
| Distinguishing policy from task | Does this improvement change how we agree to work (decision) or does it create a new piece of tracked work (action)? Different mechanisms own different commitments. |
| Capacity check | Given what the team already has in flight, is there real capacity for this action in the stated timeframe? |

### Phase 5 — Learning-loop closure

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Closing the loop | Has each action been read back with a named owner and a named date? Has the distinction between decisions and actions been preserved? |
| Preventing undone-work drift | Is there any action from a prior AAR that was declared done but wasn't fully complete? Name it now rather than carrying the illusion forward. |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| The group names a root cause without expressing any surprise at it | The 5-Whys analysis stopped at the assumed layer; the actual contributing factor lives deeper | Ask: "Are you surprised by this? If not, ask why once more." |
| The action list grows past two items before anyone objects | The batch-size discipline has failed; the group is confusing volume with thoroughness | Read back the list and ask which two the team would still be doing in four weeks |
| A senior voice speaks first in timeline reconstruction | Rank has anchored the account before all perspectives are on the table | Pause and explicitly invite accounts from those who have not spoken |
| An action is described as belonging to "the team" | No individual owns it; it will not happen | Require a named individual and a named date before moving on |
| Follow-up review reports a prior action as done without evidence | Undone work is being presented as complete, eroding the learning loop | Apply the "work in process is a liability" test: what would it mean for this to actually be done? |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this source when:
- The AAR opener needs a verbatim, blameless-by-default commitment the group can read aloud and own — the Prime Directive is the only such verbatim text in the demo corpus.
- The facilitator needs explicit confidentiality language to choose between before the session opens.
- Phase 2 analysis keeps landing on the same obvious factor without going deeper — the surprise-as-signal heuristic is the prompt.
- Phase 4 action design is producing a list longer than two items and no one is pushing back.
- Phase 5 needs a close discipline: reading back commitments with names and dates.

Do not reach for this source when the incident is large enough to warrant a formal learning review (LFUO's tool ladder applies), or when the contributory-factor analysis requires systems-mapping apparatus (Barbrook-Johnson) or just-culture sorting (NHS).

## Worked Example

A software team has completed an incident review for a deployment failure. The Phase 4 action list contains six items, none with named owners. The facilitator applies the one-or-two discipline from the Field Guide: "Let's name the two actions this team will still be doing in four weeks. Which two?" (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide*, Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda".) The group reduces to two. The facilitator then applies the decisions-vs-actions split: one item is a change to the deployment checklist (a policy decision, owned by the team lead and updated before the next sprint); the other is a new monitoring alert (a tracked action, owned by a named engineer, due in five days). The close reads both back aloud with owners and dates. At the next review, the facilitator opens by asking whether the checklist change and the monitoring alert are actually done — not declared done, but evidenced as done. The "work in process is a liability" frame (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide*, Sprint Review, "Tips") prevents the team from treating the declaration as sufficient.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Opening an AAR without reading the Prime Directive, then finding that blame language fills the vacuum.
- Leaving confidentiality implicit and discovering after the fact that accounts shared outside the room created harm.
- Stopping 5-Whys analysis at the first plausible cause because the group wasn't surprised by it.
- Producing seven actions and following through on none because the list exceeded real capacity.
- Conflating policy changes with tracked tasks, then finding that neither mechanism owned the commitment.
- Allowing the most senior person in the room to speak first and anchor the account before others share their perspective.
- Presenting progress updates on prior AAR actions without checking whether the declared-complete work was actually done.
- Running the AAR as a presentation of one account rather than as a conversation that builds a shared account.

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The Prime Directive is borrowed-through from Norm Kerth's *Project Retrospectives: A Handbook for Team Reviews* (named explicitly in the source's Tips section), which the demo corpus does not hold directly. The retrospective sample agenda structure draws from Esther Derby and Diana Larsen's *Agile Retrospectives: Making Good Teams Great* (named explicitly), also not held directly. **Named limits of the source.** The source explicitly limits itself to "the 'bare minimum' instruction required to successfully bootstrap the five standard Scrum Events and Backlog Refinement." It does not address incident review, just-culture sorting, or contributory-factor analysis. Its retrospective guidance targets iterative software teams; adaptations to cross-functional, non-software, or one-off AAR contexts are operator-applied, not source-endorsed.
