# Barbrook-Johnson & Penn, Systems Mapping, AAR Distillation

**Source:** Barbrook-Johnson, P., & Penn, A. S. (2022). *Systems Mapping: How to build and use causal models of systems*. Palgrave Macmillan / Springer Nature, Cham. ISBN 978-3-031-01833-6. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-031-01919-7. Licence: **CC BY 4.0**. Scope: open. Seven methods covered: Rich Pictures, Theory of Change, Causal Loop Diagrams, Participatory Systems Mapping, Fuzzy Cognitive Mapping, Bayesian Belief Networks, System Dynamics.

## AAR Relevance

Barbrook-Johnson & Penn's seven-method catalogue projects onto the AAR axis at the contributory-factor and scoping phases. The book's appropriateness-triangle frame — triangulating between project purpose, system characteristics, and method properties — gives the AAR facilitator a principled basis for choosing which systems-mapping tool fits the situation. Three methods carry the most load for typical AAR work: Causal Loop Diagrams as a shared-model artefact for the contributory-factor conversation (Phase 2), Theory of Change for tracing intervention pathways from actions to outcomes (Phase 4), and Rich Pictures for fast collective sense-making with low methodology overhead when the group needs to externalise competing pictures of the situation without committing to formal structure (Phase 1). Participatory Systems Mapping's submap-based analysis technique is useful when the AAR's causal map is large and the group needs to extract targeted leverage-point submaps rather than manage the full complexity at once. The book's consistent, load-bearing warning — that the process of building the map is often more valuable than the map itself — is directly applicable to the AAR: the value of drawing a CLD together is the conversation it forces, not the diagram it produces.

## Key Concepts for AAR

1. <!-- concept: appropriateness-triangle --> **Appropriateness triangle for method selection.** Method selection is a three-way triangulation between the purpose and constraints of the project, the characteristics of the system, and the nature of the methods. "Where a method fits with both the project and the system, we can say it is an 'appropriate' method." (Source: Barbrook-Johnson & Penn, *Systems Mapping*, Ch 11, "Comparing Systems Mapping Methods") The AAR facilitator who defaults to CLDs for every incident is applying Maslow's hammer, not appropriateness reasoning.

2. <!-- concept: rich-pictures --> **Rich Pictures as low-barrier collective externalisation.** "Rich Pictures are a drawing, a picture, of a system or 'situation'. Very few, if any, prompts are given by facilitators beyond asking them to 'draw the system.'" (Source: Ch 2, "What Are Rich Pictures?") Useful in Phase 1 when the group is not yet ready to negotiate causal links.

3. <!-- concept: theory-of-change --> **Theory of Change as intervention-pathway map.** ToC maps a causal chain from inputs through activities, outputs, outcomes, and impacts. For AAR Phase 4 action design, a lightweight ToC trace — *this action → these outputs → these outcomes → this impact* — forces the group to state the causal theory of their recommended action explicitly, surfacing assumptions. (Source: Ch 3, "What Is Theory of Change Mapping?")

4. <!-- concept: toc-as-theory --> **ToC diagrams are theories, not maps of reality.** "Though the clue is in the name, it is important to keep in mind that ToCs are theories, they represent the mental models of the people who constructed them. They are not maps of reality and they may contain gaps and ambiguities." (Source: Ch 3, "What Is Theory of Change Mapping?") An AAR action that cannot be traced through a ToC, however rough, lacks a stated causal theory.

5. <!-- concept: causal-loop-diagrams --> **Causal Loop Diagrams as shared-model artefacts.** "CLDs represent a system in three basic elements: boxes, connections, and feedback loops." (Source: Ch 4, "What Is a Causal Loop Diagram?") Building a CLD together in Phase 2 converts divergent participant accounts into a negotiated structural hypothesis. The core system engine — the small set of central variables whose relationships drive the system's behaviour — is where contributory-factor analysis should focus first.

6. <!-- concept: cld-qualitative-quantitative --> **CLDs hint at dynamics without quantifying them.** CLDs sit "roughly in the middle of the qualitative-quantitative spectrum of systems mapping methods, perhaps more towards the qualitative side, but hints at dynamics in a system." (Source: Ch 4, "Causal Loop Diagrams") This makes them appropriate for most AAR contributory-factor conversations: structural enough to be shared, qualitative enough to be built from participant accounts without demanding data.

7. <!-- concept: system-archetypes --> **System archetypes as CLD starting points.** The eight archetypes (Kim 2000, borrowed-through) "outline common and intuitive types of core system engines." (Source: Ch 4, "How Do You Create Causal Loop Diagrams?") In an AAR, an archetype can serve as a seed structure for the collaborative CLD — a hypothesis about the system's engine to refine against what participants actually know, not a label to confirm.

8. <!-- concept: horrendogram --> **The horrendogram failure mode.** "System maps are sometimes referred to as 'horrendograms', and much worse (!), when they show us the complexity of a system in an unfiltered manner." (Source: Ch 1, "How Can Systems Mapping Be Useful?"; Ch 4, "Causal Loop Diagrams") A CLD that captures everything and explains nothing is an AAR failure mode. The countermeasure: tune complexity to the audience's visual literacy.

9. <!-- concept: participatory-systems-mapping --> **Participatory Systems Mapping and submap extraction.** "At the core of the approach to the analysis is the idea of creating submaps... which we can use to focus in on particular questions or issues." (Source: Ch 5, "What Is Participatory Systems Mapping?") When an AAR's causal map grows large, submap techniques — one to three steps upstream or downstream from a factor of interest — extract targeted, actionable views without discarding the broader map.

10. <!-- concept: process-value-over-product --> **Process value over product.** "The opportunity to reflect and generate understanding with others is surprisingly powerful. Often more so than delivering 'answers'." (Source: Ch 12, "Our Final Take-Home Messages") The CLD or Rich Picture built in an AAR is a living document; its primary value is the conversation it generates.

11. <!-- concept: boundary-objects --> **Maps as boundary objects.** Participatory maps become "boundary objects around which stakeholders and researchers can learn." (Source: Ch 9, "Defending the Use of a Participatory Process to Build and Use Your Map") The map externalises the working theory of the event so the group can argue with the map rather than with each other.

12. <!-- concept: broken-ankle-test --> **The appropriateness gate (the broken-ankle test equivalent).** The book's consistent stance is that systems-mapping methods are useful "gateway tools" for complex causal questions, not universal tools. (Source: Ch 1, "How Can Systems Mapping Be Useful?") When the incident is simple and the fix is obvious, the overhead of formal mapping is not warranted.

## Questions to Ask During AAR

### Phase 1: Timeline and local-rationality reconstruction

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| The group has competing pictures of what happened and can't yet agree on a timeline | Ask each subgroup to draw their picture of the situation — no formal structure required. What does each picture emphasise that others leave out? (Rich Pictures, Ch 2, "What Are Rich Pictures?") |
| The facilitator needs a fast, low-overhead method for surfacing multiple perspectives | Rich Pictures: "draw the system" with minimal prompts. Small groups of three to six; thirty minutes minimum. (Ch 2, "How Do You Do Rich Pictures?") |
| The group is ready to negotiate a shared causal picture | Introduce a CLD. Start with the core system engine — the two or three feedback loops at the centre of the event — before expanding outward. Keep the first draft to twenty nodes. (Ch 4, "How Do You Create Causal Loop Diagrams?") |

### Phase 2: Contributory-factor analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| The group has agreed on a timeline and wants to build a shared causal model | Build a CLD together. Treat it as a hypothesis. Which variables can go up or down? Which causal links go in both directions? What feedback loops does the structure contain? (Ch 4, "How Do You Create Causal Loop Diagrams?") |
| The map is growing large and the group is becoming overwhelmed | Extract a submap: choose one factor and map one to two steps upstream and downstream. What drove this factor, and what did it drive? (Ch 5, "What Is Participatory Systems Mapping?") |
| The group suspects a recurring structural pattern | Use an archetype as a seed. Which system engine fits as a hypothesis? Build the CLD from the archetype template and test each link against the incident's specifics. (Ch 4, "How Do You Create Causal Loop Diagrams?") |
| The group wants to identify the highest-leverage points in the causal structure | Run a submap analysis: which factors have the most connections? Which sit on the path between the most consequential inputs and outcomes? (Ch 5, "What Is Participatory Systems Mapping?") |
| The causal map is contested and participants are defending their positions | The map is a boundary object. Redirect disagreement from participants to the map: *the map says X causes Y — does your experience confirm or contradict that link?* (Ch 9, "Defending the Use of a Participatory Process to Build and Use Your Map") |

### Phase 4: Action design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| The group is proposing an action and needs to state its theory of change | Trace the ToC: what is the action (input/activity), what does it produce (output), what does it change in the system (outcome), and what is the long-term effect the group intends (impact)? Surface the assumptions embedded in each causal link. (Ch 3, "How Do You Create Theory of Change Diagrams?") |
| The proposed action targets a symptom visible in the CLD | Does the action address a feedback loop or an event? Which loop will it weaken — and which loops will compensate? (Ch 4, "What Is a Causal Loop Diagram?") |
| The group wants to prioritise actions among several candidates | Use the submap from Phase 2: which actions address factors with high centrality? Favour actions that intervene on the core system engine over those that address peripheral nodes. (Ch 5, "What Is Participatory Systems Mapping?") |

### Phase 5: Learning-loop closure

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| The AAR is wrapping up and the group wants to capture the causal model | The CLD is a living document. Photograph and digitise it. Name the key loops and archetypes that characterised this event. Return to it at the next AAR on a similar incident to ask whether the structure has changed. (Ch 12, "Our Final Take-Home Messages") |
| The same action has been proposed after prior incidents | Was the prior action addressing the event or the system? If the system structure did not change, the same event will recur. What would addressing the core feedback loop require? (Ch 4, "Common Issues and 'Tricks of the Trade'") |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| The CLD grows to thirty-plus nodes and the group cannot name the core engine | Horrendogram failure mode — complexity has exceeded communicability | Extract a submap around the two or three most consequential factors; present that as the working model |
| An archetype is named ("this is a shifting-the-burden pattern") before anyone tests its links against the incident | Archetype as label, not hypothesis | Ask: which specific link in that archetype does the incident's evidence support? Test each link before accepting the structure |
| The action has no ToC trace — no stated mechanism from action to outcome | The causal theory of the action is implicit and untested | Run a quick ToC: if we do this, what changes, and how does that change produce the outcome we want? |
| Participants are arguing about the map rather than about each other | The boundary-object function is working | Hold it; the argument about the map is the productive form of disagreement here |
| The group skips Rich Pictures and goes straight to a formal CLD when accounts are still competing | Premature closure on causal structure | Restart with Rich Pictures to surface the competing pictures before negotiating causal links |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this source when:
- Phase 2 needs a structured method for building a shared causal model from competing accounts, not just a list of contributory factors.
- The incident is recurring and the group suspects a structural feedback pattern rather than a one-off event.
- Phase 4 action design needs to state its causal theory explicitly, not just name the action.
- The group has a large causal map and needs submap discipline to extract actionable focus.

Do not reach for this source when the incident is simple and the fix is obvious (the broken-ankle test applies — formal mapping is not warranted). Use SSDL Systems Thinking Foundations alongside this source when the group needs system-archetype vocabulary to name recurring structures.

## Worked Example

A cross-functional team is reviewing a series of late deliveries. The timeline (Phase 1) produces three competing pictures of the event: the engineering lead's picture shows resource constraints; the product manager's picture shows unclear priorities; the customer success lead's picture shows shifting scope. Rather than arbitrating, the facilitator runs a Rich Picture round — each person draws their picture of the situation with no formal structure required (Source: Barbrook-Johnson & Penn, *Systems Mapping*, Ch 2, "What Are Rich Pictures?"). The three pictures surface that all three are true simultaneously and are connected. In Phase 2, the group builds a CLD starting with the core engine: competing priorities drive scope change, which drives resource reallocation, which drives delivery delay, which drives urgency, which drives scope change again (reinforcing loop). The facilitator applies the submap technique (Ch 5, "What Is Participatory Systems Mapping?") to extract the two highest-centrality factors — scope-change rate and resource-reallocation latency — as the leverage points. In Phase 4, the group traces a ToC for the proposed action (a weekly priority lock): action → locked scope for two-week cycles → reduced reallocation frequency → reduced delivery variance → improved forecast reliability (Ch 3, "How Do You Create Theory of Change Diagrams?"). Assumptions are named at each link. The CLD is photographed and retained for the next AAR on a similar pattern.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Defaulting to CLDs for every AAR without applying the appropriateness triangle to decide whether the complexity of the incident warrants formal causal mapping.
- Building a map that captures everything and explains nothing (the horrendogram): overwhelming the group with complexity rather than focusing on the core system engine.
- Treating the CLD or Rich Picture as a product to be delivered rather than a process that generates shared understanding through the conversation of building it.
- Skipping the Rich Picture phase and going straight to a formal causal map when participants hold strongly competing views of the situation that have not yet been surfaced.
- Presenting the map as a definitive causal account rather than as a boundary object — a shared working theory that participants can critique and improve.
- Building a Theory of Change for proposed actions without surfacing the assumptions embedded in each causal link.
- Applying a system archetype as a label to the incident without testing each link in the archetype template against what participants actually know.
- Ignoring the facilitator's own method bias: choosing CLDs because they are familiar rather than because they are appropriate.
- Treating the map as finished when the workshop ends rather than as a living document to be updated as more information comes in.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The system archetypes (Kim 2000, *Systems Archetypes I*) are cited borrowed-through in the source; the deep ref records Kim 2000 as `[BT]` (Ch 4, "How Do You Create Causal Loop Diagrams?"). Kim is not held directly in this corpus. The boundary-objects framing (Star & Griesemer 1989) is cited borrowed-through in the source's Ch 9; Star & Griesemer is not held directly. Meadows 2008 (*Thinking in Systems*) is cited borrowed-through for the system definition; Meadows is not held directly in the demo corpus. The Rogers 2014 "results chain" framing for ToC categories is borrowed-through. The Barbrook-Johnson & Penn text itself is a secondary-source catalogue; its own borrowed-through chain is extensive and is recorded in the deep ref.

**Named limits of the source.** The authors explicitly exclude from scope: non-causal approaches (mind mapping, stakeholder mapping, giga-mapping, social network analysis, path analysis, viable systems model), and broader schools of practice (participatory action research, design thinking, permaculture). The criterion for inclusion is "methods which represent causality and influence in a system, and methods which can be used in a participatory way." The book does not address incident review methodology, just-culture sorting, or after-action protocol design; those functions are carried by LFUO, NHS, and TC 25-20 in this corpus.
