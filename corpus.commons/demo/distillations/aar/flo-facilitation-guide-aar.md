# FLO Facilitation Guide, AAR Distillation

**Source:** Bennett, G., Cougler Blom, B., Riessner, S., & Currie, S. (2019). *FLO Facilitation Guide*. Victoria, B.C.: BCcampus. Ebook ISBN 978-1-77420-036-0. Licence: **CC BY 4.0**. Scope: open.

## AAR Relevance

The FLO Facilitation Guide is an online facilitation craft text — and most AARs in this audience's world are virtual or hybrid. Its load-bearing contributions to AAR work are: the facilitative-vs-transmissive question frame (is the learning inside the content being delivered, or inside the conversation the participants are having?); the named facilitator-anxiety pattern (*Anxious Annie*) that keeps a facilitator productive when participants are slow to engage; Vegas-rules privacy as a trust foundation; engagement-equity tracking so no participant is consistently sidelined; the scaffolding-and-fading arc (high facilitator presence early, lighter as participants own the work); and modelling-as-through-line (the facilitator's authentic engagement with the practice is part of the curriculum). For a cross-functional AAR where participants have different seniority and are convened once rather than in an ongoing cohort, these principles translate directly: the facilitator builds the climate, asks rather than tells, tracks whose voice is missing, and names their own process out loud so participants can see and trust it.

## Key Concepts for AAR

1. <!-- concept: facilitative-vs-transmissive --> **Facilitative vs transmissive question framing.** A transmissive question delivers information through the question ("don't you think we should have escalated earlier?"); a facilitative question opens space ("what was your read of the situation at that point?"). The FLO design is built on this distinction. (Source: Bennett et al., *FLO Facilitation Guide*, Ch 1, "What is facilitation in an educational context?") For AAR work it maps directly onto TC 25-20's open-ended question discipline and LFUO's deflection-question protocol.

2. <!-- concept: sage-on-the-stage --> **The sage-on-the-stage refusal.** The Guide frames the facilitator's job as helping participants "understand course content through questioning and suggestions" rather than acting as the "'sage on the stage'" [V] (Source: Ch 1). In AAR terms: the learning is in the participants' accounts, not in the facilitator's analysis. The facilitator surfaces, not interprets.

3. <!-- concept: facilitator-anxiety --> **Anxious Annie — name the worry, trust the process.** Leonne Beebe's reflective post names the pattern: facilitator anxiety shows up when participants are slow to post, slow to engage, not on the timeline expected. The Guide's discipline: "remember facilitation is not about me; it's about helping the participants as they need and ask for help. There is a saying that RRU folks often refer to called 'Trust the process.'" [V] [AE] (Source: Ch 4, "Example of reflective post") For AARs: the facilitator who is anxious about silence or slow timelines can mistake participant processing for disengagement and rush in.

4. <!-- concept: confidentiality-rules --> **Vegas rules as a trust foundation.** "You may wish to establish 'Vegas rules,' i.e. what happens in the course, stays in the course" [V] (Source: Ch 4, "Privacy and Confidentiality") Privacy rules are not legal compliance; they are the foundation of honest exchange. For AARs this aligns with the LFUO administrative-firewall framing: explicit confidentiality commitment before the session opens.

5. <!-- concept: scaffolding-and-fading --> **Scaffolding-and-fading arc — calibrate presence to phase.** In early phases, facilitator presence is high: welcoming, clarifying, troubleshooting. As participants own the conversation, the facilitator "steps back and encourages learning… they participate in discussions but don't lead" [V] (Source: Ch 3, "Roles and functions of FLO facilitators") For an AAR: Phase 0 (scoping) and Phase 1 (timeline construction) demand active facilitative presence; Phase 2–3 (contributory factors, just-culture) should be participant-led with the facilitator weaving and summarising.

6. <!-- concept: engagement-equity-tracking --> **Engagement-equity tracking matrix.** A simple spreadsheet — participant names in the first column, marks for each interaction — distributes the facilitator's responses fairly. (Source: Ch 6, "One idea to help you track interactions") For AARs in hybrid or video settings, this is the practical tool for noticing who has not spoken in thirty minutes.

7. <!-- concept: recording-consent --> **Recording as consent, not default.** "If recording synchronous sessions, confirm OK with session participants before hitting record" [V] (Source: Ch 4, "Privacy and Confidentiality") For AARs: recording may chill candour. Explicit consent before recording; default is no recording unless all participants agree.

8. <!-- concept: modelling-as-through-line --> **Modelling-as-through-line.** FLO facilitators "model positive professional presence" and complete the FLIF reflection alongside participants. (Source: Ch 3; Ch 4, "The FLIF Facilitation Process") For AARs: the facilitator models local-rationality framing by applying it to their own moves out loud ("I'm going to ask the question this way because I want to hear your account, not test your decision").

9. <!-- concept: co-facilitation --> **Co-facilitation on complementarity criteria.** Robin Leung: "I had a more technical background and my co-facilitator had more of the facilitation background… we complemented one another very well" [V] [AE] (Source: Ch 7, "Two heads are better than one") For complex AARs: skill-mix and role-coverage matter more than seniority when selecting a co-facilitator pair.

10. <!-- concept: no-pressuring-tone --> **No-pressuring-tone discipline.** "Avoid pressuring participants to comply with course deadlines, or suggesting they are only welcome if they are committed" [V] (Source: Ch 7, "Write a welcome post") For AARs: the facilitator must not pressure participants toward conclusions or imply that candid accounts are unwelcome.

11. <!-- concept: universal-design-for-learning --> **UDL multiple-means accessibility lens.** The Guide asks facilitators: "Do I use facilitation strategies that may exclude some learners or present barriers to participation?" [V] (Source: Ch 3, "Accessible design, inclusive facilitation") For virtual AARs: written-first input, captioning for synchronous sessions, and camera-off options for low-bandwidth participants all remove barriers to full contribution.

12. <!-- concept: resist-thoroughness --> **Resist-thoroughness in feedback.** "Resist the urge to be thorough, but be clear that you're only commenting on one or two aspects of the artifact" [V] (Source: Ch 7, "Monitor the sharing of drafts and prototypes") For AARs: the facilitator who tries to address every issue exhausts the room. Pick the load-bearing two.

## Questions to Ask During AAR

### Phase 0 — Scoping

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Setting the climate and confidentiality frame | "Before we start, I want to name the rules for this conversation — what's said here stays here, unless we agree otherwise together. Does everyone confirm?" |
| Surfacing a participant who is worried about speaking freely | "Is there anything that would make it hard for you to speak openly today? Let's name it now rather than after." |

### Phase 1 — Timeline and Local-Rationality Reconstruction

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Opening a participant's account without leading | "Walk me through what you were seeing and thinking at that point — not what you know now, but what you had in front of you then." |
| Equity: a participant has not spoken in 20 minutes | "I want to hear from someone we haven't heard from yet. [Name], what's your account of that moment?" |
| Facilitator feels urgency to fill silence | (Internal check): Trust the process. The participant is processing. Wait ten seconds before prompting. |

### Phase 2 — Contributory-Factor Analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Opening multi-factor framing | "What else was in play that made this a hard situation to navigate — not just what happened, but what the conditions were?" |
| Surfacing a voice that hasn't offered a factor | "We haven't heard from [Name] on this yet — what's your read on what was contributing?" |

### Phase 3 — Just-Culture Sorting

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| The group is rushing to a verdict | "Let's slow down here. Before we reach a conclusion, I want to make sure everyone's account is on the table. What are we missing?" |

### Phase 4 — Action Design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Checking that an action is owned, not orphaned | "Who specifically is taking this on, and by when? We need a name and a date before we move on." |

### Phase 5 — Learning-Loop Closure

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Closing with a modelling move | "I'll share one thing I noticed in how I facilitated today that I'd do differently next time — then I'd like to hear one thing from each of you about what you're taking away." |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| The facilitator speaks more than any participant during Phase 2 | Sage-on-the-stage failure — the facilitator is filling the space rather than holding it | Step back; ask one open question and wait for the room to fill it |
| One or two participants have not spoken in more than twenty minutes | Equity gap — the engagement-equity tracker has not been applied | Name the person by name with a direct, non-pressuring question |
| The facilitator rushes to fill silence after asking a difficult question | Anxious Annie pattern — facilitator anxiety is overriding participant processing | Trust the process; hold silence for ten seconds before any follow-up |
| Recording is started without a verbal consent check | Privacy consent skipped — potential chilling effect on candour for the rest of the session | Pause, stop recording, ask explicitly, restart only with unanimous agreement |
| A co-facilitator pair duplicates skills rather than complements them | Skill-mix mismatch — the pair covers one dimension well and another poorly | In future scoping, name the complementarity criterion (domain knowledge vs facilitation craft) before selecting the pair |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this source when:
- The AAR is virtual or hybrid and the facilitator needs practical discipline for managing equity, consent, and engagement across distributed participants.
- The facilitator is anxious about silence or slow engagement and needs a named frame for resisting the urgency to intervene.
- A co-facilitator is being selected and the complementarity-criteria question hasn't been named.
- The facilitator wants a modelling close — ending by sharing their own reflection before asking participants to share theirs.

Do not reach for this source as the primary AAR protocol. TC 25-20 and LFUO carry the protocol; this source carries the facilitation craft layer. Use alongside, not instead of.

## Worked Example

A hybrid AAR (three participants in-room, four remote) is reviewing a customer-escalation failure. The facilitator opens with Vegas rules and explicit consent on recording (no recording — declined by two remote participants). (Source: Bennett et al., *FLO Facilitation Guide*, Ch 4, "Privacy and Confidentiality".) During Phase 1, the facilitator tracks participation on a simple spreadsheet: by thirty minutes in, two of the four remote participants have not spoken. The facilitator uses a direct equity question: "We haven't heard from [Name] on what they were seeing at that point — [Name], can you walk us through it?" (Ch 6, "One idea to help you track interactions".) During Phase 2, one participant goes silent after giving their account. The facilitator feels the urge to summarise what the participant said and move on. Instead, the facilitator applies the trust-the-process discipline (Ch 4, "Example of reflective post") and holds silence for ten seconds. The participant adds a second factor that turns out to be load-bearing. The facilitator closes Phase 5 with a modelling move: "I noticed I almost rushed past the silence in Phase 2 — I'm glad I didn't, because that's where the second factor came from. One thing each of you is taking away?"

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Opening a virtual AAR without an explicit confidentiality agreement, then being surprised when participants hedge their accounts.
- Asking leading questions that embed the facilitator's conclusion ("wasn't the root cause the handoff?") rather than opening narrative.
- Letting one or two voices dominate a virtual room because no equity-tracking discipline is in place.
- Rushing into the silence when participants are processing, then mistaking the rush for facilitation skill.
- Recording the session without explicit consent from all participants, chilling candour for the rest of the session.
- Co-facilitating with someone of similar seniority but identical skill set, leaving the domain-knowledge or facilitation-craft gap unfilled.
- Trying to cover every contributory factor in the closing summary rather than naming the two or three that are load-bearing for action.
- Modelling anxiety about participant engagement by checking in too frequently, communicating that the facilitator's comfort matters more than the participant's processing time.
- Skipping written-first input in favour of verbal-only in a hybrid room, systematically disadvantaging quieter or lower-bandwidth participants.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The Community of Inquiry model (Garrison, Anderson, Archer; Vaughan et al. 2013) is cited borrowed-through as the framing model for online facilitation; the demo corpus does not hold the Community of Inquiry literature directly. Liberating Structures (1-2-4-All, User Experience Fishbowl) is cited borrowed-through as a worked example of synchronous facilitation activities; this corpus holds the Liberating Structures Handbook directly but the FLO Guide's citation is to the LS tradition rather than to the handbook's own text. The Institute of Cultural Affairs – UK definition of the facilitator is cited borrowed-through. The International Association of Facilitators (IAF) Core Competencies is cited borrowed-through as inspiration for the FLO facilitation skills list.

**Named limits of the source.** The FLO Facilitation Guide is designed for online learning facilitation in educational settings — primarily multi-week asynchronous courses with synchronous sessions. Its direct applicability to AARs is by adaptation, not by design: it does not address incident review, contributory-factor analysis, just-culture sorting, or action design. The guide's participant set is ongoing cohorts in a learning relationship; the AAR's participant set is typically a one-off cross-functional group convened around a specific event. Adaptations to the AAR context are operator-applied throughout this distillation.
