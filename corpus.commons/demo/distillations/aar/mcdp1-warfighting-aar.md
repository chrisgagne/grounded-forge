# U.S. Marine Corps, MCDP-1 Warfighting — AAR Distillation

**Source:** U.S. Marine Corps (1997). *MCDP 1: Warfighting*. Headquarters United States Marine Corps, Department of the Navy, 20 June 1997. PCN 142 000006 00. Supersedes FMFM 1 Warfighting (1989). **Licence:** Public domain (US Government work product). **Scope:** open.

## AAR Relevance

MCDP-1 is the authoritative doctrinal statement of U.S. Marine Corps warfighting philosophy. It does not describe AAR practice directly — that is the work of TC 25-20 (slug 00o in this corpus) — but it is the *doctrinal context* every Marine AAR is conducted within. Where TC 25-20 supplies the *procedure* (Plan / Prepare / Conduct / Follow-Up; the four standard questions), MCDP-1 supplies the *philosophy* (commander's intent, mission tactics, *Schwerpunkt*, OODA, doctrine-as-way-of-thinking-not-checklist) the AAR is trying to develop and assess.

Three threads from MCDP-1 sit directly on AAR practice.

### 1. Doctrine as a way of thinking, not a checklist (Ch 3)

The pamphlet is explicit: *"Doctrine is a way of thinking, not a checklist."* For an AAR, this reframes the standard question *"did we follow doctrine?"* into *"did we exercise the thinking that doctrine teaches?"* The two are not the same. A unit can follow the letter of a runbook and miss the intent; another can violate the letter and fulfil the intent perfectly. AARs that assess only the letter measure the wrong thing.

Operationally, this means the AAR's job is to reconstruct what the sharp-end practitioners were *trying to do* — what intent they were operating from, what main effort they thought was decisive, what tempo they were trying to maintain — before judging whether they executed correctly. The four standard AAR questions (*What was planned? What actually happened? Why did it happen that way? What can we do better?*) are framed differently when the practitioner's thinking is the unit of analysis rather than their procedural compliance.

### 2. Commander's intent and mission tactics (Ch 4)

MCDP-1 names the disciplinary requirement: *intent must be understood two levels up*. If the sharp-end practitioner did not know the intent two levels up, that gap is the senior leader's responsibility, not the practitioner's compliance failure.

AAR application: when the team's actions diverged from the written plan, the AAR's first question is not *why did they deviate?* It is *did they know the intent two levels up? if yes, did their actions support it?* If they knew the intent and supported it, the deviation was correct under mission tactics. If they did not know the intent, the AAR's action item lives at the leadership level.

### 3. The OODA loop and tempo (Ch 2 Notes 18)

MCDP-1 cites Boyd's *Patterns of Conflict* explicitly at Ch 2 Notes 18 for the OODA loop. The doctrinal claim: *"the side that completes the cycle faster, or that operates in a way the adversary cannot orient to, wins."*

AAR application for high-tempo / high-uncertainty contexts: the analytical question is not just *what did we do?* but *what was our OODA tempo relative to the threat / customer / market / adversary?* Where the team operated inside the threat's loop (acting before the threat could re-orient to the previous action), the incident was contained. Where the threat was inside the team's loop (the team was always reacting to a situation that had already changed), the incident escalated. The OODA framing surfaces a different class of remediation than rule-tightening.

## AAR Cautions

MCDP-1 directly rejects the zero-defects mentality: *"severity on errors of inaction, leniency on overbold errors."* AARs that punish errors of commission while tolerating errors of omission produce inaction — which is the failure mode the doctrine is most concerned with.

The pamphlet's rejection of single-cause explanation is implicit but consistent: war is a fluid, chaotic, multi-causal phenomenon. AARs that converge on a single root cause are operating against the doctrinal frame.
