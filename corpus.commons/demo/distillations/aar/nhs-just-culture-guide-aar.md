## After-Action Review Relevance

The NHS Just Culture Guide is a single-page five-test decision tree for resolving the hardest call an AAR facilitator faces: whether an individual action warrants management intervention or whether the incident points to system-level corrective work. Its load-bearing contribution is structural — the tree is built with a default-to-system prior baked in. "Action singling out an individual is rarely appropriate — most patient safety issues have deeper causes and require wider action" (Source: NHS Improvement, *Just Culture Guide*, Preamble, "Purpose and use"). Four of the seven recommendation end-states route away from management action directed at the individual; only the residual case — deliberate harm absent, health factors absent, knowing departure from a workable protocol confirmed, substitution-exoneration absent, no significant mitigating circumstances — routes to individual management action, and even that end-state pairs with a parallel obligation to investigate wider causes. The guide is equally a communication tool: "an important part of a just culture is being able to explain the approach that will be taken if an incident occurs" (Source: Preamble, "Purpose and use"). In AAR work, it fires on Phase 0 (set the default-to-system posture before the conversation begins), Phase 3 (walk the decision tree when an individual-action concern surfaces), and Phase 4 (communicate the decision to stakeholders using the guide as a shared reference).

## Key Concepts for After-Action Review

1. <!-- concept: default-to-system-prior --> **Default-to-system prior.** "Action singling out an individual is rarely appropriate — most patient safety issues have deeper causes and require wider action" (Source: NHS Improvement, *Just Culture Guide*, Preamble, "Purpose and use"). This is the guide's organising claim and must precede the five-test tree, not follow from it.

2. <!-- concept: five-test-decision-tree --> **Five sequential tests, gated.** Q1 deliberate harm → Q2 health → Q3 foresight → Q4 substitution → Q5 mitigating circumstances. Each test gates the next; only one action or omission is taken through the tree at a time; multiple actions in one incident must be evaluated separately. (Source: Preamble, "Please note", bullet 4)

3. <!-- concept: deliberate-harm-test --> **Q1 deliberate harm test.** Single question: "Was there any intention to cause harm?" (Source: Q1) A Yes exits to Recommendation A — the only path that can involve police referral and suspension. Even Recommendation A closes with "wider investigation is still needed to understand how and why patients were not protected." A No proceeds to Q2.

4. <!-- concept: health-test --> **Q2 health test.** Three sub-questions: substance abuse, physical ill health, mental ill health. A Yes to any sub-question exits to Recommendation B or C (occupational-health or substance-abuse-at-work pathways), both paired with backward-looking system questions — could the health issue have been recognised and addressed earlier? A No to all three proceeds to Q3.

5. <!-- concept: foresight-test --> **Q3 foresight test — the load-bearing test for system contract.** Three sub-questions: Was there an agreed protocol? Was it workable and in routine use? Did the individual knowingly depart? All three must be Yes to proceed to Q4; No to any routes to Recommendation D: "Action singling out the individual is unlikely to be appropriate; the patient safety incident investigation should indicate the wider actions needed." (Source: Q3, "Recommendation D") The test isolates *knowing departure from a workable routinely-used agreed protocol* as the only path that continues toward potential individual accountability.

6. <!-- concept: substitution-test --> **Q4 substitution test — peer, training, and supervision probe.** Three sub-questions: Would peers in same circumstances behave similarly? Was training missed? Did supervision fail? Any Yes routes to Recommendation E (system-locus corrective work). A No to all three proceeds to Q5. (Source: Q4) The substitution test's name captures its operational question: would substituting another comparable individual into the same situation have produced a different outcome?

7. <!-- concept: mitigating-circumstances-test --> **Q5 mitigating circumstances — the residual.** Single question: "Were there any significant mitigating circumstances?" A Yes exits to Recommendation F: "Action directed at the individual may not be appropriate; follow organisational guidance, which is likely to include senior HR advice on what degree of mitigation applies." (Source: Q5, "Recommendation F") A No exits to Recommendation G.

8. <!-- concept: recommendation-g --> **Recommendation G — the narrow residual for management action.** Only reached after clearing all five gates. Lists a graduated set of responses: "individual training, performance management, competency assessments, changes to role or increased supervision" before reaching for suspension and disciplinary processes. Still paired with the wider-investigation obligation. (Source: Q5, "Recommendation G")

9. <!-- concept: one-action-at-a-time --> **One action at a time, evaluated separately.** "Only one action (or failure to act) through the guide at a time. If multiple actions are involved in an incident they must be considered separately." (Source: Preamble, "Please note", bullet 4) An incident involving several actions is multiple trips through the tree, not one.

10. <!-- concept: re-entrant-decision-tree --> **Re-entrant: revisit as investigation produces new facts.** "The guide can be used at any point of an investigation, but the guide may need to be revisited as more information becomes available." (Source: Preamble, "Please note", bullet 2) The tree is not a once-only assessment; facts change and the path may change with them.

11. <!-- concept: just-culture-as-communication --> **Communication tool, not just decision aid.** The guide is positioned for use by all parties — managers, staff, patients, families — to explain the approach. "An important part of a just culture is being able to explain the approach that will be taken if an incident occurs." (Source: Preamble, "Purpose and use") The guide can be shared with the team before the conversation begins.

12. <!-- concept: reason-npsa-lineage --> **Borrowed-through Reason / NPSA lineage.** The poster attributes itself to "the work of Professor James Reason and the National Patient Safety Agency's Incident Decision Tree." Reason and the NPSA Incident Decision Tree are carried borrowed-through; the demo does not hold them directly.

## Questions to Ask During After-Action Review

### Phase 0: Scoping

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Has the default-to-system posture been set? | Before the first question, has someone read aloud: "action singling out an individual is rarely appropriate — most patient safety issues have deeper causes and require wider action"? |
| Is the guide being used as a communication tool? | Has the guide been shared with all parties — managers, staff, anyone who might be named — so they know in advance the framework that will be used? |
| Is the investigation sufficiently advanced to run the tree? | Are the facts available for each test, or will the tree need to be re-entered later as more information becomes available? |

### Phase 1: Timeline and Local-Rationality Reconstruction

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Are the facts being separated by action? | If multiple actions are under review, has each been decomposed into a separate trip through the tree? |
| Is the timeline surfacing health indicators? | Are there observations suggesting substance abuse, physical ill health, or mental ill health that would feed Q2? |
| Is the foresight test's protocol question answerable? | Does an agreed protocol exist for the action in question? Was it workable and in routine use? These facts need to be established in the timeline before Q3 can be run. |

### Phase 2: Contributory-Factor Analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| What are the active failures vs latent conditions? | Active failures are at the sharp end (the individual's action); latent conditions are upstream (protocols, training, supervision). Both belong in the analysis. |
| Does the substitution test apply here? | Would peers in the same circumstances — with comparable experience and qualifications — have behaved in the same way? If yes, the locus is the conditions, not the individual. |
| Was training missed or supervision absent? | These are structural contributors that the substitution test surfaces explicitly: missed training (Q4b) and absent supervision (Q4c) both route to system-locus corrective action. |

### Phase 3: Just-Culture Sorting

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Q1: Was there any intention to cause harm? | Answer honestly; a Yes exits immediately to Recommendation A and potential regulatory/police referral. |
| Q2: Are there health indicators? | Substance abuse, physical ill health, or mental ill health — each routes to its own occupational-health pathway, not to individual management action. |
| Q3: Foresight test — all three Yes to advance. | (a) Was there an agreed protocol? (b) Was it workable and in routine use? (c) Did the individual knowingly depart? All three must be Yes to reach Q4. |
| Q4: Substitution test — any Yes routes to system locus. | (a) Would peers have behaved similarly? (b) Was the individual missed when training was provided? (c) Did supervision fail? Any Yes routes to Recommendation E. |
| Q5: Are there significant mitigating circumstances? | If the case has cleared Q1-Q4, this final filter determines whether management action is appropriate or conditional. |

### Phase 4: Action Design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| What recommendation end-state has the tree produced? | Seven end-states (A through G); name the specific recommendation and its operational guidance. |
| Does the recommendation name a wider-investigation obligation? | Every recommendation — including A and G — pairs with a directive that "the patient safety incident investigation should indicate the wider actions needed." Has that wider action been named? |
| Is the individual response, if any, graduated? | Recommendation G lists training, performance management, competency assessment, role change, and increased supervision before suspension and disciplinary processes. Is the response calibrated to the least-invasive appropriate level? |
| Has the response been explained to all parties? | The guide's dual purpose: "protecting staff from unfair targeting" and protecting patients "by removing the tendency to treat wider patient safety issues as individual issues." Has both been communicated? |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| The group moves from timeline to individual accountability without running the five-test tree | Default-to-individual failure — the tree's structural bias toward system locus was bypassed | Pause; establish the default-to-system prior; run the tree from Q1 |
| A protocol is cited as the standard but no one confirms it was workable and routinely used | Q3b and Q3c are unexamined — the foresight test may not advance to Q4 | Establish: was the protocol workable? Was it actually in routine use? Both must be true before Q3 advances |
| The group runs all five individuals in an incident through the tree together | Multiple trips have been collapsed into one — the tree must be run separately for each action or omission | Restart: "We're going to run one action at a time through the guide." |
| The group treats Recommendation G as the expected outcome | G is the narrow residual, not the default — treating it as expected inverts the guide's bias | Restate the default-to-system prior before the tree is run |
| New investigation facts emerge after the tree has been run | The path may have changed — the guide is re-entrant | Re-run the affected test with the new facts; the result may shift |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this source when:
- Phase 3 requires a structured framework for the individual-vs-system locus decision — the five-test tree is the corpus's only explicit decision aid for this call.
- The default-to-system posture needs to be established at Phase 0 before any individual accounts are heard.
- The guide is needed as a communication tool — to share with all parties in advance so the framework is known before the incident is discussed.
- New facts emerge after the initial just-culture sort and the tree needs to be re-entered.

Do not use this source as the primary AAR protocol (TC 25-20 and LFUO carry that), and do not treat it as a substitute for investigation. The guide is a decision aid that requires investigation facts as inputs.

## Worked Example

A hospital team reviews a medication incident in which a nurse administered the wrong drug from a look-alike packaging pair. The facilitator opens Phase 0 by reading the guide's default-to-system prior aloud and sharing the guide with the nurse, the charge nurse, and the unit manager before any discussion begins. (Source: NHS Improvement, *Just Culture Guide*, Preamble, "Purpose and use".) In Phase 3, the facilitator runs Q1 (no deliberate harm) and Q2 (no health indicators) and reaches Q3. Three sub-tests: (a) Was there an agreed protocol? Yes. (b) Was the protocol workable and in routine use? No — the investigation establishes that the look-alike packaging had been flagged twice before and no mitigation had been implemented. The protocol required visual comparison of drug names, but the packaging was designed to make visual comparison difficult. Q3b is No; the tree routes to Recommendation D: "Action singling out the individual is unlikely to be appropriate; the patient safety incident investigation should indicate the wider actions needed." (Source: Q3, "Recommendation D".) The Phase 4 action targets the procurement process (look-alike packaging policy) and the patient-safety reporting mechanism (prior flags had not reached a decision-maker). The guide is shared with the nurse directly: the framing makes clear that the route landed at Recommendation D, not because the incident is ignored, but because the system conditions are the primary target.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Defaulting to individual blame before running the five-test tree.
- Running the tree once and treating the result as final when new investigation facts will change the inputs to one or more tests.
- Bundling multiple actions in one incident into a single trip through the tree; each action must be evaluated separately.
- Treating Recommendation G as the default expectation rather than the narrow residual it is.
- Using the guide as a substitute for investigation; only a full investigation identifies the underlying causes that need action.
- Skipping the communication-tool function of the guide; staff, patients, and families should see the framework before incidents occur, not during the worst moment.
- Treating the health test (Q2) as an obstacle to accountability rather than a route to occupational-health support.
- Conflating the foresight test's "workable and in routine use" requirement with mere protocol existence — an unworkable or routinely-ignored protocol fails Q3b and routes to system locus.
- Citing Reason or the NPSA Incident Decision Tree as if the demo holds them directly; both are borrowed-through this guide's footer attribution.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The guide attributes its lineage to "the work of Professor James Reason and the National Patient Safety Agency's Incident Decision Tree." James Reason's work (*Managing the Risks of Organizational Accidents*, *The Human Contribution*) is not held directly in this corpus. The NPSA Incident Decision Tree is not held directly. Both are carried borrowed-through via this guide's footer attribution. Any further characterisation of Reason's theory or the NPSA IDT's structure would be outside the scope of this reference because neither is stated in sufficient detail in the source.

**Named limits of the source.** The guide is a single-page A3 poster designed for National Health Service contexts in the UK. Its terminology ("patient safety incident", "occupational health pathway") is healthcare-specific; adaptation to non-healthcare contexts is operator-applied. The guide does not address incident investigation methodology, timeline reconstruction, or contributory-factor analysis — it takes investigation facts as inputs and applies the decision tree. The guide cannot be run without prior investigation work; using it to substitute for investigation is the named anti-pattern.
