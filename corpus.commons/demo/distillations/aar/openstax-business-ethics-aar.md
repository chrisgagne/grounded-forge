# OpenStax Business Ethics, AAR Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2018). *Business Ethics*. Senior contributing authors Stephen M. Byars and Kurt Stanberry et al. Houston, TX: Rice University. Licence: CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. Scope: open-nc.

## AAR Relevance

Business Ethics is light-use for most AAR phases. It earns its place at two specific moments: when an AAR surfaces ethical exposure that needs naming and routing, and when findings must be escalated to stakeholders with competing interests. The Donaldson-Preston three approaches to stakeholder theory (descriptive, instrumental, normative) give the facilitator a decision frame for whose interests belong in the analysis and why. The Grunig-Hunt linkage model categorises which stakeholders are enabling, functional, or diffused, which shapes who must be consulted when actions cross organisational boundaries. Duty-of-care chapters (workplace safety, OSHA, whistleblower protections) name the obligations that become load-bearing when an AAR surfaces employee-safety or customer-impact findings. The social-contract framing holds that ethical minimums are rarely sufficient; when an AAR reveals a gap between what the organisation did and what it owed, this source names the gap precisely.

## Key Concepts for AAR

1. **Three approaches to stakeholder theory (Donaldson and Preston).** Descriptive (map stakeholder interests accurately), instrumental (stakeholder management drives financial outcomes), normative (stakeholders are ends in themselves, not means). The normative approach is the source's recommended ground: "the interests of all stakeholders are of intrinsic value" [V]. When an AAR must decide whose interests belong in the analysis, the normative approach is the tie-breaker. (Source: Byars & Stanberry, *Business Ethics*, Ch 3.2, "The Ethical Basis of Stakeholders' Claims")

2. **Grunig-Hunt organisational linkage model.** Four stakeholder categories: enabling (stockholders, regulators, boards — "permit the firm to function" [V]), normative (competitors and peers), functional (employees, suppliers, customers), diffused (NGOs, media — less direct but meaningful impact). When an AAR finding must be escalated, this taxonomy identifies which category must be informed and in what sequence. (Source: Ch 3.2, "Defining Stakeholder Categories")

3. **Ethical minimum and ethical maximum.** Minimum is "the least a company might do to claim it holds an ethically positive position"; maximum is "the strongest action a company can choose" [V]. When an AAR reveals a gap between what was done and what the standard of care required, this scale names the gap without conflating legal compliance with ethical adequacy. (Source: Ch 3.1, "Ethical Responsibilities Often Extend Beyond Legal Requirements")

4. **Duty of care / workplace safety.** OSHA (1970) requires a workplace "free of risk to employees' safety and health"; employer obligations include identifying and eliminating hazards, informing employees, training, and providing protective gear [V]. When an AAR surfaces a safety-condition contributory factor, OSHA frames the legal and ethical minimum the organisation owed. (Source: Ch 6.1, "Workplace Safety under the Occupational Safety and Health Act")

5. **Whistleblower protections.** Over 20 federal laws protect specific categories; the False Claims Act's *qui tam* provisions allow private relators to file on behalf of the government; OSHA (1970) included early protection. When an AAR surfaces a known-but-unreported problem, the absence of safe reporting channels becomes a named contributory factor, and this source explains why people did not speak. (Source: Ch 7.5)

6. **Social contract and CSR.** Corporations enter a social contract: in return for the right to incorporate, they owe the community that enables them to exist. When an AAR surfaces community or customer impact, this framing names the obligation the organisation held before the incident. (Source: Ch 1.2, Ch 4.1)

7. **Psychological safety and ethical culture.** "Employees are more likely to be unethical when they are dissatisfied with their jobs or see their superiors acting unethically" [V]. The tone-at-the-top argument: culture is the primary carrier of ethical behaviour, not compliance apparatus. When an AAR reveals that people knew something was wrong and did not act, cultural conditions explain the silence. (Source: Ch 11.2, "Relationships Matter")

8. **Grunig-Hunt four publics.** Nonpublic (no problem), latent (problem unrecognised), aware (problem recognised), active (organised in response). When escalating AAR findings to leadership, identifying which public category stakeholders occupy tells the facilitator how much time exists before latent becomes active. (Source: Ch 3.2, "The Chicago Tylenol Murders")

9. **Phronesis (practical wisdom).** "Tacit knowledge acquired from experience that enables people to make prudent judgments and take actions based on the actual situation, guided by values and morals" [V]. When an AAR reveals that the person on the ground made a judgment call that violated a procedure, phronesis frames the question: was this a failure of character, a failure of training, or a case where rules did not fit the situation? (Source: Ch 1.1, "Aristotle and the Concept of Phronesis")

10. **Ethics as ongoing formation, not compliance checkbox.** Many organisations focus on the letter of the law so they can claim "good faith"; "middle managers and employees often complain their ethics training consists of passing a computerized program once a year" [V]. When an AAR surfaces a repeated pattern of near-ethical-failure, compliance training is likely the wrong corrective. (Source: Ch 5.4)

## Questions to Ask During AAR

### Phase 3: Just-culture sorting

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Name the ethical dimension of the individual-vs-system call | Is the individual's action a duty-of-care violation (OSHA, professional obligation), a failure of practical wisdom, or a system failure that placed them in an impossible position? Naming the category shapes the response. |
| Check whether safe reporting channels existed | Were there whistleblower-accessible channels? Were there prior incidents the person could have reported? If channels were absent or unsafe, the absence is a structural contributory factor, not an individual character failure. |

### Phase 4: Action design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Map the stakeholder set the action must reach | Who are the enabling, functional, and diffused stakeholders affected by the proposed action? Which require direct communication, which require structural change? Use the Grunig-Hunt categories to sequence outreach. |
| Distinguish ethical minimum from ethical maximum in the proposed action | Is the proposed action the least the organisation must do, or the strongest it can choose? When the gap between them is large, name it and let the sponsoring leader decide where to land. |
| Check for prior known-but-unreported instances | Was this incident preceded by events that should have triggered a report? If yes, the absence of reporting becomes an action target — not to punish the non-reporters but to redesign the channel. |

### Phase 5: Learning-loop closure

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Assess whether the corrective addresses culture or compliance only | If the action is a training module or a policy update, is that sufficient? Or does the incident suggest a deeper cultural condition that compliance apparatus cannot fix? |
| Name the public-category trajectory | Are affected stakeholders currently latent or aware? If latent, the organisation has time to act before the public becomes active. If aware, the communication timeline is compressed. |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| Legal compliance is cited as the standard in just-culture sorting | Ethical minimum being treated as the ethical ceiling — the social-contract framing is absent | Ask: what did the organisation owe stakeholders beyond what the law required? |
| No whistleblower complaints are taken as evidence no problem was known | Absence of reports reflecting unsafe reporting conditions, not absence of problems | Map the reporting channels: were they accessible, safe, and used by people who knew? |
| Actions target the wrong stakeholder tier | Fixing a functional-stakeholder problem without engaging enabling stakeholders who must sanction the structural change | Use the Grunig-Hunt taxonomy to check whether all three implicated categories are addressed |
| Pattern of near-failure recurs across multiple individuals | Culture, not character, is the more likely explanation; compliance training will not move it | Look at tone-at-the-top conditions and what cultural carriers need to change |
| Ethical exposure is framed as a legal risk to manage | Social-contract framing is absent; the reputational and relational costs of the minimalist response are unacknowledged | Name the ethical maximum; let leadership choose with both options visible |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this source when:
- The AAR surfaces a finding with explicit ethical exposure — safety obligations, known-but-unreported problems, stakeholder harm — and the facilitator needs vocabulary to name and route it.
- Just-culture sorting must distinguish duty-of-care violation, practical-wisdom failure, and system failure, rather than collapsing all three to "individual error."
- Actions must be escalated across organisational boundaries and the right stakeholder sequence needs naming.
- The pattern of failure recurs across multiple people over time, suggesting culture rather than character is the load-bearing factor.

This source does not carry timeline reconstruction, contributory-factor methodology, or just-culture decision frameworks. LFUO, TC 25-20, and the NHS Just Culture Guide carry those. Business Ethics enters at Phase 3 (sorting) and Phase 4 (action design), not Phase 1.

## Worked Example

A team reviews a product-defect incident where field reports of a safety problem reached a regional sales manager who did not escalate. The AAR facilitator applies Business Ethics framing in Phase 3. Applying phronesis (Ch 1.1): did the manager have the practical wisdom to recognise this as an OSHA-class duty-of-care situation, or did the role context make non-escalation feel rational? Applying whistleblower-protection framing (Ch 7.5): were the channels for reporting upward safe and accessible, or did the manager have reason to believe escalation would be penalised? The investigation finds no prior retaliation cases but also no visible protection policy. The ethical minimum versus maximum frame (Ch 3.1) is applied in Phase 4: the minimum action is a policy update naming the reporting obligation; the maximum is a structural redesign of the regional reporting channel with anonymous access and explicit OSHA-class protections. The enabling stakeholders (board, legal, compliance) are named via the Grunig-Hunt taxonomy (Ch 3.2) as the group that must sanction the structural redesign before the functional stakeholders (regional managers) can act. The Grunig-Hunt four-publics frame (Ch 3.2) is applied to assess whether affected customers are latent or aware — informing how much lead time exists before public response requires a different level of communication.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Treating legal compliance as the ethical standard in an AAR; the ethical minimum is rarely the ethical bar the organisation should be setting.
- Conflating the absence of formal whistleblower complaints with the absence of known problems; absence of complaints often reflects unsafe reporting conditions, not absence of problems.
- Designing AAR actions for the wrong stakeholder tier: fixing a functional-stakeholder (employee) problem without engaging the enabling stakeholders (board, regulator) who must sanction the structural change.
- Diagnosing a character failure when a culture failure is the better-supported explanation: when patterns recur across multiple individuals, culture, not character, is the more likely explanation.
- Treating the incident's ethical exposure as a legal risk to manage rather than a social-contract obligation to honour; the reputational and relational costs of the minimalist response exceed the costs of the maximal one.
- Ignoring tone-at-the-top conditions when designing correctives; training and policy change will not move people whose superiors model the behaviour the training prohibits.
- Closing Phase 3 on the individual accountability question without asking whether the role context made the action locally rational — the phronesis frame prevents collapsing judgment-call failures into character verdicts.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The Donaldson-Preston stakeholder-theory taxonomy is cited in the source as borrowed-through from Donaldson and Preston's original work [BT] (Ch 3.2); neither Donaldson nor Preston is held directly in the demo corpus. The Grunig-Hunt organisational linkage model and four-publics framework are similarly borrowed-through [BT] (Ch 3.2); Grunig and Hunt are not held directly. Phronesis is attributed to Aristotle (via Nonaka and Takeuchi in the source) [BT] (Ch 1.1). The legal cases cited in the source — *Dodge v. Ford*, *Shlensky v. Wrigley*, *Burwell v. Hobby Lobby*, *Citizens United v. FEC* — are borrowed-through as legal authority [BT] (Ch 4.1, Ch 4.3).

**Named limits of the source.** Business Ethics does not carry timeline reconstruction, contributory-factor methodology, or just-culture decision frameworks. It is a normative business-ethics text, not a learning-review methodology. Its AAR contribution is confined to ethical categorisation (Phase 3) and stakeholder-sequencing discipline (Phase 4). The source's workplace-safety and whistleblower content is US-law-specific; OSHA provisions and federal whistleblower statutes apply in US jurisdictions only. The text explicitly addresses that ethical conduct requires going above and beyond legal compliance, but does not define domain-specific standards of care beyond the named statutes.
