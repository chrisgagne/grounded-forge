# Krivitsky, Larman & Flemm, Org Topologies Primer — AAR Distillation

**Source:** Alexey Krivitsky, Craig Larman & Roland Flemm (2025). *Strategic Org Design: The Primer*. Org Topologies™ 2025 Edition. February 2025. Licence: CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://orgtopologies.com.

## AAR Relevance

The Primer is not an incident-review text and does not address timeline reconstruction, just-culture sorting, or contributory-factor analysis directly. Its applicability to the AAR axis runs through a specific channel: **org structure as contributory factor**. When an AAR surfaces that an incident happened because of a mismatch between the work the org was being asked to do and the topology it actually has — when a team that needed to be end-to-end (`CAPS-3`) was structurally constrained to multi-skill-incomplete (`CAPS-2`) and could not deliver without a cross-silo handoff that introduced the failure mode — the Primer is the corpus's reference for *naming the contributory factor in precise vocabulary*.

This is a Phase 2 (contributory-factor analysis) and Phase 4 (action design, especially escalation) projection. In Phase 2, the LFUO position is that "cause isn't something investigators 'find' or 'discover'; cause is always something we create by recreating the event," and that "most often, the Lessons Learned Analysis will reveal that multiple improbable events were necessary for the accident to occur" (`lfuo-learning-review-guide-2024`). Among the multiple contributory factors the LLA surfaces, *org-structure-and-mandate* is a recurring class — the team's archetype was the wrong one for the work it was being asked to do; the directing-doing handoff introduced the misunderstanding; the Resource-Topology framing prevented the team from owning the end-to-end outcome. The Primer's named archetypes (CAPS-2, WHOLE-3, TASKS-1, PART-1), its four archetype groups (Directing, Doing, Delivering, Driving), and its three named topologies (Resource, Delivery, Adaptive) give the facilitator a *contributory-factor vocabulary* that is more precise than "siloed" or "wrong structure" — and that connects the contributory factor to a remediation pathway (Elevate, with Elevating Katas™).

The Primer is explicit that org-design failure has its own systemic logic: "if each element (e.g. policies, structures) is optimized in isolation, the performance of the whole system may actually decline for the desired goal (flow can get worse!)" — *which is the structural cousin of LFUO's networked-causality position*. When an AAR's contributory-factor analysis points at an org-design failure, the systems-thinking warning the Primer issues for MAP — that local optimisation produces whole-system degradation — is the same warning facilitators must give in Phase 4 when proposing remediation: a single-point structural fix (a new role, a new policy, a new committee) without systems-fit is the named anti-pattern.

In Phase 4 (action design, especially **escalating org-design implications to leadership**), the Primer's stance that "senior management only defining business objectives" is a strategic-design failure mode maps onto the AAR escalation question: when contributory factors point above the team's authority, who owns the remediation? The Primer's answer — *the same leaders who own the business objective also own the internal org design that delivers it* — gives the facilitator the framing language for escalation memos that do not let leadership treat structural contributory factors as someone else's problem.

The Primer is *not* a substitute for the actual contributory-factor analysis (LFUO + TC 25-20 + NHS), the just-culture sort (NHS five-test decision tree), or the systems-mapping discipline (Barbrook-Johnson, SSDL). It is a *naming tool* and a *remediation-pathway tool* for the org-structure subset of contributory factors. Facilitators reach for it when the Lessons Learned Analysis has surfaced an org-design contributory factor and the next step is either crisp naming (Phase 2) or escalation framing (Phase 4).

## Key Concepts for AAR

1. <!-- concept: named-archetype-language --> **Org structure as contributory factor, named with precision.** The Primer's named-archetype vocabulary (CAPS-2, WHOLE-3, TASKS-1) gives contributory-factor analysis a precision that "the team was siloed" or "communication broke down" lacks. The contributory factor becomes: *"the team was a CAPS-2 archetype (multi-skill but incomplete in capabilities) being asked to deliver a CAPS-3 outcome (end-to-end capability ownership), so the cross-silo handoff that introduced the failure was structural, not behavioural."* (Source: Krivitsky, Larman & Flemm, *Org Topologies Primer* 2025, p. 5, "Language of Org Design"; pp. 6–7, axes)

2. <!-- concept: topology-mismatch --> **Topology mismatch as a recurring contributory-factor pattern.** When an AAR surfaces that the work demanded one topology and the org carried another, the Primer names three predictable mismatch patterns. *Resource-Topology drag*: the team was being asked to discover (Adaptive work) inside a utilisation-optimised Resource Topology, and the drag from directing-doing handoffs introduced the failure. *Delivery-Topology bloat*: the team was being asked to outcome-validate (Adaptive work) inside a Delivery Topology optimised for predictable feature delivery, and the absence of an outcome-validation function let the "feature bloat" failure mode produce the wrong feature. *Adaptive-Topology variance*: the team was asked to deliver predictable output (Delivery work) inside an Adaptive Topology, and the high-variance tolerance accommodated the variance that became the incident. (pp. 11–13, three topology pages)

3. <!-- concept: local-optimisation --> **The local-optimisation warning as a contributory-factor amplifier.** "If each element (e.g. policies, structures) is optimized in isolation, the performance of the whole system may actually decline for the desired goal (flow can get worse!)" When an AAR surfaces multiple individually-defensible org-design choices that combined to produce the incident, the local-optimisation pattern is the named amplifier. The contributory factor isn't any single element; it's the absence of systems-thinking across them. (p. 16, "MADE Real: (1) MAP")

4. <!-- concept: single-team-focus --> **Single-team-focus as a contributory-factor source.** "A single high-performing team can't deliver its full potential if surrounding structures and processes remain unchanged." When an AAR shows that a previously-fixed team was the source of repeat failure inside an unchanged surround, the Primer names this as the structural source: the team-as-island intervention left the surrounding system's contributory factors untouched. (p. 4, "The Hidden Complexities of Org Change")

5. <!-- concept: resource-topology --> **Resource-Topology framing as a contributory-factor sign.** "The Directing Archetypes in the upper left quadrant treat the Doing Archetypes as 'resources,' matching skill demand with skill supply." When an AAR surfaces that frontline staff were being treated as fungible resources (rotated through assignments, denied voice in design, denied end-to-end accountability), the Resource-Topology framing is named as the structural contributor. (p. 11, "Resource Topology")

6. <!-- concept: strategic-org-design --> **Strategic-leadership decoupling as an escalation framing.** "Senior management only defining business objectives" without designing the internal org design is named as a strategic-design failure. When an AAR's contributory factors point above the team's authority, the escalation memo can use this framing: *the contributory factor is at the strategic-design level, and the leaders who own the business objective also own the structural fix.* This makes the escalation a leadership-action-item rather than a "for your information" report. (p. 10, "Strategic Org Design")

7. <!-- concept: made-method --> **MAP as a contributory-factor evidence-gathering activity.** The Primer's MAP step calls for "going to the hands-on workplace to understand how the units work: what kind of units are they, what is their input, what is their output, and which other archetypes are needed in the chain of value creation?" This is the same evidence-gathering discipline LFUO calls for in Lessons Learned Analysis — and the OT map is a candidate shared-model artefact (alongside CLDs from Barbrook-Johnson) for the Phase 2 conversation. (p. 16, "MADE Real: (1) MAP")

8. <!-- concept: elevating-katas --> **The Elevating-Katas-as-experiments framing for Phase 4 actions.** When the AAR's Phase 4 action design proposes structural remediation, the Elevating Katas™ framing — "a set of practices and principles that can be applied as thoughtful experiments to change the organizational structure, processes, policies, and so forth" — gives the actions the right shape: experiments with named hypotheses, not pronouncements. This pairs with LFUO's Learning-Review-required-for-recommendations constraint: structural-change recommendations need a Safety Action Plan, not an FLA item. (p. 19, "MADE Real: (4) ELEVATE")

9. <!-- concept: periodic-re-mapping --> **Periodic re-mapping as a learning-loop-closure tool.** "Periodic re-mapping with Org Topologies is an easy and fast way to complete a learning loop of feedback and adapting." When the AAR's structural-remediation actions are implemented, OT re-mapping is a Phase 5 (learning-loop closure) artefact: did the topology actually shift? Or did the change stall at announcement? (p. 19, "MADE Real: (4) ELEVATE")

10. <!-- concept: feature-bloat --> **The "feature bloat" failure mode as a Delivery-Topology contributory factor.** "Notice that there is no guarantee in this topology that the output effectively solves the real problem or creates outcomes people care about. Products and services with 'feature bloat' are a common consequence." When an AAR is reviewing an incident where a delivered feature caused customer harm (or where a release produced an unintended outcome), Delivery-Topology absent-outcome-validation is a named structural contributory factor worth naming. (p. 12, "Delivery Topology")

11. <!-- concept: strategic-ai-adoption --> **AI-integration org-design implications as a contemporary contributory-factor class.** "We predict that monitoring will become an important responsibility for human workers." When an AAR reviews an AI-integration incident, the absence-of-human-monitoring contributory factor has a structural source: the AI was deployed without defining the monitoring role inside the org design. The Primer's strategic-AI guidance is the prevention vocabulary. (p. 20, "Strategic AI Adoption")

12. <!-- concept: archetype-escalation-vocabulary --> **The named-archetype language as escalation infrastructure.** When the AAR's Phase 4 escalation memo needs to communicate org-design contributory factors to leadership not previously engaged in the incident, OT's framework-agnostic vocabulary ("CAPS-2 teams being asked to deliver CAPS-3 work") avoids the framework-jargon traps that get escalation memos dismissed by non-engineering leadership. (p. 5, "Language of Org Design")

## Questions to Ask During AAR

### Phase 2: Contributory-factor analysis (when org structure is implicated)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Test whether org structure is a contributory factor | Walking the LLA, is one of the multiple improbable events that had to align an org-structure mismatch — the team's archetype not matching the work being demanded of it? |
| Name the team's current archetype precisely | Using the OT vocabulary, what archetype is the team (TASKS-2 multi-skill incomplete? CAPS-2 capability-focused multi-skill? WHOLE-1 individual at whole-business focus)? Precision matters for the contributory-factor naming. |
| Name the work-demanded archetype | What archetype would the work have required for the failure not to occur? (e.g. CAPS-3 end-to-end ownership of the capability, not CAPS-2 with cross-silo handoff.) |
| Locate the topology mismatch | Is this a Resource-Topology drag pattern (utilisation-optimised structure forced to discover), a Delivery-Topology bloat pattern (output-optimised structure missing outcome-validation), or an Adaptive-Topology variance pattern (high-variance structure absorbing variance that became incident)? |
| Test for local-optimisation amplification | Were several individually-defensible org-design choices (a reward, a structure, a process) combined to produce the incident? The local-optimisation pattern is the contributory-factor amplifier. |
| Test for Resource-framing contributors | Was frontline staff being framed as "resources" (rotated, denied voice, denied end-to-end accountability)? Resource-framing predicts the contributory-factor pattern. |
| Test for single-team-island contributors | Was the team a previously-fixed island in an unchanged surround? Repeated failures inside such islands have a named structural source. |

### Phase 4: Action design (when contributory factors point at org structure)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Frame structural actions as Elevating-Kata experiments | Are the proposed structural actions framed as named experiments with hypotheses and check-points, or as decisions to be implemented? Experiment framing is the Primer's prescribed shape. |
| Test escalation framing for leadership engagement | If the contributory factors point above team authority, does the escalation memo frame the action as "leadership owns the structural fix because they own the business objective", or does it frame it as "FYI to leadership"? The former invokes the strategic-design stance directly. |
| Use the named-archetype vocabulary in the escalation memo | Does the memo describe the current and target archetypes precisely (CAPS-2 → CAPS-3), or does it use vague language ("more autonomy", "cross-functional teams")? Precise vocabulary survives transit to non-engineering leadership. |
| Defend against single-element fixes | Does the proposed remediation change only one org-design element (a structure, a policy, a reward), or does it address the systems-fit? Single-element fixes carry the local-optimisation warning. |
| Plan divergent-design accommodation if applicable | If the org has multiple business lines and only one needs the topology shift, does the action propose divergent designs ("Division A retains Resource Topology; Division B elevates to Adaptive")? |
| Define the Learning-Review threshold honestly | Does the structural recommendation rise to the LFUO Learning-Review threshold (focus groups, academic SMEs, Safety Action Plan)? If yes, the FLA cannot carry it; trigger the Learning Review. |
| Plan re-mapping as the learning-loop-closure artefact | When will the org be re-mapped to test whether the structural shift actually happened? Tie the re-mapping to a calendar date and a participant list. |

### Phase 5: Learning-loop closure (when structural actions are in flight)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Re-MAP the current state | Has the team's archetype actually shifted, or did the structural change stall at announcement? Re-mapping is the empirical test. |
| Surface new bottlenecks | Now that the original structural bottleneck is dissolved, which new ones are binding? Coordination, prioritisation, discovery — new structural contributory-factor classes for the next AAR. |
| Test for drift back to original framing | Is the language of "resources" or "headcount allocation" creeping back? Drift back to Resource-Topology framing is the named regression pattern. |
| Surface emerging Elevating Katas worth naming | Which structural-change experiments worked here? Naming them as candidate Katas builds the organisation's learning history. |
| Connect AAR learning to OT re-mapping cadence | Does the org's periodic OT re-mapping include the AAR-surfaced contributory-factor history? Linking the learning loops prevents structural lessons from being lost between cycles. |

## Anti-Patterns and Failure Modes (when projecting OT onto AAR)

1. **Letting the Primer's vocabulary substitute for contributory-factor analysis.** OT names the org-structure subset of contributory factors. It does not replace the LLA, the timeline, the deflection-question protocol, or the just-culture sort. If the AAR jumps to "the contributory factor was Resource Topology" without the LLA, the analysis is impoverished.

2. **Using OT vocabulary as a blame-shift mechanism.** "It was the structure, not the people" can become a way of avoiding the just-culture sort (NHS five-test) where it genuinely applies. The Primer's stance is *both* — system as default, individual when the five-test applies. Don't let the Primer's strong system-stance collapse the NHS protocol.

3. **Single-element structural fixes.** Proposing a new role, a new committee, a new policy as the AAR's structural remediation without addressing systems-fit. The Primer's local-optimisation warning applies directly: "performance of the whole system may actually decline (flow can get worse!)".

4. **Framework-recommendation as the AAR action.** "We should adopt SAFe / LeSS / Team Topologies to prevent this." This is the cargo-cult failure mode the Primer warns against, projected into the AAR action layer. The structural action should be a topology shift, not a framework adoption.

5. **Whole-org structural change as the AAR action.** AAR-driven structural recommendations should usually be scoped to the team and its immediate surround, not the whole organisation. Divergent designs per business line are the Primer-compatible accommodation. Whole-org recommendations from an AAR are usually disproportionate.

6. **Skipping the Learning-Review threshold for structural recommendations.** LFUO is explicit that recommendations require a Learning Review with focus groups, academic SMEs, Safety Action Plan — not an FLA item. Structural recommendations especially require this; they cannot be added to an FLA after the fact.

7. **Loss of just-culture posture in escalation memos.** OT's strong systems-stance can come across as exonerating in escalation memos. Pair OT's structural-naming with the NHS just-culture five-test result explicitly so the memo carries both: the structural contributory factor and the individual-culpability sort.

8. **Treating OT re-mapping as a replacement for the AAR action-tracking.** OT re-mapping is a Phase 5 artefact — it tests whether structural change happened. It does not replace the AAR action-tracking discipline (TC 25-20: an AAR without retraining commitment has not produced its benefit). Both are needed.

## Worked Examples (from the source, projected onto AAR)

- **The running MADE example as a post-incident reframing exercise (pp. 16–19).** A software organisation has experienced a string of late deliveries (incident pattern, multiple AAR-able events). Running the MAP step (Phase 2 contributory-factor work in OT vocabulary): the team configuration is WHOLE-1 product manager, CAPS-1 project-manager pool, TASKS-1 back-end pool, two TASKS-2 cross-functional dev+test teams. The ASSESS step shows the strategic ambition (faster releases) requires speed, but the topology (Resource) optimises for utilisation. The DESIGN step proposes Delivery Topology with cross-functional end-to-end teams. The ELEVATE step (Phase 4 action design) restructures the Doing archetypes into two cross-functional end-to-end teams. The structural contributory factor named precisely: TASKS-2 teams asked to deliver CAPS-3 outcomes inside a Resource-Topology surround.

- **The pet-supply e-commerce as a post-AI-incident reframing exercise (pp. 20–21).** A pet-supply e-commerce business has experienced an AI-integration incident: an AI agent deployed for front-end design produced an unintended customer-experience outcome that wasn't caught until customer complaints surfaced. Running the contributory-factor analysis in OT vocabulary: the front-end AI agents were deployed inside a Resource-Topology framing (skill-pool replacement) without the structural change to CAPS-3 teams with monitoring responsibility built in. The structural action: elevate to CAPS-3 teams, with the existing front-end specialists assigned as agent monitors. The Primer's worked example reads as a *prevention pattern* the AAR can recommend for the next AI deployment.

- **The SAFe / Rendanheyi mappings as escalation-memo vocabulary (p. 22).** When an AAR's structural contributory factors require escalation to leadership previously committed to a framework choice (SAFe, LeSS, Spotify model), the framework-mapping framing in the Primer lets the escalation memo *use the framework name without endorsing or attacking it*: "SAFe lands the org mostly at TASKS-and-CAPS archetypes, which is fit for some classes of work and unfit for the work this incident showed we are now being asked to do." This makes the escalation an evidence-based reframing, not an attack on the framework choice.

## Integration with Other AAR References

- **LFUO Learning Review Guide (2024) — strong cross-reference.** LFUO is the primary methodology for contributory-factor analysis; OT is the *vocabulary* for the org-structure subset of contributory factors. LFUO's Learning Review pathway is the right home for structural recommendations; OT's named-archetype precision gives the recommendations their crispness. See `lfuo-learning-review-guide-2024-aar.md`.

- **TC 25-20 Army AAR — strong cross-reference.** TC 25-20's "contributory factors, not the cause" phrasing pairs with OT's structural-naming precision. Both reject single-cause framings. TC 25-20's *real benefits come from applying results* principle pairs with OT's *periodic re-mapping completes the learning loop*. See `tc-25-20-army-aar-aar.md`.

- **NHS Just Culture Guide — pair, do not substitute.** OT's strong systems-stance must not collapse the NHS five-test. Pair them: the NHS test sorts individual-vs-system culpability for the *person*, the OT vocabulary names the *structural* contributory factor. Both can coexist in one AAR. See `nhs-just-culture-guide-aar.md`.

- **SSDL Systems Thinking Foundations — strong cross-reference.** OT's MAP-step systems-thinking discipline maps directly onto SSDL's system-archetypes catalogue (shifting the burden, fixes that fail, limits to growth). When an AAR's structural contributory factors show feedback dynamics (utilisation pressure → team burnout → quality drop → utilisation pressure), SSDL is the named-archetype catalogue and OT is the org-structural naming. See `ssdl-systems-thinking-foundations-aar.md`.

- **Barbrook-Johnson Systems Mapping — methodology pair for Phase 2.** When the contributory-factor analysis needs a shared-model artefact, OT's map and Barbrook-Johnson's CLD / System Dynamics are alternative visualisation methods. OT maps the org structure; CLD maps the dynamic interactions. Use both for incidents where the structure and the dynamic both matter. See `barbrook-johnson-systems-mapping-aar.md`.

- **OpenStax Principles of Management — pair for Phase 4 escalation.** When the AAR action design surfaces org-design implications at the Mintzberg / six-structures level, OpenStax PoM is the catalogue. OT is at the *strategic-design-stance* level; PoM is at the *structure-catalogue* level. Both useful for escalation memos to non-engineering leadership. See `openstax-principles-management-aar.md`.

- **Open Practice Library Blameless Postmortem — methodology pair for Phase 4.** OT's "Elevating Kata as thoughtful experiment" framing for structural actions pairs with the Blameless Postmortem's two foundations (information availability + psychological safety). Both reject pronouncement-style remediation in favour of experimentation. See `open-practice-library-aar.md`.
