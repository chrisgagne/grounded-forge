# Scrum Guide 2020, AAR Distillation

**Source:** Ken Schwaber & Jeff Sutherland (2020). *The Scrum Guide: The Definitive Guide to Scrum: The Rules of the Game*. November 2020 edition. scrumguides.org. CC BY-SA 4.0. Scope: open.

## AAR Relevance

The Scrum Guide is not an AAR framework. Its projection onto the AAR axis is narrow but structurally important: Scrum's empirical-process-control foundation (Transparency → Inspection → Adaptation as a causal chain) gives the AAR its epistemological grounding — the AAR is the Adaptation step made structurally real. The Sprint Retrospective is the closest Scrum analogue to an AAR; the Guide's treatment of it names the inspection scope (individuals, interactions, processes, tools, Definition of Done) and the output discipline (most impactful improvements addressed as soon as possible). The Guide's Definition of Done as a pre-committed quality gate also has direct AAR application: it is this corpus's cleanest example of a pre-event decision rule that removes ambiguity about whether work was complete when the event under review occurred.

## Key Concepts for AAR

1. **Transparency → Inspection → Adaptation as a causal chain.** "Transparency enables inspection. Inspection without transparency is misleading and wasteful." "Inspection enables adaptation. Inspection without adaptation is considered pointless." (Source: Schwaber & Sutherland, *The Scrum Guide*, Scrum Theory, "Transparency"; "Inspection") The AAR is the Adaptation step in this chain; it requires Transparency (accounts that are honest, not self-protective) and Inspection (structured review, not debrief-as-therapy) before it can produce Adaptation (decisions that actually change the next iteration).

2. **The Sprint Retrospective as Scrum's Adaptation event.** "The purpose of the Sprint Retrospective is to plan ways to increase quality and effectiveness." "The Scrum Team identifies the most helpful changes to improve its effectiveness. The most impactful improvements are addressed as soon as possible." (Source: *The Scrum Guide*, Scrum Events, "Sprint Retrospective") In AAR terms: the retrospective is an iterative Adaptation move; the AAR is its event-triggered, cross-functional counterpart. The same epistemological discipline applies.

3. **Inspection scope — individuals, interactions, processes, tools, Definition of Done.** "The Scrum Team inspects how the last Sprint went with regards to individuals, interactions, processes, tools, and their Definition of Done. Inspected elements often vary with the domain of work. Assumptions that led them astray are identified and their origins explored." (Source: *The Scrum Guide*, Scrum Events, "Sprint Retrospective") The five named domains give the AAR facilitator a non-exhaustive checklist for Phase 2 contributory-factor analysis.

4. **Definition of Done as a pre-committed decision rule.** "The Definition of Done is a formal description of the state of the Increment when it meets the quality measures required for the product." "Work cannot be considered part of an Increment unless it meets the Definition of Done." (Source: *The Scrum Guide*, Scrum Artifacts, "Increment", "Commitment: Definition of Done") In AAR context: the DoD is the agreed standard against which performance during the event under review can be honestly assessed. Without a pre-committed standard, the AAR drifts into debating what good looked like rather than what happened.

5. **Empiricism over forecasting tools.** "Various practices exist to forecast progress, like burn-downs, burn-ups, or cumulative flows. While proven useful, these do not replace the importance of empiricism. In complex environments, what will happen is unknown. Only what has already happened may be used for forward-looking decision making." (Source: *The Scrum Guide*, Scrum Events, "The Sprint") In AAR context: metrics from the event under review are inputs, not verdicts. The AAR's job is to understand what happened, not to score it.

6. **Adaptation requires authority.** "Adaptation becomes more difficult when the people involved are not empowered or self-managing." (Source: *The Scrum Guide*, Scrum Theory, "Adaptation") If the AAR's output includes actions that require authority the team does not have, the AAR must route those to the level with authority — or the adaptation will not happen.

7. **Framework immutability as a lesson for AAR protocol discipline.** "The Scrum framework, as outlined herein, is immutable. While implementing only parts of Scrum is possible, the result is not Scrum." (Source: *The Scrum Guide*, End Note) By analogy: an AAR that skips the timeline phase, or the just-culture sorting, or the action-owner naming is not a full AAR. Partial process produces partial learning.

8. **Three accountabilities — no sub-teams, no hierarchies within the working unit.** "Within a Scrum Team, there are no sub-teams or hierarchies." (Source: *The Scrum Guide*, Scrum Team) In AAR context: the AAR conversation treats all participant accounts as equally valid inputs regardless of title. The epistemic discipline is flat even when the organisational structure is not.

9. **Sprint Review is a working session, not a presentation.** "The Sprint Review is a working session and the Scrum Team should avoid limiting it to a presentation." (Source: *The Scrum Guide*, Scrum Events, "Sprint Review") Scales directly to the AAR: a review that produces one account presented to an audience is a critique, not an AAR. The conversation is the mechanism.

10. **Definition of Done items that fail cannot be presented.** "If a Product Backlog item does not meet the Definition of Done, it cannot be released or even presented at the Sprint Review." (Source: *The Scrum Guide*, Scrum Artifacts, "Increment", "Commitment: Definition of Done") In AAR context: work that was not complete at the time of the event should not be reported as complete in the AAR account. The integrity of the timeline depends on honest done/not-done accounting.

## Questions to Ask During AAR

### Phase 0 — Scoping

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Grounding the scoping decision | What was the pre-committed standard (the DoD-equivalent) against which this event's performance will be assessed? If no such standard exists, the AAR will debate what good looks like rather than what happened. |
| Participant authority | Do the people in the room have the authority to commit to the adaptations this AAR is likely to surface? If not, who needs to be included or briefed? |

### Phase 1 — Timeline and Local-Rationality Reconstruction

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Transparency check | Are the accounts being offered honest, or are they self-protective? Has the container (Prime Directive, confidentiality rule) been set well enough that transparency is safe? |
| Metrics-as-evidence | Are the metrics from the event being used to understand what happened, or to score it? If they are being used to score, redirect to the empirical question: what actually happened, and what do we now know that we didn't know before? |

### Phase 2 — Contributory-Factor Analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Scope of inspection | Have all five domains been considered: individuals, interactions, processes, tools, and the DoD (or equivalent pre-committed standard)? |
| Assumptions surfaced | What assumptions, held before the event, led the team astray? Where did those assumptions come from? |

### Phase 4 — Action Design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Most impactful first | Of the improvements identified, which one — if done — would most increase effectiveness? That is the one to address first, not the easiest or the most popular. |
| Authority check | Does the team have the authority to implement this action, or does it need escalation? Name the escalation path before closing. |

### Phase 5 — Learning-Loop Closure

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Adaptation actually happening | Is there a specific next step, with a named owner and a named date? "We'll look into it" is inspection without adaptation — pointless. |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| The AAR closes with a list of actions but no named owners or dates | Inspection without adaptation — the Scrum Guide's explicit framing ("Inspection without adaptation is considered pointless") | Before the room clears: name one owner and one date for the most impactful item |
| The post-event review uses metrics to rate team performance rather than to understand what happened | Scoring instead of inspecting — empiricism requires using only what has already happened to inform forward-looking decisions, not to grade the past | Reframe: "What does this metric tell us about what happened? What does it not tell us?" |
| A pre-committed quality standard did not exist at the time of the event, and the AAR is debating what good looked like | No DoD-equivalent — the review is not comparing performance to a standard; it is constructing the standard retrospectively | Separate the two questions: "What happened?" from "What should the standard be going forward?" Address the first in the AAR; address the second as a follow-on action |
| The AAR is presented by one function to the others | Working-session discipline violated — a presentation produces one account without building a shared account | Restructure: ask each function to contribute its account before the synthesis begins |
| The actions surfaced require authority the team does not have, and no escalation path is named | Adaptation requires authority — the Scrum Guide is explicit that empowerment is a precondition for adaptation | Before closing: name who holds the authority, and commit to how and when the escalation will happen |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this source when:
- A team asks why AARs are worth doing at all — the empiricism argument (Transparency → Inspection → Adaptation) is the cleanest available answer.
- The AAR needs a scope checklist for Phase 2 and no domain-specific framework is available — the five Retrospective domains (individuals, interactions, processes, tools, DoD) provide a non-exhaustive starting structure.
- There is no pre-committed performance standard and the facilitator needs to name why this creates a problem — the DoD concept makes the issue concrete.
- The action list is long and the team needs a principle for prioritisation — the Retrospective's discipline of addressing "the most impactful improvements... as soon as possible" is the rule.
- A team is in an iterative delivery context and the facilitator needs to connect the AAR to the team's existing working vocabulary.

This source is most useful as an epistemological framing layer and a scope-of-inspection checklist. Its distinctive contribution is the causal chain (Transparency → Inspection → Adaptation) that gives the AAR's three-phase structure a principled foundation.

## Worked Example

A software delivery team holds a retrospective after a sprint in which a critical feature shipped with a defect that reached production. The meeting opens with the tech lead presenting a timeline and a root-cause conclusion. After ten minutes the room has agreed. The facilitator pauses.

The facilitator names the pattern: a presentation to an audience is not a working session (Source: *The Scrum Guide*, Scrum Events, "Sprint Review"). The Transparency pillar requires that "important decisions are based on the perceived state of its three formal artifacts" — and a single account is low-transparency (Source: Scrum Theory, "Transparency"). The facilitator restructures: each function submits their account of what happened before the group integrates.

The expanded accounts reveal that the Definition of Done for this sprint included a mandatory code-review gate that had been informally relaxed under time pressure. The defect-shipped event is now traceable to two factors: the gate was not met (DoD integrity failed, Source: Scrum Artifacts, "Increment", "Commitment: Definition of Done"), and the relaxation was not flagged as a team-level decision (Transparency degraded). The action design focuses on these structural conditions: restore the gate as non-negotiable, and create a visible escalation path when time pressure conflicts with the DoD. The most impactful improvement is addressed first (Source: Scrum Events, "Sprint Retrospective"). A named owner and date are recorded before the room clears.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Running an AAR whose output never reaches someone with the authority to act on it (inspection without adaptation).
- Treating post-event metrics as a verdict on people rather than as evidence for understanding what happened (scoring instead of inspecting).
- Skipping the timeline or contributory-factor phase because time is short — partial process produces partial learning.
- Conflating the AAR's output with the event's outcome: a poor outcome does not mean poor process, and a good outcome does not mean good process.
- Presenting a single account to an audience rather than running a working session that builds a shared account.
- Accepting "we'll look into it" as an action — adaptation requires a specific next step, named owner, and named date.
- Assessing performance against a standard that wasn't committed in advance, then discovering the debate is about what good looks like, not what happened.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The Scrum Guide is a self-contained framework definition with minimal external citation. Its only explicit external pointer within the text is the Creative Commons licence reference. Early collaborators (Jeff McKenna, John Scumniotales, Mike Smith, Chris Martin) and early adopting organisations (Individual Inc., Newspage, Fidelity Investments, IDX) are named in the End Note without external citation. The empiricism and lean-thinking foundations are asserted rather than cited; neither Deming, Ohno, nor the agile-movement antecedents are referenced within the Guide's body. No borrowed-through gaps affect the AAR-relevant concepts in this distillation directly.

**Named limits of the source.** The Scrum Guide defines a framework for iterative delivery work; it does not address post-incident review, just-culture methodology, or facilitator practice. Its AAR application is by analogy: the Retrospective's epistemological discipline transfers; the mechanics do not. The Guide's five Retrospective inspection domains (individuals, interactions, processes, tools, Definition of Done) are a starting checklist, not a complete contributory-factor framework. The Guide does not address attribution error, memory reliability, psychological safety mechanisms, or the structural conditions that make transparency achievable; those are carried by Psychology 2e, OPL, and TC 25-20 in this corpus. The Guide explicitly states it applies beyond software ("developers, researchers, analysts, scientists, and other specialists do the work") but its worked vocabulary remains software-team-shaped.

**Evidence-marker continuity.** All quotations in this distillation are `[V]` in the deep ref; the Scrum Guide is a short text with high verbatim-citation fidelity. The distillation quotes the Transparency, Inspection, and Adaptation causal-chain statements directly from the deep ref's verbatim extractions and preserves the section-name citation format `(Scrum Theory, "Transparency")` throughout. The Definition of Done gate rule ("Work cannot be considered part of an Increment unless it meets the Definition of Done") and the Sprint Review working-session rule ("the Scrum Team should avoid limiting it to a presentation") are both `[V]` in the deep ref and are applied here as paraphrased concepts with their source section preserved.
