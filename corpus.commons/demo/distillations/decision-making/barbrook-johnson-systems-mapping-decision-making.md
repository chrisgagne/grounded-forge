# Barbrook-Johnson & Penn, Systems Mapping — Decision-Making Distillation

**Source:** Barbrook-Johnson, P., & Penn, A. S. (2022). *Systems Mapping: How to build and use causal models of systems*. Palgrave Macmillan / Springer Nature. 197 pages. **Licence:** CC BY 4.0 (Open Access). **Distribution note:** Unrestricted with attribution.

## Decision-Making Relevance

*Systems Mapping* is not a decision-making book — it is a methods book for building causal models of complex systems — but it is dense with decision support because every mapping project requires structured choices: which method to use, which boundary to draw around the system, which factors and connections matter, what evidence to admit, what the analysis should answer, and (where the map is being used to support an external decision) where to intervene in the system. The guide makes four distinct decision-making contributions worth surfacing here:

1. **A structured framework for choosing a systems-mapping method.** The book's central decision-making artefact is the *appropriateness triangle* — a three-way triangulation between project purpose/needs/constraints, system characteristics, and method nature (Ch 11, Fig 11.1). Method choice is framed not as personal preference but as a triangulation problem with named biases that interfere with it.

2. **A framework for analysing where to intervene in a complex system.** Several methods produce intervention-relevant outputs: PSM's submap analysis surfaces vulnerable and controllable factors; FCM and System Dynamics simulate what changes propagate to which outcomes; BBN quantifies the impact of an intervention on outcome probabilities; Theory of Change makes the intervention-impact pathway explicit. The book is honest about the limits — none of these methods *predict* in the strict sense; they *clarify implications* of stated assumptions.

3. **A framework for choosing what data and evidence to admit.** Chapter 9's four-mode classification (participatory / qualitative / existing evidence / quantitative) and the "plea for creativity" in combining them is a decision-support tool for matching data to question.

4. **A discipline against three recurring decision-making failure modes in systems work.** The *horrendogram* failure mode (complexity displays that paralyse rather than guide); the *product-over-process* failure mode (treating the map as the deliverable rather than the discussion that produced it); the *Maslow's hammer* failure mode (applying one familiar method to every problem). All three are framed as decision-making anti-patterns.

The distillation projects all four contributions onto a working pattern. Where the guide is silent (e.g., on formal decision-theoretic frameworks like expected utility or multi-attribute utility), the silence is acknowledged.

## Key Concepts for Decision-Making

1. <!-- concept: systems-mapping --> **Method choice is appropriateness, not familiarity.** The decision triangulates project purpose / system characteristics / method nature. "Where a method fits with both the project and the system, we can say it is an 'appropriate' method" [V] (Source: Barbrook-Johnson & Penn, Ch 11, "Comparing Systems Mapping Methods"). Bias toward familiar methods, instinct toward quantitative or qualitative, and pressure from funders or clients are explicitly named as forces that interfere with the triangulation.

2. <!-- concept: data-availability --> **Data availability constraints are often overstated.** "In practice, people often feel that data availability constrains their modelling choices tightly. However, in reality, this is often only to fit into typical or accepted modes of using methods." The decision-making move is to be honest about how data availability shapes choice and about how that choice constrains interpretation, rather than to over-narrow methods on data availability grounds. (Source: Ch 11, "Choosing Systems Mapping Methods")

3. <!-- concept: systems-mapping --> **The seven methods sit on a qualitative-to-quantitative spectrum.** Rich Pictures (most qualitative) → Theory of Change → CLD → PSM → FCM → BBN → System Dynamics (most quantitative). The position on the spectrum is a primary decision input. Methods at the qualitative end are best at orientation and surfacing perspectives; methods at the quantitative end are best at extrapolating to implications under stated assumptions. (Source: Ch 1, "What's in the Rest of This Book?"; Ch 11, Tables 11.1–11.2)

4. <!-- concept: systems-mapping --> **The five-uses framework for what a system map will do for you.** *Helping us think* (forcing specificity); *helping us orient* (revealing positions); *helping us synthesise* (bringing different data types together); *helping us communicate* (surfacing assumptions); *helping us extrapolate* (running implications under assumptions). The first four are essentially process-value; only the fifth is extrapolation-value. Many decisions can be supported well by methods that deliver only the first four — the decision-maker needs to be clear which they need. (Source: Ch 1, "How Can Systems Mapping Be Useful?")

5. <!-- concept: process-vs-product-value --> **The process-vs-product value decision.** "Be clear about whether you want value from the process, outputs, or both, of a mapping process." Often the process — discussions and mutual understanding generated through map-building — delivers more value to stakeholders than the map itself. The decision affects how to design the engagement: a process-value-led design uses workshops, participation, and iteration; a product-value-led design uses focused researcher time and refined outputs. (Source: Ch 12, "Our Final Take-Home Messages")

6. <!-- concept: participatory-design --> **The choice between participation and conceptual rigour.** "In an ideal world we could do deeply participatory work which also produced models which were completely conceptually and scientifically sound. In practice, this is rarely possible." The decision-maker must pick one to prioritise, and manage stakeholder expectations accordingly. Stakeholders will include things a neutral expert would not, leading the model off on tangents that impede design decisions for a conceptually sound model. (Source: Ch 12, "Our Final Take-Home Messages")

7. <!-- concept: participatory-systems-mapping --> **PSM's submap analysis as a decision-relevant intervention scan.** Submaps generated from "system-suggested" factors (high centrality, bridging structure) surface where the system has structural sensitivity. Submaps generated from "stakeholder-suggested" factors (important, vulnerable, controllable) surface where stakeholders perceive structural sensitivity. A factor that is both *influential* and *controllable* is a *system lever* — a candidate intervention point. A factor that is *influential* and *vulnerable to change* is a *vulnerability*. The combinations support intervention design without requiring quantitative simulation. (Source: Ch 5, "What Is Participatory Systems Mapping?")

8. <!-- concept: theory-of-change --> **The Theory of Change "fill the gaps backward" decision pattern.** Start with the intervention (inputs and activities); jump to long-term impacts; *then* fill the gap by starting near the long-term impacts and working backwards through medium-term and short-term outcomes, then defining the outputs that lead to them. The reverse-engineering pattern surfaces missing intermediary steps that forward-only construction tends to gloss. (Source: Ch 3, "How Do You Create Theory of Change Diagrams?")

9. <!-- concept: theory-of-change --> **The Theory of Change "what if no change" prompt.** The authors recommend also specifying 'theories of no change' or 'theories of negative change' in the diagram — forcing realism about why the intervention might fail, or produce unintended consequences. Reasoning through those alternatives surfaces assumptions that a success-focused ToC hides. (Source: Ch 3, "How Do You Create Theory of Change Diagrams?")

10. <!-- concept: system-archetypes --> **CLD's eight system archetypes as decision-frame patterns (Kim 2000).** *Drifting goals* — goals erode in response to system stress; *escalation* — competitive dynamics where each party's response provokes another's; *fixes that fail* — short-term fixes that create longer-term problems; *growth and under-investment* — capacity does not scale with demand; *limits to success* — successful initiatives reaching constraints that flatten growth; *shifting the burden* — symptomatic fixes that obscure root causes; *success to the successful* — winners take a disproportionate share of resources; *tragedy of the commons* — individually rational behaviour depleting shared resources. When a system feels familiar from one of these patterns, the named pattern is a starting hypothesis for the core engine. (Source: Ch 4, "How Do You Create Causal Loop Diagrams?", citing Kim 2000)

11. <!-- concept: bayesian-belief-networks --> **BBN's probability-of-outcome computation as a structured intervention-test tool.** Setting intervention-type nodes to "on"/"off" and reading the resulting probability distribution on outcome nodes makes the intervention's claimed effect testable against expert and stakeholder belief. Conversely, setting an observed outcome state and reading the probability distribution on root nodes performs structured *backward attribution* — what conditions most likely contributed to this outcome? (Source: Ch 7, "How Do You Use Bayesian Belief Networks?")

12. <!-- concept: bayesian-belief-networks --> **BBN's wider-context capture via non-zero conditional probabilities.** When both interventions are "on" and the outcome still has a non-zero probability of not happening, the wider context "everything else going on in the system" is being captured in the probability table even though it is not in the network. This is the structural way BBN handles complexity without expanding the network. (Source: Ch 7, "What Are Bayesian Belief Networks?")

13. <!-- concept: system-dynamics --> **System Dynamics' "what if?" framing for testing intervention assumptions.** System Dynamics is well-suited to testing assumptions and exploring potential intervention impacts — the authors call these "'what if?' questions." The strength is high-level strategic insight on dynamical questions, not operational micro-decisions. (Source: Ch 8, "What Is System Dynamics Good and Bad At?")

14. <!-- concept: system-dynamics --> **System Dynamics' "soft factor inclusion" decision.** "Soft" factors — qualitative or hard-to-quantify components like *Attractiveness of the Maldives* in the worked tourism model — can be included in System Dynamics models if their behaviour can be sensibly described "going up and down" and parameterised. The decision is whether to include them (with the cost of analytic uncertainty) or to leave them out (with the cost of missing important dynamics). The authors' default is to include them. (Source: Ch 8, "What Is System Dynamics?")

15. <!-- concept: horrendogram --> **The horrendogram failure mode as a decision-making anti-pattern.** System maps that show complexity in an "unfiltered manner" can be unhelpful — even paralysing — communication tools: "system maps are sometimes referred to as 'horrendograms', and much worse (!), when they show us the complexity of a system in an unfiltered manner" [V] (Source: Barbrook-Johnson & Penn, Ch 1, "How Can Systems Mapping Be Useful?"). The decision-design move is to layer or filter the map for the audience, not to assume more complexity is more useful.

16. <!-- concept: theory-of-change --> **The ToC's "narrowness pressure" trap.** Multiple forces push toward an overly simple ToC: limited stakeholder input, slide-deck readability constraints, teams insisting on narrow scope close to their control. The authors recommend *two versions* — "the 'real one' and a simplified one, which you can use when you cannot resist the pressure to simplify." (Source: Ch 3, "Common Issues and 'Tricks of the Trade'")

17. <!-- concept: ladder-of-using-methods --> **The "ladder of using methods" as a meta-decision frame.** Rung 1: learn one method, apply to everything (the hammer-nail failure mode). Rung 2: become aware of similar methods and when to use them instead. Rung 3: master more methods and "understand the detail and nuance of how each method operates and so can make informed decisions, with stakeholders, about what to use and when". The decision-making move is to recognise which rung you (or your team) are on and to plan upward movement. (Source: Ch 12, "Our Final Take-Home Messages")

18. <!-- concept: method-combination --> **Combining methods as a decision-improvement move.** Sequentially (one method's output feeds another) or as hybrids (one map combining elements of two or more methods). When time and resources allow, "combining methods in some form will normally be useful or valuable" [V] (Source: Barbrook-Johnson & Penn, Ch 11, "Combining Systems Mapping Methods") because it produces holistic coverage and surfaces methodological innovation.

19. <!-- concept: complex-adaptive-systems --> **The participatory steering stance.** "We must accept that we cannot force or control the complex adaptive systems we live in and make up, but we can work with them to steer and nurture the change we want" [V] (Source: Barbrook-Johnson & Penn, Ch 12, "Our Final Take-Home Messages"). For decision-making, this is the meta-stance: decisions in complex systems are nudges and probes, not commands.

## Questions to Ask During Decision-Making

### Phase 1: Choosing the systems-mapping method (the appropriateness triangle)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| What is the project's purpose? | Is the map for orientation, communication, evaluation of an intervention, intervention design, or simulation of dynamics? Different purposes route to different methods. |
| What are the project's constraints? | How much stakeholder time? How much researcher time? What budget? Stakeholders' visual literacy? Existing data availability? |
| What is the system like? | Does it have important feedbacks (CLD, System Dynamics if so; BBN problematic if so)? Is it a whole system or a specific dynamical sub-problem (System Dynamics fits the latter)? Are there clear interventions and outcomes (Theory of Change, BBN)? Or is it a complex tangle to be mapped first (PSM, FCM, Rich Pictures)? |
| Am I being honest about which methods I'm biased toward? | Familiarity, quantitative-vs-qualitative instinct, funder/client pressure, recent successful project — these forces shape the triangulation before it becomes conscious. What would I choose if the biases were removed? |
| Is the data-availability constraint real? | Am I narrowing to methods that "fit the data" only because that's the typical mode? Most methods can be used in most data modes if I'm clear about what the choice means for output interpretation. |

### Phase 2: Process vs product — what value should this work deliver?

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Is the value I expect from this work in the process or the product? | Workshops, discussions, shared understanding (process value) — or the finished map and its analysis (product value)? Or both? |
| Are stakeholders expecting product value while the real value is process? | Often stakeholders are "surprised just how much they gain" from the process. Have I managed their expectations toward both? |
| If process value is paramount, am I designing a long enough, participatory enough engagement? | Single-workshop designs over-emphasise product; multi-touchpoint designs with discussion time produce process value. |
| If product value is paramount, am I getting researcher time concentrated enough? | Heavy stakeholder participation can dilute focus on producing a refined, conceptually sound product. |

### Phase 3: Participation vs conceptual rigour

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Am I aiming for a deeply participatory map or a conceptually rigorous one? | Both at once is rarely achievable. Stakeholders will want things included that a neutral expert would not. |
| If participation is the priority, am I prepared for the model design tensions? | Participation will mean tangents, inclusions that look conceptually off-key, lower confidence in "validity" claims. The trade is for ownership, learning, and applicability. |
| If conceptual rigour is the priority, am I being clear with stakeholders about that? | A more researcher-driven design needs careful framing so stakeholders don't feel sidelined. |
| Have I considered hybrid designs? | Participatory at the beginning (ownership, scoping), researcher-driven in the middle (rigour), participatory at the end (refinement, validation). |

### Phase 4: Defining boundaries and focal factors (cross-method)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| What is "your system" for this project? | The authors' working stance: don't dwell on what a system is in general; focus on what the system is for this purpose. |
| What is the system boundary? | A physical system (catchment, ecosystem)? A conceptual one (policy domain)? Defined by a problem rather than a physical space? Boundary choice will dramatically shape the analysis. |
| What are the focal factors / nodes / outcomes? | What does the project care about? What do stakeholders care about? Are the most important parts of the system covered by focal factors? Are there absent stakeholder groups whose focal factors should be added? |

### Phase 5: Choosing data and evidence to admit

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Should this map draw on a participatory process? | Default for complex adaptive systems with limited data; default for projects where stakeholder ownership matters; default when "system effects might indeed be important" but no quantitative grounding is available. |
| Should this map draw on qualitative interview/focus-group data? | When deep contextual understanding matters but participatory mapping is not feasible. Warning: combining accounts produces "a composite that no stakeholder has described". |
| Should this map draw on existing evidence (literature)? | When a domain has strong systematic-review traditions; when evidential support for individual nodes/edges is required. Warning: coverage will be patchy with systematic gaps. |
| Should this map draw on quantitative data? | When pairwise statistical relationships are available; when there's time-series data across many relevant variables for *causal discovery*. Warning: restricts the map to what is quantifiable and measured. |
| Should I combine modes? | "Given enough time and resources, it is hard to think of a reason why we would not want to combine them wherever possible." |

### Phase 6: Using the map to support an external decision

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| What kind of decision-support is the map providing? | Orientation (where am I in the system?); option enumeration (what could I do?); option evaluation (which option produces the desired outcome under stated assumptions?); intervention scan (where are the system levers and vulnerabilities?); communication (how do I explain this to others?). |
| If using PSM, what submaps are decision-relevant? | "Two-three steps upstream/downstream" of a candidate intervention shows immediate effects; an ego network shows the immediate context; paths between an intervention and an outcome show the causal logic. Combine via unions/intersections. |
| If using Theory of Change, has the assumption surface been adequate? | The "what if no change?" and "what if negative change?" prompts force realism about the intervention's failure modes. |
| If using CLD, which feedback loops am I trying to disrupt or strengthen? | "Disrupting a 'vicious cycle' or encouraging a 'virtuous' one is probably the most intuitive way in which we can think about interaction with system dynamics." |
| If using BBN, what scenario is the decision under? | Set the intervention-node states, read the outcome distributions. Compare scenarios as conditional probability changes. |
| If using FCM, am I treating the analysis as "magic"? | The dynamic analysis "can seem like magic (especially when done live in a workshop) and offer a false sense of certainty". Use as discussion tool, not as forecast. |
| If using System Dynamics, am I asking a dynamical question? | System Dynamics is for dynamical sub-problems, not whole systems and not micro-operational decisions. |

### Phase 7: Avoiding decision-analysis anti-patterns

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Am I producing a horrendogram? | Is the map filterable, layerable, or staged for the audience? Or is it a single overwhelming display? |
| Am I treating the map as the product when the process is the real value? | Stakeholders may signal interest in "the final map" but the real value is often in the workshop discussions; design accordingly. |
| Am I on rung 1 of the ladder (hammer-nail)? | Am I choosing this method because it's the only one I know? Could a different method better fit the appropriateness triangle? |
| Am I narrowing the ToC to please clients? | "Pressures to create a relatively simple ToC diagram"; consider keeping the real version even if a simplified one is also produced for communication. |
| Am I over-quantifying soft factors? | In FCM and System Dynamics, soft factors often need to be included but should not be over-precisified — their inclusion is to make dynamics visible, not to claim measurement. |
| Am I letting threshold-function choice dominate FCM analysis? | If using FCM in the causal mode, the threshold function form can reverse results — extensive sensitivity analysis is required. |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| Team reaches for the same method on every project regardless of project purpose or system type | Maslow's hammer failure mode — rung 1 of the ladder of using methods (Source: Ch 12, "Our Final Take-Home Messages") | Apply the appropriateness triangle: triangulate project purpose/constraints, system characteristics, and method nature before committing |
| Stakeholder engagement designed around producing a polished final map rather than working discussions | Product-over-process failure mode — the real value often lives in the discussions, not the deliverable (Source: Ch 12, "Our Final Take-Home Messages") | Redesign the engagement for process value: multi-session workshops, open iteration, explicit surfacing of what participants learned |
| Systems map that shows every factor and every connection at once, intended as the output | Horrendogram failure mode — complexity displays that paralyse rather than guide (Source: Ch 1, "How Can Systems Mapping Be Useful?"; Ch 4, "Causal Loop Diagrams") | Layer or filter the map for the audience; use submap extraction or staged display; allocate half of design resources to communication |
| Data availability presented as the main reason for method choice | Over-narrow selection on data grounds — most methods can be used in most data modes if constraints on interpretation are stated (Source: Ch 11, "Choosing Systems Mapping Methods") | Ask: "Is this constraint real, or is it the typical mode?" State the constraint explicitly and adjust interpretation claims rather than ruling out methods |
| FCM dynamic analysis results reported as simulation output or near-certain forecast | Over-interpretation failure mode — FCM is "a form of quantitative storytelling", not prediction; threshold-function choice can reverse results (Source: Ch 6, "What Is Fuzzy Cognitive Mapping Good and Bad At?"; Ch 6, "Common Issues and 'Tricks of the Trade'") | Reframe FCM outputs as discussion tools; report sensitivity analysis; do not present the analysis as magic |
| Theory of Change diagram has been simplified to a handful of boxes under funder or client pressure | Narrowness-pressure trap — the simplified version hides the real causal theory (Source: Ch 3, "Common Issues and 'Tricks of the Trade'") | Maintain two versions: the full "real" ToC and the simplified communication version; use the "what if no change?" prompt to surface hidden assumptions |
| Feedbacks are structurally important but the team is building a BBN | Wrong-method-for-feedbacks signal — BBN is acyclic; feedbacks are its core structural weakness (Source: Ch 7, "What Are Bayesian Belief Networks?") | Switch to CLD, FCM, or System Dynamics; or use dynamic BBN with time-period instances, acknowledged as a workaround |

## When to Use This Reference

- A project involves a complex system and the team needs to choose between mapping methods — the appropriateness triangle is the primary tool.
- A project team is defaulting to a familiar method before asking whether that method fits the project's purpose and the system's characteristics.
- A stakeholder engagement needs a participation-vs-conceptual-rigour decision: the Guide names the trade explicitly and equips the decision-maker to choose.
- A mapping project is at risk of producing a horrendogram: use the submap-based analysis, the layering and filtering techniques, and the "allocate half to communication" heuristic.
- A Theory of Change is being built and needs to surface its assumptions: the fill-gaps-backward pattern and the "what if no change?" prompt are the tools.
- A PSM project needs to identify candidate intervention points: the submap-based lever/vulnerability analysis is the method.
- A project is considering combining methods — the Guide's sequential-combinations diagram and hybrid-elements table are the reference.
- FCM dynamic analysis has been done in a workshop and the results need framing for stakeholders: the "quantitative storytelling, not prediction" framing is critical.
- A BBN has been built but feedbacks are proving important: the Guide gives the structural reason to switch methods and the dynamic-BBN workaround.
- The meta-question is not which tool to use now, but how to build the team's capacity to make better tool choices over time — the ladder-of-using-methods frame.
- Reach for the SSDL Systems Thinking Foundations distillation when the question is conceptual foundations and system archetypes rather than which mapping method to choose for a project.

## Worked Example

A policy team has been asked to develop a programme to reduce hospital readmissions in a regional health system. The team's immediate instinct is to build a System Dynamics model because a team member used one successfully on a previous project. Before committing, the facilitator applies the appropriateness triangle (Source: Ch 11, "Comparing Systems Mapping Methods"): the project's purpose is to identify candidate interventions and communicate options to a mixed clinical-administrative stakeholder group; the system is complex, with important feedbacks between readmission rates, care-coordination capacity, and patient behaviour; the time budget is four stakeholder workshops over six weeks.

System Dynamics is formally quantitative and suited to dynamical sub-problems with available data — but the team has no time-series data on the relevant variables, and the stakeholder group's visual literacy is mixed. The appropriateness triangle routes instead toward Participatory Systems Mapping: PSM is suited to data-poor contexts, supports participatory engagement, and produces submaps that make the analysis accessible without a quantitative simulation (Source: Ch 5, "What Is Participatory Systems Mapping?").

In workshops one and two, stakeholders build a PSM map of approximately 60 factors, annotating each with importance, vulnerability, and controllability ratings. In workshop three, the facilitating team extracts submaps: a two-steps-upstream submap from the "readmission rate" outcome factor reveals three high-centrality bridging factors — care-transition handoffs, patient health literacy, and post-discharge follow-up capacity (Source: Ch 5, "What Is Participatory Systems Mapping?"). A controllability × influence overlay identifies care-transition handoffs as both a system lever and a stakeholder-perceived point of intervention. In workshop four, the team uses a Theory of Change map to make the intervention-to-impact pathway explicit and surfaces two "what if no change?" scenarios to test the causal assumptions (Source: Ch 3, "How Do You Create Theory of Change Diagrams?").

The final deliverable is not the full 60-factor map but three targeted submaps plus the ToC — a layered output that avoids the horrendogram failure mode (Source: Ch 1, "How Can Systems Mapping Be Useful?"). All scenario citations trace back through the deep reference.

## Anti-patterns this distillation helps avoid

- **Choosing a method by familiarity rather than appropriateness** — the appropriateness triangle is the antidote.
- **Letting data availability narrow the method choice prematurely** — most methods can be used in most data modes if the constraints on interpretation are made explicit.
- **Producing a horrendogram** — unfiltered complexity displays that paralyse rather than guide.
- **Treating the map as the deliverable when the process delivers most of the value** — stakeholders often experience the process value as the surprise.
- **Trying to be both fully participatory and fully conceptually rigorous** — the trade is real; pick one as primary and design accordingly.
- **The Maslow's hammer failure mode** — applying one method to every problem because it's the one we know.
- **Treating System Dynamics as a whole-system method** — it is best at dynamical sub-problems.
- **Over-interpreting FCM dynamic analysis as forecast or simulation** — the analysis is a "form of quantitative storytelling", not prediction.
- **Letting FCM threshold-function choice silently dominate causal-mode results** — sensitivity analysis is required.
- **Constructing a ToC that hides its assumptions** — the "fill gaps backward" pattern and "what if no change?" prompt force assumptions to the surface.
- **Treating BBN outputs as prediction-precision rather than belief-update** — non-zero conditional probabilities are the structural way wider context enters the analysis.
- **Adding feedback loops to a BBN by force** — if feedbacks are central, the right method is CLD, FCM, or System Dynamics, not BBN with cyclical workarounds.
- **Modelling "soft" factors as if they were objective measurements** — they belong in the model, but their inclusion is to make dynamics visible, not to claim measurement precision.
- **Single-method tunnel vision** — combining methods (sequential or hybrid) often surfaces what single-method work misses.
- **Treating "validation" as a binary on quantitative grounds** — in genuine complex adaptive systems, we will rarely have the data needed to validate every node and edge, and this is not a reason to abandon mapping.

**Named limits of the source.** The book explicitly excludes formal decision-theory frameworks (expected utility, multi-attribute utility, decision trees with probability distributions), cost-benefit analysis of decision options, agent-based modelling, behavioural decision-making at the individual level, decision-making under deep uncertainty (scenario planning, robust decision-making), and real-time operational decision-making. All seven methods support deliberative analysis, not real-time choice. The book is honest that for genuine complex adaptive systems, quantitative validation of every node and edge is rarely achievable and is not a reason to abandon mapping.
