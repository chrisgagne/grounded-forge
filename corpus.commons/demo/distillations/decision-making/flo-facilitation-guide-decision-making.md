# Currie, Riessner, Bennett & Cougler Blom, FLO Facilitation Guide — Decision-Making Distillation

**Source:** Bennett, G., Cougler Blom, B., Riessner, S., & Currie, S. (2019). *FLO Facilitation Guide*. Victoria, B.C.: BCcampus. **Licence:** CC BY 4.0. Open scope; attribution required on derivative works.

## Decision-Making Relevance

The FLO Facilitation Guide is primarily a facilitation-craft handbook, not a decision-making framework — but it carries a dense set of *facilitation design decisions* that recur across any synchronous-or-asynchronous learning engagement. The Guide's load-bearing decision-making contributions are (a) the *facilitate-vs-transmit* framing as a fundamental method-choice rule for any learning conversation, (b) the *scaffolding-and-fading* presence allocation rule that decides where facilitator attention goes across the arc of a course, (c) the *synchronous-vs-asynchronous* design-mode decision with named criteria, (d) the *two-track choice architecture* (FLO Synchronous's Reviewing Participant vs Practicing Facilitator) as a worked example of designed-in difficulty selection, (e) the *facilitator-stance calibration* decisions (heavy-handed → balanced presence → light hand, calibrated to course type and phase), (f) the *recording-and-tool consent* decisions, (g) the *counsel-out decision* about whether a struggling participant should continue, (h) the *develop-vs-grade* default and its escape hatch, and (i) the *modelling-as-deliberate-design* rule that constrains facilitator behaviour in the same way it constrains design choices.

The frame to bring with you when reading the Guide for decision-making content: most of the load-bearing decisions are made *before* the course begins. Each course chapter opens with task lists structured around several-weeks-before, one-to-two-weeks-before, and a-few-days-before phases. The in-course decisions are mostly small ones at the point of facilitator move (which post to make, whom to reach out to, when to step back); the large decisions (course shape, technology, accessibility, recording, privacy, assessment mode, mentoring pairings) are largely pre-committed and the in-course work depends on the pre-course design.

## Key Concepts for Decision-Making

1. <!-- concept: facilitation --> **Facilitate, don't transmit, is the method-choice rule.** The shift from "sage on the stage" to "guide on the side" (Ch 1, "What is facilitation in an educational context?") is positioned as the foundational method decision for any learning engagement. When designing a learning conversation, the first question is whether the design is transmissive (one source-of-truth delivering knowledge) or facilitative (a group co-constructing meaning through questions and shared work). The two modes produce different artefacts and different learner experiences; treating one as a substitute for the other is a category error.

2. <!-- concept: scaffolding --> **Scaffolding-and-fading presence allocation.** The Guide's role arc (Ch 3, "Roles and functions of FLO facilitators") is an explicit presence-budget allocation: high presence in Week 1 (community builder, guide), tapering to lighter presence in later weeks (coach, mentor stepping back). The decision is when and how fast to fade. The default is "by Weeks 4 and 5 you should find yourself pulling back" (Ch 4, "Your shifting role"); the indicator is participant capacity to manage the discussion. Decision-makers running staged engagements should pre-plan the presence arc, not improvise it.

3. <!-- concept: online-facilitation --> **Synchronous vs asynchronous design-mode decision.** The Guide treats the mix as a design choice with named criteria. FLO Fundamentals defaults to mostly asynchronous with optional synchronous (Ch 4, "What is FLO Fundamentals?"); FLO Synchronous is explicitly about the synchronous mode; FLO MicroCourses are asynchronous (Ch 7, Synopsis/abstract). The Guide cites Hrastinski (2008) and Yamagata-Lynch (2014) for the "when, why and how" of each mode (App 1). Decision-makers choosing a mode should not default; they should match the mode to the learning outcome and the participant constraints.

4. <!-- concept: choice-architecture --> **Two-track choice architecture.** FLO Synchronous (Ch 6) offers participants a track choice — Reviewing Participant (lighter commitment, focused on observation and feedback) or Practicing Facilitator (heavier commitment, focused on leading a session and receiving feedback). The decision-design rule: when an engagement requires varied levels of stretch, design two tracks rather than one default and an opt-out. The Guide's evidence is that without active encouragement, participants default to the lighter track ("their natural inclination might be to play it safe"), so the facilitator's job includes encouraging risk-taking onto the harder track when class composition requires it (Ch 6, "Help participants choose").

5. <!-- concept: facilitator-development --> **Facilitator-stance calibration by course type and phase.** The Guide explicitly calibrates facilitator stance differently across courses. In FLO Fundamentals participant-led weeks, the facilitator is "less visible" (Ch 4, "What is FLO Fundamentals?") — visible enough to support, invisible enough to let the participant team facilitate. In FLO MicroCourses, the facilitator stance is "light hand": "Be concerned only if an uncomfortable amount of time goes by without any replies. Only offer genuine feedback. Resist the urge to be thorough, but be clear that you're only commenting on one or two aspects of the artifact" [V] (Source: Bennett, Cougler Blom, Riessner & Currie, *FLO Facilitation Guide*, Ch 7, "Monitor the sharing of drafts and prototypes"). In FLO Design Week 3, the stance is "both broad and deep" — connect with strugglers, celebrate aha moments, deepen feedback (Ch 5, "Week 3"). The decision is which stance the moment calls for; staying in one stance regardless of phase degrades the engagement.

6. <!-- concept: recording-consent --> **Recording is a consent decision, not a default.** "If you plan on recording your synchronous sessions, confirm that this is OK with the session participants *before* you hit the record button" (Ch 4, "Privacy and Confidentiality"). The decision is per-session, made explicit at the start, and recorded as agreement; not assumed because the platform supports it.

7. <!-- concept: tool-adoption-vetting --> **Tool-adoption vetting with six questions.** When considering a tool not supported by the institution, six diagnostic questions: Why do I want to use it? Does my institution have rules? What affordances? What's the learner impact (privacy, sign-up)? Can I provide alternatives? What happens to recorded/created content? (Ch 3, "Using open source or social media tools"). The decision rule: tools are vetted on the dimensions that matter to learners and to the institution, not on apparent novelty or convenience.

8. <!-- concept: counsel-out --> **Counsel-out is part of the facilitator's role.** "If you find out a participant is deeply struggling, perhaps from an overwhelming schedule, your role is to help them decide if now is the right time to take the course. We strongly advise against allowing participants to audit FLO courses" [V] (Source: Bennett, Cougler Blom, Riessner & Currie, *FLO Facilitation Guide*, Ch 6, "Be in touch with participants who haven't logged in/aren't participating"). The decision: when a participant cannot meet the design's participation requirements, the facilitator helps them choose to defer rather than allowing degraded participation that compromises both learner outcomes and group dynamics.

9. <!-- concept: develop-vs-grade --> **Develop-vs-grade default with explicit opt-in.** "This course is designed to be developmental; it is not a graded course in the traditional sense. Unless you decide otherwise, your participants will not be marked or assigned a formal grade on their performance" (Ch 4, "Course assessment strategies"). The rubric "could be used to assign a grade, if needed" (Ch 4). The decision rule: developmental engagements default to self-assessment via rubric; grading is the explicit opt-in. Decision-makers designing any developmental engagement should be deliberate about which default applies and why.

10. <!-- concept: modelling-as-design --> **Modelling as deliberate design.** The Guide treats modelling as a designed-in mechanism, not as facilitator-style. "It is important in FLO Synchronous to model the kinds of synchronous online facilitation skills that we hope participants will be able to learn and practice in the course" [V] (Source: Bennett, Cougler Blom, Riessner & Currie, *FLO Facilitation Guide*, Ch 6, "Create/update the lesson plan and slide deck"). The decision is what to model: lesson plans (not just slide decks); interactive sessions; feedback-eliciting activities. Modelling decisions are made at the design stage and constrain what the facilitator does in-session.

11. <!-- concept: learning-climate --> **No-do-or-die calibration of intensity.** "Be open to modifying your approach/expectations to adapt to the needs of your cohort. The purpose is to help them develop, NOT to create a 'do-or-die' atmosphere" [V] (Source: Bennett, Cougler Blom, Riessner & Currie, *FLO Facilitation Guide*, Ch 5, "Week 3: Projects under construction"). The decision-design rule: design intensity to be challenging without being threatening; if the cohort can't handle Week-3 pace, modify the plan rather than amplify pressure.

12. <!-- concept: course-design --> **Class-size and time-budget pre-commitments.** Fundamentals 14–20; Synchronous 10–20 with two facilitators; MicroCourses 10+ active with no upper limit (Ch 4, 6, 7). Facilitator weekly time 15–25 hours (Fundamentals), ~15 hours (Design/Synchronous), ≥5 hours (MicroCourse). Participant weekly time 6–12 hours (Fundamentals), 6–8 hours (Design/Synchronous), ~5 hours (MicroCourse). The decisions are made at the recruit-and-promote stage; deviation degrades the design.

13. <!-- concept: co-facilitation --> **Co-facilitator selection on complementarity, not seniority.** Robin Leung's reflection: "I learnt that you need a co-facilitator that balances with your life well. I had a more technical background and my co-facilitator had more of the facilitation background. I am a night owl (catching most of the activities at night), and she's the early riser (catching most of the activities in the day time). I think we complemented one another very well" (Ch 7, "Two heads are better than one"). The decision criterion is complementarity along multiple dimensions (skills, working hours, life rhythm), not seniority or convenience.

14. <!-- concept: anxious-annie-pattern --> **The Anxious-Annie pattern as a recognised facilitator-decision failure mode.** "Then, I remember facilitating is not about me; it's about helping the participants as they need and ask for help" (Ch 4, Leonne Beebe's reflection). The decision-design rule: when the facilitator's anxiety is driving a move (chasing inactive participants, over-responding, intervening early), name the anxiety, attribute it to one's own pattern rather than to a participant problem, and trust the process. The pattern is recognisable and the response is structured.

15. <!-- concept: point-of-decision-support --> **Sidebar-form as point-of-decision support.** The Guide deploys named short instructional blocks at the point in the workflow where the relevant decision is made: "Be pro-active in the first week"; "Welcome feedback throughout the course"; "Encourage richer, deeper reflection"; "Stay calm and carry on". The form-and-placement convention is itself a decision-support pattern: surface the relevant guidance where the decision is made, not in a separate decision-aid document.

## Questions to Ask During Decision-Making

### Phase 1: Framing (recognising what kind of decision is being made)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Are we designing a facilitative or transmissive engagement? | The Guide's method-choice rule is foundational. If we are about to default to one-source-of-truth content delivery in an engagement that should be facilitative (because the learning is in the conversation, not the content), or vice versa, we are setting up the wrong shape from the start. |
| Are we choosing a synchronous, asynchronous, or mixed design? | The Guide cites Hrastinski (2008) and Yamagata-Lynch (2014) for the criteria. What does the learning outcome require? What do participant constraints (schedule, time zone, technology) permit? Defaulting to one mode without considering the other is a missed design decision. |
| Is this a develop-vs-grade engagement, and what is the default? | FLO defaults to developmental self-assessment via rubric; grading is the explicit opt-in. If we are about to grade, why? If we are not, are we making that default visible to participants up front? |
| Is the engagement participatory enough to require commitment, or can it accommodate light participation? | FLO Fundamentals/Design/Synchronous "require a lot of commitment"; FLO MicroCourses tolerate non-engagement explicitly. The design tier matters: choosing the wrong tier produces either burnout or no learning. |
| Have we identified the Anxious-Annie pattern in ourselves? | Is a current move driven by the facilitator's anxiety about participant inactivity, slow uptake, or sparse forum posts? If so, is the trust-the-process response appropriate, or is intervention genuinely needed? |

### Phase 2: Bounding (defining the decision space)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| What is the class-size sweet spot for this engagement type? | Fundamentals 14–20; Synchronous 10–20; MicroCourses 10+ active. If we are about to run with materially fewer or more participants, what design adjustments are required? |
| What is the realistic facilitator and participant time budget? | Fundamentals 15–25 / 6–12 hours weekly; Design ~15 / 6–8 hours; MicroCourse ≥5 / ~5 hours. Are we budgeting realistically for what the design requires, or are we pretending the engagement will fit in half the time? |
| How many co-facilitators does this design need, and on what complementarity criteria? | Two facilitators for Synchronous classes in the 20s; co-facilitation recommended across all four courses. What is the complementarity criterion — skills, working hours, life rhythm? Have we picked on those dimensions rather than on seniority or convenience? |
| What is the lead time on the engagement? | Each course chapter opens with several-weeks-before tasks. If we are convening last-minute, what tasks in the several-weeks-before phase are being skipped, and what does the design look like with those tasks absent? |
| What is the technology stack and the privacy posture? | LMS + persistent forums + resource storage + synchronous web conferencing is the baseline. For additional tools, the six-question vetting (purpose, institutional rules, affordances, learner impact, alternatives, content-ownership) applies. |
| What is our recording posture? | Recording is per-session consent, not default. Are we set up to ask consent at the start of each synchronous session, or are we set up to assume? |

### Phase 3: Exploring (generating alternatives)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Are there two distinct levels of stretch in this engagement, and should the design make them an explicit track choice? | FLO Synchronous's Reviewing Participant vs Practicing Facilitator is the worked example. If our engagement has participants with varying readiness for stretch, would explicit-track-architecture serve better than single-track-with-opt-out? |
| Are we designing for the participant who completes the full programme, or for the participant who can only afford a short dip? | The FLO MicroCourse design exists specifically to address the second; it is short, single-topic, and tolerates non-engagement. If our population includes people who cannot commit to a multi-week course, do we have an equivalent? |
| Are we using studio-based, activity-packet, or week-by-week-team-facilitation as the participatory structure? | FLO Design uses weekly Studio forums; FLO Fundamentals uses week-by-week participant team facilitation; FLO MicroCourse uses the 5-step Activity Packet. Each maps to a different learning-outcome shape (design project; facilitation skill; single artefact). Which fits our outcome? |
| What is the modelling decision for this engagement? | What practices do we want participants to learn? Have we designed in opportunities for facilitators to model those practices visibly? (Lesson plans not just slide decks; interactive sessions; feedback-eliciting activities; reflective practice.) |
| What is the reflection structure we will design in? | Learning Journal + weekly nuggets, FLIF six-question survey, 3-2-1 reflection, Looking-Back/Looking-Forward closing? Which structure fits the engagement length, the participant readiness, and the learning outcome? |
| Have we considered group-agreements as a Week-1 design move? | FLO Design uses agreements posted in Week 1, reviewed during the synchronous session. What are the corresponding agreements we need to surface and refine with the cohort? |

### Phase 4: Deciding (analysing and selecting)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Calibrate facilitator stance to course phase. | Early high presence, later lighter; participant-led weeks "less visible"; peer-review phases "light hand"; mid-course feedback "broad and deep". Are we choosing the right stance for the phase, or applying one stance across the entire engagement? |
| Calibrate intensity to cohort capacity. | "Be open to modifying your approach/expectations to adapt to the needs of your cohort. The purpose is to help them develop, NOT to create a 'do-or-die' atmosphere." If our Week-3-equivalent is pacing the cohort into burnout, what is our modification? |
| Apply the develop-vs-grade default deliberately. | Is this engagement defaulting to developmental self-assessment via rubric? If we are grading, why is that the right move? If we are not, is the no-grade default visible to participants up front? |
| Counsel-out the participant who cannot meet the design's requirements. | "Help them decide if now is the right time." When a participant is deeply struggling and the design depends on participation, our move is to support a defer-decision, not to allow degraded participation. |
| Apply the no-pressuring-tone discipline. | "Avoid pressuring participants to comply with course deadlines, or suggesting they are only welcome if they are committed to following the activity plan." If our communication is policing rather than supporting, we have slipped tone. |
| Recording-consent decision is per-session. | At the start of each session, ask consent. Make the recording decision explicit, time-stamped, and recorded as agreement. |
| Tool-adoption decision uses six-question vetting. | Purpose, institutional rules, affordances, learner impact, alternatives, content-ownership. If we are adopting a tool without going through all six, we are making the decision incompletely. |

### Phase 5: Ratifying (committing to and communicating the decision)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Welcome post sets the tone and the decision-frame. | "This is your first impression"; establish instructor presence; clear message neither too long nor too short; ends with invitation. Does our convening communication carry the decisions we want participants to know we have made? |
| Lock in pre-course design decisions in the convening agreement. | Each course chapter's several-weeks-before / one-to-two-weeks-before task list is the worked example. What is our equivalent pre-course commitment? Attendance, schedule, technology, privacy posture, recording posture, assessment mode. |
| Modelling decisions are part of the design, not the in-session improvisation. | Lesson plans (not just slide decks); interactive sessions; feedback-eliciting activities; visible reflection. Have we pre-decided what we will model, or are we hoping it will happen? |
| The closing decision lives in the design. | "Closing the course: An important facilitator role is to draw the course to a close and celebrate learning, connections and accomplishments." The closing structure (synchronous celebration, asynchronous bulletin board, mind map, video) is a pre-decided move, not an improvised end. |
| Communicate the privacy and Vegas-rules decisions explicitly at the start. | "Our learning relies on the exchange of honest, constructive feedback, and we need to agree on rules that support this foundation of trust." The decisions are stated, not assumed. |

### Phase 6: Monitoring (post-decision review)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Has the scaffolding-and-fading actually faded? | "By Weeks 4 and 5 you should find yourself pulling back." If we are still in heavy-presence mode at Week 5, is the cohort actually not ready, or are we failing to fade? Use the tracking matrix to check. |
| Has the develop-vs-grade default produced developmental signal? | Are participants using the self-assessment rubrics? Are they completing the FLIF (or equivalent)? If the developmental design is producing only attendance data, the design is not landing. |
| Has the FLIF (or equivalent reflective practice) produced learning? | "The FLIF (Feel, Like, Improve, Feedback) survey is a valuable way to help your participant facilitation teams debrief their facilitation experience." Are we using the FLIF output to inform next-round design decisions, or filing it? |
| Has the post-course debrief happened? | "Don't part company with your co-facilitators without scheduling a debrief. It's important to share experiences and ideas about the facilitation process, otherwise you are destined to repeat what could have been improved!" The debrief is a decision-loop closure, not a courtesy. |
| Has the next-iteration design incorporated this iteration's lessons? | The Guide's after-the-course tasks include "Make notes of things that you'd like to enhance or change for next time – especially while it's fresh in your memory!" Are the changes captured, prioritised, and integrated into the next pre-course design? |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| Facilitator posts more frequently in Weeks 4–5 than in Week 1 | Presence-fading arc has reversed — the scaffolding-and-fading rule requires pulling back as participant capacity grows (Source: Ch 4, "Your shifting role: From facilitator to guide") | Use the tracking matrix to audit your post frequency week-by-week; deliberately reduce post volume and shift to summary and coaching posts |
| Participants are completing rubrics without reducing their dependence on facilitator guidance | Developmental-default is not landing — self-assessment is the signal, not a substitute for facilitator withdrawal (Source: Ch 3, "Roles and functions of FLO facilitators") | Ask directly what participants feel they could manage without facilitator input; introduce explicit participant-led facilitation responsibility |
| A participant has not logged in or posted for several days and the facilitator is checking in daily | Anxious-Annie pattern — anxiety about inactivity is driving a facilitator move that should be prompted by participant need, not facilitator discomfort (Source: Ch 4, "Example of reflective post") | Name the anxiety; check whether the absence is a signal (participant struggling) or noise (life disruption); if struggling, offer a defer conversation rather than escalating contact |
| Team adopted a new synchronous platform without discussing privacy, data ownership, or learner alternatives | Tool-adoption vetting bypassed — the six diagnostic questions were not applied (Source: Ch 3, "Using open source or social media tools") | Run the six questions retroactively; if any question cannot be answered, suspend use and find an alternative |
| Two co-facilitators chosen because one is senior and the other is available | Seniority-and-convenience selection — complementarity on skill mix, working hours, and life rhythm is the criterion (Source: Ch 7, "Two heads are better than one") | Re-examine co-facilitation pairing on complementarity dimensions before the course runs; add a co-facilitation plan that divides tasks explicitly |
| Week 3 equivalent produces participant anxiety or burnout signals | Intensity miscalibration — the do-or-die atmosphere failure mode (Source: Ch 5, "Week 3: Projects under construction") | Modify approach explicitly: reduce scope, extend timeline, or provide direct facilitator scaffolding; name the adjustment to participants so the course doesn't feel like a failure |
| Course closes without a structured closing activity | Designed-closing omission — the closing structure is a pre-committed move, not improvised (Source: Ch 5, "Week 4: Showcase designs"; Ch 7, "Write a closing post") | Restore the closing: synchronous celebration, Looking Back/Looking Forward activity, or final forum summary; debrief with co-facilitators before parting |

## When to Use This Reference

- An online or blended facilitation engagement is being designed from scratch and the core decisions — synchronous vs asynchronous mode, class size, facilitator-to-participant ratio, co-facilitation pairing, assessment mode — need a structured frame.
- The facilitation design includes participants with varied readiness for stretch and the question is whether a single-track or two-track architecture fits better.
- A facilitator is experiencing a presence-arc decision: how quickly to fade from community-builder to coach, and what signals indicate the cohort is ready.
- A course is about to record its synchronous sessions and the consent posture needs establishing before the first session.
- A new tool is under consideration for adoption; the six-question vetting is the reference.
- A participant is deeply struggling and the question is whether to continue or to counsel out; the Guide frames this as part of the facilitator's role, not an escalation.
- The question is develop-vs-grade: the Guide's default is developmental self-assessment via rubric, with grading as an explicit opt-in; use it when the client or organisation is assuming the opposite.
- A co-facilitation pairing needs designing; complementarity on skill mix, working hours, and life rhythm is the criterion.
- A course-closing structure needs deciding; the Guide provides multiple options (synchronous celebration, asynchronous bulletin board, Looking Back/Looking Forward, co-facilitator debrief).
- Reach for the Liberating Structures distillation when the question is *which specific facilitation microstructures to run within* a session the Guide has helped design.

## Worked Example

A team of three institutional facilitators is launching a new five-week professional-development course for 18 online instructors. The design question is how to structure the experience to be developmental — not a transmissive content course — and how to handle the facilitation workload across the five weeks.

Before the course begins, the lead facilitator applies the Guide's pre-course task structure (Source: Ch 4, "Several weeks (or months) before the course begins"): the technology stack is confirmed with an institutional LMS plus one web-conferencing tool; privacy posture is set (Vegas rules, per-session recording consent); two co-facilitators are paired on complementarity (one with strong asynchronous-discussion facilitation skills, one with synchronous technical expertise, covering morning and afternoon availability respectively); class size is confirmed at 18, within the Guide's 14–20 sweet spot (Source: Ch 4, "What's the right number of participants for this course?").

Week 1 presence is high: the lead facilitator posts a Welcome that establishes instructor presence and ends with an invitation; all three facilitators post daily to build community and model good facilitation practices (Source: Ch 4, "Week 1: Building Online Community"). By Week 3, participant teams are facilitating their assigned topics; the facilitators move to summary and coaching posts and pull back from leading discussions (Source: Ch 4, "Your shifting role: From facilitator to guide"). When Week 3 pace produces anxiety signals in two participants, the facilitators name the adjustment explicitly — extending one deadline and offering direct studio feedback on a struggling design — rather than amplifying pressure (Source: Ch 5, "Week 3: Projects under construction").

The FLIF is used after each participant-facilitated week; facilitators complete it alongside participants to model reflective practice (Source: Ch 4, "The FLIF Facilitation Process"). The course closes with a synchronous celebration and a Looking Back/Looking Forward activity; the co-facilitators debrief before parting and capture three design changes for the next iteration. All framework citations trace back through the deep reference.

## Anti-patterns this distillation helps avoid

- Defaulting to transmissive content delivery when the learning is in the conversation, not the content.
- Choosing synchronous-or-asynchronous mode without considering what the learning outcome requires.
- Designing a single-track engagement when the participant population has varied readiness for stretch.
- Treating modelling as facilitator-style rather than as designed-in mechanism.
- Maintaining heavy facilitator presence into later weeks when participant capacity to manage discussion has grown.
- Maintaining a light-hand stance during the phase where deep feedback is the right move (FLO Design Week 3).
- Grading by default when the engagement is developmental, then being surprised when candour drops.
- Allowing a participant who cannot meet the design's participation requirements to audit, then being surprised when group dynamics suffer and the participant does not achieve learning outcomes.
- Recording synchronous sessions without explicit per-session consent.
- Adopting tools because they are novel or convenient, without applying the six-question vetting.
- Pressuring participants into compliance with course deadlines in a developmental adult-learning context, then being surprised when participants disengage.
- Pacing the cohort into burnout in Week 3 (or its equivalent) when modifying the approach to cohort needs would produce better learning.
- Acting on facilitator anxiety (Anxious-Annie pattern) without first locating the anxiety as the facilitator's own pattern.
- Convening last-minute and skipping the several-weeks-before pre-course design tasks, then being surprised when the in-course running consumes more facilitator time than budgeted.
- Choosing a co-facilitator on seniority or convenience rather than on complementarity (skill mix, working hours, life rhythm).
- Closing the course without a designed closing structure — synchronous celebration, asynchronous bulletin board, mind map, or final forum post.
- Failing to debrief with co-facilitators after the course ends, then repeating avoidable problems in the next iteration.
- Failing to capture and integrate this iteration's lessons into the next pre-course design.

- *Yamagata-Lynch (2014)* — not held. Cited on combining synchronous and asynchronous communication strategies.
- *Wiggins & McTighe (2007), Schooling by Design* — not held. Source of the three teacher roles and the definition of facilitative teaching cited in the deep reference's connections section.
- *Mor & Mogilevsky (2013)* — not held. Cited for the studio-based learning approach that FLO Design adopts.
- *Pratt (1998), Five Perspectives on Teaching in Adult and Higher Education* — not held. Cited as the teaching-perspectives framework FLO Fundamentals uses for participant self-assessment.
- *Scott (2015), The Futures of Learning 3 (UNESCO)* — not held. Cited for the 21st-century-skills argument in Ch 1.

**Named limits of the source.** The Guide explicitly scopes to the FLO family of four courses (Fundamentals, Design, Synchronous, MicroCourse) in a higher-education professional-development context.
