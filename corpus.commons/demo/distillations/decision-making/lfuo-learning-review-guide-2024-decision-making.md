# LFUO 2024, Decision-Making Distillation

**Source:** US Forest Service, Risk Management Council & Office of Safety & Occupational Health (2024). *Learning From Unintended Outcomes and Learning Review Implementation Guide*. Revised April 2024. 98 pages. **Licence:** Public domain (17 USC §105 — US federal-employee work). **Distribution note:** No copyright obligation propagates from this source; derivative works are unrestricted under US copyright law.

## Decision-Making Relevance

The LFUO guide is not primarily a "decision-making" text — it is a manual for facilitated learning from unintended outcomes — but it is densely populated with decision frameworks because the *job* of the Delegating Official, the FLA team, and the participants is repeated, consequential decision-making under uncertainty in the wake of an event. The guide makes three decision-making contributions worth surfacing here:

1. **A structured framework for choosing the right learning response.** The Delegating Official faces a choice between four tools (AAR, RLS, FLA, Learning Review) and has to decide which one fits the event. The guide's argument is that the *outcome* (how bad it was) is a poor criterion; the *learning opportunity* (how much can the organisation learn?) is the right criterion. The two-inch tree-cutting case is the canonical illustration.

2. **A framework for reconstructing past decisions in the FLA process itself.** The Five Hows / Lessons Learned Analysis is a structured decision-reconstruction tool. It is the central craft method by which an FLA team reaches "the same level situational awareness, belief, and expectation that was held by those involved in the accident — which they had before the accident" (Part 7, "The Lessons Learned Analysis"). This applies *to* the decisions made in the field during the event.

3. **A discipline against three recurring decision-making failure modes in incident analysis.** Counterfactual reasoning, causal attribution, and the recommendations-as-default reflex are all framed as decision-making anti-patterns that degrade learning. The deeper claim is that *in complex systems* root-cause / counterfactual / recommendation-driven decision-making is itself the wrong tool. This is one of the guide's most quotable positions.

The distillation projects all three contributions onto a working pattern. Where the guide is silent (e.g., on cost-benefit analysis in non-incident decision-making, on portfolio-level decision-making across many events), the silence is acknowledged.

## Key Concepts for Decision-Making

1. <!-- concept: learning-review --> **Tool selection by learning opportunity, not by severity of outcome.** The natural tendency is to scale review-team resources to the severity of the outcome — fatalities get big teams, near-misses get nothing. The guide argues this misses the learning. The tree-cutting case (firefighter knocked unconscious by a glancing blow; released same day) is the worked example: two inches of tree-fall variance separates a "non-significant" outcome from a fatality, and a chance occurrence of two inches should not be the criterion for whether the organisation learns. (Source: Part 3, "Starting the process")

2. <!-- concept: facilitated-learning-analysis --> **The four tools form a scale-of-learning ladder.** AAR (immediate, local, closed) → RLS (one to two pages, shareable, often anonymous) → FLA (signed delegation, no punitive action, learning product without recommendations) → Learning Review (FLA plus focus groups, academic SMEs, Learning Review Board, recommendations, Safety Action Plan). Choosing the right rung is a decision about how much organisational learning the event justifies. (Source: Part 2, "The tools")

3. <!-- concept: delegating-official --> **The Delegating Official's pre-team decision checklist.** Eleven decision-support questions are explicitly listed: how deep will you look? is there enough trust here to support such an analysis? how might we exploit this event to make an impact on organisational safety? what kinds of specialised SMEs might be needed? how long should this take? how much time and money? are there conflicting stories surrounding the event? regardless of outcome, do people close to the accident believe it could have been much more severe? what could be the cost of NOT choosing an FLA? how close are you to the incident? are recommendations going to be essential (necessitating a Learning Review)? (Source: Part 3, "Questions to consider when deciding to mobilize a team")

4. <!-- concept: recommendations-vs-lessons --> **The recommendations-vs-lessons choice is a one-way decision.** Once the Delegating Official decides recommendations are essential, the Learning Review must be initiated — recommendations cannot be appended to an FLA. The reason: recommendations require vetting through communities of practice (focus groups), academic SME input, and a Learning Review Board, and a Safety Action Plan with named owners and deadlines. Recommendations on a single data point, without this vetting, are framed as decision-making errors. The guide's diagnosis: "In many circumstances, recommendations actually interfere with learning. Often recommendations, when implemented, add complexity to the workplace, which has the paradoxical effect of increasing risk" [V] (Source: Part 5, "Recommendations vs Lessons Learned"; Part 10, "What is a Learning Review?")

5. <!-- concept: networked-causality --> **Causal statements in complex systems are constructions, not discoveries.** "Cause isn't something investigators 'find' or 'discover'; cause is always something we create by recreating the event showing how certain anomalies led to the accident" [V] (Appendix B). The guide's underlying position is stated directly: "FLAs must avoid causal statements" [V] (Source: Part 5, "Recommendations vs Lessons Learned"). For decision-making, this means the deliverable of incident analysis is not a cause-and-effect chain but a reconstruction of the *conditions* (environmental, organisational, cultural, cognitive) that shaped the participants' sensemaking. The replacement vocabulary is *networked causality*. (Source: Appendix B, "A Deeper Dive into Causal Factors"; Glossary, "Networked causality")

6. <!-- concept: counterfactual-reasoning --> **Counterfactual reasoning is forbidden in the analysis.** "If this person had done X, then Y would have happened" is the canonical pattern to avoid. The discipline is: learn why people did what they actually did, not why they did not do what hindsight suggests they should have. Decisions in the field were reasonable to the people making them at the time, given the conditions they understood; the analyst's job is to understand that reasoning. (Source: Part 9, "Avoid Counterfactual Arguments"; Handout C, "FLA 'Watch outs'")

7. <!-- concept: five-hows --> **The Five Hows is a structured decision-reconstruction tool.** For each key decision or action in the event, the team dialogues through the Five Hows to reach the participants' pre-event situational awareness. The instruction is explicit: "You may find some of these 'hows' do not apply and some will be redundant. Use those that are helpful and don't waste time trying to cook-book the process." The tool's purpose is to make clear how decisions that "seemed wrong in hindsight were actually valid or at least legitimate in context at the time." (Source: Part 7, "The Five Hows – A Sensemaking Tool")

8. <!-- concept: lessons-learned-analysis --> **The Lessons Learned Analysis dialogues five categories of pre-event mental state.** For each key decision or action, the team identifies and discusses the *beliefs*, *perceptions*, *expectations*, and *paradigms* held before the experience that — in hindsight — turned out to be wrong, inaccurate, misleading, or deceptive. This is a decomposition tool for decision-making analysis. (Source: Part 7, "A Process for conducting a Lessons Learned Analysis")

9. <!-- concept: reckless-willful-disregard --> **Termination criterion is reckless and willful disregard for human safety.** Defined as "conduct that is intentional, unjustifiable, and occurred with the foreknowledge that the conduct was likely to result in serious harm, death, or injury to a human" [V] (Appendix C, "Is an FLA the Right Tool?"). Below this threshold, the FLA is the right tool: "FLAs are for the vast majority of events in which good people, doing what they thought made sense at the time, ended up in an unexpected situation" [V] (Appendix C, "Is an FLA the Right Tool?"). At or above this threshold, the FLA is terminated and the case is referred to administrative or law enforcement investigation. The decision to terminate is the team leader's, communicated to the Delegating Official via memo without disclosing details. (Source: Part 9, "Serious Crimes or Reckless and Willful Disregard for Human Safety"; Appendix C, "Is an FLA the Right Tool?")

10. <!-- concept: reasonableness-of-risk --> **Safety = "the reasonableness of risk."** Decisions about whether something is "safe enough" are decisions about reasonableness, not about absolutes. "It is a feeling. It is not an absolute. It is personal and contextual and will vary between people even within identical situations." Two experienced, competent professionals can disagree on whether a risk is reasonable, and both can be right. This is the underlying frame for tradeoff decisions between safety and production. (Source: Handout A, "Understanding the Work Under a Just Culture"; Part 6, "Principles and Agreements for All Involved in the Facilitation")

11. <!-- concept: risk-as-construct --> **Risk-as-construct rather than risk-as-objective.** Quoted from Paul Slovic via Kahneman's *Thinking Fast and Slow*: "Risk does not exist 'out there,' independent of our minds and culture, waiting to be measured. Human beings have invented the concept of 'risk' to help them understand and cope with the dangers and the uncertainties of life. Although these dangers are real, there is no such thing as a 'real risk' or 'objective risk.'" Decisions about risk are decisions about a constructed quantity, not a measured one. (Source: Handout A, "Understanding the Work Under a Just Culture", quoting Slovic via Kahneman)

12. <!-- concept: outcome-based-safety --> **Outcome-based safety programmes are shortsighted.** "Our natural intuition (our common sense) is to let outcomes draw the line between success and failure and to base safety programs on outcomes. This is shortsighted and eventually dangerous." Risk management is "wholly concerned with managing risks, not outcomes. Risk management is counterintuitive." This is the underlying frame for choosing not to base decision quality on outcome-quality alone. (Source: Handout A, "Understanding the Work Under a Just Culture")

13. <!-- concept: hindsight-bias --> **Hindsight bias contaminates post-event reasoning.** Human memory builds a coherent post-event story; sensemaking does not stop after the event. Interview language drifts from "what I wished I had done differently" to "what other people should have done" over the weeks following. The decision-making counter-move is to interview as soon as possible and keep participants focused on telling the story solely from their perspective. (Source: Handout C, "FLA 'Watch outs'")

14. <!-- concept: cognitive-biases --> **Fundamental attribution error is the most common framing trap.** Blaming the employee — rather than examining the system that shaped the conditions — is a form of the Fundamental Attribution Error. Conditions, not characters, are the unit of analysis. (Source: Part 3, "Administrative assurance of no punitive actions")

15. <!-- concept: recognition-primed-decision-making --> **Recognition Primed Decision Making is the default operational pattern.** Defined in the Glossary, citing Gary Klein: "a model people can use experience to generate a plausible option as the first one they consider." The implication for incident analysis is that participants did not deliberate between multiple options — they generated an experience-based first option and acted. The analyst's job is to understand *why* that first option seemed right, not to enumerate the options the participants did not consider. (Source: Glossary, "Recognition primed decision making")

16. <!-- concept: etto-principle --> **The ETTO principle — efficiency-thoroughness trade-off.** Hollnagel: "If demands to productivity or performance are high, thoroughness is reduced until the productivity goals are met. If demands to safety are high, efficiency is reduced until the safety goals are met." Every decision in the field is a position on this tradeoff; every decision in the review is, too. (Source: Glossary, "Efficiency-Thoroughness Trade-Off", quoting Hollnagel)

17. <!-- concept: multiple-improbable-events --> **Multiple improbable events are the norm, not the exception.** "Most often, the Lessons Learned Analysis will reveal that multiple improbable events were necessary for the accident to occur." Decision analysis that anchors on a single causal factor will systematically miss the actual structure of complex events. (Source: Part 7, "The Lessons Learned Analysis")

18. <!-- concept: high-reliability-organisation --> **HRO-style dialogue as an alternative to AAR-style dialogue.** Handout D presents two question sets: AAR-style (the standard five questions about what was planned, what happened, what worked, what didn't, what to sustain/improve) and HRO-style (organised around Weick & Sutcliffe's five HRO hallmarks — preoccupation with failure, reluctance to oversimplify, sensitivity to operations, commitment to resilience, deference to expertise). For high-reliability environments, the HRO framing tends to surface decision dynamics the AAR framing misses. (Source: Handout D, "Two Different Styles for Dialogue Focused Questions"; Glossary, "High Reliability Organization")

## Questions to Ask During Decision-Making

### Phase 1: Choosing the response (Delegating Official decision)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Am I scaling the response to severity-of-outcome or to learning-opportunity? | If the event had been two inches different (more severe, less severe), would I choose a different tool? If yes, am I making the right choice now? |
| Is the right tool an AAR, RLS, FLA, or Learning Review? | What is the audience for the learning — the immediate work unit (AAR), anyone who might benefit (RLS), the broader organisation as a story (FLA), or a system-of-work analysis with recommendations (Learning Review)? |
| Is trust strong enough to support an honest analysis? | If people close to the event do not trust that no punitive action will follow, the FLA will not surface what it needs to surface. Do I need to do trust-rebuilding work first? |
| Are there conflicting accounts of the event? | If yes, the dialogue has to be designed to surface points of departure rather than to adjudicate which account is "right." |
| Do I want recommendations? | If yes, I am committing to a Learning Review, with focus groups, an LRB, and a Safety Action Plan with named owners and deadlines. I cannot add recommendations to an FLA after the fact. |
| What does it cost me NOT to do this analysis? | Opportunity-cost framing: what learning opportunity am I closing off if I let this event pass without a structured review? |

### Phase 2: Setting the scope of the analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Am I extending learning beyond accidents? | Close calls, near-misses, and surprising-but-non-negative events are legitimate FLA scope. Have I considered whether *being surprised* — by a positive outcome, an unexpected emergence, a near-miss — justifies an FLA? |
| Have I separated learning from administrative action? | The administrative firewall is non-negotiable. Have I committed in writing that no disciplinary action, forced retraining, removal from incident, or reduction in planned overtime will result from FLA findings? |
| Have I committed to disseminating the lessons? | Appendix C asks the Delegating Official to confirm willingness to disseminate lessons in a public report, even if uncomfortable. Am I prepared to share findings that may make me uncomfortable? |
| Have I committed to learning rather than punishing? | If punishment is the implicit intent, the FLA process should be dropped — punishment and FLA are incompatible. |

### Phase 3: Reconstructing the participants' decisions

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| What were the participants' beliefs, perceptions, expectations, and paradigms *before* the event? | The four-category decomposition from the Lessons Learned Analysis (beliefs, perceptions, expectations, paradigms) is the question scaffold. For each, what did the participants hold to be true that hindsight reveals was incomplete or wrong? |
| What were the key decisions or actions? | Identify the ones that, in hindsight, look like mistakes or "bad" choices. The Five Hows is applied to these. |
| How did the decision make sense to the participant at the time? | Recognition-primed decision-making: the participant generated a first plausible option from experience. Why was that option plausible? What past experience supported it? What information was available at the time? |
| What were the conditions that shaped the decision? | Environmental (weather, topography), social (team dynamics, supervision), cultural (organisational norms, deference patterns), political (resource constraints, leadership pressures). Conditions are the unit of analysis, not characters. |
| What efficiency-thoroughness tradeoff was the participant making? | Was the decision driven by time pressure, resource constraint, fatigue, or some other ETTO factor? |
| Were multiple improbable events required for this outcome? | If yes, then the event is not single-cause, and the analysis should reflect that. |

### Phase 4: Avoiding decision-analysis anti-patterns

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Am I making a counterfactual argument? | "If they had done X..." is forbidden. Reframe: why did they do what they actually did? |
| Am I labelling a finding "causal"? | Cause is constructed by the investigator, not discovered. The replacement vocabulary is *conditions* that *influenced* the outcome. |
| Am I anchoring on a single root cause? | Multiple improbable events are the norm. If the analysis points to a single cause, look for the chance combination that the analysis is hiding. |
| Am I exhibiting hindsight bias? | The post-event story feels coherent because memory rewrites it that way. Am I treating the post-event coherence as evidence of pre-event clarity? |
| Am I committing the fundamental attribution error? | Am I blaming the individual when the system shaped the conditions? |
| Am I reflexively prescribing more training? | "When 'we just need more training' comes up… Try to dig deeper." Sometimes the answer is training; often the work-system design is the issue. |

### Phase 5: Deciding when to terminate

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Is there evidence of reckless and willful disregard? | The threshold is high: conduct that is intentional, unjustifiable, and occurred with foreknowledge that serious harm was likely. Below this, the FLA continues. |
| Is litigation against an employee or the agency likely? | If yes, a confidential administrative investigation is the right tool, not an FLA. |
| If terminating, what is the procedure? | Team leader writes a memo to the Delegating Official recommending administrative or law enforcement action, without disclosing details. Physical evidence goes to the Delegating Official; interview notes are sealed by the lead facilitator; team members do not discuss the case with anyone except external authorities outside agency control. |

### Phase 6: Deciding on recommendations (Learning Review only)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Has this recommendation been vetted by the community of practice? | Recommendations must be circulated through focus groups drawn from the relevant CoP before submission to the Learning Review Board. |
| Will this recommendation, when implemented, add complexity that increases risk? | Recommendations often paradoxically increase risk by adding workplace complexity. Has this been weighed? |
| Does the recommendation have a named owner and a deadline? | If not, it does not belong on the Safety Action Plan. |
| Does the recommendation address the system, not the individual? | "Safety is in the entire system." Recommendations that target sharp-end behaviour without addressing blunt-end conditions are likely to fail. |
| Have I checked the recommendation against work-as-done, not just work-as-imagined? | The community-of-practice vetting is the mechanism for this check. |

### Phase 7: Post-decision review

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Did the FLA process surface what I expected it to surface? | If yes, that's suspicious — sensemaking should reveal something unexpected. Where am I anchored? |
| What does this event tell me about my decision-making about *similar* events? | The Learning Review's job is to ask "Is our organisation sending mixed messages to field-going personnel?" and "Do our employees face goal conflicts of which management is unaware?" |
| Is the Safety Action Plan being followed through? | The SAP designates owners and deadlines; the Delegating Official is responsible for follow-up. |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| Post-incident investigation opens with "who made the wrong call?" or "who broke the rule?" | Fundamental Attribution Error framing — blame is being placed on the sharp-end individual rather than the blunt-end system conditions (Source: Part 3, "Administrative assurance of no punitive actions") | Reframe: what were the conditions — environmental, social, cultural, political — that shaped the participants' sensemaking? Conditions are the unit of analysis |
| An FLA or review produces a single root cause and a training recommendation | Single-root-cause anchoring plus the "we just need more training" reflex — multiple improbable events are the norm; training recommendations often add workplace complexity (Source: Part 7, "The Lessons Learned Analysis"; Part 9, "Avoid Counterfactual Arguments") | Ask: what multiple conditions were each necessary? What would the training actually change in the work-system? Run the Five Hows to a deeper level |
| Review narrative includes "if they had done X, Y would not have happened" | Counterfactual reasoning — explicitly prohibited by the FLA discipline (Source: Part 9, "Avoid Counterfactual Arguments"; Handout C, "FLA 'Watch outs'") | Replace with: why did they do what they actually did? What situational awareness, beliefs, expectations, and paradigms made that action sensible? |
| The Delegating Official scales review-team size to outcome severity (fatality gets a large team; near-miss gets nothing) | Severity-of-outcome selection criterion — learning opportunity, not outcome, is the right filter (Source: Part 3, "Starting the process") | Apply the tree-cutting heuristic: would a two-inch difference in outcome change the tool choice? If yes, the selection criterion is wrong |
| Recommendations are added to an FLA report after the Delegating Official did not commit to a Learning Review | Recommendations-on-FLA error — recommendations require community-of-practice vetting, academic SMEs, a Learning Review Board, and a Safety Action Plan (Source: Part 5, "Recommendations vs Lessons Learned") | Either initiate a Learning Review (if recommendations are needed) or remove the recommendations and strengthen the lessons-learned narrative |
| Interviews happen weeks after the event and focus on "what should have been done" | Hindsight bias compounding — memory rewrites the post-event story; interviews should happen as soon as possible and focus on perspective-faithful storytelling (Source: Handout C, "FLA 'Watch outs'") | Interview as early as possible; instruct participants to tell the story from their perspective as it unfolded, not as it looks in hindsight |

## When to Use This Reference

- An organisation needs to choose between AAR, RLS, FLA, and Learning Review for a specific event — the four-tool ladder and the Delegating Official's eleven decision-support questions are the routing tools.
- A Delegating Official is about to scale the review response to severity-of-outcome alone — the tree-cutting case and the learning-opportunity criterion are the counter-frame.
- An incident analysis is falling into counterfactual reasoning, single-root-cause anchoring, or the training-reflex — the Five Hows, the Lessons Learned Analysis, and the networked-causality vocabulary are the discipline.
- A review needs to be administratively firewalled from potential disciplinary action — the delegation-of-authority framing and the explicit no-punitive-action commitment are the mechanism.
- The question is whether recommendations belong in this process — they do not belong in an FLA; they belong in a Learning Review, with community-of-practice vetting and a Safety Action Plan.
- A review team is working on a Learning Review and needs to understand when to terminate the FLA (reckless and willful disregard threshold) and what the termination procedure is.
- HRO-style dialogue questions are needed instead of standard AAR questions — Handout D provides both sets.
- Reach for the NHS Just Culture distillation when the question is the clinical or UK-context application of the same blameless-review stance; both sources share the Just Culture foundational principle.
- Reach for the Barbrook-Johnson Systems Mapping distillation when the network-of-influences mapping concept needs a methods-level companion.

## Worked Example

A wildland fire crew experiences an entrapment event during a prescribed burn. The crew shelters and escapes without serious injury. The Delegating Official's first instinct is to classify it as "low severity" because no one was hurt and to issue a routine After-Action Review. A member of the Risk Management Council applies the tree-cutting heuristic (Source: Part 3, "Starting the process"): under slightly different wind conditions, the entrapment would have been a fatality. The learning opportunity is high regardless of the outcome; the Delegating Official initiates an FLA.

The FLA team interviews participants within 48 hours, before memory rewrites the narrative. Following the Lessons Learned Analysis structure (Source: Part 7, "A Process for conducting a Lessons Learned Analysis"), the team identifies the four-category mental state for each key decision: the crew leader's *belief* that the prescribed burn conditions were within the prescription; the *perception* that the wind shift was within normal variation based on that morning's forecast; the *expectation* from training that spot fire formation would take longer than observed; the *paradigm* that the crew had successfully worked similar terrain the previous week.

The Five Hows (Source: Part 7, "The Five Hows — A Sensemaking Tool") surface that the crew's situational awareness was shaped by a weather forecast that was not updated after 0600, by an organisational culture in which questioning a burn boss's go/no-go call required explicit permission, and by a compressed timeline that had compressed the briefing. None of these is a single root cause; all are conditions in a network.

The learning product is a narrative that makes the entrapment "vivid, engaging, and relevant" without counterfactual reasoning, and without a training recommendation. The DO and the review team discuss whether the weather-update gap warrants a Learning Review; they decide the conditions are systemic enough to warrant community-of-practice vetting and initiate one. All framework citations trace back through the deep reference.

## Anti-patterns this distillation helps avoid

- **Scaling review-team resources to severity-of-outcome** rather than to learning opportunity, missing the close-calls and near-misses where the richest learning often lives.
- **Treating recommendations as the default output of an investigation** — recommendations, when implemented, often add workplace complexity that paradoxically increases risk.
- **Constructing causal chains in complex systems** as if they were discovered rather than constructed; the replacement is *networked causality* — the combined conditions that collectively influenced the event.
- **Counterfactual reasoning** ("if they had done X, then Y would have happened") in the analysis; the discipline is to learn why people did what they actually did.
- **Single-root-cause anchoring** when multiple improbable events were required for the outcome.
- **Hindsight-bias contamination** — treating the post-event coherent story as evidence of pre-event clarity.
- **Fundamental attribution error** — blaming individuals at the sharp end without examining the blunt-end conditions that shaped the work.
- **The "we just need more training" reflex** — sometimes that's the answer; often the issue is that the work-system design makes the work impossible to do safely while following procedure.
- **Outcome-based safety programmes** that judge decisions by their outcomes rather than by the reasonableness of the risk at the time of decision.
- **Treating risk as objective and measurable** rather than as a constructed quantity contextual to the people involved.
- **Adding recommendations to an FLA** when the Delegating Official did not commit to a Learning Review and Safety Action Plan upfront.
- **Recommendations vetted by neither the community of practice nor academic SMEs** — the Learning Review process exists specifically to add those layers of vetting.
- **Terminating an FLA without following the procedure** — memo to the Delegating Official, evidence to the Delegating Official, notes sealed by the lead facilitator, no further discussion by team members.
- **Choosing the FLA tool when the case is actually a criminal matter** — reckless and willful disregard for human safety is the bright line; below it, the FLA is right; at or above, the case is administrative or law enforcement.
