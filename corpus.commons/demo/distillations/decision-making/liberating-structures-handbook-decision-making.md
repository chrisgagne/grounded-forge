# Heft & Pattillo (Group Jazz), Liberating Structures Handbook — Decision-Making Distillation

**Source:** Heft, L. & Pattillo, A. (compilers), with materials by H. Lipmanowicz and K. McCandless (2010). *Engaging Everyone with Liberating Structures Handbook*. Washington, DC: Group Jazz. Licence: CC BY-NC-SA 3.0 Unported. https://www.grassrootsgrantmakers.org/wp-content/uploads/Engaging-Everyone-31.pdf.

## Decision-Making Relevance

The handbook is, at its core, about how groups *own* decisions rather than have decisions delivered to them. Its central framing distinction — *ownership versus buy-in* — is a decision-process claim: buy-in is what happens when "someone else, or some group of people, has done the development, the thinking and the deciding, and now they have to convince you to come along and buy-in to their idea -- so that you can implement their idea without your involvement in the initial conversations or resulting decisions." That structure produces "lukewarm, pallid implementation and mediocre results." Ownership is the structural alternative: "you have participated in [the decision's] development; that it is your choice freely made." The handbook positions every named structure as an instrument for the ownership path — not by motivating people into it but by changing the room, the rhythm, the boundaries, and the question that shape how decisions get made.

A second decision-discipline thread runs through the handbook's treatment of *complexity*. Borrowing Ralph Stacey's Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix and Brenda Zimmerman's recipe/airplane/child distinction, the handbook makes one structural decision-quality claim: "Many of our strategies and heuristics for working on problems are designed for simple or complicated situations. These strategies don't work well when things are complex because the situation is emergent and unpredictable." For complex decisions, expert-led analysis followed by buy-in fails; the distributed intelligence of the group has to participate in framing the problem itself.

A third thread is *which* structures fit collective decision-making and which do not. The handbook is unusually explicit about this. The 25-to-10 rating ritual surfaces high-value ideas through a five-round milling and rating process, but the handbook flags directly: "This is not recommended to make a decision. It's a way to get a sense of the wisdom of the crowd." By contrast, Wise Crowds, Troika Consulting, Discovery & Action Dialogues, Just In Time Topics, and the What/So What/Now What debrief sequence are explicit structures for moving a group toward owned next-step decisions. The line the handbook draws is between *surfacing* tools (which broaden the option set) and *committing* tools (which produce shared next-step language). Both kinds of tool are useful, but they are not interchangeable, and treating a surfacing tool as a decision-rule is a common error mode.

A fourth thread is the handbook's quiet contrarianism about decision *forums*. Multiple sections name forum-level failure modes that distort decisions: large conference-centre tables that push people too far apart for "juicy conversation"; agendas that hide the participant experience the storyboard would surface; report-outs from breakout groups that drain the energy of any decision that might emerge; Q-A-Q-A patterns that "reinforce the 'central tendency' versus the distributed intelligence of the group"; facilitators who start talking 6 seconds after asking a question and signal that they are the one with the answers. These are decision-quality issues, not aesthetics, because they shape who actually contributes to the decision.

This distillation gathers these threads into a working pattern: how to recognise when a decision needs ownership rather than buy-in; how to choose among LS structures depending on the decision-stage (framing, exploring, deciding, committing); and how to avoid forum-level failures that look procedural but are decision-distorting.

## Key Concepts for Decision-Making

1. <!-- concept: ownership-vs-buy-in --> **Ownership versus buy-in as the load-bearing decision-quality distinction.** Ownership is "your choice freely made" through participation in development; buy-in is being convinced after the fact to implement someone else's decision: "Buy-in is the exact opposite. Someone else, or some group of people, has done the development, the thinking and the deciding, and now they have to convince you to come along and buy-in to their idea -- so that you can implement their idea without your involvement in the initial conversations or resulting decisions. Aiming for buy-in creates lukewarm, pallid implementation and mediocre results" [V] (Source: *Engaging Everyone with LS Handbook*, "Ownership versus Buy-in"). The diagnostic move is to listen for the word *buy-in* in the room — it is "a danger signal telling you that your development and implementation process is missing the essential ingredient of involving all who should be involved" [V] (Source: "Ownership versus Buy-in").

2. <!-- concept: structural-change --> **Structural change, not motivational change, is the lever.** "For things to really change, structural elements need to change, too. Otherwise change is short-lived. Liberating structures are the forms that make it easy for people to be generative together and make a significant impact with their creativity" [V] (Source: "Introduction"). The decision-making implication: a structural intervention (who is in the room, how they are seated, what question they are answering, who is listening to whom) often changes outcomes more reliably than asking people to "think differently" about the same situation.

3. <!-- concept: agreement-uncertainty-matrix --> **Complex decisions require structures designed for complexity.** "Many of our strategies and heuristics for working on problems are designed for simple or complicated situations. These strategies don't work well when things are complex because the situation is emergent and unpredictable" [V] (Source: "Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix"). Borrows Stacey's Agreement/Uncertainty axes and Zimmerman's recipe/airplane/child distinction. The decision-rule: classify the situation before choosing the method.

4. <!-- concept: surfacing-vs-committing-tools --> **Surfacing tools are not committing tools.** 25-to-10 in particular is flagged: "This is not recommended to make a decision. It's a way to get a sense of the wisdom of the crowd" [V] (Source: "25-to-10"). Mini Constellations similarly surface positions without producing commitment. Treating either as a decision-rule converts a perception-broadening exercise into a vote, losing the distributed-intelligence signal in the process.

5. <!-- concept: wise-crowds --> **Wise Crowds: silent listening as a decision-quality move.** The client describes a challenge, then turns their back while the consulting team converses about what might help — questions, paraphrases, metaphors. The handbook is explicit about the structural rule: "The Consulting Team's job is not to decide 'this is what The Client ought to do.'" The client decides; the team's job is to make a wider option-space audible. This separates option-generation from option-selection structurally rather than relying on the client to do both at once. ("Wise Crowds")

6. <!-- concept: troika-consulting --> **Troika Consulting: peer iteration toward a next step.** Three-person, 10-minute-per-focal-person rounds. Each focal person describes a challenge; partners ask honing questions first, engage with each other, then with the focal person about handling and moving forward. The structure produces an owned next-step decision per focal person, with peer accountability replacing facilitator authority. ("Troika Consulting")

7. <!-- concept: discovery-action-dialogues --> **Discovery & Action Dialogues (DADs): surfacing positively-deviant solutions already in the system.** Six-question protocol: "What do you know/think about ____? What do YOU do about ____? What keeps you from doing that all the time? Who/Where have you seen overcome those barriers? What other ideas do you have about removing barriers? What has to happen next to make that happen? Who will do what when next?" The final two questions are explicit commitment moves — naming next-step actions and assignees within the conversation. ("Discovery & Action Dialogues")

8. <!-- concept: triz --> **TRIZ: an inversion move for "what's stopping us?" decisions.** "Design a comprehensive system that makes it absolutely impossible to get that result. What policies, practices, and ways of operating would make it 100% certain there is no way any of the things you want can happen. Does the system you've designed have anything in common with the current state of affairs?" The decision-quality value is in surfacing currently-invisible self-sabotaging structures before deciding on positive moves. ("TRIZ")

9. <!-- concept: wicked-questions --> **Wicked Questions: surfacing the paradox before deciding on the response.** "A question is 'wicked' if there is an embedded paradox or tension in the question… Wicked questions do not have obvious answers. Their value lies in their capacity to open up options, inquiry and bring to the surface the fundamental issues that need to be addressed." Worked examples: "How can we sustain quality standards across the system while allowing for local innovation?" and "How can we maintain top down discipline needed for safety and level the playing field for bottom-up creativity?" Distinguished sharply from a complaint or a trick question. ("Wicked Questions")

10. <!-- concept: liberating-structures-methods --> **15% Solutions: the locus-of-control reframe.** "Most people have about 15-percent control over their work situations. The other 85 percent rests in the broader context, shaped by the general structures, systems, events and culture in which they operate." The decision-quality move is "Where do you have freedom to act? What's in your 15%?" — converting a paralysing whole-system decision-frame into a tractable personal-agency decision-frame, without denying the 85%. ("15% Solutions"; Gareth Morgan as cited source)

11. <!-- concept: simple-rules --> **Simple Rules: surfacing the minimum-viable structural commitment.** Five categories — How To, Boundary, Priority, Timing, Exit rules. The test: "What would happen if you left that rule out? Is this really required versus just an artifact of what we've been doing all along?" A way to make collective design decisions about the constraints that will govern future decisions, by stripping accumulated artefacts. ("Simple Rules")

12. <!-- concept: what-so-what-now-what --> **What/So What/Now What: the three-question post-event decision sequence.** "WHAT? What is emerging here? What data stands out? What actually happened? SO WHAT? What do the data imply? Do you see a pattern? Does it make a difference? NOW WHAT? What action may help us move forward? Who else should be here?" The sequence forbids skipping ahead — Now-What decisions land badly when the data and pattern questions have not been answered first. ("After Action Debrief")

13. <!-- concept: just-in-time-topics --> **Just In Time Topics: agenda formation as a participatory decision.** Self-organising agenda from Open Space lineage. Operating laws: "Whoever comes are the right people. Don't worry about who or how many – it can even be valuable to spend the hour reflecting on the topic by yourself" and "The law of two feet. If you're not contributing or getting value, use your feet to go somewhere else." Surfaces what the group most needs to talk about — surfacing decisions that fixed agendas suppress. ("Just In Time Topics")

14. <!-- concept: participatory-scenarios --> **Participatory Scenarios: who builds the scenario decides whether it's used.** "Many companies develop scenarios to support strategic planning. These scenarios are often created by 'experts' or small teams. As a result, many companies discover that lack of ownership on the part of the whole community means that the scenarios don't really play a part in decision making." Five-step community-driven 2×2 scenario build replaces the expert-then-buy-in pattern. ("Participatory Scenarios")

15. <!-- concept: storyboard-planning --> **Storyboard before agenda: visible-experience decision-aid.** "Try using a storyboard instead of an agenda… Unlike an agenda, it also includes and illustrates something about what the participants will experience." Diagnostic: "if your storyboard has a whole lot of podium sessions in a row, it's not going to be very engaging!" Move post-it components around to test combinations before committing the meeting design. ("Storyboard Design and Planning")

16. <!-- concept: engage-unusual-suspects --> **Engage the Unusual Suspects upstream of the decision.** "Who's not here that could contribute to our conversation?" is named as "one of the most important questions you can ask." Decisions made without the people most affected (a) miss critical context and (b) fail at the implementation step. Companion principle: "Nothing About Me Without Me." ("Engage the Unusual Suspects"; "Nothing About Me Without Me")

17. <!-- concept: six-words --> **Six Words: compression as a decision-clarifying exercise.** Forcing a mission, vision, or strategy to ≤8 words with a verb, target population, and measurable outcome surfaces what is actually load-bearing. "Taking some of the seriousness out of these things can actually help people get to the core of an important idea." Useful as a decision-input step for mission/strategy decisions, not as the decision itself. ("Six Words"; Mulago Foundation criteria)

## Questions to Ask During Decision-Making

### Phase 1: Framing (Recognising the decision and its situation)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Test for buy-in framing | Is anyone in this conversation using the word *buy-in*? If yes, that's a "danger signal" — the process is missing involvement of people who should be involved. Re-frame as: how do we get them into the development, not into the implementation? |
| Classify the situation by complexity | Where does this sit on the Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix? Is this a recipe (simple — known method, known agreement), an airplane (complicated — many parts but known how to combine), or a child (complex — emergent, unpredictable)? Methods designed for recipes/airplanes fail on child-class problems. |
| Test for "expert-only" framing | Is this scenario, plan, or decision being built by "experts" with the intent to bring it to the wider group for buy-in? If yes, predict the outcome: "lack of ownership on the part of the whole community means that the scenarios don't really play a part in decision making." Re-frame as participatory. |
| Surface the Unusual Suspects | Who's not here that could contribute to our conversation? Whose absence will hurt either the quality of the decision or its implementation? "This may be one of the most important questions you can ask." |
| Detect "Nothing About Me Without Me" violations | Is this decision being made about people who are not in the room — patients, customers, frontline workers, downstream consumers? If yes, what would it take to broaden the conversation to include them? |
| Frame the question as wicked when warranted | Is the underlying question a paradox or a tension between two values the group must hold together (quality vs local innovation; top-down discipline vs bottom-up creativity)? If yes, name it as a wicked question rather than treating it as a problem with a hidden right answer. |
| Test for storyboard visibility | Have we drawn the storyboard of how this decision will actually unfold? Or is the "agenda" hiding what participants will experience? "If your storyboard has a whole lot of podium sessions in a row, it's not going to be very engaging!" |

### Phase 2: Bounding (Defining the decision and its constraints)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply the 15% Solutions reframe | What part of this decision sits inside our 15-percent of control? Where do we have freedom to act, leaving the 85% acknowledged but not blocking? Move the bounds inward rather than waiting for the broader context to change. |
| Surface the minimum-viable rules | What are the few simple rules — How To, Boundary, Priority, Timing, Exit — that must hold? Test each: "What would happen if you left that rule out? Is this really required versus just an artifact of what we've been doing all along?" Strip the artefacts. |
| Invert with TRIZ before bounding positively | What would a system that makes the desired result *impossible* look like? Does the current state resemble that adverse system in places? Eliminate similarities before adding positive structure. |
| Bound by the space and physical container | Does the room support the decision we're making? Are tables in the way; is lighting fluorescent; is the seating too far apart for "juicy conversation"; are there walls for graphic recording? Space is a structural decision-input, not a logistics detail. |
| Bound by the rhythm of rounds | Is the time-shape of the conversation right for this decision — a single long open conversation, three 20-minute World-Café rounds, three 10-minute Troika rounds, five 25-to-10 rating rounds? Different rhythms surface different signal. |
| Compress to six words | Can this decision be compressed to ≤8 words with a verb, target population, and measurable outcome? If not, the decision may still be too vague to bound. (Use for mission/strategy decisions; not for tactical ones.) |

### Phase 3: Exploring (Generating alternatives)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Run 1-2-4-Whole Group on the option space | Have participants reflected individually, paired up, joined groups of 4–6, and then surfaced insights to the whole? "More than six is too big to give everyone a chance to talk." Use this when the team has stopped at two options too quickly. |
| Use Wise Crowds for an option-broadening session on a single decision | A "client" describes the decision and turns their back while a consulting team of 5–6 talks about what might help — for at least 15 minutes. The team's "job is not to decide 'this is what The Client ought to do.'" The client decides; the team's job is to make a wider option-space audible. |
| Use Troika Consulting when several people have parallel decisions | Three-person, 10-minute-per-focal-person rounds. Each focal person gets honed by two peers; after all three rounds, the group talks about insights and patterns. Produces three owned next-step decisions per troika. |
| Run Discovery & Action Dialogues to surface in-system solutions | Six-question protocol that ends in "Who will do what when next?" Use when the group already contains people who have solved a version of this problem locally and the group has not surfaced their solutions. |
| Use 25-to-10 to surface high-value ideas without committing | Five rounds of mill-trade-rate cards 1–5; countdown surfaces the top-rated. Remember the explicit scope: "This is not recommended to make a decision. It's a way to get a sense of the wisdom of the crowd." Surface, don't decide. |
| Run Mini Constellations to surface positional diversity | Place an object on the floor; participants position themselves by degree of agreement; speak from the distance-position as a "role." Surfaces the range of views without forcing a vote. |
| Q Storming for the question that has not been asked | Post-it brainstorm of "courageous and provocative" and "silly" questions. "Questions are fateful!" (Diana Whitney). The question generating the option-space matters more than the options themselves. |
| 5 Whys when the option-space is treating a symptom | Pair-based; ask "WHY?" at least five times. Explicitly relaxable: "Keep going until you feel you've pared away enough layers to get at a fundamental truth." Reroute the decision from symptom to underlying purpose or cause. |

### Phase 4: Deciding (Selecting from alternatives)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Recognise that LS does not provide a "select the option" structure | The handbook is explicit that LS surfaces and broadens — Wise Crowds, Troika, DADs, 25-to-10 all produce option-spaces and convergence on what intrigued/resonated. The final decision is made by whoever has the legitimate authority (the "client" in Wise Crowds; the focal person in Troika; the implementer in DADs; the convening leader for community-wide decisions). Identify that authority explicitly. |
| Use the "intrigued/surprised/resonated" frame for client-side selection | In Wise Crowds, the client opens the re-joined conversation with "Here's what I heard that intrigued, surprised, or resonated with me." That phrasing is the decision-selection move — naming what landed without committing to act on everything. |
| Convert insights into named next steps before adjourning | DADs end with "What has to happen next to make that happen? Who will do what when next?" The handbook treats this as the structural commitment move; without it, the conversation has happened but no decision has landed. |
| Use What/So What/Now What in order | "WHAT? What is emerging here? SO WHAT? What do the data imply? NOW WHAT? What action may help us move forward?" Do not let the group jump to Now-What before they have done the What and So-What. Decisions made without the data-pattern questions land badly. |
| Avoid using surfacing tools as committing tools | 25-to-10's top-ten countdown is a surfacing signal, not a decision. Mini Constellations' positional clustering is a perception, not a vote. If a decision is needed, route to a committing tool (DAD, Troika, named-owner-and-next-step). |
| Audit the seven Levels of Accountability ladder | Where is the group sitting — "Take Action and Learn / Make it happen, renewal" at the top, or "Wait 'n Hope / Somebody should do something" / "Blame others" / "It has nothing to do with me" at the bottom? Decisions made from below the line tend not to be acted on. "What do we need to do to work 'above the line?'" |

### Phase 5: Ratifying (Implementing the decision)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Anchor on the named next-step language | If the decision came out of a DAD, has the "Who will do what when next?" question been answered with named people and dates? If it came out of Wise Crowds, what did the client say "intrigued, surprised, or resonated"? If it came out of a World Café, what was the harvest? |
| Make the simple rules explicit | If the decision is to design or redesign something, are the Simple Rules — How To, Boundary, Priority, Timing, Exit — written down? If a rule could be left out without harm, leave it out. |
| Surface ownership across the implementing population | Are the people who will implement the decision the same ones who participated in making it? If not, the buy-in trap is open. What would it take to widen the decision-making circle before ratifying? |
| Plan a Sprint of 15% Solutions | If the decision is too large for any one actor to ratify alone, what are the 15%-of-control next-step actions each implementer can take? "Where do you have freedom to act?" makes the ratification tractable. |
| Use the storyboard for the implementation arc | The same tool that diagnosed engagement risk in the original meeting design can be used to design the implementation rollout — what will participants experience over time, not just what's on the timeline. |

### Phase 6: Monitoring (Was the decision a good one?)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Run What/So What/Now What as a recurring debrief | Use the three-question sequence at the conclusion of a project phase, a sprint, or a major milestone. The order matters; the Now-What question is informed by the data and the pattern, not generated independently. |
| Check the half-life of the decision | The handbook names the half-life problem directly: "The half-life of the energy and commitment to new ways of being after these events can be short when participants return to their organizations and fall back into default ways of meeting." If commitment is fading, has the structural change actually landed, or only the motivational one? |
| Use Appreciative Interviews on the success cases | Pairs interview each other about "a time when…" the decision worked. The framing is generative: "What we focus on tends to get bigger. So focusing on what works rather than on what doesn't work creates the context for more progress." |
| Watch for the "Levels of Accountability" slide | If the conversation has drifted toward blame ("It's their fault"; "It's my fault"; "Woe is me"; "This has nothing to do with me"), the decision is being defended against rather than evaluated. The reframe: "What do we need to do to work 'above the line?'" |
| Test for Q-A-Q-A central-tendency drift | When the group discusses how the decision is going, is one strong voice anchoring the conversation? Batch questions (3–4 at a time) and use the Take 20 Seconds rule to make space for other voices. |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| The phrase "we just need to get buy-in" appears in the planning conversation | Buy-in framing detected — the decision was made without involving those who will implement it (Source: "Ownership versus Buy-in") | Reframe: which people need to be in the development conversation, not just the implementation conversation? Design their entry upstream |
| A strategy or scenario was built by a small expert team and is now being "rolled out" | Participatory-scenarios failure mode — decisions built without the broader community tend not to shape that community's actual decisions (Source: "Participatory Scenarios") | Surface what would be required to rebuild the scenario participatorily; or at minimum run a Wise Crowds session with the implementing population to adapt it |
| A complex, emergent problem is being addressed with a project-plan-and-milestone approach | Complicated-method-on-complex-problem mismatch — Stacey's Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix routes this case away from linear plans (Source: "Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix") | Classify the situation explicitly; for complex problems, use distributed-intelligence structures (DADs, 1-2-4-Whole, Wise Crowds) rather than linear-expert-then-buy-in |
| 25-to-10 or Mini Constellations output is being used as the decision itself | Surfacing tool misapplied as committing tool — "This is not recommended to make a decision" (Source: "25-to-10"; "Mini Constellations") | Route the output to a committing tool: DAD final questions, Troika next-step, Wise Crowds client decision, or a named-owner-and-date |
| A meeting debrief jumps directly to "so what do we do next?" without pausing on the data | What/So What/Now What sequencing violated — Now-What decisions land badly when the data and pattern questions are skipped (Source: "After Action Debrief") | Run the three questions in order; the Now-What is only as good as the What and So-What that precede it |
| The facilitator starts responding 6 seconds after asking a question | Central-tendency reinforcement — the facilitator's quick response signals they are the authority, suppressing distributed intelligence (Source: Q-A-Q-A pattern) | Apply Take 20 Seconds (look at your shoes); batch questions 3-4 at a time before responding |

## When to Use This Reference

- A complex problem is being treated with simple-or-complicated methods (linear plan + buy-in) and the plan keeps not landing.
- A decision needs ownership across an implementing population and the current shape is expert-development followed by buy-in.
- A whole-group conversation needs a structure that gets everyone speaking early, or that broadens the option space, or that hones a peer's next-step decision.
- A post-event debrief needs the discipline of the What/So What/Now What sequence rather than jumping straight to action.
- A meeting design needs visible scrutiny via storyboard rather than the usual agenda + slides.
- A strategic scenario or vision is being built by experts and there is a risk that the resulting artefact will be ignored.
- A facilitation question needs distinguishing from a complaint or a trick question — naming the wicked-question quality changes how the group engages.
- An intervention design needs the TRIZ inversion move (what would make it impossible?) before adding positive structure.
- A collective sense-making question needs surfacing high-value ideas without forcing a vote — 25-to-10 or Mini Constellations as surfacing tools, not decision rules.
- The handbook does not provide strategic-decision frameworks (SWOT, Porter's), quantitative tools (NPV, IRR), voting mechanisms with decision rules, decision-rights frameworks (RACI), or post-incident structured protocols; reach for other references for those.

## Worked Example

A regional hospital quality team needs to address a persistent medication-reconciliation gap that has been identified across three departments. A previous improvement initiative built a new protocol using a small working group and then rolled it out for "buy-in." Two years later, the gap persists.

The team's facilitator applies the ownership-vs-buy-in diagnostic (Source: "Ownership versus Buy-in"): the previous approach produced lukewarm implementation because the people closest to the work — ward pharmacists and nurses doing handoffs at night — were not in the development conversation. The facilitator also classifies the problem on the Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix (Source: "Agreement/Uncertainty Matrix"): the medication-reconciliation gap is complex — multiple departments, shifting patient populations, competing time pressures — not merely complicated. A linear process redesign will not hold.

The team runs three Discovery & Action Dialogues (Source: "Discovery & Action Dialogues") with ward staff across the three departments. The DAD six-question protocol surfaces that two wards have already developed informal workarounds that have cut their own gap by 40%; these solutions have not propagated because there is no lateral-learning mechanism. The final two DAD questions — "What has to happen next? Who will do what when next?" — produce 11 named next-step commitments with owners and dates, without the facilitator prescribing any of them.

A What/So What/Now What debrief (Source: "After Action Debrief") three weeks later: the What surfaces that 8 of 11 commitments were completed; the So What identifies that the two incomplete commitments share the same supervisor's schedule constraint; the Now What is targeted and narrow, not a new improvement project. All framework citations trace back through the deep reference.
