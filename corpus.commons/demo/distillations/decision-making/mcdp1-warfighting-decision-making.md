# U.S. Marine Corps, MCDP-1 Warfighting — Decision-Making Distillation

**Source:** U.S. Marine Corps (1997). *MCDP 1: Warfighting*. Headquarters United States Marine Corps, Department of the Navy, 20 June 1997. PCN 142 000006 00. Supersedes FMFM 1 Warfighting (1989). **Licence:** Public domain (US Government work product). **Scope:** open.

## Decision-Making Relevance

MCDP-1 is, in significant part, a treatise on decision-making under uncertainty — when uncertainty is constitutive of the situation, not engineerable away. Six threads from the pamphlet sit directly on decision practice.

### 1. The nature of the decision environment (Ch 1)

The doctrine's starting position is that war's environment is *friction, uncertainty, fluidity, disorder, complexity, danger* — constitutive features, not exceptions. This is the operating frame for any decision-maker working in conditions that resemble war's structure: complex socio-technical systems, fast-moving markets, ambiguous customer needs, multi-party negotiations, crisis management, public-sector politics.

The implication for decision practice: methods that assume the environment is reducible, predictable, or fully analysable will fail. Decision frameworks suitable for stable / well-bounded contexts are inappropriate for uncertain / fluid contexts. The pamphlet is explicit: *"no degree of technological development or scientific calculation will diminish the human dimension in war."*

### 2. The OODA loop as the cycle of decision (Ch 2 Notes 18)

MCDP-1 credits Boyd's *Patterns of Conflict* explicitly: Observe → Orient → Decide → Act. The side that completes the cycle faster, or that operates in a way the adversary cannot orient to, wins. The most important node in the cycle — though MCDP-1 does not develop this as fully as Boyd's original — is *Orient*, where past experience / culture / new information / analysis-and-synthesis interact to shape what counts as a meaningful situation.

For decision-making practice: tempo matters. A decision made faster than the situation changes is operative; a decision made slower than the situation changes is responding to a situation that no longer exists. The decision-maker's question is *what is my OODA tempo relative to the situation's tempo?*

### 3. Mission tactics / commander's intent (Ch 4)

The doctrinal answer to the question *who should decide what?* is *mission tactics*: senior gives what and why (intent), never how. Subordinates execute within intent. Intent must be understood *two levels up*.

For decision-making practice in organisations: the question of *which decisions should be centralised and which decentralised* is answered by the principle of *enabling decentralisation through intent-sharing*, not by *enabling decentralisation through reduced authority*. Decentralised decision-making works only when subordinates know the intent two levels up; without it, decentralisation produces incoherence.

### 4. Main effort (Schwerpunkt) as the decision-filtering discipline (Ch 4)

Every decision the doctrine recognises is filtered through *Schwerpunkt*: which work is the main effort, which is supporting? The supporting effort yields to the main effort when they conflict.

For decision-making practice: when an organisation faces too many simultaneous priorities, the doctrinal answer is not *prioritise better* (which preserves the multi-priority frame). The answer is *name one main effort; everything else is supporting; supporting yields when in conflict*. The discipline is structural, not analytical.

### 5. Surfaces and gaps (Ch 2)

The decision heuristic for action: *avoid enemy strength; exploit weakness*. *Reconnaissance pull* (let the encountered weakness pull the main effort) over *command push* (push the main effort forward regardless of what is encountered) — citing Lind 1985.

For decision-making practice: when evaluating where to invest scarce attention / resources / capital, the question is *where is the system strong (a surface to avoid) and where is it weak (a gap to exploit)?* Most failed strategies push against strength because that is where the institutional opponent is most visible; the opportunity is usually where no-one is looking.

### 6. Doctrine is a way of thinking, not a checklist (Ch 3)

The pamphlet's most general decision-making claim: doctrine is *a way of thinking*. Decision frameworks (the Vroom-Yetton model, the DACI matrix, the Cynefin sense-act-respond protocols) are useful insofar as they develop judgment; they are corrosive insofar as they substitute for judgment.

For decision-making practice: a framework executed mechanically without the underlying thinking produces drift. The maintenance question is not *are we following the framework?* but *are we still exercising the thinking the framework was designed to develop?*

### 7. Severity on errors of inaction (Ch 3)

The pamphlet rejects zero defects directly: *severity on errors of inaction, leniency on overbold errors*. For decision-making cultures in which the safest move is to defer / wait / not-decide, the asymmetry is producing the inaction it ought to discourage.

For decision-making practice in organisations: the asymmetry of consequences is the structural condition. Decisions that should be made faster require the asymmetry to be inverted — errors of commission tolerated, errors of omission penalised. Without the inversion, the structure produces caution-by-default.

## Decision-Making Cautions

The doctrine is written for combat. Translation to civilian decision contexts (corporate strategy, public policy, regulatory engagement, product launches) is usually fruitful but requires judgment about what does and does not transfer. The pamphlet's framing of *war as Zweikampf* (a violent struggle between two hostile irreconcilable wills) is rarely the right civilian framing — most decision environments involve negotiable parties whose interests partly align. The OODA / Schwerpunkt / mission-tactics / surfaces-and-gaps machinery transfers; the *Zweikampf* framing usually does not.
