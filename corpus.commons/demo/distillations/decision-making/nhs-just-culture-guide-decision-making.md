# NHS Just Culture Guide, Decision-Making Distillation

**Source:** NHS Improvement (2018). *A Just Culture Guide*. A3 landscape poster, single page. Open Government Licence v3.0 (CC BY 4.0-equivalent). Based on the work of Professor James Reason and the National Patient Safety Agency's Incident Decision Tree. https://cpcw.org.uk/wp-content/uploads/sites/19/2018/03/180316-NHSi_just_culture_guide_A3.pdf.

## Decision-Making Relevance

The NHS Just Culture Guide is a *decision aid*, not a body of theory — the entire substance of the source is a procedural decision tree for managers evaluating whether a member of staff involved in a patient safety incident requires individual support or intervention to work safely, versus whether the locus of corrective work is the system rather than the individual. The guide is shaped to be used at a single, recurring decision point: an incident has occurred; an investigation has begun to surface a concern about an individual action; the manager must now decide what response is proportionate before formal action is directed at the individual.

The decision frame the source enforces is unusually explicit. The default is set *toward the system* and *away from the individual* — "action singling out an individual is rarely appropriate — most patient safety issues have deeper causes and require wider action" (Source: Preamble, "Purpose and use"). Four of the seven recommendation end-states route away from singling out the individual; only the deliberate-harm branch (Recommendation A) and the residual after all other filters have routed elsewhere (Recommendation G) carry the strongest individual-directed response. Every recommendation, including Recommendation A, preserves a parallel obligation to wider investigation — the system question is never extinguished by the individual finding.

The procedural shape of the source is also load-bearing: five sequential tests applied in order, each test conditioning the next, with eleven sub-questions distributed across them and seven distinct recommendation end-states. The discipline is in the *order* (deliberate harm before health before foresight before substitution before mitigation) and in the *conjunctions* (e.g., the foresight test only advances if all three sub-conditions hold). This distillation projects the source onto the decision-making task by surfacing the questions a manager asks at each stage, the routing logic, and the recommendation each branch terminates in.

This distillation is shaped for the just-culture decision specifically — the response to a staff member involved in a patient safety incident. The source is not a general-purpose decision aid; its tests, its sub-questions, and its default-to-system prior are calibrated to the patient-safety domain. The five tests do, however, generalise as a *pattern* — separate intent from impairment from system contract from peer behaviour from mitigation — and the pattern is portable to other operational settings where the question is whether to direct corrective action at an individual or at a system that produced the conditions for error.

## Key Concepts for Decision-Making

1. <!-- concept: just-culture --> **The five sequential tests.** Q1 deliberate harm → Q2 health → Q3 foresight → Q4 substitution → Q5 mitigating circumstances. Each test is gated on the prior; only one action (or failure to act) is taken through the tree at a time. The order matters: intent is separated from impairment before system contract is examined; peer behaviour is examined before mitigation. (Source: Q1-Q5)

2. <!-- concept: default-to-system --> **Default to system, not to individual.** "Action singling out an individual is rarely appropriate — most patient safety issues have deeper causes and require wider action" [V] (Source: Preamble, "Purpose and use"). Four of seven end-states route away from singling out the individual. The decision tree is calibrated to this prior. The guide positions the manager's task as supporting "a conversation between managers about whether a staff member involved in a patient safety incident requires specific individual support or intervention to work safely" [V] (Source: Preamble, "Purpose and use"; Q1-Q5 recommendations).

3. <!-- concept: deliberate-harm-test --> **The deliberate-harm filter (Q1).** The only test addressing intent. A Yes routes to Recommendation A — organisational guidance for management action, possibly including regulatory bodies, suspension, police, and disciplinary processes — with wider investigation still required. A No proceeds to Q2. Intent is the most serious filter; every subsequent test addresses conditions surrounding an act done without intent to harm. (Source: Q1, "Deliberate harm test"; Q1, "Recommendation A")

4. <!-- concept: health-test --> **The health filter (Q2).** Three sub-questions: substance abuse (2a); physical ill health (2b); mental ill health (2c). Yes to 2a routes to substance-abuse-at-work guidance (Recommendation B). Yes to 2b or 2c routes to organisational guidance for health issues affecting work, likely occupational-health referral (Recommendation C). No to all three proceeds to Q3. The decision frame is procedural-not-punitive: impairment is a condition to be addressed, not individual culpability. Both recommendations carry the backward-looking system question: could the impairment have been recognised and addressed earlier? (Source: Q2, "Health test"; Q2, "Recommendation B"; Q2, "Recommendation C")

5. <!-- concept: foresight-test --> **The foresight filter (Q3) — the system-contract test.** Three sub-questions: are there agreed protocols / accepted practice in place that apply (3a)? were they workable and in routine use (3b)? did the individual knowingly depart from them (3c)? *Only the conjunction of all three Yes answers advances the tree*. Any No routes to Recommendation D — action singling out the individual is unlikely to be appropriate; the locus of corrective work is the system. The foresight test isolates the case of knowing departure from a workable, routinely-used, agreed protocol; if any link in that chain is missing, the system rather than the individual is the locus. (Source: Q3, "Foresight test"; Q3, "Recommendation D")

6. <!-- concept: foresight-test --> **The substitution filter (Q4).** Three sub-questions: would peers in the same circumstances behave the same way (4a)? was the individual missed out when relevant training was provided (4b)? did senior team members fail to provide normal supervision (4c)? *Any Yes routes to Recommendation E* — same default as Recommendation D, different gating test. Recommendations D and E are textually identical: the same "action singling out the individual is unlikely to be appropriate" response, reached by two different exoneration routes. The substitution test's operational question: would substituting another comparable individual into the same situation have produced a different outcome? (Source: Q4, "Substitution test"; Q4, "Recommendation E")

7. <!-- concept: mitigation-test --> **The mitigation filter (Q5) — the residual.** Single sub-question: were there any significant mitigating circumstances? Reached only by cases that have cleared deliberate-harm, health, foresight (knowing departure from workable agreed protocol), and substitution (no peer-behaviour, training-gap, or supervision-failure exoneration). Yes routes to Recommendation F — "action directed at the individual *may not* be appropriate"; senior HR advice on the degree of mitigation. No routes to Recommendation G — graduated management action starting at individual training and ranging through performance management, competency assessments, changes to role, increased supervision, and on toward regulatory bodies, suspension, and disciplinary processes. (Source: Q5, "Mitigating circumstances"; Q5, "Recommendation F"; Q5, "Recommendation G")

8. <!-- concept: recommendation-wording --> **The "may not" vs "is unlikely to" wording.** The deep reference notes the distinction: Recommendations D and E say singling out the individual "is unlikely to be appropriate" (the case has been routed away from the individual by an exoneration finding); Recommendation F says "may not be appropriate" (the case has cleared the exonerations and turns on the degree of mitigation). The softer formulation is structurally appropriate to the residual point in the tree. (Source: comparison of Recommendations D, E, F)

9. <!-- concept: re-entrant-assessment --> **Re-entrancy: the guide is not a one-shot assessment.** "A just culture guide can be used at any point of an investigation, but the guide may need to be revisited as more information becomes available" [V] (Source: Preamble, "Please note", bullet 2). The inputs to the tests are facts surfaced by the investigation; as those facts change, the path through the tree may change.

10. <!-- concept: action-decomposition --> **One action at a time.** "The guide can only be used to take one action (or failure to act) through the guide at a time. If multiple actions are involved in an incident they must be considered separately" [V] (Source: Preamble, "Please note", bullet 4). Decomposition of incidents into distinct actions is part of the discipline of using the tree.

11. <!-- concept: decision-aid-scope --> **Not a replacement for investigation or HR advice.** The guide is a conversation aid, not a substitute. Only a full investigation can identify the underlying causes; the guide does not replace HR advice and should be used in conjunction with organisational policy. (Source: Preamble, "Please note", bullets 1 and 3)

12. <!-- concept: wider-investigation --> **The wider-investigation parallel.** Every recommendation end-state, including the deliberate-harm branch, pairs its operational guidance with a directive about wider investigation. Patient-safety outcomes are not exhausted by the individual response. The system question is preserved even where individual action is also directed. (Source: Q1-Q5 recommendations)

13. <!-- concept: just-culture-pattern --> **The transferable pattern: intent → impairment → system contract → peer behaviour → mitigation.** The five tests, taken as a sequence, are a portable pattern for any operational decision about whether to direct corrective action at an individual or at the system that produced the conditions for error. The pattern is calibrated to patient safety in the source; the order of filters generalises. (Source: Q1-Q5; characterisation transferred to a general pattern is a distillation-level claim, not in the source)

## Questions to ask during the just-culture decision

### Phase 0: Decide whether the guide applies at all

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Test the trigger | Has the investigation of the incident begun to suggest a concern about an individual action? If not, do not start the tree. |
| Decompose if needed | Are there multiple distinct actions or omissions in this incident? If so, each one is a separate trip through the tree. |
| Confirm the conversation | Is this a manager-to-manager conversation that needs structuring? The guide is positioned as a conversation aid. |
| Locate parallel obligations | Have I confirmed that a full investigation is also under way? The guide does not replace it. |
| Check HR coupling | Is organisational HR policy being applied alongside the guide? The guide does not replace HR advice. |

### Phase 1: Apply Q1 — deliberate harm test

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Test for intent | Was there any intention to cause harm? (Q1.1a) |
| Route on Yes | If Yes, follow organisational guidance for management action — potentially regulatory bodies, suspension, police, disciplinary processes — and ensure wider investigation continues. |
| Proceed on No | If No, move to Q2; do not skip steps. |

### Phase 2: Apply Q2 — health test

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Substance abuse | Are there indications of substance abuse? (Q2.2a) |
| Physical health | Are there indications of physical ill health? (Q2.2b) |
| Mental health | Are there indications of mental ill health? (Q2.2c) |
| Route on substance abuse | If Yes to 2a, follow organisational substance-abuse-at-work guidance; wider investigation should ask whether the substance abuse could have been recognised and addressed earlier. |
| Route on ill health | If Yes to 2b or 2c, follow organisational guidance for health issues affecting work (likely occupational-health referral); wider investigation should ask whether the health issue could have been recognised and addressed earlier. |
| Proceed if none | If No to all three, move to Q3. |

### Phase 3: Apply Q3 — foresight test

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Protocol existence | Are there agreed protocols / accepted practice in place that apply to the action / omission in question? (Q3.3a) |
| Protocol viability | Were the protocols / accepted practice workable and in routine use? (Q3.3b) |
| Knowing departure | Did the individual knowingly depart from these protocols? (Q3.3c) |
| Route on any No | If No to any of 3a, 3b, or 3c, action singling out the individual is unlikely to be appropriate; the locus is the system. Move the focus of corrective work to the wider investigation. |
| Proceed only on Yes-to-all | If Yes to all three, move to Q4. (The conjunction is required; partial Yes does not advance the tree.) |

### Phase 4: Apply Q4 — substitution test

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Peer behaviour | Would other individuals from the same peer group, with comparable experience and qualifications, behave in the same way in similar circumstances? (Q4.4a) |
| Training equity | Was the individual missed out when relevant training was provided to their peer group? (Q4.4b) |
| Supervision adequacy | Did more senior members of the team fail to provide supervision that normally should be provided? (Q4.4c) |
| Route on any Yes | If Yes to any of 4a, 4b, or 4c, action singling out the individual is unlikely to be appropriate; the locus is the system (peers, training programme, supervision). |
| Proceed on No-to-all | If No to all three, move to Q5. |

### Phase 5: Apply Q5 — mitigating circumstances

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Test for mitigation | Were there any significant mitigating circumstances? (Q5.5a) |
| Route on Yes | If Yes, action directed at the individual may not be appropriate; seek senior HR advice on the degree of mitigation; preserve the wider investigation. |
| Route on No | If No, follow organisational guidance for appropriate management action — graduated from individual training, performance management, competency assessments, changes to role, increased supervision, through to regulatory bodies, suspension, and disciplinary processes. Wider investigation still needed. |

### Phase 6: After the decision

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Communicate proportionately | Can the response be explained to staff, patients, and families in a way that shows how the appropriate response can and should differ according to circumstances? |
| Preserve the parallel obligation | Is the patient safety incident investigation also producing the wider corrective actions, regardless of which branch the individual response sits on? |
| Re-enter if new information surfaces | Have any facts emerged from the investigation that would change the answer to any of Q1-Q5? If so, walk the tree again. |
| Confirm the recordkeeping | Has the guide's use been documented with reference to organisational HR and incident reporting policies? |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| A manager's first post-incident question is "who made the error?" | Outcome-framing detected — the guide's prior routes attention to circumstances, not to outcome severity or identity (Source: Preamble, "Purpose and use") | Start with the incident circumstances, not the person; run through Q1 in order before naming any individual response |
| Q3 is being treated as a single test (one sub-question suffices) | Conjunction-structure misread — all three of Q3's sub-questions must be Yes to advance toward individual action; any No routes to Recommendation D (Source: Q3, "Foresight test") | Run all three sub-questions; if any is No, route to Recommendation D and move the corrective focus to the system |
| Q4 is being treated as requiring all three sub-questions to be No | Disjunction-structure misread — any one Yes among Q4's three sub-questions routes to Recommendation E (Source: Q4, "Substitution test") | Confirm that all three Q4 sub-questions are genuinely No before proceeding; one Yes is enough to route away from individual action |
| A serious patient-safety outcome is used to justify jumping directly to management action, skipping Q3 and Q4 | Severity-of-outcome override — the guide is explicit that outcome does not change the filter logic; the tests are circumstance-based, not outcome-based (Source: Preamble, "Purpose and use") | Apply Q1-Q5 in full sequence regardless of outcome severity |
| A recommendation is reached without a wider investigation commitment | Parallel-obligation omission — every recommendation end-state, including Recommendation A, preserves a directive about wider investigation (Source: Q1-Q5 recommendations) | Add the wider investigation commitment explicitly to any individual-response plan |
| The guide is applied once and closed when new facts surface later in the investigation | Re-entrant-property ignored — "the guide may need to be revisited as more information becomes available" (Source: Preamble, "Please note") | Treat the guide as a live conversation aid; re-run if investigation facts change any Q1-Q5 answer |

## When to Use This Reference

- An investigation of a patient safety incident has begun to surface a concern about an individual action, and a manager needs to decide whether the appropriate response is individual-directed, system-directed, or both.
- A multi-action incident requires decomposition — the guide applies one action at a time; complex incidents require multiple separate trips through the tree.
- The question is whether to refer an individual to occupational health, substance-abuse guidance, or management action — Q2 is the filter.
- The question is whether a knowing departure from a workable, agreed, routinely-used protocol occurred — Q3 is the filter; only the conjunction of all three Yes answers advances to individual consideration.
- The question is whether peer behaviour, training gaps, or supervision failures exonerate the individual from individual-directed action — Q4 is the filter; any one Yes routes to Recommendation E.
- A previously-concluded assessment needs reopening because new investigation facts have changed the picture — the re-entrant property is the mechanism.
- Reach for the LFUO 2024 distillation when the question is a multi-tool, multi-stakeholder incident analysis with a learning focus and an administrative firewall; the NHS guide is the per-individual incident decision aid; LFUO is the broader organisational learning framework.
- Reach for the Liberating Structures distillation when the question is how to structure the wider-investigation conversation that every NHS recommendation requires in parallel.

## Worked Example

A ward pharmacist is involved in a medication incident in which a patient receives an incorrect dose. The investigation surfaces a concern about the pharmacist's action at a specific step in the dispensing process. The manager applies the guide, one action at a time (Source: Preamble, "Please note", bullet 4).

Q1 deliberate harm test: no indication of intent to harm. Routes to Q2. Q2 health test: no indications of substance abuse, physical ill health, or mental ill health documented in the investigation at this point. Routes to Q3. Q3 foresight test: (3a) There is an agreed dispensing protocol that applies — Yes. (3b) The protocol is workable and in routine use — Yes. (3c) Did the pharmacist knowingly depart from it? The investigation reveals the pharmacist followed the steps she had been shown by a senior colleague on joining the ward three months ago, which differed from the written protocol. Answer: No. Conjunction not met — routes to Recommendation D (Source: Q3, "Recommendation D"). Action singling out the pharmacist is unlikely to be appropriate; the corrective locus is the system.

The manager documents the Recommendation D finding and opens a wider investigation (Source: Q3, "Recommendation D"): who else was shown the same informal practice? What is the gap between written protocol and actual practice? When the investigation later surfaces that the senior colleague who trained the pharmacist also uses the same deviation, the manager re-enters the guide for the senior colleague's action separately. All framework citations trace back through the deep reference.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Treating outcome as the test.** The appropriate response differs according to the *circumstances in which an error was made*, not the outcome that followed. A serious patient-safety outcome with no deliberate-harm, no health, no knowing-departure, no peer-distinguishing factors, and no mitigating reduction still routes through the tree on its circumstances.
- **Skipping the system tests because the outcome was severe.** The default is to system, not to individual. Q3 and Q4 are the load-bearing filters; jumping from a serious outcome to Q5 or to management action bypasses the source's explicit prior.
- **Treating Q3 as a single test rather than a conjunction.** All three foresight sub-questions must be Yes to advance. A workable protocol that is not in routine use (3b No) is a system finding, not a foresight finding against the individual.
- **Treating Q4 as a single test rather than a disjunction.** Any one Yes among 4a-4c routes to "not appropriate to single out". If peer behaviour would have been the same, training was missed, or supervision was absent, that is enough to exit toward the system locus.
- **Conflating Recommendation A's individual response with closure on the system question.** Even on the deliberate-harm branch, wider investigation is required to understand how and why patients were not protected.
- **Using the guide as a substitute for investigation or HR advice.** The "Please note" caveats are explicit on the guide's scope; ignoring them recasts the guide as a more authoritative artefact than it is.
- **Treating the guide as a one-shot assessment.** As the investigation surfaces new facts, the path through the tree may change. The re-entrant property is part of the design.
- **Failing to decompose multi-action incidents.** One incident comprising several actions is multiple trips through the tree, one per action — not a single composite trip.
