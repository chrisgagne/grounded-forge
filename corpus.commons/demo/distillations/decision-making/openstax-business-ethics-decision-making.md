# OpenStax Business Ethics, Decision-Making Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2018). *Business Ethics*. Rice University. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/business-ethics. Senior contributing authors Stephen M. Byars and Kurt Stanberry; seven contributing authors.

## Decision-Making Relevance

OpenStax's *Business Ethics* equips a decision-maker with three normative lenses (utilitarianism, deontology, virtue), a method for stakeholder weighing (descriptive / instrumental / normative; power × interest × exigency), and a vocabulary of ethical choice (moral minimum, ethical minimum, ethical maximum, single ethical standard, moral agency, conscience formation). The book treats ethical decision-making as a recurring competence rather than a once-off skill: most chapters end with a practical decision context (compliance versus accommodation; legal minimum versus ethical maximum; short-term profit versus long-term goodwill).

The book argues two unifying positions about the *process* of ethical decision-making. First, a single ethical standard applies across contexts; deontology, utilitarianism, and virtue ethics all converge on this. Second, the *moral minimum* (legal compliance) is rarely sufficient; ethical maximums protect long-term profitability and reputation more reliably. The lead author's "succinct theory" (Appendix C) prefers deontology over utilitarianism in business: the means ennoble the ends; consequences alone are insufficient justification.

This distillation consolidates these threads into a working pattern: how to recognise that a decision has an ethical dimension; how to choose between minimum and maximum responses; how to apply each ethical lens; how to surface stakeholder-impact asymmetries; and how to maintain moral agency under pressure.

## Key Concepts for Decision-Making

1. <!-- concept: ethics --> **Three normative ethical theories.** Decisions can be evaluated through *utilitarianism* (greatest good for the greatest number; consequences only), *deontology* (Kantian categorical imperative; act only on maxims you can will universal; treat people as ends, never merely as means), or *virtue ethics* (Aristotelian or Confucian — does this decision express the character of the person I wish to be?). The book argues that all three converge on a single ethical standard: "A deontologist would argue that you should adhere to particular duties in performing your actions, regardless of the parties with whom you interact. A utilitarian would say that any act you take should result in the greatest good for the greatest number. A virtue ethicist would state that you cannot be virtuous if you lack integrity in your behavior toward all" [V] (Source: *Business Ethics*, Ch 1.3, "Multiple versus Single Ethical Standards"; foundations in Ch 1.1, "Ends, Means, and Character in Business" and Ch 2 throughout).

2. <!-- concept: phronesis --> **Phronesis (practical wisdom).** Aristotle's intellectual virtue of practical wisdom — "tacit knowledge acquired from experience that enables people to make prudent judgments and take actions based on the actual situation, guided by values and morals". Phronetic leaders practice moral discernment in every situation, quickly assess situations, create shared sense of purpose, communicate via metaphor and story. (Source: Ch 1.1, "Aristotle and the Concept of Phronesis")

3. <!-- concept: single-ethical-standard --> **Single ethical standard.** Rejecting *ethical relativism* — the view that "what is ethical depends solely on the context" — the text recommends a coherent code applied across business, family, religious community, strangers, and competitors. The single-standard reasoning protects against hypocrisy, builds reputation, and reduces decision overhead. (Source: Ch 1.3, "Multiple versus Single Ethical Standards")

4. <!-- concept: moral-minimum --> **Moral minimum, ethical minimum, ethical maximum.** *Moral minimum* is the floor a business must meet to claim ethical positioning at all (honouring obligations to all stakeholders, not just shareholders). *Ethical minimum* is the least a company might do that complies with the law. *Ethical maximum* is the strongest action available. The Samsung washer recall (full refunds plus free pickup for ~3 million units) is a paradigmatic ethical-maximum response. (Source: Ch 3.1, "Ethical Responsibilities Often Extend Beyond Legal Requirements"; Ch 4.1, "The Two Sides of the Corporate Responsibility Debate")

5. <!-- concept: deontology --> **Categorical imperative (Kantian deontology).** Kant: "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can, at the same time, will that it should become a universal law" [V] (Ch 2.5, "Aroused From 'Dogmatic Slumber'"). Two suppositions: act on goodwill rather than self-interested motives benefiting yourself at others' expense; never treat others merely as means without consideration of them as ends in themselves. The book's lead author endorses deontology over utilitarianism for business decisions: "Business ethics should be grounded in deontology more than in utilitarianism. That is, the ends should not typically be considered sufficient justification for the means when it comes to framing a business strategy. Rather, it is the means that ennoble the ends" [V] (App C, "The Nature of Business Ethics").

6. <!-- concept: justice-theory --> **Veil of ignorance (Rawlsian justice as fairness).** Imagine the decision before knowing your own position: would you accept this rule for those most disadvantaged? Pure procedural justice illustration: the cake-divider takes the last piece. Useful for distributional decisions and compensation policy. (Source: Ch 2.6, "Justice Theory")

7. <!-- concept: harm-principle --> **Harm principle (Mill).** "The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilized community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others. His own good, either physical or moral, is not a sufficient warrant" [V] (Ch 2.4, "Synthesizing Rights and Utility"). Pertinent to product-safety decisions, monitoring policies, paternalism in wellness programs.

8. <!-- concept: long-term-perspective --> **Short-term versus long-term perspective.** Quarterly-profit framing systematically undervalues stakeholder welfare; the book argues for long-term reasoning: goodwill, brand, employee retention, customer loyalty are slow-built and slow-eroded. The Toyota Way — selling at a slight loss for years to build US trust — is the worked example. (Source: Ch 1.2, "Profitability and Success: Thinking Long Term")

9. <!-- concept: goodwill --> **Goodwill as decision-relevant intangible.** Goodwill is "the positive feeling stakeholders have for any particular company" — reputation, brand, intellectual capital, workforce attitude, customer loyalty. Decisions that erode goodwill are paid for over time; decisions that build goodwill compound. (Source: Ch 1.2, "Stockholders, Stakeholders, and Goodwill")

10. <!-- concept: moral-agency --> **Moral agency and bias.** *Moral agency* is "the self-awareness, freedom, and ability to make choices based on our perception of right and wrong". Bias diminishes moral agency. Employees often rationalise unethical conduct as sanctioned by superiors — abdicating personal responsibility. The single-standard rule and conscience formation are the protections. (Source: Ch 5.4, "Normative Business Ethics")

11. <!-- concept: bribery --> **Bribery decisions and the FCPA.** Bribery is illegal under US law extra-territorially and under the OECD Anti-Bribery Convention (43 signatories). "Facilitating payments" are a narrow exception. The text takes a strong position that "when in Rome" cultural relativism is not a defence — the single ethical standard applies internationally. (Source: Ch 7.4, "Bribery and the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act")

12. <!-- concept: insider-trading --> **Insider-trading decisions.** Buying or selling securities based on material non-public information violates Section 10(b)5 of the Securities Exchange Act. "Temporary insiders" — bankers, brokers, attorneys, journalists — are also covered. The Drexel Burnham Lambert case illustrates the financial-and-firm consequences. (Source: Ch 7.4, "Insider Trading")

13. <!-- concept: whistleblowing --> **Whistleblowing decisions.** Internal reporting first; require evidence; ensure the violation is serious; ensure whistleblowing has some likelihood of stopping the wrongful act. The False Claims Act qui tam provision rewards relators 15-30%. The Sherron Watkins / Enron and Sallie Krawcheck / Merrill Lynch cases illustrate that even ethical action carries career cost. (Source: Ch 7.5, "Whistleblowing: Risks and Rewards")

14. <!-- concept: stakeholder-theory --> **Three stakeholder-claim approaches (Donaldson and Preston).** *Descriptive* (who are they, what do they want?), *instrumental* (managing them well improves the bottom line), *normative* (they are ends in themselves, not means). The text endorses normative as fundamental. Useful for framing whether a decision treats stakeholders instrumentally or with intrinsic respect. (Source: Ch 3.2, "The Ethical Basis of Stakeholders' Claims")

15. <!-- concept: ethical-leadership --> **Tone at the top.** Ethical behaviour is most reliably learned from leadership modelling. Compliance training without ethical leadership produces hollow compliance. The Bill Swanson plagiarism case illustrates the inverse: top-down ethical claims undermined by personal failure compromise organisational ethics. (Source: Ch 11.3, "The Role of Ethical Top Leadership")

## Questions to Ask During Decision-Making

### Phase 1: Recognising the ethical dimension

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Detect the ethical dimension early | Is this a question of compliance only, or are stakeholders affected whose interests are not represented in the conversation? Whose rights, welfare, or dignity is at stake? |
| Distinguish moral from legal | What is the legal minimum? What is the moral minimum? Is this decision content with legal compliance, or does the situation call for going above and beyond? |
| Test for self-deception | If this decision were on the front page of a major newspaper tomorrow, would I feel guilty or proud? Which feeling is the decision-relevant signal? |
| Test for the single-standard rule | Would I make this same decision if all my friends, family members, and adversaries could observe my reasoning? Am I applying a different standard at work than at home? |
| Identify the relevant ethical lens | Is this a consequence-dominated decision (utilitarian framing fits)? A duty / rights framing (deontology fits)? A character / reputation framing (virtue fits)? Often all three apply; do they converge? |

### Phase 2: Bounding the decision

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify all stakeholders | Beyond shareholders, who is affected? Employees, customers, suppliers, communities, the environment, regulators, future generations? Have I included those without voice (the indirect stakeholders)? |
| Categorise the stakeholders | Are they enabling (regulators, board, stockholders), normative (peers, competitors), functional (suppliers, employees, customers), or diffused (NGOs, voters, media)? Different categories warrant different treatment. |
| Surface power asymmetries | Who has high power and high interest in this decision? Who has low power but high interest (most likely to be ignored)? Who has voice and who does not? |
| Detect the moral-minimum line | What would honour the basic rights of all stakeholders affected, even if the law does not require it? |
| Frame ethical-minimum versus ethical-maximum responses | What is the least we can do that the law permits? What is the strongest stakeholder-honouring response available? Where on the spectrum does this decision sit, and why? |

### Phase 3: Applying the ethical lenses

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply the utilitarian lens | What outcome produces the greatest good for the greatest number across the relevant time horizon? Whose pleasure / utility / welfare am I aggregating? Does the calculus include all stakeholders or only those with voice? |
| Apply the deontological lens | Could the maxim of this decision be made universal? Are we treating any stakeholder merely as a means? Is there a duty being violated, even if the consequences would otherwise justify the act? |
| Apply the virtue lens | What kind of organisation does this decision express? What kind of professional am I becoming through this choice? What would a phronetic leader do — practically wise, prudent, deliberative? |
| Apply Mill's harm principle | Is this decision preventing harm to others, or limiting choice for the actor's own good? The latter requires special justification. |
| Apply the Rawlsian veil | If I did not know my own position in the affected population (worker or executive, customer or shareholder, current or future generation), would I accept this decision? |

### Phase 4: Stakeholder-impact testing

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Test for hidden costs to indirect stakeholders | Are we externalising costs onto the environment, future generations, communities, or workers without voice? Should those costs be priced or compensated (cap-and-trade, carbon tax, Pigovian tax patterns)? |
| Test for greenwashing | Is the ethical claim attached to this decision substantive or PR? Does the underlying behaviour change, or is the claim cover for unchanged practice? (Coca-Cola's water footprint vs its sustainability statements is the worked example.) |
| Test for short-term-profit bias | Is this decision optimising quarterly results at the expense of long-term goodwill? Would the long-term reasoning produce a different choice? |
| Test for compliance-as-checkbox | Are we satisfying the letter of a regulation while violating its spirit? Are training requirements being met without culture change? |
| Test for pay-and-power equity | If this decision involves pay, hiring, or advancement, does it preserve a fair distribution? Does the CEO-to-worker ratio remain reasonable? Does this decision narrow or widen pay gaps? |

### Phase 5: Implementation

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Communicate transparently | Have I explained the reasoning, not just the outcome, to those affected? Stakeholders accept difficult decisions more readily when the reasoning is shared. |
| Model the decision visibly | Will leadership embody the decision through their own behaviour, or is this a one-off announcement? Tone at the top matters more than the announcement itself. |
| Anticipate retaliation costs | If this decision identifies wrongdoing or whistleblowing, what protections are in place? The career-cost data on whistleblowers is sobering even when their action was unambiguously ethical. |
| Build feedback channels | Will affected stakeholders have a way to report consequences I have not anticipated? The MITRE five-step pattern (identify, gather concerns, present results, decide, inform stakeholders) is a model. |

### Phase 6: Reviewing and re-deciding

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Distinguish bad luck from bad decision | A bad outcome may follow a sound decision (uncertainty was high). A good outcome may follow a poor decision. Which case is this? |
| Review whether the lens applied was the right one | Was a consequentialist analysis run when a deontological line should have settled the matter? Or were duties asserted when the practical consequences should have weighed more? |
| Review whether all stakeholders were represented | Looking back, were any stakeholders surfaced in implementation that should have been included in the original framing? |
| Review whether escalation of commitment is operating | Is leadership defending a prior decision against new evidence rather than re-evaluating freshly? Is the sunk cost being treated as recoverable? |
| Capture a phronesis lesson | What did this decision-process teach that I can apply to the next decision of this type? Practical wisdom is built through reflection on cases. |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: A decision is being framed as a compliance question only.** Signal: discussion focuses on "is this legal" rather than "is this right". Diagnosis (in source vocabulary): the moral-minimum threshold is being conflated with the ethical-minimum (legal-compliance) threshold; ethical reflection has been short-circuited. Follow-up: surface the ethical-maximum option explicitly; ask what stakeholder welfare would require.
- **Pattern: Stakeholder claims are being weighed by power alone.** Signal: high-power-low-interest stakeholders dominate; low-power-high-interest stakeholders are silent. Diagnosis: the prioritisation is descriptive without the normative lens (Donaldson and Preston). Follow-up: ask which stakeholders are ends in themselves, not just instruments to outcomes.
- **Pattern: Cultural relativism is offered as a justification for bribery, kickbacks, or sweatshop conditions.** Signal: arguments invoke "when in Rome", "this is how business is done here", or "everyone does it locally". Diagnosis: ethical relativism short-circuiting the single-standard rule; FCPA / OECD framework is being ignored. Follow-up: apply the categorical imperative — could this maxim be willed universal?
- **Pattern: An ethical-maximum response is rejected as too costly.** Signal: ethical-minimum is chosen with the rationale "the law doesn't require more". Diagnosis: short-term profit framing dominates; goodwill and long-term reputation costs are unweighted. Follow-up: model the multi-year reputation effect; bring stakeholder welfare into the cost-benefit calculation.
- **Pattern: Compliance training is being ramped up after a scandal.** Signal: ethics training programmes proliferate; tone at the top is unchanged. Diagnosis: compliance-as-checkbox; conscience formation and culture change are not being addressed. Follow-up: examine leadership behaviour; ask whether senior leaders model the conduct the training requires.
- **Pattern: Whistleblowing is treated as career-ending betrayal.** Signal: known reporters of wrongdoing are isolated, "reorganised out", or quietly blocked from promotion. Diagnosis: an institutional pattern that punishes ethical dissent; the Sallie Krawcheck cost is being normalised. Follow-up: measure whether internal reporting channels are protected in fact, not just on paper.
- **Pattern: A "greenwashing" CSR claim sits next to unchanged extractive practice.** Signal: prominent sustainability statements and ESG reports coexist with high water / carbon / pollution intensity. Diagnosis: greenwashing as defined in Ch 3.4; CSR is functioning as PR. Follow-up: test the underlying practice against the claim; ask what sustained behaviour change would look like.
- **Pattern: An employee or contractor is being paid through an at-will or contractor relationship while functioning as a full-time employee.** Signal: hours, supervision, equipment, and continuity match employment, but classification is contractor. Diagnosis: misclassification to dodge benefits and payroll taxes (the Microsoft pattern). Follow-up: review classification against the IRS criteria; consider the ethical implication that the 30% benefits saving is "the margin between middle class and below".
- **Pattern: A non-compete is being applied to a low-wage worker.** Signal: a sandwich-shop employee, a cleaner, or a barista is being held to a multi-year geographic non-compete. Diagnosis: the Jimmy John's pattern; non-compete weaponised against low-wage workers' mobility. Follow-up: review against state law (California voids most post-employment non-competes) and apply the moral-minimum test.
- **Pattern: Wellness program data is being used in HR decisions.** Signal: employees with high BMI, smokers, or those with health conditions face subtle differences in evaluation. Diagnosis: privacy intrusion via wellness programs (the Hiltzik caution). Follow-up: review what employee health data is collected and how it influences employment decisions; apply the harm principle.

## When to Use This Reference

- A decision affects multiple stakeholder groups whose interests diverge and shareholders are not the only group with standing.
- A decision is legally compliant but ethically uncomfortable; the question is whether to do more than the law requires.
- A decision is in international territory where local norms permit conduct (bribery, sweatshop conditions, environmental dumping) the firm's own standards prohibit.
- A high-power leader is modelling conduct that is at odds with the company's stated ethics; questions about tone-at-the-top arise.
- A scandal has emerged and the firm is choosing between minimum compliance response and maximum stakeholder-honouring response.
- A whistleblower is considering reporting; questions about timing, evidence, and protection arise.
- A pay or hiring decision raises equity and disparate-impact questions.
- A product, sustainability, or supply-chain decision involves environmental or community stakeholders without voice.
- A management is evaluating whether a wellness program, monitoring policy, or contractor classification is ethically defensible regardless of legal coverage.

## Worked Example

A medium-sized US technology company has been given the opportunity to enter a large Southeast Asian market. The local agent indicates that securing a key government contract will require a "facilitation payment" of approximately $50,000 to a senior official's adviser — in addition to the publicly listed bid. The CFO tells the new director of international business that "this is just how business is done here; everyone does it; it's a tiny share of the revenue".

**Recognising the ethical dimension.** The director slows down. The conversation has been framed as a compliance question — does the FCPA cover this? — but multiple stakeholders are affected: the company's shareholders (legal and reputational risk); the local population (paying for a contract awarded by corruption); employees (asked to participate in an illegal act); the foreign government's institutional integrity (eroded by every such payment).

**Bounding.** The director maps stakeholders. High-power-high-interest: shareholders, board, US regulators, the local government. High-interest-low-power: local population (whose taxes ultimately fund the contract), the company's employees (whose careers are exposed if the act is discovered). She notes the moral-minimum line: paying a bribe to obtain the contract treats the local population merely as a means to revenue, never as ends in themselves.

**Applying the lenses.** *Utilitarian:* near-term, payment maximises company revenue; long-term, the FCPA / OECD enforcement risk is severe (BHP Billiton and GlaxoSmithKline each paid $25 million for less; Telia paid $965 million). Total expected utility, properly counted, is negative. *Deontological:* the maxim "pay bribes when locally tolerated" cannot be willed universal — a market in which all firms bribe destroys allocative efficiency and rule of law. The local population is being treated merely as a means. *Virtue:* a phronetic leader does not act this way; reputation among employees and across the industry would be damaged. *Single-standard test:* the director would not pay a similar bribe to a US official; the FCPA framework is built precisely to deny the cultural-relativism defence.

**Stakeholder-impact testing.** The "everybody does it" framing is exactly the failure mode the FCPA was designed to address. The local agent's framing assigns no weight to the local population or to the long-term institutional cost.

**Implementation.** The director declines the facilitation payment. She communicates the decision to the local agent and asks whether the company can compete on the listed terms. She communicates the decision to her direct report and to the CFO with the reasoning explicit. She updates the company's international-business compliance training to make this case a teaching example. She documents the decision so that future questions about the foregone revenue can be answered with the long-term reputation argument.

**Reviewing.** The contract is awarded to a competitor. Six months later, news emerges that the competitor is under DOJ investigation for FCPA violations connected to that very contract. The CEO acknowledges that the director's decision protected the firm from the same exposure. The director files this as a phronesis lesson: the consequence-only calculation that the CFO offered was incomplete; the deontological line settled the matter and the long-term consequences vindicated it.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **"It's legal, so it's ethical."** Conflating compliance with ethics. The moral minimum often exceeds legal compliance.
- **"It's the way business is done here."** Cultural relativism used to justify bribery, sweatshops, environmental dumping. The single-standard rule rejects this defence.
- **"We're maximising shareholder value."** Strict shareholder primacy applied as exclusive duty. The book traces the legal evolution away from this position.
- **"The training programme covers it."** Compliance-as-checkbox without culture change or tone-at-the-top.
- **"The cost-benefit analysis works out."** Pure utilitarian framing without deontological constraints. Some acts are wrong even when the consequences favour them.
- **"Quarterly results matter most."** Short-term profit framing systematically undervaluing long-term goodwill, reputation, and stakeholder welfare.
- **"They're contractors, not employees."** Misclassification to evade benefits and payroll taxes.
- **"We have a sustainability page on our website."** Greenwashing — CSR claim without substantive practice change.
- **"The CEO earns 300× the median worker because the market sets executive pay."** The lead author treats this as evidence of distorted distribution, not market efficiency.
- **"Whistleblowers are disloyal."** Treating ethical dissent as betrayal; the False Claims Act and protected disclosure statutes exist precisely because internal channels often fail.

**Named limits of the source.** The book addresses business ethics at the level of managerial decision-making; it does not provide legal advice, regulatory guidance, or audit-quality analysis. The lead author's preference for deontology over utilitarianism (Appendix C) is explicitly a personal position, not the book's settled conclusion. The book's international scope is broad but its FCPA/OECD analysis is introductory; practitioners making live bribery-compliance decisions need legal counsel, not this text alone. The book does not address AI ethics, algorithmic accountability, or platform-governance decisions in depth.
