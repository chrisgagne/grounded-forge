# OpenStax Introduction to Business, Decision-Making Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2018). *Introduction to Business*. Rice University. CC BY 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/introduction-business. Lead author Lawrence J. Gitman; six senior contributing authors.

## Decision-Making Relevance

OpenStax's Introduction to Business treats decision-making as a core managerial activity threaded across functional areas. Ch 6 ("Management and Leadership in Today's Organizations") names decision-making as one of the manager's key roles and distinguishes programmed (routine, rule-amenable) from non-programmed (novel, ambiguous) decisions, anchoring the five-step decision process (recognise, gather information, select, implement, gather feedback) as the systematic approach for non-programmed cases. Ch 6.8 frames crisis-management decision-making with worked guidance from named CEOs responding to Hurricane Katrina.

Beyond Ch 6, decision-making recurs across the survey: Ch 2 frames ethical decision-making with the feelings test and newspaper/social-media test; Ch 7 discusses centralisation vs decentralisation as a decision about who decides; Ch 9 covers motivation theories that shape how employees decide whether to exert effort (expectancy, equity, goal-setting); Ch 10 develops production-control decisions through Gantt charts, CPM, and PERT; Ch 14 frames financial decisions through ratio analysis; Ch 16 develops the risk-return trade-off as the core financial-decision principle; and Ch 17 applies the same six-step planning framework to career decisions.

This distillation consolidates these threads: how to recognise decision type and choose the right process, how to surface and challenge biases, how to evaluate alternatives systematically when stakes are high, and how to handle crisis-mode decisions when contingency planning is incomplete.

## Key Concepts for Decision-Making

1. **Programmed vs non-programmed decisions.** Programmed decisions occur frequently in routine settings and benefit from established procedures (hiring policy, ordering supplies). Non-programmed decisions are infrequent, unforeseen, or unusual, lack precedent, and require systematic processing. The five-step process applies to non-programmed decisions: recognise/define the problem; gather information; select alternatives; act; gather feedback. (Source: OpenStax, *Introduction to Business*, Ch 6.6)

2. **Risk-return trade-off.** "The higher the risk, the greater the return that is required." Financial managers consider risk and return in every investment, financing, and capital-budgeting choice. The principle applies to non-financial decisions too — the cost of new product development, market entry, or technology investment must be weighed against the magnitude and likelihood of benefit. (Source: Ch 16.1)

3. **Five-step decision-making process.** Used across the book for any non-trivial decision: (1) recognise or define the problem or opportunity; (2) gather information to identify alternative solutions or actions; (3) select one or more alternatives after evaluating strengths and weaknesses; (4) put the chosen alternative into action; (5) gather information to obtain feedback on effectiveness. The text warns: "It can be easy (and dangerous) for managers to get stuck at any stage of the decision-making process," citing analysis paralysis. (Source: Ch 6.6, Exhibit 6.7)

4. **Four planning types ordered by horizon.** Strategic (1-5 years; top management; broad/general; high uncertainty); tactical (less than 1 year; middle management; more specific; moderate certainty); operational (current; supervisory; specific/concrete; reasonable certainty); contingency (alternative paths for crises). Choosing the right planning horizon is the first decision before structuring any other decision. (Source: Ch 6.2, Table 6.2)

5. **Six environmental forces require continuous scanning.** Cultural/social, demographic, economic, technological, political/legal, competitive forces shape the context within which decisions are made. *Environmental scanning* gathers and evaluates this data continuously to identify opportunities and threats. (Source: Ch 11.2)

6. **Two ethical-decision tests.** The *feelings test*: after deciding, does the discomfort or unease pass? The *newspaper or social-media test*: how would an objective reporter describe the decision on the front page? Both tests surface conflicts of interest and ethical violations the rational analysis may miss. (Source: Ch 2.2)

7. **Carroll's pyramid of CSR for stakeholder-affecting decisions.** Decisions stack from foundation upward: economic responsibility (without profit, others don't matter), legal responsibility (obey the law), ethical responsibility (do what is right), philanthropic responsibility (be a good corporate citizen). All four must be considered for any decision affecting stakeholders. (Source: Ch 2.3)

8. **Make-or-buy decision criteria.** Quantity needed (low quantity favours buy); secrecy of design (high secrecy favours make); supplier reliability and quality (uncertain favours make); competitive advantage at stake (favours make). The make-or-buy decision applies broadly — not only to manufacturing parts but to outsourcing services, in-house vs vendor IT, etc. (Source: Ch 10.4)

9. **Five competitive forces shape choice between mechanistic and organic structure.** Strategy (innovation favours organic; cost-control favours mechanistic), size (larger firms tend mechanistic), and external environment (complex/unstable favours organic). The structure decision is not abstract — it shapes the speed and quality of subsequent decisions. (Source: Ch 7.6)

10. **Expectancy theory for effort decisions.** When evaluating whether subordinates will exert effort, decisions depend on three relationships: effort-to-performance (will my effort produce performance?); performance-to-outcome (will performance produce the outcome?); outcomes-to-individual-needs (do I value the outcome?). The decision to make outcomes contingent on performance is the practical implication. (Source: Ch 9.6)

11. **Equity theory and procedural justice in distribution decisions.** When designing reward systems or compensation structures, equity theory predicts that workers compare their outcome-input ratio to a referent's. Perceived inequity triggers restorative behaviours — changed work habits, demands for raises, distortion of self/other perceptions, departure. The decision-design implication is that *process* matters as much as outcome — procedural justice can sustain commitment even when distributive outcomes disappoint. (Source: Ch 9.6)

12. **Critical Path Method and PERT for project decisions.** For complex projects with many interdependent tasks, CPM identifies the longest path through linked activities; PERT extends with three time estimates (optimistic, most probable, pessimistic) to anticipate delays. The decision: where to invest schedule-protection effort. Tasks on the critical path get priority. (Source: Ch 10.5)

## Questions to Ask During Decision-Making

### Phase 1: Framing (Recognising the decision and gathering context)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify the decision type | Is this a programmed (routine, rule-amenable) decision or a non-programmed (novel, ambiguous) one? Programmed decisions can use existing procedures; non-programmed ones warrant the five-step process. |
| Choose the planning horizon | Is this strategic (1-5 years; broad), tactical (less than 1 year; specific), operational (current), or contingency (crisis-alternative)? |
| Surface ethical implications | Does this decision affect stakeholders without voice? Whose welfare is at stake? Does it pass the feelings test and the newspaper/social-media test? |
| Identify environmental forces | Which of the six environmental forces (cultural/social, demographic, economic, technological, political/legal, competitive) most shape this decision? |
| Detect crisis vs business-as-usual | Is this happening within a contingency plan, or beyond what was planned? If beyond, who has the authority to make non-programmed decisions in real time? |

### Phase 2: Bounding (Defining the decision and its constraints)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Clarify what is being decided | What is the decision actually about? What is in scope and what is out? |
| Identify stakeholders affected | Who has standing — employees, customers, society (including environmental and community), investors, regulators? Whose interests should be considered even if they have not yet asserted themselves? |
| Identify time and resource constraints | How long can I take? What resources can I draw on? |
| Identify regulatory and legal constraints | Are there laws, antitrust rules, consumer protection laws, contract terms, or other legal constraints that bound the decision space? |
| Surface ethical constraints | What four-tier responsibility frame (economic, legal, ethical, philanthropic) applies? Where are the deontological lines? |

### Phase 3: Exploring (Generating alternatives)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Generate enough options | Have I generated three to five real alternatives, or stopped at two? |
| Engage diverse perspectives | Have I sought out colleagues with different functional backgrounds (operations, marketing, finance, HR, legal)? Survey-textbook structure suggests every functional area has a stake. |
| Test the make-or-buy frame | If the decision involves capability — internal vs external? Quantity, secrecy, reliability, competitive-advantage criteria. |
| Consider evidence-based methods | What internal data, industry benchmarks, or external research bear on the decision? |
| Account for cross-cultural variation | If the decision affects employees across cultures, has cultural variation been considered (English-speaking individualism vs Northern European personal-life priorities, etc.)? |

### Phase 4: Deciding (Analysing and selecting)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply the risk-return trade-off | What is the magnitude of return? What is the magnitude of risk? Does the return justify the risk given the firm's risk appetite? |
| Use ratio analysis where applicable | For financial decisions, what do liquidity, profitability, activity, and debt ratios suggest about the firm's capacity to absorb risk? |
| Test for groupthink | Is the decision being endorsed by consensus that may suppress dissent? Have I asked the room "what evidence would change our view?" |
| Test for analysis paralysis | Have I gathered enough information? Am I delaying past the point of useful additional information? |
| Apply the two ethical tests | Feelings test: how does the decision feel? Newspaper/social-media test: how would it look on the front page? |

### Phase 5: Ratifying (Implementing the decision)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Determine whose ratification is required | Board-level? Senior management? Stakeholder consultation? |
| Communicate clearly | Have I communicated the reasoning, not just the outcome, to those affected? Especially for stakeholder decisions, the *process* matters as much as the result. |
| Address conflict from the decision | Some decisions create unhappy stakeholders. Have I prepared for conflict productively rather than avoiding it? |
| Preserve unity of command | If implementing through line organisation, are reporting relationships clear? Avoid matrix-style ambiguity. |

### Phase 6: Monitoring (Evaluating effectiveness)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Define monitoring metrics | What indicators will tell me whether the decision is working? Are these specific, measurable, and time-bound? |
| Distinguish bad luck from bad decision | A bad outcome may follow a sound process if uncertainty was high. Am I evaluating *process* as well as outcome? |
| Be willing to reverse | If new information indicates the decision was wrong, am I able to reverse course? Or am I escalating commitment to defend the prior choice? |
| Capture learnings | What did the process teach? Build the lesson into the next decision of this type. |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: Decision-makers cannot articulate why they need to decide now.** Signal: pressure to decide without clear bounding. Diagnosis (in source vocabulary): the decision lacks framing — the "right" planning horizon hasn't been chosen. Follow-up: identify whether the decision is strategic, tactical, operational, or contingency-driven; bound the scope before proceeding.
- **Pattern: Crisis response is improvised because contingency plans are inadequate.** Signal: confusion about chain of command; non-programmed decisions made under high pressure. Diagnosis: contingency planning gap. Follow-up: empower local decision-makers (Norfolk Southern model) to make non-programmed decisions; conduct a postmortem after the crisis to inform the next round.
- **Pattern: Sales/effort decisions are misaligned with reward structure.** Signal: employees not exerting effort despite stated incentives. Diagnosis: failure of effort-performance, performance-outcome, or outcome-needs link in expectancy theory. Follow-up: check each link explicitly; redesign reward to make outcomes contingent on performance.
- **Pattern: Reward decisions provoke complaints despite seemingly fair distribution.** Signal: high performers complain; turnover rises. Diagnosis: equity theory at work — workers comparing outcome-input ratio to referents. Follow-up: examine procedural justice; communicate the basis for differential rewards.
- **Pattern: Project schedules consistently slip.** Signal: late deliverables; quality compromises to meet deadlines. Diagnosis: critical-path tasks unprotected; PERT/CPM not in use. Follow-up: identify critical-path tasks; protect them with buffer; use PERT three-estimate technique for complex projects.
- **Pattern: Manager defends a decision that early evidence suggests was wrong.** Signal: more resources committed despite signals to the contrary. Diagnosis: escalation of commitment; sunk-cost fallacy. Follow-up: precommit to monitoring metrics; commit in advance to act on what the evidence shows; treat sunk costs as sunk.
- **Pattern: Ethical concerns are framed as compliance questions only.** Signal: discussion focuses on "is this legal" rather than "is this right." Diagnosis: Carroll's pyramid being collapsed — legal responsibility being treated as ethical responsibility. Follow-up: separate the four CSR levels explicitly; apply the feelings test and newspaper test.
- **Pattern: Cross-cultural team's commitment to a decision differs by region.** Signal: same decision generates enthusiastic uptake in one region, foot-dragging in another. Diagnosis: cultural variation in motivation drivers (e.g., English-speaking individualism vs Northern European collectivism). Follow-up: tailor the rationale and communication to cultural context; use Adler/Gunderson framework as orientation.

## When to Use This Reference

- A non-programmed decision is approaching and the manager wants to use the systematic five-step process.
- A crisis is unfolding without a contingency plan; non-programmed decisions must be made in real time.
- A capital expenditure or major investment requires risk-return analysis.
- A reward structure or compensation system is being designed and expectancy/equity considerations matter.
- A project schedule is at risk; CPM/PERT logic should guide where protection is invested.
- An ethical dimension to a decision is unclear and needs structured consideration.
- An organisation is choosing between mechanistic and organic structural design.
- A career or life-planning decision is being made and the six-step planning process applies.

## Worked Example

A regional pizza chain owner must decide whether to invest in an automated pizza-making robot for one of its 30 stores. The robot costs $600,000, requires 18 months of integration, and will reduce per-pizza labor cost by ~30%. Local labor markets are tight, but the robot risks customer pushback (the chain's brand emphasises "hand-crafted").

Framing: This is a non-programmed strategic decision (1-5 year horizon). The owner uses the five-step process rather than improvising. Six environmental forces in play: technological (robot is new and improving), economic (tight labor market raises costs), competitive (national chains automating), social (consumer attitudes toward automation in food), demographic (younger customers more accepting of automation), legal (food safety regulation).

Bounding: The decision is whether to invest in *one store as a pilot* — not to automate the chain. Ethical considerations: employees affected by job displacement; customers' expectation of quality. Carroll's pyramid: economic responsibility favors the investment if ROI is sufficient; ethical responsibility raises the displacement question.

Exploring: The owner generates four alternatives: (1) install the robot in one store as a pilot; (2) wait two years for the technology to mature; (3) negotiate higher wages and retain hand-crafted positioning; (4) outsource pizza production to a centralised commissary serving all 30 stores. The owner consults operations head, marketing head, finance head, and HR head — engaging diverse functional perspectives.

Deciding: Risk-return analysis: $600,000 investment with 18-month payback period if labor savings materialize and customer experience holds; risk if customers reject the automated pizza (~25% probability of meaningful brand impact, per the chain's market research). Make-or-buy decision applied: the chain considers whether the technology is competitive-advantage-worthy in-house. The two ethical tests are applied: the feelings test surfaces the founder's discomfort about employee displacement; the newspaper test asks how a story about robot pizza vs hand-crafted positioning would land.

Selection: The owner chooses option (1) — pilot in one store — with explicit success criteria: maintained customer satisfaction at 4.0/5.0 or above; payback within 24 months; no more than 3 jobs eliminated per store (workers offered transfer to other stores). Communication plan ensures employees in the pilot store know the criteria and timeline.

Implementation: Pilot launched. Weekly reviews track customer satisfaction, throughput, and employee transition.

Monitoring: At 18 months, customer satisfaction has dropped to 3.7/5.0 — below the 4.0 threshold. Despite favorable financials, the owner reverses the decision and removes the robot, citing the precommitted monitoring criteria.

This worked example illustrates several key concepts: the five-step process, the risk-return trade-off, the make-or-buy frame, both ethical tests, expectancy theory (employees' perception of fair treatment), and the discipline of precommitting to monitoring metrics so the manager doesn't escalate commitment beyond evidence.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Treating non-programmed decisions as programmed.** Skipping the five-step process for novel decisions produces poor outcomes and prevents learning.
- **Defending sunk-cost decisions.** Escalation of commitment leads managers to throw more good resources after bad.
- **Premature consensus in groups.** Groupthink produces unchallenged decisions that look good in the room and bad in the world.
- **Conflict-avoidance leading to compounding problems.** Avoiding necessary process conflict prevents productive exploration of options.
- **Treating ethical questions as compliance questions only.** Reduced moral attention produces decisions that are technically legal but harmful to stakeholders.
- **Centralisation overdone.** Centralisation can prevent quick local decisions in dynamic environments.
- **Decentralisation without skills or training.** Pushing authority down without ensuring competence can produce costly mistakes.
- **Cost-cutting that erodes service-differentiation.** When >80% of US GDP is services, cost-only competitive advantage erodes; decisions to cut service quality are rarely sustainable.
- **Ignoring the equity-theory dimension of pay decisions.** Reward structures that ignore procedural justice predict disengagement.
- **Failing to monitor.** Without precommitted monitoring metrics, decisions cannot be evaluated and learning is lost.
