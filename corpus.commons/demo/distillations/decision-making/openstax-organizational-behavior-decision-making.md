# OpenStax Organizational Behavior, Decision-Making Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2019). *Organizational Behavior*. Rice University. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/organizational-behavior. Lead author J. Stewart Black; 11 contributing authors.

## Decision-Making Relevance

OpenStax's Organizational Behavior dedicates an entire chapter (Ch 6, "Perception and Managerial Decision Making") to decision-making, treating it as a core managerial activity that ranges from small daily choices to strategic organisational moves. The text grounds decision-making in dual-system cognitive theory — a reflective system (logical, analytical, deliberate) and a reactive system (quick, impulsive, intuitive) — and argues that effective managers learn which system to engage in which context.

Beyond Ch 6, the book frames decision-making as a recurring component of broader organisational processes: motivation theory addresses how employees decide whether to exert effort (Ch 7); equity theory addresses how employees decide whether outcomes are fair and what restorative actions to take (Ch 7-8); leadership theories examine the decision authority and style of leaders (Ch 12); the conflict-resolution chapter distinguishes five decision-making modes for handling disagreements (Ch 14); and the change-management chapter contrasts top-down (deficit-based, mechanistic) and bottom-up (appreciative, emergent) approaches to making organisation-wide change decisions (Ch 16).

This distillation consolidates these threads into a working pattern: how to recognise when a decision needs to be made; how to choose between programmed and non-programmed processes; how to surface biases and barriers; how to use group decision-making well; and how to recognise when the type of decision matters more than the technique used to make it.

## Key Concepts for Decision-Making

1. **Reflective versus reactive systems.** The brain processes decisions through one of two routes at a time: a reflective system (prefrontal cortex, logical, analytical, deliberate) and a reactive system (basal ganglia and amygdala, quick, impulsive, intuitive). The reactive route is appropriate when experience teaches the right response and speed matters; the reflective route is appropriate for novel or complex situations. Strong emotions push decision-making toward the reactive route. (Source: OpenStax, *Organizational Behavior*, Ch 6.2, "How the Brain Processes Information to Make Decisions")

2. **Programmed versus non-programmed decisions.** Programmed decisions are repeated and rule-amenable; they support heuristics (mental shortcuts) that save time without guaranteeing optimal solutions. Non-programmed decisions are novel, ambiguous, high-involvement; they require systematic processing through the reflective system and the six-step process (recognise, generate alternatives, analyse, select, implement, evaluate). (Source: Ch 6.3)

3. **Six barriers to effective decision-making.** Bounded rationality (cognitive limits), escalation of commitment (staying with poor decisions as costs mount), time constraints, uncertainty, personal biases (similar-to-me, confirmation bias), and conflict avoidance. (Source: Ch 6.4)

4. **Process conflict versus relationship conflict.** Process conflict — disagreement about the best way to do something — can improve decision quality through deeper exploration of options. Relationship conflict — disagreement that becomes personal — degrades decision quality and pushes participants into reactive-system processing. The implication: surface and welcome process conflict; quell relationship conflict early. (Source: Ch 6.4, "Conflict")

5. **Evidence-based decision-making.** Managers should systematically collect the best available evidence — including external evidence about stakeholders, contextual factors, costs, benefits — rather than rely on intuition alone. This approach is particularly valuable for new managers and for experienced managers starting something new. (Source: Ch 6.5)

6. **Critical thinking and logical fallacies.** A disciplined process of evaluating information quality and arguments. Seven common logical fallacies are catalogued: non sequitur, false cause, ad hominem, genetic fallacy, appeal to tradition, bandwagon approach, appeal to emotion. Recognising these in others' arguments — and one's own — improves decision quality. (Source: Ch 6.5, Table 6.2)

7. **Rest's four-component ethical decision-making.** Moral sensitivity (recognising the issue has a moral component), moral judgment (determining right versus wrong), moral motivation/intention (deciding to do the right thing), moral character/action (actually doing it). A failure at any point in the chain produces unethical action. (Source: Ch 6.5, "Are there ethical implications?")

8. **Groupthink and suppression of dissent.** Group decisions can incorporate diverse perspectives and reduce bias, but only when membership is genuinely diverse and members feel free to speak. Groupthink occurs when members suppress concerns to keep peace; suppression of dissent occurs when one member dominates. The devil's advocate role (intentionally taking the critic position) is one mitigation. (Source: Ch 6.6)

9. **Bounded rationality and the cognitive-complexity trait.** The text presents bounded rationality as foundational: managers cannot fully grasp all alternatives or implications. High-cognitive-complexity managers cope better — they seek more information, integrate discrepant data, consider more solutions, and use more complex strategies. Cognitive complexity is partly trait, partly cultivable through structured habits. (Source: Ch 2.2 and Ch 6.4)

10. **Equity in decision outcomes.** Equity theory (Adams) predicts that when employees compare their outcome-input ratio to a referent other's and find inequity, they will engage in restorative behaviours. The implication for decision-making is that the *process* matters as much as the outcome — procedural justice can sustain commitment even when distributive outcomes disappoint. (Source: Ch 7.3 and Ch 7.4)

## Questions to Ask During Decision-Making

### Phase 1: Framing (Recognising the decision and gathering context)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify the decision type | Is this a programmed (rule-amenable, recurring) decision or a non-programmed (novel, ambiguous) one? Programmed decisions should be addressed quickly using heuristics; non-programmed decisions warrant the six-step process. |
| Avoid premature reaction | Am I reacting through the reactive system because the situation feels familiar, or because strong emotion is short-circuiting reflection? If emotion is high, can I delay until reflection is possible? |
| Surface the moral dimension | Does this decision have ethical implications I should make explicit before proceeding? Whose interests are affected? |
| Detect emerging escalation | Is there a prior decision I am defending rather than evaluating freshly? Where does the cost-of-reversal calculation actually stand? |
| Determine information adequacy | Have I gathered enough evidence to decide well, or am I about to act on too little? Conversely, am I delaying to avoid the decision itself? |

### Phase 2: Bounding (Defining the decision and its constraints)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Clarify what is and is not being decided | What is the decision actually about? What is in scope and what is out? |
| Identify stakeholders affected | Who has standing in this decision? Who is affected by the outcome but lacks voice? |
| Identify time and resource constraints | How long can I take? What resources can I draw on? Is the time pressure real or self-imposed? |
| Identify ethical constraints | Are there decisions I should not make even if they appear optimal on other criteria? Where are the deontological lines? |

### Phase 3: Exploring (Generating alternatives)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Generate enough options | Have I generated three to five real alternatives, not just one or two? The text observes that managers often stop at two and pick the better. |
| Avoid similarity bias | Am I generating options that resemble what I already prefer? Have I deliberately considered options outside my comfort zone? |
| Engage diverse perspectives | Have I sought out colleagues whose backgrounds, experiences, or thinking styles differ from mine? Diverse input alters majority cognition for the better. |
| Test for confirmation bias | What evidence would change my preferred view, and am I actively seeking it? |
| Consider creative options | Have I given my mind permission to wander, combine ideas in novel ways, or step back from the traditional framing? |

### Phase 4: Deciding (Analysing and selecting)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply critical thinking | Am I noticing logical fallacies in my own reasoning or others'? Are arguments standing on their own merits or relying on tradition, popularity, or emotion? |
| Consider long-term consequences | What are the multi-year implications, beyond the immediate decision frame? |
| Assess ethical implications squarely | If this decision were on the front page of a major newspaper tomorrow, would I feel guilty or proud? Which feeling is the decision-relevant signal? |
| Balance evidence and judgment | Where evidence is strong, follow it. Where evidence is thin, what is my best-supported judgment? Am I being honest about which case I am in? |
| Recognise that perfection is unattainable | Have I waited long enough to gather evidence without paralysing the process? Is "good enough given the constraints" the appropriate target? |

### Phase 5: Ratifying (Implementing the decision)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Communicate clearly | Have I explained the reasoning, not just the outcome, to those affected? |
| Address conflict from the decision | Some decisions create unhappy stakeholders; have I prepared for the conflict productively rather than avoided it? |
| Act on the decision | Am I willing to follow through, including parts that involve confronting people or pushing through resistance? |

### Phase 6: Monitoring (Evaluating effectiveness)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Evaluate the outcome | Did the decision produce the expected results? What evidence am I using to judge? |
| Distinguish bad luck from bad decision | A bad outcome may follow a sound process if uncertainty was high. Reverse: a good outcome may follow a poor process. Which case is this? |
| Be willing to reverse | If new information indicates the decision was wrong, am I able to reverse course, or am I escalating commitment to defend the prior choice? |
| Capture learnings | What did the process teach me that I can apply to the next decision of this type? |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: A team is iterating on alternatives without converging.** Signal: meetings repeat similar discussions; no commitments made. Diagnosis (in source vocabulary): the decision lacks a clear bounding, or the team is operating in non-programmed-decision territory without applying the systematic six-step process. Follow-up: explicitly bound the decision; choose a process; commit to a decision date.
- **Pattern: A decision is being defended after evidence has shifted against it.** Signal: investments increase as outcomes worsen; criticism is dismissed. Diagnosis: escalation of commitment. Follow-up: re-evaluate freshly; treat sunk costs as sunk; ask "what would I do if I had not made the prior choice?"
- **Pattern: Heated debate without a name for the binding constraint.** Signal: arguments escalate but stakeholders cannot agree what the dispute is actually about. Diagnosis: relationship conflict has overtaken process conflict; participants have shifted into reactive-system processing. Follow-up: pause; reframe to process conflict explicitly; surface what each party actually disagrees about.
- **Pattern: A group reaches consensus too quickly.** Signal: a meaningful decision is endorsed without challenge; concerns are shared privately afterward. Diagnosis: groupthink or suppression of dissent. Follow-up: assign a devil's advocate; structure dissenting opinions explicitly; revisit with explicit invitation to challenge.
- **Pattern: Manager is reacting to feedback or news through the reactive system.** Signal: snap judgments under emotional pressure; later regret. Diagnosis: emotion is short-circuiting reflective processing. Follow-up: build in a delay; defer significant decisions until emotional intensity passes; develop self-awareness of emotional triggers.
- **Pattern: Decision-makers cannot articulate why an evidence-based option is being rejected.** Signal: gut-feel justifications under-explained. Diagnosis: implicit bias may be operating, possibly similarity bias or confirmation bias. Follow-up: state the evidence explicitly; ask what evidence would change the decision; consult someone with a different bias profile.
- **Pattern: Ethical concerns are being framed as compliance questions.** Signal: discussion focuses on "is this legal" rather than "is this right". Diagnosis: moral sensitivity (Rest stage 1) is underdeveloped. Follow-up: name the ethical dimension; consider the news-test ("how would this look on the front page"); cultivate awareness of consequences for stakeholders without voice.

## When to Use This Reference

- The decision is non-programmed and warrants systematic process.
- A decision-making process has stalled, escalated, or produced an outcome no-one is willing to defend.
- A team is repeatedly making decisions in domains with unclear ethical implications.
- A manager is preparing to make a high-stakes choice under time pressure and wants to avoid reactive-system errors.
- An organisation is choosing between top-down and bottom-up approaches to a major change decision.
- A group is composing itself for a decision and the question is who should be in the room.

## Worked Example

A regional sales director must decide whether to retain a long-tenured sales manager whose recent results have declined sharply. The director's first impulse is to extend the rope: the manager has delivered for years and the slump may be temporary. Aware that this is a high-stakes non-programmed decision, the director slows down.

Framing: this is not a programmed performance-review decision; it is a judgment about whether continued investment is justified. The reactive system is pulling toward loyalty (similarity bias: the director and the manager came up together). The reflective system needs to engage.

Bounding: the decision is whether to continue investment for another quarter, set explicit performance milestones, or transition the manager. It is *not* a question of personal worth or past contribution.

Exploring: the director generates four options — extend; set explicit milestones; reassign to a different role; transition. The director consults two colleagues with different bias profiles, including one who has worked with the manager less directly.

Deciding: evidence shows the slump traces to a market shift the manager has not adapted to. Asked which evidence would change his view, the director cannot identify any beyond personal sentiment — a confirmation-bias signal. Long-term implications: extending without milestones risks setting a precedent that tenure substitutes for results.

Selection: set explicit 90-day milestones with weekly check-ins. The director acknowledges this may produce conflict with the manager and prepares an honest, respectful conversation rather than avoiding it.

Implementation: the conversation happens; milestones are agreed; weekly meetings begin.

Monitoring: at 90 days, evidence is reviewed. The director commits in advance to act on what the evidence shows, regardless of how uncomfortable that action will be — a precommitment against escalation of commitment.

This worked example illustrates several key concepts in the source: dual-system processing, the six-step process, similarity bias, confirmation bias, escalation precommitment, and the productive use of process conflict (the milestone conversation) without descent into relationship conflict.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Snap judgement on novel problems.** The reactive system is treating a non-programmed decision as if it were programmed; the cost is poor outcome and inability to learn.
- **Defending a sunk-cost decision.** Escalation of commitment leads to throwing more good resources after bad.
- **Premature consensus in groups.** Groupthink produces unchallenged decisions that look good in the room and bad in the world.
- **Conflict-avoidance leading to compounding problems.** Avoiding process conflict prevents productive exploration; avoiding relationship conflict allows poor performance to continue.
- **Outcome-only judgement.** Judging a decision purely by its outcome conflates good luck with good process and discourages learning.
- **Treating ethical questions as compliance questions.** Reduced moral sensitivity produces decisions that are technically legal but deeply harmful to stakeholders without voice.
- **Reward systems disconnected from decision quality.** Equity theory predicts disengagement when the link between effort, performance, and reward is unclear; expectancy theory predicts the same. Decisions about reward design are themselves high-stakes decisions.

## Integration with Other References

| Reference | Connection |
|---|---|
| Bounded rationality (Simon) | The OpenStax text treats bounded rationality as foundational. Practitioners working in decision-making contexts should treat Simon's framing as the explanation for why the six-step process and evidence-based methods exist. |
| Cognitive dissonance (Festinger) | The text invokes cognitive consistency in the attitudes chapter (Ch 3.4); the implication for decision-making is that decisions inconsistent with one's beliefs produce tension that managers can either resolve through behaviour change or attitude change — and that escalation of commitment is one of those resolutions. |
| Goal-setting theory (Locke) | Goal theory and decision-making are paired: difficult, specific, accepted goals shape what decisions get made and which alternatives feel salient. |
| Expectancy theory (Vroom) | Decision-making about effort exertion follows expectancy logic. Practitioners working on motivation should pair the OpenStax decision-making framework with expectancy analysis. |
| Conflict-resolution modes (Thomas) | The five Thomas modes (competing, collaborating, compromising, avoiding, accommodating) are themselves decision-making frames for how to handle disagreement; the choice of mode is itself a decision. |
| Appreciative Inquiry (Cooperrider) | For organisation-wide decisions involving change, the AI 5-D cycle (define, discover, dream, design, destiny) offers a bottom-up alternative to top-down systematic decision-making and may produce stronger commitment to the resulting decision. |
