# OpenStax Principles of Management, Decision-Making Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2019). *Principles of Management*. Rice University. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/principles-management. Lead author David S. Bright; 15 contributing authors.

## Decision-Making Relevance

OpenStax's Principles of Management dedicates Chapter 2 to managerial decision-making, treating it as a core managerial activity ranging from small daily choices to strategic organisational moves. The text grounds decision-making in dual-system cognitive theory — a reflective system (logical, analytical, deliberate) and a reactive system (quick, impulsive, intuitive) — and argues that effective managers learn which system to engage in which context. The framing closely tracks OpenStax Organizational Behavior's Ch 6 treatment but anchors it within the broader leading-planning-organising-controlling architecture.

Beyond Ch 2, decision-making is a recurring component of organisational processes addressed throughout the book. The history chapter (Ch 3) presents Mary Parker Follett's three conflict-resolution paths — dominance, compromise, integration — as foundational decision-making vocabulary, and contrasts Taylor's and Fayol's prescriptive approaches with Woodward's contingency-school "it depends". The strategic-analysis and strategic-management chapters (Chs 8 and 9) develop systematic decision-making about competitive positioning through SWOT, PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, VRIO, and the strategic management cycle. The change-management chapter (Ch 10) contrasts top-down deficit-based and bottom-up appreciative approaches to change decisions. The HR chapter (Ch 11) addresses decisions about performance, pay, and talent. The motivation chapter (Ch 14) addresses how employees decide whether to exert effort. The planning-and-controlling chapter (Ch 17) treats planning itself as systematic decision-making about future action — distinguishing goal, domain, and hybrid planning, and offering Drucker's eight-area goal hierarchy.

Where Organizational Behavior contributes the cognitive process, Principles of Management adds the planning, strategic, and historical lenses. This distillation consolidates these threads into a working pattern: how to recognise when a decision needs to be made; how to choose between programmed and non-programmed processes; how to position the decision within the strategic and planning cycles; how to surface biases and barriers; how to use ethical lenses; and how to evaluate and learn from decisions over time.

## Key Concepts for Decision-Making

1. **Reflective versus reactive systems.** The brain processes decisions through one of two routes at a time: a reflective system (prefrontal cortex, logical, analytical, deliberate) and a reactive system (basal ganglia and amygdala, quick, impulsive, intuitive). Strong emotions push processing toward the reactive route. Effective decision-making relies on both logic and emotions, with the manager learning which system to engage. (Source: OpenStax, *Principles of Management*, Ch 2.2, "How the Brain Processes Information to Make Decisions")

2. **Programmed versus non-programmed decisions.** Programmed decisions are repeated and rule-amenable; they support heuristics that save time without guaranteeing optimality. Non-programmed decisions are novel, unstructured, and ambiguous; they require systematic processing through the reflective system and the six-step process: recognise, generate alternatives, analyse, select, implement, evaluate. (Source: Ch 2.3)

3. **Six barriers to effective decision-making.** Bounded rationality (cognitive limits), escalation of commitment (staying with poor decisions as costs mount), time constraints, uncertainty, personal biases (similar-to-me, confirmation bias), and conflict. The text distinguishes process conflict (productive — improves quality through deeper exploration) from relationship conflict (harmful — pushes participants into reactive-system processing). (Source: Ch 2.4)

4. **Evidence-based decision-making.** Managers should systematically collect the best available evidence — including external evidence about stakeholders, contextual factors, costs, benefits — rather than rely on intuition alone. Particularly valuable for new managers and for experienced managers starting something new. (Source: Ch 2.5)

5. **Critical thinking and seven logical fallacies.** A disciplined process of evaluating information quality and arguments. Seven common fallacies: non sequitur, false cause, ad hominem, genetic fallacy, appeal to tradition, bandwagon approach, appeal to emotion. (Source: Ch 2.5, Table 2.2)

6. **Rest's four-component ethical decision-making.** Moral sensitivity (recognising the issue has a moral component), moral judgment (right vs wrong), moral motivation (deciding to do the right thing), moral character (actually doing it). A failure at any point produces unethical action. The "front-page-of-the-newspaper" test surfaces moral judgment. (Source: Ch 2.5)

7. **Maximising shareholder wealth is rejected as decision criterion.** The text takes an explicit position: "Maximizing shareholder wealth is often a short-sighted decision … because it can harm the organization's financial viability in the future. … More importantly, increasing the wealth of shareholders is not an acceptable reason for causing harm to others". (Source: Ch 2.1)

8. **Groupthink and devil's advocate.** Group decisions improve quality with diverse membership and free expression of dissent. Groupthink occurs when members suppress concerns to keep peace; suppression of dissent occurs when one member dominates. The devil's advocate role mitigates both. (Source: Ch 2.6)

9. **Follett's three conflict-resolution paths.** Dominance (one party dictates terms — short-term resolution, long-term cost), compromise (neither party fully gets what they want), and integration (both parties state preferences and reach a creative agreement that satisfies both). Integration is the strongest decision-making outcome when stakes are high and the relationship is ongoing. (Source: Ch 3.6, "Follett and Conflict Resolution")

10. **Strategic decision frameworks: SWOT, PESTEL, Five Forces, VRIO.** SWOT identifies strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats. PESTEL examines political, economic, sociocultural, technological, environmental, legal forces. Porter's Five Forces evaluate industry rivalry, threat of new entrants, threat of substitutes, supplier power, buyer power. VRIO evaluates resources by value, rarity, imitability, organisation. Together they ground strategic-positioning decisions. (Source: Ch 8)

11. **The strategic management cycle.** Vision and mission → strategic analysis → strategic objectives → strategy formulation → implementation → measurement and evaluation. Activities run simultaneously rather than strictly sequentially. (Source: Ch 9.1)

12. **Goal vs domain vs hybrid planning.** Goal planning specifies goals and action statements; domain/directional planning identifies a domain and moves toward it without specifying outcomes (suits flexibility, goal disagreement, unstable environments, startups). Hybrid planning starts in domain mode and transitions to goal mode as learning accumulates. Goal planning produces higher performance when the conditions support it. (Source: Ch 17.2)

13. **Drucker's eight-area goal framework.** Market standing, innovation, productivity, profitability, physical and financial resources, manager performance and development, employee performance and attitude, public responsibility. Decisions should be evaluated for their effect across all eight, not just financial outcomes. (Source: Ch 17.4)

14. **Three change models for organisational decisions.** Lewin (unfreeze-change-refreeze, stable contexts), Kotter (eight-step, mechanistic, top-down, deficit-based), Cooperrider's Appreciative Inquiry (5-D cycle, abundance-based, bottom-up), Olson-Eoyang Complex Adaptive Systems (emergent, self-organising). The text argues choice depends on whether the organisation is in deficit or abundance, whether change is top-down or bottom-up, and whether the mindset is conventional or appreciative. (Source: Ch 10.3)

15. **Stuck in the middle.** Porter's position: trying to combine cost leadership and differentiation produces firms that excel at neither. Strategic decisions should commit to one generic strategy. The text reproduces this position favourably. (Source: Ch 8.6)

## Questions to Ask During Decision-Making

### Phase 1: Framing (Recognising the decision and gathering context)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify the decision type | Is this a programmed (rule-amenable, recurring) decision or a non-programmed (novel, ambiguous) one? Programmed decisions can be addressed quickly using heuristics; non-programmed decisions warrant the six-step process. |
| Identify the strategic level | Is this an operational, tactical, or strategic decision? The level determines who should be involved, what evidence is needed, and what time horizon applies. |
| Avoid premature reaction | Am I reacting through the reactive system because the situation feels familiar, or because strong emotion is short-circuiting reflection? |
| Surface the moral dimension | Does this decision have ethical implications I should make explicit before proceeding? Whose interests are affected, including stakeholders without voice? |
| Detect emerging escalation | Is there a prior decision I am defending rather than evaluating freshly? |
| Reject pure shareholder framing | Is this decision being framed only as shareholder-wealth maximisation? What harm to other stakeholders might that framing obscure? |

### Phase 2: Bounding (Defining the decision and its constraints)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Clarify what is and is not being decided | What is the decision actually about? What is in scope and what is out? |
| Identify stakeholders affected | Who has standing? Who is affected but lacks voice? |
| Identify time and resource constraints | How long can I take? Is the time pressure real or self-imposed? |
| Choose goal vs domain vs hybrid framing | Are the goals well-defined, or do I need to commit to a domain and let the goals emerge? Hybrid planning may suit the in-between case. |
| Apply Drucker's eight-area framework | Across market standing, innovation, productivity, profitability, resources, manager development, employee attitude, public responsibility — what does this decision affect? |

### Phase 3: Exploring (Generating alternatives and analysing context)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Generate enough options | Have I generated three to five real alternatives, not just one or two? |
| Avoid similarity bias | Am I generating options that resemble what I already prefer? |
| Engage diverse perspectives | Have I sought out colleagues whose backgrounds, experiences, or thinking styles differ from mine? Diverse input reduces bias and groupthink. |
| Test for confirmation bias | What evidence would change my preferred view, and am I actively seeking it? |
| Apply strategic-analysis tools | If this is a strategic decision: have I run SWOT, PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, and VRIO as appropriate? Or am I deciding without environmental and resource analysis? |
| Apply ethical principles | Which lens fits — utilitarianism (greatest good for greatest number), universalism (duty), rights, justice, virtue ethics, common good? Where does ethical relativism end and the single standard begin? |

### Phase 4: Deciding (Analysing and selecting)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply critical thinking | Am I noticing logical fallacies in my own reasoning or others'? Are arguments standing on their own merits? |
| Consider long-term consequences | What are the multi-year implications, beyond the immediate decision frame? Has short-term-profit bias crept in? |
| Use the news-test for ethics | If this decision were on the front page of a major newspaper tomorrow, would I feel guilty or proud? |
| Balance evidence and judgment | Where evidence is strong, follow it. Where evidence is thin, what is my best-supported judgment? |
| Resist "stuck in the middle" | If this is a strategic decision, am I genuinely committing to one generic strategy (cost leadership, differentiation, focus), or trying to do everything? |
| Recognise that perfection is unattainable | Am I waiting too long to gather evidence and paralysing the process? Is "good enough given the constraints" the appropriate target? |

### Phase 5: Implementation (Acting on the decision)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Communicate the reasoning | Have I explained the reasoning, not just the outcome, to those affected? |
| Choose the change model | If this implies organisational change, which model fits — Lewin (stable contexts), Kotter (mechanistic top-down), Appreciative Inquiry (abundance bottom-up), Complex Adaptive Systems (emergent)? |
| Address conflict productively | Some decisions create unhappy stakeholders. Am I prepared for conflict productively, applying Follett's integration approach where possible rather than dominance? |
| Set milestones and measurement | What will success look like in three, six, twelve months? Have I built the Plan-Do-Check-Act cycle into the implementation? |

### Phase 6: Monitoring and Evaluating (Closing the cycle)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Evaluate the outcome | Did the decision produce the expected results? What evidence am I using to judge? |
| Distinguish bad luck from bad decision | A bad outcome may follow a sound process if uncertainty was high; a good outcome may follow a poor process. Which case is this? |
| Be willing to reverse | If new information indicates the decision was wrong, am I able to reverse course, or escalating commitment to defend the prior choice? |
| Apply the Deming cycle | Plan → Do → Check → Act → Plan again. Has the learning from this decision fed back into the next one? |
| Capture phronesis lessons | What does this decision-process teach me — practical wisdom for the next decision of this type? |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: A team is iterating on alternatives without converging.** Signal: meetings repeat similar discussions; no commitments made. Diagnosis: the decision lacks clear bounding, or the team is treating a non-programmed decision without the systematic six-step process. Follow-up: explicitly bound the decision; choose a process; commit to a decision date.
- **Pattern: A decision is being defended after evidence has shifted against it.** Signal: investments increase as outcomes worsen; criticism is dismissed. Diagnosis: escalation of commitment. Follow-up: re-evaluate freshly; treat sunk costs as sunk; ask "what would I do if I had not made the prior choice?"
- **Pattern: Heated debate without naming the binding constraint.** Signal: arguments escalate but stakeholders cannot agree what the dispute is actually about. Diagnosis: relationship conflict has overtaken process conflict. Follow-up: pause; reframe to process conflict; surface what each party actually disagrees about.
- **Pattern: A group reaches consensus too quickly.** Signal: a meaningful decision is endorsed without challenge; concerns are shared privately afterward. Diagnosis: groupthink or suppression of dissent. Follow-up: assign a devil's advocate; structure dissenting opinions explicitly.
- **Pattern: A strategic decision is being made without environmental analysis.** Signal: the team commits to a strategy without examining external forces or internal capability. Diagnosis: SWOT, PESTEL, Five Forces, or VRIO has not been applied. Follow-up: pause and run the appropriate strategic-analysis tools; the decision quality will improve materially.
- **Pattern: A firm is trying to be both cost leader and differentiator.** Signal: cost-cutting initiatives running alongside premium-positioning campaigns. Diagnosis: stuck-in-the-middle risk. Follow-up: choose a generic strategy and commit; or explicitly segment the business so different units run different strategies.
- **Pattern: The decision is framed only in shareholder-wealth terms.** Signal: discussion focuses on financial impact; non-financial stakeholders are invisible. Diagnosis: the rejected criterion is operating implicitly. Follow-up: surface stakeholders without voice; apply Drucker's eight-area framework; test against the news-test.
- **Pattern: Goals are vague, but the team is trying to plan as if they are clear.** Signal: planning sessions produce non-actionable statements. Diagnosis: the situation calls for domain or hybrid planning rather than goal planning. Follow-up: name the domain; identify direction; let the goals emerge as learning accumulates.
- **Pattern: A change initiative is failing despite strong top-down sponsorship.** Signal: resistance, low engagement, missed milestones. Diagnosis: the wrong change model was chosen — Kotter's deficit-based mechanistic approach may not fit a complex situation requiring bottom-up engagement. Follow-up: revisit the change model; consider Appreciative Inquiry or Complex Adaptive Systems framings.

## When to Use This Reference

- The decision is non-programmed and warrants systematic process.
- A strategic decision is approaching and the team needs structured environmental and internal analysis.
- A planning cycle is starting and the team must choose between goal, domain, and hybrid framings.
- A change initiative is being scoped and the team must choose a change model.
- A decision-making process has stalled, escalated, or produced an outcome no-one is willing to defend.
- A group is composing itself for a decision and the question is who should be in the room.
- A manager is preparing to make a high-stakes choice under time pressure and wants to avoid reactive-system errors.
- A retrospective evaluation of a prior decision is needed; learning needs to feed forward.

## Worked Example

A regional manufacturing firm faces declining margins on its commodity product line and is considering whether to invest in a premium product line to diversify. The CEO's first impulse is to pursue both — keep the volume business and add the premium business — using shared production assets.

Framing: this is a non-programmed strategic decision. The reactive system is pulling toward "do both" because both options have appeal. The reflective system needs to engage. The decision-type is strategic, demanding systematic analysis.

Bounding: the decision is whether to commit to one generic strategy (cost leadership in commodity, or differentiation in premium) or to attempt both. The decision is *not* about specific product features — those follow from the strategy choice. Drucker's eight-area framework reveals the decision affects market standing (which segment), innovation (whether premium R&D is required), productivity (volume economics differ), profitability, resources (capital allocation), manager development, employee attitude, and public responsibility (community employment if commodity is exited).

Exploring: SWOT identifies the firm's strength in operational efficiency (commodity advantage) and weakness in brand and design (premium gap). PESTEL surfaces sociocultural shifts toward premium goods and economic pressures on commodity margins. Porter's Five Forces show high industry rivalry in commodity and lower (so far) in the targeted premium segment, with substitute risk in commodity. VRIO suggests operational efficiency is valuable and somewhat rare but imitable. The team generates four options: pure cost leadership doubling down on commodity; pure differentiation pivoting to premium; segmented approach with separate divisions; the original "do both with shared assets".

Deciding: the team applies Porter's stuck-in-the-middle test. The "do both with shared assets" option fails — it would compromise commodity efficiency and premium quality. The segmented approach passes the test but requires capital. The CEO consults two colleagues, including one with experience at a multi-brand firm. The news-test surfaces no immediate ethical concerns, but employee impact (commodity workforce) requires explicit consideration.

Selection: segmented approach with separate divisions, with explicit milestones for each division and a commitment to evaluate at twelve months. The CEO chooses Kotter's eight-step model for the structural change (it is mechanistic and top-down) but adopts Appreciative Inquiry for the cultural change in the new premium division (where the team is being built from scratch and abundance-framing is appropriate).

Implementation: the change model is announced; the guiding coalition is formed; small wins are defined for the first quarter. The Deming cycle is built into the milestone reviews.

Monitoring: at twelve months, evidence is reviewed against the original objectives. Premium division has higher margins but lower volumes than projected; commodity division is steady. The CEO commits in advance to act on what the evidence shows — including the possibility of further commitment, consolidation, or reversal. This precommitment guards against escalation of commitment.

This worked example illustrates several key concepts in the source: dual-system processing, the six-step process, strategic-analysis tools (SWOT, PESTEL, Five Forces, VRIO), generic-strategies discipline, Drucker's eight-area goal framework, the change-model choice, and the Deming cycle for monitoring.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Snap judgement on novel problems.** The reactive system is treating a non-programmed decision as if it were programmed; the cost is poor outcome and inability to learn.
- **Defending a sunk-cost decision.** Escalation of commitment leads to throwing more good resources after bad.
- **Premature consensus in groups.** Groupthink produces unchallenged decisions that look good in the room and bad in the world.
- **Conflict-avoidance leading to compounding problems.** Avoiding process conflict prevents productive exploration; the dominance approach (Follett) wins the moment but loses the relationship.
- **Outcome-only judgement.** Judging a decision purely by its outcome conflates good luck with good process and discourages learning.
- **Treating ethical questions as compliance questions.** Reduced moral sensitivity produces decisions that are technically legal but harmful to stakeholders without voice.
- **Pure shareholder-wealth framing.** The text rejects this as a primary decision criterion; harm to other stakeholders cannot be justified on shareholder-return grounds.
- **Stuck in the middle.** Trying to combine cost leadership and differentiation produces firms that excel at neither.
- **Goal planning when goals are not actually clear.** Forcing specific-goal planning into a domain-planning situation produces brittle plans and demoralised teams.
- **Wrong change model for the context.** Imposing Kotter's deficit-based mechanism on a situation that requires bottom-up emergence (or vice versa) produces resistance and failed change.

## Integration with Other References

| Reference | Connection |
|---|---|
| OpenStax, Organizational Behavior (Ch 6) | The two books treat decision-making with very similar dual-system, six-step, six-barrier framings. Pair them: OpenStax OB anchors the cognitive process; Principles of Management adds the strategic, planning, and change-model lenses. Where both apply, use both rather than choose. |
| Bounded rationality (Simon) | The text treats bounded rationality as foundational. Practitioners working in decision-making contexts should treat Simon's framing as the explanation for why the six-step process and evidence-based methods exist. |
| Goal-setting theory (Locke) | Goal theory and decision-making are paired: difficult, specific, accepted goals shape what decisions get made and which alternatives feel salient. The text endorses goal theory as one of the strongest in OB. |
| Expectancy theory (Vroom) | Decision-making about effort exertion follows expectancy logic. Practitioners working on motivation should pair the text's decision-making framework with expectancy analysis. |
| Conflict-resolution (Follett, Thomas) | Follett's three paths (dominance, compromise, integration) and Thomas's five modes (competing, collaborating, compromising, avoiding, accommodating) provide the choice apparatus for handling disagreement within decisions. |
| Appreciative Inquiry (Cooperrider) | For organisation-wide decisions involving change, the AI 5-D cycle offers a bottom-up alternative to top-down systematic decision-making and produces stronger commitment when the situation is abundance-framed. |
| Strategic analysis (Porter, Barney VRIO, SWOT, PESTEL) | The strategic-decision tools are explicit in this text. Pair them with bounded-rationality and dual-system framings: strategic analysis structures the evidence-gathering that the reflective system relies on. |
| Servant leadership (Greenleaf) and stakeholder theory (Freeman) | The text's rejection of shareholder-primacy implies a stakeholder-pluralist framing for decisions. Pair with Greenleaf for the leadership style and Freeman for the stakeholder analytical apparatus. |
| Deming-Shewhart cycle (PDCA) | The text builds the Plan-Do-Check-Act cycle into planning and controlling. Decisions are not one-off events but iterative learning loops. |

**Named limits of the source.** The book is an introductory management survey; it develops a wide range of decision-making frameworks but rarely at depth. Strategic-analysis tools (SWOT, PESTEL, Five Forces, VRIO) are introduced without the strategic-planning context needed to apply them well in practice — that requires supplementing with strategy-specialist sources. The ethical decision-making section covers Rest's model and seven ethical principles but does not develop case-level ethical analysis; practitioners facing hard ethical choices need more than a survey treatment.
