# Approach Perfect Field Guide to Scrum Events, Retro Distillation

**Source:** Chris Gagné (2020). *The Approach Perfect Field Guide to Scrum Events*. April 3, 2020 edition. Approach Perfect, Limited. CC BY 4.0 (author-relicensed for this corpus). Scope: open.

## Retro Relevance

This is the demo corpus's closest-to-canonical retrospective source. The Field Guide carries the Sprint Retrospective in its fullest practitioner form: a concrete 1½-hour five-segment Derby-Larsen agenda (borrowed-through from *Agile Retrospectives*), Norm Kerth's Retrospective Prime Directive verbatim (borrowed-through from *Project Retrospectives*), an explicit Vegas Rule / Chatham House Rule confidentiality choice, √n voting, 5-Whys with *surprise as the signal*, the one-or-two-improvements discipline, the decisions-vs-actions split, capacity-adjusted velocity for honest commitment, and the retro-close pattern that reads back owners and dates. The guide is deliberately repetitive by design — each section is self-contained enough to be printed and used alone — which makes it well-suited to the retro task axis where the facilitator needs the protocol in the moment, not as background reading.

## Key Concepts for Retro

1. <!-- concept: retrospectives --> **Retrospective Prime Directive — verbatim.** "Regardless of what we discover, we understand and truly believe that everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time, their skills and abilities, the resources available, and the situation at hand." (Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda") Attributed to Norm Kerth's *Project Retrospectives* (borrowed-through). Read aloud at every retro, especially when blame language is anticipated. This is the structural blameless-by-default commitment; skipping it leaves the container unset.

2. <!-- concept: confidentiality-choice --> **Vegas Rule and Chatham House Rule — explicit confidentiality choice.** "The Scrum Team should explicitly use either the 'Vegas Rule' ('Whatever happens in Vegas, stays in Vegas', meaning nothing is discussed about the retro without unanimous explicit consent) or 'Chatham House Rule' ('participants are free to use the information received, but neither the identity nor the affiliation of the speaker(s)... may be revealed.')." (Sprint Retrospective, "Tips") Pick one explicitly at Phase 0 setup. Both create a safe container; neither is a default.

3. <!-- concept: derby-larsen-agenda --> **Five-segment Derby-Larsen agenda for 1½ hours.** The sample agenda: Set the stage (5 min: feeling check-in, Prime Directive, appreciations) → Gather data (5 min: positives/deltas/insights on stickies) → Read and deduplicate (3 min) → Vote on topics (2 min: √n votes) → For each of top 3 topics, spend 15-20 min generating insights and designing actions (70 min total) → Wrap up (5 min). (Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda") The agenda is borrowed-through from Esther Derby and Diana Larsen's *Agile Retrospectives* [BT].

4. <!-- concept: sqrt-n-voting --> **√n voting heuristic.** "Give people the approximate square root of the topic count in votes (~9 topics = 3 votes, ~16 topics = 4 votes, ~25 topics = 5 votes, etc)." (Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda") The heuristic balances signal (enough votes to express preference) with noise reduction (not so many that everything gets a vote). Apply in Phase 1 priming / Phase 2 data gathering when the team needs to select which topics to deep-dive.

5. <!-- concept: five-whys --> **5-Whys with surprise as the signal.** "If you're rarely surprised by your root causes, you may not be digging deep enough." (Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda") Surprise is a diagnostic indicator: if the root cause was already obvious before the first Why, the analysis hasn't reached a new layer. Apply in Phase 3 insight when the team lands quickly on a familiar answer.

6. <!-- concept: one-constraint-discipline --> **One or two improvements, not a list.** "It's better to have one or two improvement items for the next Sprint than to have too much to change at once." (Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda") This is the retro's constraint discipline. Five experiments mean zero follow-through. One constraint addressed is worth seven items acknowledged.

7. <!-- concept: decisions-vs-actions --> **Decisions vs actions split.** "I like to expand this into Decisions and Actions... your suggestions fall naturally into two buckets: decisions, which are changes to the Definition of 'Done', Definition of 'Ready', Working Agreement, and actions, which are simply new Backlog Items." (Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda") Working Agreement changes are decisions with a different registration mechanism than Backlog Items. Both require a named owner; the mechanism that tracks each is different.

8. <!-- concept: written-first-data-gathering --> **Positives / deltas / insights as data-gathering device.** "Ask each team member to come up with as many positives, deltas or things they'd like to change, and insights as possible. Have them write each positive, delta, and insight individually on sticky notes." (Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda") Written-first, individual-then-grouped. The written-first pattern equalises participation before grouping; it prevents the first voice from anchoring the field.

9. <!-- concept: capacity-adjusted-velocity --> **Capacity-adjusted velocity for honest commitment.** "If the work in process in your carried-over Sprint Backlog meets or exceeds your forecast velocity and it is all still valuable to work on, we strongly recommend against taking in any new work this Sprint." (Sprint Planning, "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda") For retro Phase 4: only commit to experiments the team has real capacity for, adjusted for what is already in flight. Honest empirical process control, not wishful thinking.

10. <!-- concept: manager-speaks-last --> **Manager speaks last / strict participant control.** "No other may attend unless specifically invited to do so by the entire Scrum Team." (Sprint Retrospective, "Participants") And non-team participants "must understand that this is not their event and they may not interfere with the team's process, only support the team." (Sprint Planning, "Participants") When a manager is on the call, they observe; they do not shape the data-gathering phase.

11. <!-- concept: sprint-review --> **Sprint Review is a working session.** "This is not a status meeting. Rather, it is an opportunity for stakeholders to collaborate with the team and improve the Product Backlog with shared context and learnings." (Sprint Review, "Tips") Scales to the retro: the retro is a working session for the team, not a report to management.

12. <!-- concept: definition-of-done --> **Don't demonstrate undone work.** "Work in process is a liability, not an asset." (Sprint Review, "Tips") For retro Phase 0.5 experiment review: an experiment that was declared done but wasn't fully done should be named as incomplete, not carried forward as a success. Honest accounting of what was actually done is the start of the next cycle.

13. <!-- concept: retro-close-readback --> **Retro close — read back with names and dates.** The wrap-up reads back commitments, names owners, and names when each lands. (Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda") Phase 5 close discipline: an experiment without a named owner and a named date is a wish, not a commitment.

14. <!-- concept: meta-retrospective --> **Meta-retrospective on the retrospective.** "Spend a few minutes retrospecting on the retrospective. How did it go for the team? What would they like to try next time?" (Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda") The format itself is subject to continuous improvement; the last five minutes of the retro are the loop-closure on the retro process.

## Questions to Ask During Retro

### Phase 0 — Setup

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Prime Directive | Has the Prime Directive been read aloud? ("Regardless of what we discover, we understand and truly believe that everyone did the best job they could...") Skip it only when blame language is guaranteed not to surface — which is never. |
| Confidentiality choice | Vegas Rule or Chatham House Rule? Pick one explicitly, out loud, before anything else is said. |
| Manager on the call | Is anyone in the room with authority over the team's outcomes? Name the manager-speaks-last convention before data gathering begins. |

### Phase 0.5 — Experiment review

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Honest done/not-done | Was each experiment from last retro actually done, or declared done? Name incomplete experiments as incomplete before the new cycle opens. |
| Experiment ownership failure | If an experiment wasn't done, was it unowned? Ownership failure at design time is the diagnosis, not execution failure at delivery time. |

### Phase 1 — Priming

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Agenda choice | Is the 5-segment Derby-Larsen pattern the right shape for this retro, or does the team need a different structure this cycle? The Field Guide agenda is the default; deviation should be a well-formed choice, not drift. |

### Phase 2 — Data gathering

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Written-first | Has each person written their positives, deltas, and insights before the group discussion begins? Verbal-first means the first voice anchors the field. |
| Topic count for voting | How many unique topics are on the wall? Apply the √n heuristic to set the vote count. |
| Too-quick agreement | If the group converged on the same three topics in 30 seconds, is that genuine alignment or artificial harmony? Ask one more round of written input before calling it. |

### Phase 3 — Insight / cause analysis

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Surprise check | When the 5-Whys analysis lands on a root cause, is anyone surprised? If not, keep digging — the familiar answer may be the surface, not the layer. |
| Blame language | Has the Prime Directive been re-read? Blame language is a signal the container isn't holding, not a signal that someone is guilty. |

### Phase 4 — Experiment design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| One constraint | Of the topics deep-dived, which one experiment — if done — would most change the next Sprint? One constraint addressed is worth five items acknowledged. |
| Decisions vs actions | Does this improvement change how we agree to work (decision, goes into Working Agreement) or does it create a new piece of tracked work (action, goes into Backlog)? The mechanism is different. |
| Capacity check | Given what is already in flight, does the team have real capacity for this experiment in the stated timeframe? Honest empirical process control, not wishful thinking. |

### Phase 5 — Close

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Named owner and date | Has each commitment been read back with a specific owner and a specific date? "We'll try to..." is not a commitment. |
| Meta-retro | How did the retro itself go? What would the team try differently next time? |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| The team converged on three topics in 30 seconds | Artificial harmony or conformity; written-first input wasn't used | Ask one more round of written input before calling the vote closed |
| 5-Whys root cause surprises no one | Analysis stopped at the surface layer, not the structural cause | Apply one more Why; ask whether the cause was already named before the first Why |
| Team commits to five or seven experiments | Experiment design failed the one-constraint discipline | Run a forced-choice: which single experiment, if done, would most change the next Sprint? |
| "Decisions" and "actions" are on the same board | Mechanism confusion; neither will survive handoff | Sort explicitly: which go to Working Agreement and which to Backlog? |
| Experiment has no named owner or date | The commitment is a wish, not an agreement | Refuse adjournment until every commitment has a name and a date |
| Last retro's experiments weren't reviewed | The retro is running as a fresh event rather than a cycle | Open Phase 0.5 explicitly: honest done/not-done for each prior experiment before new data |
| Manager present and speaks in data-gathering phase | Conformity pressure is shaping what the team surfaces | Re-state manager-speaks-last before the next sticky is posted |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this distillation when:
- The team is new to retrospectives and needs a concrete, runnable agenda (the five-segment Derby-Larsen structure is the default).
- Blame language is expected or has surfaced — the Prime Directive is the structural pre-commitment this source carries.
- Confidentiality has not been set explicitly — Vegas Rule or Chatham House Rule must be chosen before anything else.
- The experiment-design phase is producing too many items — the one-or-two-improvements discipline is this source's load-bearing constraint.
- The team is conflating Working Agreement changes with Backlog Items.
- The retro close needs a named structure: read back commitments with owners and dates, then meta-retro the retro itself.

Prefer the SSDL or Barbrook-Johnson distillation when the recurring problem has a feedback structure. Prefer the Liberating Structures distillation when specific facilitation moves are needed for one voice dominating or artificial harmony. Prefer the Scrum Guide distillation when the team is questioning whether the retro is necessary at all.

## Worked Example

A five-person team is running its eighth Sprint retro. The lead opens by reading the Retrospective Prime Directive aloud and asks each person to confirm they accept the Vegas Rule before the session starts. Three people wrote stickies privately; two joined late and will write during the read-aloud phase.

In Phase 2, the lead applies the √n heuristic: twelve unique topics, so each person gets three votes. The team selects "deployment pipeline failures" as the top topic. In Phase 3, the 5-Whys analysis lands quickly on "the staging environment doesn't match production." The lead asks whether anyone is surprised. Two people say no — they knew this. The lead asks one more Why: why does the staging-production gap persist? The team surfaces that no one owns environment parity as a named responsibility. This is the surprise.

In Phase 4, the team proposes three experiments. The lead applies the one-constraint discipline: which single experiment, if done, would most change the next Sprint? The team votes: naming an environment-parity owner and adding a parity-check to the Definition of Done. This becomes the one decision (DoD change) with a named Accountable individual and a date. The other two items go to a visible "not this Sprint" list, not to a commitment board.

Phase 5: the lead reads back the commitment — named owner, DoD change wording, date by which the DoD update is confirmed. The meta-retro question: "How did this retro go?" One person notes the 5-Whys ran better once the lead asked the surprise-check question. The lead logs this for next retro's format.

The source's five-segment structure, √n heuristic, surprise-as-signal discipline, one-or-two-improvements rule, and read-back close are all from the Field Guide (Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda"). The DoD as a mechanism for the decision traces to the decisions-vs-actions split (Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda").

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Opening the retro without reading the Prime Directive, then discovering blame language fills the vacuum the Prime Directive would have held.
- Leaving confidentiality implicit and finding after the fact that something said in the retro was shared outside without consent.
- Allowing the most vocal or senior person to speak first in data gathering, anchoring the field before others have written their input.
- Running the 5-Whys analysis to a familiar answer, calling it the root cause, and moving on without checking whether anyone was surprised by it.
- Committing to five or seven experiments because the list feels productive, then following through on none.
- Conflating decisions (Working Agreement changes) with actions (Backlog Items) and registering both as the same thing, so neither mechanism owns either.
- Committing to experiments without checking actual team capacity, then carrying them over to the next retro as incomplete.
- Closing the retro without naming owners and dates, producing a list of intentions with no mechanism to turn them into commitments.
- Skipping the experiment-review phase and opening data gathering fresh, treating each retro as a new event rather than a link in a cycle.
- Running the retro as a report to management rather than as a working session owned by the team.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The Retrospective Prime Directive traces to Norm Kerth's *Project Retrospectives: A Handbook for Team Reviews* [BT] (Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda"; Sprint Retrospective, "Tips"). The five-segment Derby-Larsen agenda traces to Esther Derby and Diana Larsen's *Agile Retrospectives: Making Good Teams Great* [BT] (Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda"). Jeff Sutherland's *Shock Therapy* is cited for the 1-week sprint recommendation [BT] (The Sprint, "Tips"). The Vegas Rule and Chatham House Rule are named devices borrowed-through without a primary source citation [BT] (Sprint Retrospective, "Tips"). Theory of Constraints is cited for "work in process is a liability, not an asset" [BT] (Sprint Review, "Tips"). None of Kerth, Derby, Larsen, Sutherland, or Theory of Constraints are held directly in this corpus; those works are not openly licensed and citations requiring depth are not available in this open distribution.

**Named limits of the source.** The Field Guide is explicitly the "bare minimum instruction required to successfully bootstrap" the Scrum events — it names itself as a floor, not a ceiling [V] (Introduction). The guide covers only Scrum events and Backlog Refinement; it does not address team coaching, facilitation craft beyond the agenda structure, or psychological safety conditions. The retro agenda is a sample, not a prescription: the guide acknowledges that deviation from textbook Scrum should be a "well-formed experiment" after diligent practice [V] (Introduction). The guide does not address distributed or asynchronous retros, non-Scrum contexts, or team dynamics beyond the manager-participation note.

**Evidence-marker continuity.** The Prime Directive is `[V]` in the deep ref (verbatim from the source) and paraphrased in this distillation's Key Concepts section — paraphrase is correct here because the distillation does not need to carry verbatim text that the deep ref already holds. The √n heuristic and one-or-two-improvements rule are both `[V]` in the deep ref; the distillation cites them by section and preserves their precision. The capacity-adjusted velocity and decisions-vs-actions distinctions are `[V]` in the deep ref; this distillation paraphrases both, which is the appropriate tier.
