## Retro Relevance

The Liberating Structures Handbook projects onto the retro axis as the facilitation-craft layer that keeps iterative team learning from collapsing into flat complaint lists, artificial consensus, or vague actions with no owners. The handbook's central framing — "buy-in is the exact opposite [of ownership]… Aiming for buy-in creates lukewarm, pallid implementation and mediocre results" — names the most common retro failure: the lead announces actions the team did not design, the team complies without owning them, and nothing changes. LS structures are the tool set that converts a retro from a facilitated verdict into a team-owned learning conversation. Most of the structures named in the retro.md task spec come directly from this handbook: 1-2-4-All, Troika Consulting, Mini Constellations, Six Words, Discovery & Action Dialogues, 15% Solutions, What/So What/Now What, Wise Crowds, and the Levels of Accountability ladder. The structures are not the agenda — they are the facilitation moves the lead reaches for when a specific diagnostic moment fires: one voice dominating, artificial harmony, helplessness, blame language, too many experiments, no clear owner.

## Key Concepts for Retro

1. <!-- concept: 1-2-4-all --> **1-2-4-All (against HiPPOs).** Four-stage scaling: individual reflection ("a couple of minutes is fine"), pairs (5–10 minutes), small groups of 4–6 ("More than six is too big to give everyone a chance to talk"), then whole group. The explicit anti-HiPPO design: by the time the whole-group round happens, every participant has already formulated and tested their view in small-group context, making it structurally harder for a dominant voice to anchor the room. "Three rounds are usually good" for Impromptu Speed Networking, the warm-up version. Used at Phase 2 (data gathering) as the default written-first, spoken-second pattern when one voice is dominating or the group is defaulting to the highest-status person's opinion. (Source: "1-2-4-Whole Group"; "Impromptu Speed Networking")

2. <!-- concept: what-so-what-now-what --> **After Action Debrief: What / So What / Now What.** Three sequential questions in strict order, attributed to Center for Creative Leadership and named *After Action Debrief* in the handbook: "WHAT? What is emerging here? What data stands out? What actually happened? SO WHAT? What do the data imply? Do you see a pattern? Does it make a difference? NOW WHAT? What action may help us move forward? Who else should be here?" The sequence discipline matters for retros: the most common retro failure is jumping directly to Now-What (experiments) before What (data) and So-What (pattern) have been answered. The three-question structure is the retro's Phase 1 (priming) through Phase 3 (insight) backbone. (Source: "After Action Debrief")

3. <!-- concept: troika-consulting --> **Troika Consulting (peer iteration on a stuck problem).** Groups of three, 10 minutes per focal person. Focal person describes a challenge; partners ask honing questions first, engage with each other, then with the focal person about handling and moving forward. The handbook's suggested framing questions: "What's the question you most need to answer in order to move forward? What's the biggest obstacle to making the changes you want to make? What can you do now to move beyond that obstacle?" After all three rounds, the group surfaces patterns. In a retro, Troika Consulting is Phase 3 (insight) and Phase 4 (experiment design) — when several team members are each carrying a stuck problem, three parallel peer-consultation rounds run in 30 minutes and each produces an owned next step. (Source: "Troika Consulting")

4. <!-- concept: discovery-action-dialogues --> **Discovery & Action Dialogues (DADs) — closing on "Who will do what when next?".** Six-question protocol that ends in the structural commitment move. Full sequence: "What do you know/think about ___? What do YOU do about ___? What keeps you from doing that all the time? Who/Where have you seen overcome those barriers? What other ideas do you have about removing barriers? What has to happen next to make that happen? Who will do what when next?" The handbook frames this as the owned-experiment move: the last two questions produce named owners and named next steps within the conversation rather than a collective intention that disperses when the meeting ends. Used at Phase 4 (experiment design) to close the retro on owned experiments rather than a board of stickies. (Source: "Discovery & Action Dialogues")

5. <!-- concept: fifteen-percent-solutions --> **15% Solutions (locus-of-control reframe).** "Most people have about 15-percent control over their work situations. The other 85 percent rests in the broader context, shaped by the general structures, systems, events and culture in which they operate." Gareth Morgan borrowed-through. The diagnostic question: "Where do you have freedom to act? What's in your 15%?" "It doesn't matter if you're a General or an enlisted soldier, a Senior Executive or a member of the team. You still have only your 15 percent." In a retro where the team feels helpless ("nothing we can do, this is a leadership problem"), 15% Solutions is the locus-of-control reframe that opens a tractable experiment space without denying the 85%. Used at Phase 2 (data gathering) when helplessness surfaces and at Phase 4 (experiment design) when proposed experiments are outside the team's authority. (Source: "15% Solutions")

6. <!-- concept: mini-constellations --> **Mini Constellations (positional diversity).** Spatial-positioning: "Place an object on the floor to represent an idea, choice, move, etc. Ask participants to place themselves somewhere in the room that represents to what degree they feel positively about the idea." Participants speak from their distance-position as a "role," not as a personal view — "this way of giving the instruction invites them to take the 'role' of someone with that perspective and not simply be representing a personal view." In a retro where the group is agreeing too quickly, Mini Constellations surfaces positional diversity without forcing a vote or shaming the dissenters. Used at Phase 2 (data gathering) and Phase 3 (insight) when artificial harmony is suspected. (Source: "Mini Constellations")

7. <!-- concept: six-words --> **Six Words (compress the take-away).** Inspired by the attributed Hemingway anecdote. Mulago Foundation criteria for a valid compression: fewer than 8 words; include a verb; include a target population; describes an outcome you can measure. "Taking some of the seriousness out of these things can actually help people get to the core of an important idea." In a retro, Six Words is Phase 5 (close) — asking each participant to compress the one thing this retro decided into fewer than eight words surfaces what is actually load-bearing in the room's output. The handbook's framing: especially useful for "mushy stuff like missions, vision, purpose, or strategy." (Source: "Six Words"; Mulago Foundation)

8. <!-- concept: wise-crowds --> **Wise Crowds (5-6 person consulting team).** Groups of 5–6, sitting knee-to-knee. Client describes a challenge for ~10 minutes; turns chair around and listens silently while the consulting team talks — "questions, paraphrases, metaphors, examples, musings" — for at least 15 minutes. The structural rule: "The Consulting Team's job is not to decide 'this is what The Client ought to do.'" Client rejoins with "Here's what I heard that intrigued, surprised, or resonated with me." In a retro, Wise Crowds is the Phase 3 (insight) and Phase 4 (experiment design) structure for a team lead who is stuck on a recurring problem and needs a peer consulting round without the group drifting into prescription. (Source: "Wise Crowds")

9. <!-- concept: levels-of-accountability --> **Levels of Accountability ladder (above-the-line vs below-the-line).** Stair-step from "Denial / Woe is me" at the bottom through "Blame others," "Blame self," "Wait 'n Hope," "Acknowledge situation," "Accept ownership," "Find Solutions," to "Take Action and Learn" at the top. Diagnostic question: "What do we need to do to work 'above the line?'" In a retro where blame language surfaces ("if X had just done their job") or where the team is sitting in helplessness, the ladder gives the facilitator a named redirect without shaming. Paired with Norm Kerth's Retrospective Prime Directive — the Prime Directive sets the blameless-by-default tone; the Levels of Accountability ladder names where the conversation has drifted when that tone breaks down. (Source: "Levels of Accountability")

10. <!-- concept: triz-inversion --> **TRIZ (inversion before naming experiments).** Single LS move drawn from Altshuller: "Design a comprehensive system that makes it absolutely impossible to get that result. What policies, practices, and ways of operating would make it 100% certain there is no way any of the things you want can happen. Does the system you've designed have anything in common with the current state of affairs?" In a retro the TRIZ inversion is Phase 3 (insight) — before the team names experiments, the inversion move surfaces the currently-invisible structures that are actively producing the outcome they want to change. A team that can see the system that makes the problem inevitable is better placed to design an experiment than a team that is still treating the problem as random. (Source: "TRIZ")

11. <!-- concept: wicked-questions --> **Wicked Questions (paradox, not trick).** "A question is 'wicked' if there is an embedded paradox or tension in the question… Wicked questions do not have obvious answers. Their value lies in their capacity to open up options, inquiry and bring to the surface the fundamental issues that need to be addressed." Worked example: "How can we sustain quality standards across the system while allowing for local innovation?" The handbook's counter-example of what is not wicked: "How can we succeed when the guys at headquarters are such bozos?" — a complaint, not a paradox. In a retro, the Wicked Questions move at Phase 3 (insight) names the systemic tension the team is trying to hold rather than treating it as a solvable problem with a right answer. (Source: "Wicked Questions")

12. <!-- concept: q-storming --> **Q Storming (generate the question before the answer).** Post-it brainstorm of "courageous and provocative questions as well as 'silly' and 'dumb' questions." "Questions are fateful!" (Diana Whitney, cited borrowed-through). Categorise, prioritise, read aloud. In a retro's Phase 1 (priming) or Phase 3 (insight), Q Storming surfaces the question the team most needs to answer — "What's the question you still need to answer for yourself to get to a significantly new place?" — rather than jumping to answers the team already has. Lineage: Marilee G. Adams, Q-Storming process. (Source: "Q Storming")

13. <!-- concept: engage-unusual-suspects --> **Nothing About Me Without Me / Engage the Unusual Suspects.** "Who's not here that could contribute to our conversation?" The retro-level application: in a team retro, which perspectives are structurally absent because of who was not invited, who is intimidated into silence, or who leaves before the close? The companion principle: "Thinking about the Unusual Suspects is an important part of creating an invitation list for any gathering you design." Used at Phase 0 (setup) — who must be present for this retro to reach the real contributory factors? (Source: "Nothing About Me Without Me"; "Engage the Unusual Suspects")

14. <!-- concept: ownership-vs-buy-in --> **Ownership versus buy-in as the experiment-design discipline.** The experiment design is not finished when the board shows stickies with labels. It is finished when the people who will do the work have participated in designing the experiment and named themselves as owners. The handbook's diagnostic: hearing the word *buy-in* in a retro is the danger signal — someone is about to be handed an action item they did not design. "Liberating Structures help you create true ownership and avoid the pitfalls of buy-in. When it comes to solving intractable socio-technical behavioral problems in systems the notion of buy-in is just not useful — people in the system need to own the new behaviors." (Source: "Ownership versus Buy-in")

## Questions to Ask During Retro

### Phase 0 — Setup

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Setting a blameless tone | Is the ownership-vs-buy-in distinction named before the retro opens? Do participants understand that the experiments they design will be theirs, not assigned? |
| Who should be here? | "Who's not here that could contribute to our conversation?" — whose absence will produce a partial data set or an unworkable experiment? |

### Phase 0.5 — Experiment review

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Last retro's experiments weren't done | 15% Solutions: "Where do you have freedom to act? What's in your 15%?" — if the experiment was unowned, the experiment design failed, not the team. |
| Same experiment proposed again | Wicked Questions: is the unresolved tension actually a paradox rather than a solvable problem? TRIZ: does the current system actively produce this outcome? |

### Phase 1 — Priming

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Choosing the retro shape | What / So What / Now What as the default backbone — do not skip to Now What. Q Storming if the team needs to surface the question before the data-gathering begins. |
| Low energy | Impromptu Speed Networking or Knee-to-Knee Conversation as opener: "everyone gets to speak early in the meeting. The distance between not having said anything and making a first comment is vast." |

### Phase 2 — Data gathering

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| One voice dominating | 1-2-4-All: individual → pairs → groups of 4–6 → whole group. The structure ensures every participant has articulated their view before the whole-group round. |
| Team agrees too quickly | Mini Constellations: place an object on the floor; where does each person stand? Speak from the role of someone with that position. |
| Team is helpless | 15% Solutions: "Where do you have freedom to act? What's in your 15%?" Acknowledge the 85% without letting it block the 15%. |
| Blame language surfacing | Levels of Accountability: where on the ladder is this conversation? "What do we need to do to work 'above the line?'" |

### Phase 3 — Insight

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Surfacing the So What | So What? What do the data imply? Do you see a pattern? Does it make a difference? Hold this question before moving to experiments. |
| Naming the system | TRIZ: what policies and practices make it 100% certain this outcome keeps happening? Does the current system resemble that? |
| Naming the paradox | Wicked Questions: is there an embedded paradox or tension? Name it rather than treating it as a solvable problem. |
| Individual peer consulting | Troika Consulting: three rounds of 10 minutes, each team member as focal person; partners hone, engage with each other, then with the focal person. |

### Phase 4 — Experiment design

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Closing on named owners | Discovery & Action Dialogues: "Who will do what when next?" Refuse adjournment without a named owner and date. |
| Experiment outside the team's authority | 15% Solutions: "Where do you have freedom to act?" — scope the experiment to what the team can run without leadership approval. |
| Lead is stuck on a recurring problem | Wise Crowds: consulting team of 5–6, client's back turned, team's job is not to decide what the client ought to do. Client rejoins with what intrigued, surprised, or resonated. |

### Phase 5 — Close

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Compressing the take-away | Six Words: fewer than 8 words, a verb, a target population, a measurable outcome. What is the one thing this retro decided? |
| Reading back commitments | Now What? What action may help us move forward? Who else should be here? Name owners before the room clears. |
| Checking ownership vs buy-in | Are the people who will do the experiment the same ones who designed it? If not, the buy-in trap is open. |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| One voice anchors the data-gathering phase | HiPPO dynamic; others haven't formed their view independently | 1-2-4-All: individual → pairs → groups of 4–6 → whole group before any group discussion |
| Team agrees too quickly | Artificial harmony; positional diversity hasn't been surfaced | Mini Constellations: place an object on the floor, ask each person to stand where they stand on the idea |
| Team feels helpless about a systemic factor | Locus-of-control collapse; 85% is crowding out the 15% | 15% Solutions: "Where do you have freedom to act? What's in your 15%?" |
| Blame language surfaces in Phase 2 or 3 | Conversation has dropped below the line on the accountability ladder | Levels of Accountability: where is this conversation on the ladder? "What do we need to do to work 'above the line?'" |
| The lead announces experiments at close | Buy-in instead of ownership; team didn't design the action | Discovery & Action Dialogues: "Who will do what when next?" — the owned-experiment close |
| The same experiment is proposed again | Wicked Question may be lurking; the tension is a paradox, not a solvable problem | TRIZ: what currently-in-place system makes this outcome inevitable? |
| No question is driving the data-gathering | Answers arriving before the question is clear | Q Storming at Phase 1 priming: surface the question before the data |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this distillation when a specific facilitation move is needed for a specific diagnostic moment:
- 1-2-4-All for any Phase 2 situation where one voice is dominating or the team is defaulting to the most senior person.
- Discovery & Action Dialogues for Phase 4 closing — the structural owned-experiment close.
- 15% Solutions when the team is helpless or the proposed experiment is outside the team's authority.
- Mini Constellations when the team agrees too quickly and positional diversity needs to be surfaced without shaming dissent.
- Levels of Accountability when blame language is present and a named redirect is needed.
- TRIZ when a recurring problem hasn't yielded to previous experiments and the system that makes it inevitable hasn't been named.
- Six Words for Phase 5 close when the take-away needs to be compressed to something the team will remember.

This source is the facilitation-craft layer. Pair it with the Approach Perfect Field Guide for the protocol structure and the SSDL or Barbrook-Johnson distillation for the systems-thinking analysis layer.

## Worked Example

A team's Phase 2 data-gathering has stalled. The lead is asking for input and getting nods. The coach recognises the HiPPO dynamic: the lead's framing has anchored the room. The coach signals to the lead: switch to 1-2-4-All.

Each person writes their positives and deltas individually for two minutes. Pairs share for five minutes. Groups of four share for eight minutes. The whole-group round finds two perspectives the lead's framing missed: deployment anxiety and a hidden dependency on another team. Both were present in the room but hadn't surfaced because the lead's frame made them seem off-topic.

In Phase 3, the team has named a recurring "cross-team dependency" problem. The coach offers TRIZ: "Design a comprehensive system that would make it 100% certain this dependency never gets managed. What would that system look like?" The team laughs and starts listing: no shared calendar, no named contact on the other team, no joint refinement, no visibility into each other's backlogs. The coach follows: "Does the current state of affairs have anything in common with that system?" The team is quiet. Several items are present. The TRIZ inversion has surfaced the system that makes the problem inevitable.

In Phase 4, the closing discipline is Discovery & Action Dialogues. The lead asks: "Who will do what when next?" Each person names a specific action they own. The retro closes on owned experiments, not a board of stickies.

The 1-2-4-All anti-HiPPO structure, TRIZ inversion move, and Discovery & Action Dialogues closing discipline all trace to the Liberating Structures Handbook ("1-2-4-Whole Group"; "TRIZ"; "Discovery & Action Dialogues").

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- The lead announces actions at the close of the retro that the team did not design — solved by Discovery & Action Dialogues' "Who will do what when next?" closing discipline.
- One voice anchors the data-gathering phase before others have spoken — solved by 1-2-4-All's structural sequencing.
- The group reaches artificial consensus on an experiment because no one is willing to push back — solved by Mini Constellations' positional-diversity surfacing.
- The retro jumps straight to Now-What before What and So-What have been answered — solved by the strict three-question sequence of After Action Debrief.
- Participants feel helpless in the face of structural factors outside the team's authority — solved by 15% Solutions' locus-of-control reframe.
- Blame language surfaces and the facilitator has no named redirect — solved by the Levels of Accountability ladder's above-the-line / below-the-line diagnostic.
- The retro surfaces a genuine systemic paradox but treats it as a solvable problem — solved by naming it as a Wicked Question.
- Five experiments on the board at close, none of them owned — solved by Troika Consulting and Discovery & Action Dialogues producing one owned commitment per focal person before the group closes.
- The team generates the answer before surfacing the question the answer is supposed to answer — solved by Q Storming's question-first discipline.
- The retro close is memorable in the room but leaves nothing load-bearing for next time — solved by Six Words' compression exercise surfacing what actually mattered.
