## Retro Relevance

SSDL's five briefs project onto the retro axis at two phases where team learning most often stalls. In Phase 2 (data gathering), the mental-models brief (1.03) gives the facilitator a structural explanation for why written-first input patterns matter and why quick team agreement can be false: each member holds a partial-but-valid model of the sprint, shaped by their role, their position in the team's communication network, and what they could and could not see. "Each of us has our own, valid mental picture of the parts, but our understanding of the whole is limited by our own unique vantage point." (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT", figure caption) Artificial harmony is often one mental model winning by volume rather than by weight.

In Phase 3 (insight and cause analysis), the system-archetypes brief (1.07) supplies five hypothesis-generators for recurring problems — the signal the retro task treats as a priority diagnostic moment. When the same pattern surfaces a second or third retro, an archetype is a structured hypothesis to test against the specifics: *fixes that fail*, *shifting the burden*, *drifting goals*, *success to the successful*, *limits to growth*. The feedback-thinking brief (1.05) grounds the causal loop diagram as a shared-model tool when the team needs a picture for an insight they can feel but cannot yet name.

The broken-ankle test from Brief 1.02 applies here too: systems-thinking apparatus is overhead the team does not need on a simple, one-off sprint problem. Match the tool to the complexity of the situation. (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.02, "+CONSIDERATIONS")

## Key Concepts for Retro

1. <!-- concept: complex-problems --> **Complex-problem characteristics as a triage test.** Five markers flag when a retro topic is complex rather than simple: dynamic (evolving across sprints), multiple stakeholders with conflicting goals, time delays between action and result, unintended consequences, and accumulations with history dependence. When a topic carries three or more of these markers, linear diagnosis ("the cause was X") will fail. (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education")

2. <!-- concept: mental-models --> **Mental models as partial-but-valid team views.** "Mental models are the cognitive 'maps' that help us each, individually, understand the world around us. They are how we imagine in our minds that complex systems work." Each team member's view of the sprint is a partial model of the same system. Written-first input operationalises this insight: it gets every partial model on the board before the loudest voice collapses the group to one view. (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT")

3. <!-- concept: mental-model-incompleteness --> **Mental models are incomplete by design.** "None of us have complete knowledge of systems. Our understanding is informed by our lived experiences, values, assumptions, power, and biases." A team member who says "I had no idea that was happening" is not describing failure; they are describing a different vantage point. The retro's data-gathering phase should surface vantage points, not just votes. (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT")

4. <!-- concept: mental-model-stickiness --> **Mental-model stickiness and power asymmetry.** "Mental models are resistant to change, especially among individuals who aren't personally experiencing any negative system effects." A senior team member or manager whose role shields them from the friction the team is experiencing is the hardest person to move toward a systems reading of a recurring problem. (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.03, "+CONSIDERATIONS")

5. <!-- concept: group-model-building --> **Group model building as the participatory method.** "Group model building (GMB) is a process for bringing people together to develop a model of the system, to create shared insights and common language, and to build consensus for implementing change." In a retro, GMB-adjacent moves — building a shared causal map of a recurring problem together — are the Phase 3 tool for the team that has named the pattern but cannot agree on its structure. (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT")

6. <!-- concept: feedback-loops --> **Linear thinking versus feedback thinking.** "In many fields, we are conditioned to approach problems through linear thinking... When looking at problems, we look for causal relationships in one direction: 'X' causes 'Y'." A retro that produces "the problem was poor communication" has produced a linear cause, not a feedback analysis. The facilitator's question is: what was the loop that kept poor communication in place? (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.05, "+Linear vs. Feedback Perspective")

7. <!-- concept: causal-loop-diagrams --> **Causal loop diagrams as hypotheses.** "A CLD is a hypothesis — there is not one single 'right' answer to the variables that are included or the relationships that exist between them. The model we produce is informed by each of our individual mental models." A CLD drawn during Phase 3 is a shared working theory. The team tests it against their sprint experience; it is not a document to defend. (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.05, "+ CONSIDERATIONS")

8. <!-- concept: accumulations --> **Accumulations and history dependence.** "The behavior of a system is not only influenced by what is going on right at this moment, but the history of accumulated experiences of people in that system." The team's trust, technical debt, psychological-safety level, and prior experiment backlog all entered this sprint carrying their history. A retro that treats the sprint as a fresh start will misread the team's starting state. (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education"; Brief 1.06, "+ WHY DOES THIS ACCUMULATION CONCEPT MATTER?")

9. <!-- concept: accumulation-intuition --> **Intuition is unreliable about accumulations.** "Even students with strong mathematical training make persistent mistakes understanding even simple dynamic systems that include accumulations." Team members' intuitions about how quickly trust eroded, or how much technical debt built up, are not to be taken at face value. Making the stock explicit — naming and estimating it — is the discipline. (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.06, "+ CONSIDERATIONS WHEN APPLYING THE CONCEPT OF ACCUMULATIONS")

10. <!-- concept: system-archetypes --> **System archetypes as recurring-problem diagnostics.** "System archetypes are visual descriptions of generic, recurring system structures in the form of Causal Loop Diagrams." Five apply across team and organisational contexts: *fixes that fail*, *success to the successful*, *shifting the burden*, *drifting goals*, *limits to growth*. When a problem reappears for the second or third retro, test it against these five before proposing another experiment. (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.07, "+ CONCEPT")

11. <!-- concept: archetypes-as-hypotheses --> **Archetypes are hypothesis-generators, not labels.** "System archetypes are not meant to erase the uniqueness of a situation. Oversimplifying a problem or jumping to the conclusion that it is best represented by an archetype can impede the creative and iterative process." The facilitator names the archetype as a question — *does this pattern fit?* — and the team tests it. Labelling without testing is expertise-as-imposition. (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.07, "+ CONSIDERATIONS")

12. <!-- concept: expert-imposition --> **Archetypes may not be necessary.** "Jumping in to use archetypes when facilitating a group model building workshop or community session has the potential to reinforce a dynamic of expert vs. novice. The modeler draws on their modeling 'expertise' to diagnose an archetype in a way that overshadows the lived experience of the participants." Introduce archetype thinking only when the team's own analysis has stalled and they are ready to test a structural hypothesis. (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.07, "+ CONSIDERATIONS")

## Questions to Ask During Retro

### Phase 0.5: Experiment review

| Need | Question | Source-tag |
|---|---|---|
| The same experiment has been proposed three retros running with no lasting effect | Which archetype fits as a hypothesis? Is this *fixes that fail* — the experiment addressed the symptom and the original problem accumulated while the symptom was suppressed? Or *shifting the burden* — the experiment was easier than the fundamental fix, and relying on it is now making the fundamental fix harder to reach? | Brief 1.07, "+ CONCEPT" |
| An experiment produced results that surprised the team | What feedback loop did the experiment trigger that was not anticipated? Drawing the loop — even roughly — makes the surprise legible for next time. | Brief 1.05, "+AN EXAMPLE OF LINEAR VS FEEDBACK IN EDUCATION" |

### Phase 2: Data gathering

| Need | Question | Source-tag |
|---|---|---|
| The team agrees too quickly on what went well and what did not | Quick agreement may mean one mental model is dominating. Whose view of the sprint has not yet been surfaced? What did the person with the most peripheral role see that others in the centre could not? | Brief 1.03, figure caption |
| One voice is dominating the data-gathering phase | Each person holds a partial-but-valid model. Use written-first input to get all models on the board before discussion. What patterns emerge when every view is present that are not visible when only the loudest views are heard? | Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT" |
| The team is treating the sprint's outcome as the full picture | What accumulated going into this sprint? Trust level, technical debt, prior experiment outcomes, team morale — these stocks shaped what was possible in the sprint. The sprint did not start from zero. | Brief 1.06, "+ WHY DOES THIS ACCUMULATION CONCEPT MATTER?" |
| A team member says "I had no idea that was happening" | That is a mental-model gap, not a communication failure. What would they have needed to see to know? Where in the team's communication structure is that information not flowing? | Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT" |

### Phase 3: Insight and cause analysis

| Need | Question | Source-tag |
|---|---|---|
| The team has named the problem and wants to understand why it persists | Switch from linear to feedback thinking. What is feeding the problem? What is the loop that keeps it in place? If the team fixed the named symptom, which loop would bring the problem back? | Brief 1.05, "+Linear vs. Feedback Perspective" |
| A pattern has recurred across two or more sprints | Test it against the five archetypes. Does *fixes that fail* fit — each sprint's experiment addressed the symptom and the underlying problem got worse? Does *shifting the burden* fit — each experiment was easier than the fundamental fix, and relying on experiments is now making the fix harder? | Brief 1.07, "+ CONCEPT" |
| The team wants to draw a shared causal map of a recurring problem | Build a rough CLD together. Treat it as a hypothesis. Which variables can go up and down? Which causal links run in both directions? What are the feedback loops? Keep the first draft small — five to eight variables is enough to start. | Brief 1.05, "+ GETTING STARTED" |
| The team is stuck on cause analysis and the facilitator is tempted to name the archetype | Ask first: what structure does the team think is keeping this problem in place? If the team's own analysis stalls, offer the archetype as a hypothesis to test — not as a diagnosis to accept. | Brief 1.07, "+ CONSIDERATIONS" |

### Phase 4: Experiment design

| Need | Question | Source-tag |
|---|---|---|
| The proposed experiment targets the symptom the team has always targeted | Which feedback loop in the causal structure would this experiment change? If it targets an event rather than a loop, what happens to the loop when the event is addressed? | Brief 1.05, "+Linear vs. Feedback Perspective" |
| The experiment assumes a direct line from action to outcome | What are the unintended consequences this experiment might introduce? Which loops will it strengthen or weaken — and which will compensate by pushing back? | Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education" |

## What to Look For

| Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| Quick team agreement in data gathering — everyone nods early | One mental model dominating; the group has converged before all vantage points were surfaced | Use written-first input; ask explicitly for the view of the most peripheral team member |
| "I had no idea that was happening" from a team member | Mental-model gap — not a communication failure but a structural information-flow gap | Ask what they would have needed to see; where in the team's structure does that information not travel? |
| The same experiment shows up for the second or third retro with no sustained effect | Archetype pattern active — the experiment is addressing a symptom or applying an easier fix while the fundamental problem accumulates | Name an archetype as a hypothesis (*fixes that fail*, *shifting the burden*) and test each link against the team's actual experience |
| Phase 3 produces "the cause was poor communication" with no further analysis | Linear cause identified, feedback loop not yet named | Ask what kept poor communication in place; draw the first loop together — even five variables is enough to start |
| The team proposes an experiment without asking what loops it would change | Event-level intervention on a loop-level problem; unintended consequences will be invisible until they arrive | Apply the feedback test: which loop does this experiment change, and what does the rest of the system do in response? |
| The facilitator names an archetype before the team has reached its own analysis limit | Expertise-as-imposition — overshadowing lived experience with a diagnostic label | Hold the archetype as an internal hypothesis; offer it as a question only when the team's own analysis has clearly stalled |
| The retro treats the sprint as a fresh start with no reference to accumulated states | History-dependence blind spot — trust, debt, morale, and prior experiment backlog all shaped what was possible | Surface the relevant stocks explicitly: name the accumulated level and ask how it shaped this sprint's constraints |

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for this distillation when:
- The same sprint problem has surfaced across two or more retros and previous experiments have not held — this is the signal to test an archetype hypothesis rather than propose another experiment.
- The data-gathering phase is converging too quickly and the facilitator suspects one mental model is winning by volume.
- A team member's "I had no idea" reveals a structural information-flow gap that deserves its own cause analysis.
- Phase 3 is producing linear causes ("it was X") and the team needs a tool for naming the loop rather than the event.
- The team needs to design an experiment against a feedback structure rather than against a named symptom.

The SSDL briefs do not carry retrospective facilitation structure, ceremony design, experiment-commitment discipline, or psychological safety technique. The Field Guide, Scrum Guide, and TC 25-20 carry those; SSDL is reached for when the retro topic is complex enough that a feedback lens will do better work than linear cause analysis.

## Worked Example

A team has retrospected on "we keep underestimating front-end complexity" for four sprints. Each retro generates an experiment: better story-pointing, more front-end time in estimation, a complexity-flagging step in refinement. Each experiment produces some movement in the first sprint after adoption, then the problem returns. The team is at Sprint 5's retro; the facilitator recognises the recurring-experiment signal.

Phase 0.5: the facilitator notes three prior experiments, each with initial movement and subsequent return. This is the *fixes that fail* hypothesis signal: each fix addressed a symptom, and the original problem accumulated while the symptom was suppressed. (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.07, "+ CONCEPT")

Phase 3: the facilitator asks the team — not labels the team — what structure they think is keeping the problem in place. After discussion, the team identifies a loop: underestimation creates sprint overload, overload compresses the front-end refinement time available, compressed refinement produces the next underestimation. The symptom-fix experiments all targeted the estimation step; none addressed the overload-compresses-refinement link. The team draws the five-variable CLD together, treating it as a hypothesis. (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.05, "+ GETTING STARTED")

Phase 4: the experiment is redesigned to target the loop, not the event — a sprint-load ceiling on front-end work that protects refinement time, with a hypothesis and a check-point. The team also names the stock that entered this sprint: four sprints of accumulated front-end debt, which will not drain instantly even if the flow changes. The experiment is sized against that history. (Source: Farrell et al., *Methods Briefs Series 1*, Brief 1.06, "+ WHY DOES THIS ACCUMULATION CONCEPT MATTER?")

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

1. **Treating quick team agreement in data gathering as consensus.** Quick agreement often means one mental model won by volume. Written-first input is the operationalisation of the insight that each participant holds a partial-but-valid model. Use it before any discussion narrows the field.

2. **Running the retro as if the sprint started from zero.** The team's trust, technical debt, morale, and prior experiment backlog are stocks that shaped what was possible in the sprint. A retro that ignores these stocks will misread the causes it surfaces.

3. **Diagnosing a recurring problem with linear cause-effect thinking.** "The cause was X" is an event-level diagnosis on a loop-level problem. If the problem keeps coming back, a feedback loop is keeping it in place; naming the loop is the diagnostic work.

4. **Applying a system archetype as a diagnostic label without testing each link.** Archetypes are hypothesis-generators. The facilitator offers the archetype as a question — "does this fit?" — and the team tests each causal link against their actual sprint experience. Labelling without testing is expertise imposed over lived knowledge.

5. **Naming an archetype before the team's own analysis has stalled.** Introducing the archetype too early reinforces a facilitator-as-expert dynamic that overshadows the team's view of their own situation. The archetype is a last resort when the team's own analysis has genuinely hit a wall.

6. **Using written-first data gathering but treating the output as votes.** Written-first input is not a polling mechanism; it is a way to surface multiple partial models before discussion collapses them. Treat the output as a set of vantage points to synthesise, not a ranked list to count.

7. **Proposing the same experiment a third time without asking which archetype explains why the previous two failed.** The recurring experiment is the strongest retro signal available. If the team is about to propose the same fix again, pause and name the hypothesis: which archetype explains why the fix is not holding?

8. **Treating a CLD built during Phase 3 as a finished causal account.** A CLD is a shared hypothesis the team will keep refining as they run experiments and observe what changes. It is a tool for thinking, not a document to defend.

9. **Reaching for systems-mapping apparatus on a simple, one-off sprint problem.** The broken-ankle test applies. If the problem is simple and the path forward is obvious, systems-thinking tools add overhead rather than insight.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The five SSDL briefs borrow-through several authors whose primary works are not held in this corpus. Donella Meadows (*Thinking in Systems*, Chelsea Green, 2008) is cited in Briefs 1.03 and 1.06 for the mental-models-into-the-light framing and the bathtub accumulation model [BT]. John Sterman (*Business Dynamics*, 2000; *Learning from Evidence in a Complex World*, 2006) is cited in Briefs 1.02 and 1.05 as the influence behind the complex-problem characteristics and the feedback-thinking conclusion [BT]. Daniel Kim (*Systems Archetypes I*, Pegasus Communications, 1993) is the direct source for the five archetype templates in Brief 1.07 [BT]. Peter Senge (*The Fifth Discipline*) and Philip Johnson-Laird (mental-models theory, 1983) are acknowledged in the Senge lineage note and Brief 1.03 respectively [BT]. Peter Hovmand (*Community Based System Dynamics*, 2014) is cited for group model building in Brief 1.03 [BT]. None of these authors are held as primary references in the demo corpus; their concepts reach this distillation through the SSDL briefs as the intermediary.

**Named limits of the source.** The five briefs use K-12 education-equity worked examples throughout. The concept prose (definitions, structural explanations, archetype templates) is field-agnostic; the examples are the authors' illustrative case material, not the structural claim. This distillation translates the concept prose into the retro working vocabulary and reconstructs worked examples in that vocabulary, consistent with the deep ref's coverage note on field-agnosticism. The series deliberately excludes Briefs 1.01 (*Systems Thinking Iceberg*) and 1.04 (*Framing Dynamic Problems*) from this ingest because their education frame is structural to the concept's operational meaning rather than illustrative. Concepts from those two briefs are not available in this corpus. The series does not address retrospective facilitation structure, psychological safety techniques, experiment-commitment discipline, or ceremony design.

**Evidence-marker continuity.** The five core complex-problem characteristics are `[AP]` in the deep ref (Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education"), with each characteristic supported by `[V]` or `[AE]` examples; the distillation applies them as a triage test for retro topics without upgrading the evidence marker. The mental-models definition, the incomplete-mental-models claim, and the group-model-building definition are all `[V]` in the deep ref (Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT"); the distillation carries them at the same level. The CLD-as-hypothesis claim is `[V]` in the deep ref (Brief 1.05, "+ CONSIDERATIONS"); the distillation carries it directly. The archetype definitions are `[V]`; the not-prescriptive and may-not-be-necessary cautions are also `[V]` in the deep ref (Brief 1.07, "+ CONSIDERATIONS"); both are carried through at the same evidence level. The intuition-is-unreliable-about-accumulations claim is `[V]` with a `[BT]` attribution to Cronin, Gonzalez & Sterman (2009) in the deep ref (Brief 1.06, "+ CONSIDERATIONS"); the distillation carries the claim as stated without upgrading it to `[V]` on the primary source.
