# Gagné, The Approach Perfect Field Guide to Scrum Events — Software-Business Distillation

**Source:** Gagné, C. (2020). *The Approach Perfect Field Guide to Scrum Events*. April 3, 2020 edition. Approach Perfect, Limited. Public licence: CC BY-ND 4.0; relicensed CC BY 4.0 by the author for this corpus. https://approachperfect.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/04/The-Approach-Perfect-Field-Guide-to-Scrum-Events-2020-04-03.pdf.

## Software-Business Relevance

The Field Guide is a facilitation manual for the Scrum events, but at the software-business intersection it functions as something tighter: a working set of economic and governance disciplines that turn the cadence of building software into commitments a board, a CFO, or a peer C-suite executive can reason about *without* mistaking forecasts for contracts.

Three software-business threads run through the guide.

**First, the Capacity-adjusted Velocity formula is engineering capacity priced in a form the business can use.** The author gives an explicit formula — average the last 3-4 sprints' Capacity-adjusted Velocity, multiply by forecast Capacity — and a worked example: 25, 24, 31 Story Points completed over 45, 43, 50 days of Capacity, with forecast Capacity of 47 → ≈30 Story Points (Sprint Planning, "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda"). The formula is the practitioner-pointed answer to the founder-or-CTO question "what can engineering actually deliver next sprint?" — and to the CFO question "what does the engineering Capacity number on the headcount slide mean operationally?". The "mature team delivers 85-115% of planned work or Velocity every sprint" band (Sprint Planning, "Tips") is the calibration the business should expect; persistent under-delivery is named as a signal that the team is not being truly empirical, not as a performance problem.

**Second, the contrarian framings on commitment, status, and Done are software-business governance disciplines.** The Sprint Backlog and Sprint Goal are explicitly *not* firm commitments — they are "a focusing tool that limits context switching and a good-faith forecast that the *Development Team* attempts to live up to"; treating them as firm commitments "will only lead to burnout, poor quality, loss of trust, and/or attempts at 'gaming the system'" (Sprint Planning, "Tips"). The Daily Scrum "is not a status review or report for anyone, including *Product Owners*, *Scrum Masters*, or (especially) stakeholders" (Daily Scrum, "Tips"). The Sprint Review is "*not* a status meeting" — it is a working session where stakeholders collaborate with the team on the Product Backlog (Sprint Review, "Tips"). And the trust-protection rule: "Don't demonstrate work that isn't '*Done*.' It can significantly erode trust between the *Scrum Team*, stakeholders, and end users if the demonstrated work doesn't get shipped as expected" (Sprint Review, "Tips"). Each of these is a software-business governance discipline encoded as a facilitation rule: they protect the engineering org's ability to forecast honestly to the business, and they protect the business's ability to make decisions on real Done-ness rather than on demo theatre.

**Third, the carry-over rule and outcome-not-implementation framing protect business decision quality.** The carry-over rule: if work-in-process from the previous Sprint meets or exceeds forecast Velocity, take in zero new work and consider removing items — "honest empirical process control" (Sprint Planning, "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda"). This is the in-team mechanism that produces the Capacity number the CFO will be looking at, and the discipline that prevents optimistic over-commitment from spreading from engineering into commercial commitments. The outcome-not-implementation framing — the coffee-temperature worked example contrasting "As a coffee drinker, I can have coffee that's just the right temperature so that I can delight in its ideal taste" with "Integrate the Acme steam-heating element into the brew assembly" (Sprint Planning, "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda") — is the decision-rights line between Product Owner / stakeholder ("what") and Development Team ("how") that keeps build-vs-buy and feature-pricing conversations from collapsing into implementation arguments.

This distillation gathers these threads as a working pattern: where each Scrum-event facilitation depth helps price the technical-commercial trade-off, defend honest forecasts to the board, protect customer-and-stakeholder trust against demo-theatre, and keep Done-as-decision-gate intact when commercial pressure pushes toward shipping incomplete work. The Field Guide complements the canonical Scrum Guide (which defines the framework) by adding the facilitation depth at which the framework's economic and governance disciplines become operationally enforceable.

## Key Concepts for Software-Business

1. <!-- concept: capacity-adjusted-velocity --> **Capacity-adjusted Velocity formula with worked example.** Average the last 3-4 sprints' Capacity-adjusted Velocity (Story Points completed ÷ days of Capacity available) and multiply by forecast Capacity. The author's worked example: 25, 24, 31 SP / 45, 43, 50 days, forecast Capacity 47 → ≈30 SP. The formula is engineering capacity in a form a board can audit; the worked example is the calibration. (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide to Scrum Events* 2020, Sprint Planning, "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda")

2. <!-- concept: mature-team-delivery-range --> **Mature-team delivery range as the calibration band.** "A mature team should be able to deliver 85-115% of the planned work or Velocity every sprint. If a team consistently underdelivers, this usually means that they are not being truly empirical and they are taking on more work than their historical performance supports." The 85-115% band is what business-side stakeholders should expect; persistent under-delivery is an over-commitment signal, not a performance problem. (Sprint Planning, "Tips")

3. <!-- concept: sprint-goal --> **No firm commitment to the Sprint Backlog or Sprint Goal.** The Sprint Backlog and Sprint Goal are "a focusing tool that limits context switching and a good-faith forecast that the *Development Team* attempts to live up to" — not contracts. Treating them as firm commitments "will only lead to burnout, poor quality, loss of trust, and/or attempts at 'gaming the system.'" Stakeholders who pressure for firm commitments degrade the very forecast quality they need. (Sprint Planning, "Tips")

4. <!-- concept: sprint-goal-vs-backlog --> **The Sprint Goal is not coextensive with the Sprint Backlog.** "It should be possible to meet the *Sprint Goal* without completing the entire *Sprint Backlog*." For business decisions about feature commitments and milestone scope: anchor on the Sprint Goal (the outcome the business needs), not the Sprint Backlog (the team's forecast plan for how to get there). The Backlog can be renegotiated mid-Sprint without changing what the business has been told to expect. (Sprint Planning, "Outputs")

5. <!-- concept: sprint-carry-over-rule --> **The carry-over rule protects forecast honesty.** "If the work in process in your carried-over *Sprint Backlog* meets or exceeds your forecast velocity and it is all still valuable to work on, we strongly recommend against taking in *any* new work this *Sprint*. You may even need to *remove* some items." A discipline that prevents optimistic over-commitment from compounding across Sprints and into commercial commitments downstream. Painful but honest. (Sprint Planning, "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda")

6. <!-- concept: definition-of-done --> **Don't demonstrate work that isn't Done — trust erosion is the business cost.** "Don't demonstrate work that isn't '*Done*.' It can significantly erode trust between the *Scrum Team*, stakeholders, and end users if the demonstrated work doesn't get shipped as expected. It also allows the *Scrum Team* to persist the illusion that incomplete work is still valuable. Remember: work in process is a liability, not an asset." The "WIP is a liability" framing (borrowed-through to Theory of Constraints) is the cost-accounting case for the rule: demonstrating undone work registers a commercial promise the business cannot keep. (Sprint Review, "Tips")

7. <!-- concept: sprint-review --> **The Sprint Review is a working session, not a presentation.** "This is *not* a status meeting. Rather, it is an opportunity for stakeholders to collaborate with the team and improve the *Product Backlog* with shared context and learnings." For software-business: the Sprint Review is where stakeholders' market and context updates become Product Backlog adjustments — a governance event that turns commercial input into prioritised engineering work. Run as a presentation, it forfeits that mechanism. (Sprint Review, "Goals", "Tips")

8. <!-- concept: sprint-review-agenda --> **The 1-hour Sprint Review agenda allocates stakeholder voice deliberately.** 5 min PO welcome + Sprint Goal status; 5 min Development Team highs/lows/blockers; 30 min demo of Done items soliciting "honest and critical feedback"; 5 min PO on Product Backlog state with forecasts; **5 min stakeholders share new knowledge about market or context changes**; 5 min next-release planning; 5 min collaborative what-next. Each segment has a named function in the cadence. (Sprint Review, "Sample 1-Hour Sprint Review Agenda")

9. <!-- concept: calibrated-forecast-formats --> **Three calibrated forecast formats for stakeholder communication.** Within the Sprint Review, the Product Owner "can also offer forecasts, such as the likelihood of completing a given scope by a given date, the expected scope for a given date, or the expected date for a given scope." Three named formats that give stakeholders the calibrated futures they need without making firm commitments. (Sprint Review, "Sample 1-Hour Sprint Review Agenda")

10. <!-- concept: output-vs-outcome --> **Capture outcomes from the customer's perspective, not implementations.** The coffee-temperature contrast: "As a coffee drinker, I can have coffee that's just the right temperature so that I can delight in its ideal taste" (good) versus "Integrate the Acme steam-heating element into the brew assembly" (bad). The decision-rights line: outcomes belong to the Product Owner and stakeholders; implementation belongs to the Development Team. The line keeps feature-pricing and build-vs-buy conversations on the value side, not the implementation side. (Agile principles #1 and #11.) (Sprint Planning, "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda")

11. <!-- concept: daily-scrum --> **The Daily Scrum is not a status report — protects forecast quality.** "The *Daily Scrum* is not a status review or report for anyone, including *Product Owners*, *Scrum Masters*, or (especially) stakeholders. Don't let it become one." Stakeholders pulling daily status corrupts the event's information quality; the Parking Lot mechanism is the named device for re-routing legitimate stakeholder questions without sacrificing the 15-minute timebox. (Daily Scrum, "Tips")

12. <!-- concept: sprint-cancellation --> **Sprint cancellation is a Product Owner decision when the Sprint Goal is obsolete.** "The *Product Owner* may cancel a *Sprint* before the time-box has elapsed, usually in consultation with the *Scrum Master* and *Development Team*. This usually happens when the *Sprint Goal* is obsolete. A *Sprint* cancellation is traumatic to the team and consumes resources, so should be avoided if possible." For software-business: the named accountability for high-cost course-correction sits with the role accountable for product value. (The Sprint, "Tips")

13. <!-- concept: velocity-as-decision-input --> **Velocity is a Capacity-adjusted decision input, not the decision.** The formula gives a starting point for the forecast conversation; the team still decides whether the formula's number reflects the real situation. For business stakeholders: Velocity numbers are inputs to a forecast, not outputs the team must hit. Asking "what's Velocity?" instead of "what Velocity assumption is the team operating under, and what would change it?" misreads the artefact. (Sprint Planning, "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda")

14. <!-- concept: retrospectives --> **The Sprint Retrospective is the routine cadence for process-improvement decisions.** Output split: *decisions* (changes to the Definition of Done, Definition of Ready, or Working Agreement) and *actions* (new Backlog Items for next-Sprint improvement experiments). One or two improvement experiments per Sprint beats many. The mechanism gives engineering a structured, low-overhead way to invest in itself without making it a separate transformation programme. (Sprint Retrospective, "Output"; "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda")

15. <!-- concept: retrospective-prime-directive --> **The Retrospective Prime Directive frames blameless interpretation.** "Regardless of what we discover, we understand and truly believe that everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time, their skills and abilities, the resources available, and the situation at hand." A pre-commitment about how the team interprets evidence that maps onto the same blameless-by-default posture NHS Just Culture and the US Forest Service Learning Review extend to incident-scale review. (Sprint Retrospective, "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda")

16. <!-- concept: backlog-refinement --> **Backlog Refinement budgeted at ~10% of Capacity is the demand-shaping investment.** Up to 10% of Sprint Capacity, with the author's preferred minimum of "at least 1½ hours of *Backlog Refinement* per week." A continuous prioritisation-and-readiness investment that prevents the Sprint Planning event from absorbing it. For business stakeholders: budget for the refinement work; it isn't free. (Backlog Refinement, "Timing")

17. <!-- concept: estimate-the-conversation --> **Estimate the conversation, not the number.** "Suppose one developer rates a *Backlog Item* as a '1,' while another rates it as a '5'… If the *Scrum Team* accepts an average or the most senior developer's opinion, they may miss out on an opportunity for shared knowledge. Perhaps the developer who estimated the Backlog Item a '1' is aware of a faster technique. Perhaps the other is aware of a challenging edge case." The decision-quality rule: the estimation spread carries information that averages and seniority deference erase. (Backlog Refinement, "Tips")

18. <!-- concept: estimate-everything-including-defects --> **Estimate everything, including defects.** "Estimate all *Backlog Items*, even defects… The advantage of making the attempt is that your team will be better able to maintain a smooth, steady flow of work when the expected cost of resolving defects is also accounted for." For capacity-modelling: defect cost belongs in the model even when it is hard to forecast. Pretending it isn't there understates the real run-rate. (Backlog Refinement, "Tips")

19. <!-- concept: planning-horizon-discipline --> **Don't detail-plan too far into the future.** "Don't do detailed planning too far in the future. Scrum is an empirical, iterative approach to product development… If you plan too far in the future, you run the risk of wasting time trying to plan without enough real-world feedback and becoming too attached to a poorly-informed strategy." A planning-budget discipline: planning resources spent ahead of the team's feedback-loop bandwidth produce attachments to plans that the next Sprint Review will already need to discard. (Backlog Refinement, "Tips")

20. <!-- concept: practise-form-before-deviating --> **Practise the form before deviating — well-formed experiments, not quiet drift.** "A high-performing team will eventually find a better framework than 'textbook' Scrum for their environment. However, I suggest that you deviate from it only with well-formed experiments after practicing diligently for several sprints under the guidance of a knowledgeable Scrum Master or Agile Coach." A discipline against process arbitrage — the business should expect the team to follow the form before any deviation, and any deviation should be a named experiment, not a quiet erosion. (Introduction)

## Questions to Ask During Software-Business Decisions

### Phase 1: Strategic positioning (pricing, market entry, build-vs-buy, AI integration, contrarian commitment-framings)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Reject firm-commitment framing in feature-economics conversations | Is leadership or sales treating the Sprint Backlog or Sprint Goal as a firm commitment? Field Guide is explicit: both are focusing tools and good-faith forecasts, not contracts. Pressure for firm commitments degrades the forecast quality the business needs. |
| Anchor commercial commitments on Sprint Goal, not Sprint Backlog | If the business needs to commit externally to a feature/milestone, is the commitment anchored on the Sprint Goal (the outcome) or on every Backlog item? The Goal "should be preserved whenever possible"; the Backlog can be renegotiated mid-Sprint. Goal-level commitments survive scope renegotiation; Backlog-level commitments don't. |
| Separate the *what* (outcomes) from the *how* (implementation) in feature decisions | Is this a pricing or build-vs-buy discussion that's collapsing into an implementation argument? The coffee-temperature line keeps the *what* with the PO and stakeholders ("the served coffee is between 60-70°C / 140-155°F") and the *how* with the Development Team. Engineering effort estimates belong on the implementation side; the value question belongs on the outcome side. |
| Bound deviation from textbook Scrum as a well-formed experiment | Is the team being asked to deviate from Scrum for a commercial reason (a partner roadmap, a sales commitment, a customer-driven release)? Field Guide rule: practise the form for several sprints under a knowledgeable Scrum Master or Agile Coach first; deviation is a well-formed experiment, not quiet drift. Quiet drift produces process arbitrage that is hard to recover from. |
| Check that AI-integration proposals don't break the cadence | Is the AI-integration proposal compatible with the Scrum cadence the team currently runs (Sprint Planning capacity assumptions, Done definition, Sprint Goal scope)? Or does it require an out-of-cycle exception that the Field Guide's "practise-the-form" discipline would treat as a quiet deviation? |

### Phase 2: Product and engineering economics (capacity, velocity, ROI, technical-debt servicing)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Use the Capacity-adjusted Velocity formula honestly | Is the team's Velocity number a Capacity-adjusted average of the last 3-4 sprints, projected forward against the next Sprint's Capacity? Or is it a peak Velocity number cherry-picked for a board slide? Use the formula's discipline; the worked example (25, 24, 31 SP / 45, 43, 50 days, forecast 47 → ≈30 SP) is the calibration. |
| Expect 85-115%, not "100% delivery" | Is the business expecting the team to deliver exactly 100% of planned work every Sprint? Field Guide range is 85-115% for mature teams. Outside the band is a signal: persistent under-delivery means the team is over-committing; persistent over-delivery means the forecast is sandbagged. |
| Apply the carry-over rule when previous-Sprint WIP dominates forecast | If last Sprint's carry-over work-in-process meets or exceeds the new forecast Velocity, the rule is *zero* new work this Sprint, and possibly *remove* items. Painful for commercial commitments but honest. The alternative is compounding optimistic over-commitment that surfaces later as a missed external date. |
| Budget Backlog Refinement as a continuous investment, not free overhead | Is up to ~10% of Sprint Capacity being budgeted for Backlog Refinement (at least 1½ hours per week)? Or is refinement assumed to happen inside Sprint Planning, which then bloats? Treating refinement as overhead instead of investment produces under-prepared Sprint Plans and slower velocity downstream. |
| Estimate defects as part of capacity | Is the team estimating defects when they enter the Backlog? Excluding defect work from the capacity model understates the real run-rate. Better to estimate roughly and improve over time than to leave defect cost outside the model. |
| Use the estimation conversation as a risk-discovery mechanism | When estimators are split (one '1', another '5'), is the team exploring the spread or averaging it away? The spread surfaces hidden risk (a developer aware of an edge case, a developer aware of a faster technique) that averaging erases. Risk-discovery at refinement is cheaper than risk-realisation at release. |
| Don't detail-plan beyond the feedback-loop horizon | Is the team being asked to commit detailed estimates 3, 6, or 12 months out? Field Guide rule: each Sprint Review's real-world feedback updates the Product Backlog; detail planning ahead of the feedback loop produces plans that next Sprint will need to discard. Detail planning bandwidth has a hard horizon. |

### Phase 3: Team and capability building (hiring, structure, levelling, culture)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Use the per-event participant tier model to clarify cross-function expectations | For each Scrum event, what is the Required / Strongly Suggested / Invited tier expected of business stakeholders, sales, customer-success? Field Guide's three-tier model is the shape; communicating it explicitly to non-team peers reduces ad-hoc interference. |
| Honour the no-sub-teams rule when shaping cross-function roles | "Scrum does not recognize individual titles for *Development Team* members such as software engineer, QA tester, or even designer. There are no 'sub-teams' in Scrum." Cross-function hires (PM, designer, QA, DevOps) need to integrate into a self-organising team, not occupy a structurally separate role at the Sprint cadence — at least within the textbook framing. |
| Define the Definition of Done as an organisational artefact | Is the team's Definition of Done explicit, agreed, and current? It is the release-gate decision rule and the trust-protection mechanism across the Scrum cadence — and an organisational standard that survives team-membership changes. |
| Use the Retrospective decisions/actions split to organise team-improvement investment | Are improvements being registered as *decisions* (changes to Definition of Done, Definition of Ready, or Working Agreement) or *actions* (new Backlog Items)? Different mechanisms for different commitment shapes. Verbal team-improvement promises without an artefact dissolve. |
| Apply the "one or two improvements per Sprint" discipline to team-development budget | Is the team committing to too many simultaneous improvement experiments? "It's better to have one or two improvement items for the next *Sprint* than to have too much to change at once." A capacity-discipline that applies to organisational change as well as in-team improvement. |
| Schedule the connect-and-celebrate time as named team-engagement investment | Is unstructured time after the Retrospective being preserved? "*This is not an official Scrum event*, but it's very helpful to spend quality, unstructured time hanging out with colleagues." Engagement endures because the relationships endure; informal time is a named component of the cadence. |

### Phase 4: Operations and process (delivery cadence, governance, decision rights, change management)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Pick the right event for the question | Sprint Planning (Sprint commitment), Daily Scrum (today's plan, 15 min), Sprint Review (what-next with stakeholders, 1 hour ideal), Sprint Retrospective (process change, 1½ hours), Backlog Refinement (prioritisation-and-readiness, ~10% Capacity). Don't make Sprint-level decisions in the Daily Scrum or retrospective decisions in the Sprint Review. |
| Defend the Daily Scrum against status-meeting drift | Is the Daily Scrum being treated as a status report for the PO, SM, or stakeholders? Field Guide is explicit it isn't. Status pressure corrupts the event's information quality; route legitimate stakeholder questions through the Parking Lot or the Sprint Review. |
| Defend the Sprint Review against presentation drift | Is the Sprint Review being run as a one-sided demo for stakeholders to receive, rather than a working session for the Product Backlog to be improved? "Less mature teams try to complete this in 30 minutes, but we've found that this becomes a rapid-fire, one-sided demo, not a true inspection and adaptation opportunity." Allocate the 1 hour; protect the working-session frame. |
| Apply the Done-only demo rule | Is undone work being demonstrated to make a milestone look better than it is? Field Guide: "Don't demonstrate work that isn't '*Done*.'" The cost is trust erosion and the persistence of "the illusion that incomplete work is still valuable" — both of which compound across the next several decisions. |
| Run the Sprint Review's three forecast formats for stakeholder updates | When the PO offers forecasts to stakeholders, are they expressed as one of the three named formats (likelihood of given scope by given date; expected scope for given date; expected date for given scope)? Three forms that communicate calibrated futures without firm commitments. |
| Make decision rights legible across events | PO owns: Product Backlog ordering, Sprint cancellation, Backlog Refinement final ordering, stakeholder invitation curation for the Sprint Review. Development Team owns: sizing, how-to-build, Daily Scrum format, when the Sprint Backlog has enough work. Whole team: Sprint Goal, Definition of Done. Is the org communicating these decision rights to peers, or is each event treated as ambiguously authorised? |
| Use the Vegas / Chatham House Rule explicitly for retrospectives | Have the retrospectives explicitly chosen the Vegas Rule (unanimous consent before sharing) or the Chatham House Rule (information shareable, identities not)? The safe container is a precondition for raising difficult issues — including issues with how the engineering org engages with the rest of the business. |

### Phase 5: Risk, reliability, compliance (incidents, technical debt, regulatory)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Use the Sprint Retrospective as the routine learning loop | Is the team running a Sprint Retrospective every Sprint (1½ hours for 2-week Sprints), or are they shortening or skipping it under delivery pressure? The Retrospective is the team's primary engagement-with-self mechanism; skipping it surrenders the structured learning that protects against repeated failure modes. |
| Apply the Retrospective Prime Directive frame to any blameful interpretation | Is the team's posture honouring "everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time"? Without that frame, retrospective analysis tilts toward blame and the decision quality of the resulting improvement experiments degrades. The same posture maps onto NHS Just Culture and Forest Service Learning Review for incident-scale learning. |
| Distinguish routine-cadence learning from incident-scale review | A failed Sprint or a missed milestone is routine-cadence material (Sprint Retrospective). A serious incident (security breach, customer-impacting outage, regulator-relevant defect) is incident-scale material — reach for NHS Just Culture or the US Forest Service Learning Review for that, not the Field Guide retro. |
| Estimate all Backlog Items including defects as part of compliance / reliability investment | When compliance or reliability work is added to the Backlog, is it being estimated as a Backlog Item like any other? Field Guide rule includes defects; reliability and compliance work that bypasses estimation distorts the capacity model and produces unplanned displacement. |
| Use the "one or two improvements" discipline on incident-derived corrective actions | If an incident retrospective produces ten corrective actions, the Field Guide's "one or two improvement items per Sprint" discipline suggests sequencing them. Trying to land all ten in one Sprint dilutes every experiment and may degrade the team's ability to learn from any single change. |
| Surface ambiguity-by-design in the Definition of Done | Are reliability, security, or compliance acceptance criteria part of the Definition of Done — or are they being treated as case-by-case judgements at the boundary? Without DoD-level inclusion, each release reopens the question; with it, the gate is enforced uniformly. |

### Phase 6: Stakeholder communication (board, exec, investor, customer)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Communicate forecasts in calibrated language | When the PO or CTO communicates timing to a board, is it in one of the three Field Guide forecast formats — likelihood of scope by date, expected scope for date, expected date for scope? Or is it a firm commitment? Calibrated forecasts protect against missed-date disappointment that erodes board trust. |
| Defend "no firm commitment" framing without sounding evasive | The Field Guide is explicit that firm commitments lead to burnout, gaming, and quality loss. When a board or commercial peer pushes for a firm commitment, the discipline is to translate to the calibrated forecast formats — *not* to refuse engagement. The trust-build is in producing a useful answer in the right shape, not in resisting the question. |
| Translate the no-status-meeting framings to upward communication | A CTO communicating engineering progress upward shouldn't import the Daily Scrum's no-status-meeting framing literally. The framing protects the *Daily Scrum*; it doesn't excuse skipping board-level communication. The right artefact for board-level update is a deliberate communication (often a structured memo), not a daily standup transcript. |
| Use the Sprint Review's 5-minute stakeholder-market-update slot deliberately | At a Sprint Review with business-side stakeholders present, is the 5-minute market/context-update segment being used to surface real changes, or is it slipping into an extended commercial conversation? Used well, this segment is where commercial signal becomes engineering priority; used poorly, it bloats the event. |
| Communicate the Sprint Goal in language a non-engineering audience can carry | "The Sprint Goal communicates why the Sprint is valuable to stakeholders." Is the Sprint Goal expressed as a customer-outcome (coffee-temperature framing) or as engineering-implementation (heating-element framing)? Outcome-language travels upward to commercial peers; implementation-language doesn't. |
| Protect the trust-building goal of the Sprint Review across Sprints | "Foster a culture of transparency and trust between the *Scrum Team* and stakeholders" is co-equal with feedback elicitation. The trust is built over many Sprints; demonstrating undone work or framing demos as defence erodes the trust faster than later Sprints can rebuild it. |
| Use Definition of Done as the artefact for "are we shipping?" decisions | When a stakeholder asks whether a feature is shipping, the answer is anchored on the Definition of Done, not on demo-state. "Done" is a pre-committed standard; "looks done" is theatre. |

## What to Look For

| Pattern | Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|---|
| Firm-commitment framing on the Sprint Backlog | Sales has committed externally to delivering "the Sprint" by a date; the team is under pressure to deliver every Backlog Item or face consequences | Stakeholder/business-side has confused the Sprint Backlog (forecast) with a contract. Field Guide framing: focusing tool + good-faith forecast, not contract. Pressure produces burnout, gaming, quality loss. | Reframe to Sprint-Goal-level commitment (the outcome the business needs) with Sprint-Backlog-level flexibility; renegotiate the external commitment to a calibrated forecast format; identify the source of the firm-commitment expectation and address it upstream. |
| Demo-theatre pattern | Team is demonstrating partially-done work to make a milestone look better than it is; stakeholders express satisfaction; downstream releases miss their dates | Done definition isn't enforced at the Sprint Review boundary. "Work in process is a liability, not an asset." The illusion of value is being banked while the actual cost is accruing. | Restate Definition of Done at the next Sprint Review opening; explicitly mark which items are Done and which aren't; refuse to demonstrate undone items; absorb the short-term reputational cost to restore the long-term trust signal. |
| Velocity gaming | Team's Velocity has trended steadily upward over multiple sprints with no methodology change; estimates have been quietly inflated | "A mature team should be able to deliver 85-115% of planned work or Velocity every sprint" — out-of-band consistent over-delivery is as much a signal as consistent under-delivery. Inflation can be a response to commitment-as-contract framing (Pattern 1) or to leadership pressure on "improving velocity." | Audit estimation conversations at Backlog Refinement; surface whether the spread between estimators has narrowed (a possible inflation signal); reframe Velocity as a Capacity-adjusted starting-point, not an output to maximise. |
| Daily-Scrum-as-status-meeting drift | PO, SM, or stakeholders are using the Daily Scrum to ask for progress reports; team members shape what they say for the upward audience rather than for the Development Team's planning | Information quality of the Daily Scrum degrades when the audience is upward; the Development Team's actual coordination need goes unmet, and the team learns to perform rather than coordinate. | Reread the Field Guide framing in a Sprint Retrospective; agree explicitly on the Daily Scrum's purpose (development-team coordination); set up the Parking Lot for stakeholder questions; PO and SM continue to participate but not demand status. |
| Sprint-Review-as-presentation drift | Sprint Review is being run as a slide deck with stakeholders as audience; the 30-minute demo segment has compressed into 10 minutes of "show and tell" and most of the hour is presentation | "This is *not* a status meeting" framing has been lost. The Sprint Review's mechanism (stakeholder collaboration on the Product Backlog) is being forfeited; market/context updates from stakeholders never enter the engineering pipeline. | Restructure the next Sprint Review to the 1-hour agenda explicitly; allocate the 30 minutes for demo and feedback; allocate the 5 minutes for market/context updates; reframe the event purpose at the opening. |
| Carry-over over-commitment | Previous Sprint's WIP has rolled into this Sprint; the team has added new work on top; forecast Velocity will be exceeded; the team will under-deliver and the over-commitment will compound | Carry-over rule has been violated. "Honest empirical process control" requires zero new work when carry-over meets or exceeds forecast Velocity; the team may need to remove items. | Apply the rule at the next Sprint Planning: no new work this Sprint; consider removing items; communicate the rule to commercial stakeholders so the discipline is legible (not hidden as "the team is just slow"). |
| Outcome-implementation collapse in feature decisions | A pricing or build-vs-buy conversation has collapsed into a debate about engineering effort and technical implementation; the customer-outcome question is being treated as resolved | The decision-rights line between "what" (PO + stakeholders) and "how" (Development Team) has blurred. The coffee-temperature framing: outcomes belong on one side, implementations on the other. | Re-anchor on the customer-outcome question ("what changes for the customer if we ship this feature?"); separate the implementation effort estimate; price the outcome, not the implementation. |
| Retrospective short-cutting under delivery pressure | Sprint Retrospective has been shortened to 30 minutes or skipped entirely "because we're behind"; team-improvement experiments have stopped landing | The Sprint Retrospective is the team's primary engagement-with-self and learning loop. Skipping it under delivery pressure compounds the conditions producing the delivery pressure in the first place; it doesn't relieve them. | Restore the 1½ hour timebox; identify the source of the delivery pressure (over-commitment? unmaintained backlog? unresolved structural issue?); address the source with the retrospective's improvement mechanism (decisions vs actions split). |
| Detailed planning ahead of feedback horizon | The team is being asked to commit detailed estimates 6 or 12 months out for budget cycles; the resulting plan is treated as ground truth by commercial peers | Detail-planning is exceeding the feedback-loop horizon. "If you plan too far in the future, you run the risk of wasting time trying to plan without enough real-world feedback and becoming too attached to a poorly-informed strategy." | Provide forecasts in calibrated language at the long horizon (one of the three Field Guide forecast formats); reserve detailed planning for the near horizon (the next 1-2 Sprints); name the planning-horizon discipline explicitly to commercial peers. |

## When to Use This Reference

- A *board paper or exec memo* needs an engineering-capacity number — reach for the Capacity-adjusted Velocity formula with its worked example, and the 85-115% mature-team band as a calibration claim that's auditable from sprint data.
- A *firm-commitment pressure* is showing up in commercial conversations — reach for the Field Guide's no-firm-commitment framing and the three calibrated forecast formats as the productive translation.
- A *milestone is being celebrated with a demo of undone work* — reach for "WIP is a liability, not an asset" and the don't-demonstrate-work-that-isn't-Done rule as the trust-protection case.
- A *Sprint Review is drifting into presentation mode* — reach for the 1-hour agenda with the working-session frame and the 5-minute stakeholder market-update slot as the structural reset.
- A *Daily Scrum is drifting into status reporting* — reach for the no-status-meeting framing and the Parking Lot mechanism as the structural reset.
- A *carry-over over-commitment* — reach for the "zero new work, possibly remove items" rule and "honest empirical process control" as the commercially legible discipline.
- A *feature-pricing or build-vs-buy conversation is collapsing into implementation debate* — reach for the coffee-temperature outcome-vs-implementation framing as the decision-rights line.
- A *Sprint Retrospective is being shortened under delivery pressure* — reach for the 1½-hour timebox, the decisions/actions split, and the one-or-two-improvements discipline as the case for protecting the learning loop.
- An *incident or compliance investment* needs to be sequenced — reach for the one-or-two-improvements rule and the Backlog Refinement budget (~10% of Capacity) as the sequencing discipline.
- A *team-improvement decision* needs an artefact — reach for the decisions (DoD / DoR / Working Agreement) vs actions (Backlog Items) split as the registration mechanism.
- A *non-team observer is asking to attend the Sprint Retrospective* — reach for "No other may attend unless specifically invited to do so by the entire *Scrum Team*" and the Vegas/Chatham House Rule as the structural protection.

## What this distillation does not cover

- *Strategic / financial frameworks* — Porter's Five Forces, PESTEL, SWOT, NPV, CAPM, breakeven, CVP. The Field Guide stays at the per-event facilitation level; for strategy/finance, reach for OpenStax *Principles of Management*, *Principles of Marketing*, *Principles of Finance*, *Accounting Vol 2*, and *Entrepreneurship* distillations.
- *Empirical critique of Agile / Scrum* — Jones's post-1980 evidence collapse and the KLOC-power-law mythology question whether Scrum's evidentiary base supports the claims practitioners make for it. The Field Guide is unapologetically Agile-positive and operational. For the evidence base, reach for `jones-evidence-based-sweng-software-business.md`.
- *Theory-of-constraints integration* — Goldratt's TOC framework underlies the "WIP is a liability" framing (the source explicitly cites ap.tips/toc). For TOC primitives applied to capacity decisions, reach for OpenStax *Accounting Vol 2* (TOC chapter).
- *Cross-team scaling at enterprise scale* — SAFe, LeSS, Nexus, Scrum-of-Scrums. The Field Guide stays at the single-Scrum-Team level. For enterprise-scale agile coordination, the corpus's primary source is Letaw's *Handbook of Software Engineering Methods*.
- *Conway's Law and team-structure-vs-product-structure mapping* — the team-structure-economics question runs through Letaw, not the Field Guide.
- *AAR / post-incident learning at incident scale* — the Field Guide's Sprint Retrospective is per-Sprint process improvement, not incident-scale review. For AAR / Learning Review, reach for TC 25-20, the US Forest Service Learning Review Guide, and the NHS Just Culture Guide.
- *Technical-debt economics in financial terms* — relevant-cost discipline, opportunity cost of remediation versus revenue work. The Field Guide treats Backlog Items uniformly (defects included in estimation) but doesn't price them in cost-accounting terms. OpenStax *Accounting Vol 2* carries the relevant-cost framing.
- *Risk pricing and reliability statistics* — Jones's bi-exponential fault-report pattern and the survival-adjusted maintenance-to-development ratio. Field Guide treats reliability tactically (within the Definition of Done); for the empirical pricing, reach for Jones.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

| Anti-pattern | Signal | Diagnosis | Follow-up |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sprint-Backlog-as-contract.** Sales / commercial / leadership treats the Sprint Backlog as a firm delivery commitment; team is held accountable for every Backlog Item. | Phrases like "you committed to this Sprint," "the team failed to deliver the Sprint," "what was promised vs what was delivered" at the Backlog-item level | The non-firm-commitment framing has been lost. Pressure regenerates burnout, gaming, and quality erosion. | Reframe to Sprint-Goal-level commitment; absorb the immediate political cost; build trust over multiple Sprints with calibrated forecasts that prove out. |
| **Demo theatre.** Team demonstrates undone or near-done work as if it were Done; stakeholders express satisfaction; downstream releases miss their dates. | Disconnect between "what was shown at the demo" and "what shipped"; trust erosion in stakeholder communications | Definition of Done not enforced at the boundary; WIP-as-asset framing has crept in; trust signal is being banked against actual delivery. | Restate Definition of Done; explicitly mark Done vs undone at the next Sprint Review; refuse to demo undone work; absorb the short-term reputational cost. |
| **Velocity-as-performance-metric.** Engineering leadership treats Velocity as a number to be maximised; team is rewarded for higher Velocity, penalised for lower. | Velocity trends upward over multiple sprints with no methodology change; estimates inflate; pressure on standups to "go faster" | Velocity has been confused with productivity. The Capacity-adjusted formula is a decision input, not an output to maximise. Inflation produces unreliable forecasts. | Audit estimation conversations at refinement; reframe Velocity as Capacity-adjusted starting point; the 85-115% band is the calibration, not the floor. |
| **Daily-Scrum status-reporting.** PO / SM / stakeholders use the Daily Scrum for upward status; team performs status for the upward audience. | Team members address the PO/SM/stakeholder in their answers; standup runs over 15 minutes; substantive coordination happens elsewhere | No-status-meeting framing has eroded. The event's information quality is degraded for the Development Team's actual planning need. | Reread Field Guide framing in a retro; agree explicitly on the Daily Scrum's purpose; set up Parking Lot; PO and SM continue to participate but do not demand status. |
| **Sprint-Review-as-status-deck.** Sprint Review is a slide deck with stakeholders as audience; 30-min demo segment compresses to 10 min show-and-tell; market/context updates from stakeholders never enter the engineering pipeline. | Slide deck is the main artefact; team is presenting rather than collaborating; stakeholder voice is absent or perfunctory | "This is *not* a status meeting" framing has been lost. Sprint Review's mechanism (stakeholder→Product Backlog) is forfeited. | Restructure to the 1-hour agenda; allocate 30 min for demo and feedback; allocate 5 min for market/context; reframe purpose at opening. |
| **Carry-over compounding.** Each Sprint, previous-Sprint WIP plus new work exceeds forecast Velocity; the team consistently under-delivers; commercial commitments downstream miss dates because the slip compounds. | Carry-over Backlog Items grow Sprint over Sprint; Velocity declines or oscillates; missed external dates trace back several Sprints | Carry-over rule has been violated repeatedly. Empirical process control has been replaced with optimistic over-commitment. | Apply the rule at the next Sprint Planning: zero new work, possibly remove items; communicate the discipline to commercial peers as a feature, not a bug. |
| **Outcome-implementation collapse.** Feature-pricing or build-vs-buy conversation collapses into engineering-effort debate; customer-outcome question is treated as resolved before it has been asked. | Discussions about features default to implementation specifics; the "what does this change for the customer" question is missing or dismissed | Decision-rights line (PO/stakeholders own outcomes; Development Team owns implementation) has blurred. Coffee-temperature framing has been forgotten. | Restart the conversation on the outcome side ("what changes for the customer if we ship this?"); separate the implementation effort estimate; price the outcome, not the implementation. |
| **Retrospective skipping under pressure.** Sprint Retrospective is shortened to 30 minutes or skipped "because we're behind"; team-improvement experiments stop landing; the conditions producing the pressure persist. | Retrospectives are perfunctory, shortened, or absent; team complains about systemic issues that never get addressed; same problems recur Sprint after Sprint | The team's primary engagement-with-self mechanism is being sacrificed for short-term delivery, which compounds the medium-term delivery problem. | Restore the 1½-hour timebox; identify the source of the delivery pressure with the retrospective; address it with the decisions/actions split. |
| **Over-detailed long-horizon planning.** Team is asked to commit detailed estimates 6 or 12 months out; commercial peers treat the plan as ground truth; mid-year reality diverges sharply from the plan. | Detailed Gantt charts or roadmaps for 12-month horizons; budget cycles treating the team's long-horizon estimates as commitments; surprise when reality diverges | Detail-planning exceeds the feedback-loop horizon. The plan becomes an anchor that the next several Sprint Reviews need to discard. | Provide calibrated forecasts at the long horizon (one of the three forecast formats); reserve detail-planning for the near horizon; communicate the planning-horizon discipline to commercial peers. |
| **Quiet drift from textbook Scrum.** The team has been skipping events, watering down accountabilities, weakening the Definition of Done, or modifying the framework without naming the deviation; outcomes degrade slowly. | Events skipped without an experiment frame; accountabilities blurred; Definition of Done weakened "to ship"; standup running 30 minutes; retro skipped | "Practise the form before deviating" discipline has been replaced by undeclared drift. The framework's economic and governance disciplines are lost without a corresponding gain. | Run a Sprint Retrospective specifically on framework deviation; convert each undeclared deviation either to a well-formed experiment (with criteria for evaluation) or back to the textbook form. |

## Worked Example

A founder-CTO at a 60-engineer software-product company is preparing the next quarterly board update. The previous quarter, sales committed externally to delivering "the Q3 release" by 15 September on the strength of a Sprint Backlog the engineering team had just landed on. Two Sprints later, the team is carrying 30 Story Points of WIP into the next Sprint with a forecast Velocity of 28. The CRO is pressing for "what got cut" and asking how the team will "make up the gap." The board agenda includes a slide on "engineering reliability."

The CTO reaches for the Field Guide distillation. The diagnostic sequence:

**Phase 1 (strategic positioning):** The external commitment was anchored on the Sprint Backlog ("the Q3 release scope" = every Backlog Item), not on the Sprint Goal (the outcome the business needs). The Field Guide rule: anchor commercial commitments on the Sprint Goal so the Backlog can be renegotiated mid-Sprint without changing what the business has been told. The reframe: name the Sprint Goal in the board update as the outcome customers are getting; show that scope adjustments inside the Sprint preserved that outcome.

**Phase 2 (economics):** The team's forecast Velocity (28) is exceeded by carry-over WIP (30). The Field Guide carry-over rule: zero new work this Sprint, possibly remove items. Painful for the CRO; honest. The board slide should communicate the Capacity-adjusted formula's discipline — Velocity is a starting point for the conversation, not a target to hit — and the 85-115% mature-team band as the calibration. The persistent over-commitment over the previous Sprints (now appearing as 30 SP of WIP) is the signal the team is not being truly empirical.

**Phase 4 (operations / governance):** The last Sprint Review had a slide deck. The 30-minute demo segment compressed to 12 minutes; stakeholders received a presentation; the 5-minute market-update slot never happened; the CRO's market intelligence about a competitive launch never entered the Product Backlog. The reset: at the next Sprint Review, restructure to the 1-hour agenda explicitly; allocate the demo time; allocate the market-update time; reframe the working-session purpose at opening.

**Phase 6 (stakeholder communication):** The board update should be in one of the three calibrated forecast formats — likelihood of the named scope by an honest date, expected scope for a given date, or expected date for a given scope. Not a firm commitment. The CTO reframes the CRO conversation similarly: the question "what got cut" is misformed; the right question is "what calibrated forecast can engineering land on for the next sprint and the next two after that?"

The board slide reads: "Sprint Goal achieved across the last two Sprints. Capacity-adjusted Velocity 28 SP over the past three Sprints (range 25-31, capacity 43-50 days). Carry-over rule applied this Sprint: no new work added; one item removed. Next-Sprint forecast for the Q3 release: 90% likelihood of named scope by 22 September; 70% by 15 September; recommended commercial commitment: 22 September." The CRO conversation reframes around the calibrated forecast format. The Sprint Review next week is restored to the 1-hour working-session structure with the 5-minute market-update slot reserved for the competitive-launch input.

## Citation and Source-Integrity Notes

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The Field Guide cites several authors and frameworks it does not reproduce in full. Norm Kerth's *Project Retrospectives: A Handbook for Team Reviews* is the named source of the Retrospective Prime Directive [BT]; Derby and Larsen's *Agile Retrospectives: Making Good Teams Great* is the basis for the sample Retrospective agenda [BT]; Jeff Sutherland et al.'s *Shock Therapy* is the source for the one-week-Sprint recommendation for new teams [BT]; the Agile Manifesto (Beck et al., 2001) is cited five times by principle number [BT]; Theory of Constraints via `ap.tips/toc` is the source for "work in process is a liability, not an asset" [BT]; the Scrum Guide 2020 (Schwaber & Sutherland) is the source for the three Daily Scrum questions [BT]; Jeff Patton's *User Story Mapping* and Mike Cohn's *User Stories Applied* are book recommendations for Backlog Refinement [BT]; the Chatham House Rule and Vegas Rule are borrowed-through confidentiality devices [BT]. None of these are held as primary corpus references.

**Named limits of the source.** The author states the Field Guide is an *illustrated guide* to the Scrum Guide, not a replacement for it; the Scrum Guide is the authoritative framework definition (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide to Scrum Events*, Introduction). The Field Guide does not address cross-team or enterprise-scale coordination (SAFe, LeSS, Nexus) and stays at the single-Scrum-Team level. It is silent on the ethics of *what* a team builds and on the empirical evidence base for Agile claims; the author explicitly directs readers to the Scrum Guide for framework authority. The "firm commitment" refusal is a deliberate scope boundary: the source frames calibrated forecasts as the correct commercial artefact and does not offer methods for producing firm-date commitments.

**Evidence-marker continuity.** The deep reference at `corpus.commons/demo/references/approach-perfect-field-guide-scrum-events-deep.md` carries [V] markers for direct author claims (the Capacity-adjusted Velocity formula, the 85-115% mature-team band, the carry-over rule, the done-only demo rule, the three forecast formats), [AP] for the author's event-design positions, [AE] for the coffee-temperature and Maxwell-House worked examples, and [BT] for the borrowed-through citations listed above. This distillation's Key Concepts and Questions sections paraphrase material the deep ref carries as [V] and [AP]; no verbatim blockquotes appear here. Evidence-class markers are preserved where they matter to the conclusions: the 85-115% band is an [AP] claim (author position, not empirically demonstrated) and is treated as such rather than as a [V] verified finding.
