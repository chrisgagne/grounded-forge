## Software-Business Relevance

LFUO is a wildland-fire manual, not a software text — but the post-incident learning machinery it specifies maps cleanly onto the question a CTO, engineering manager, or product leader faces after a software incident: *what scale of review does this incident justify, what discipline does that review apply, and what does it produce?* Three contributions land on Phase 5 of the software-business axis:

1. **A four-tool ladder of post-incident learning tools, scaled to incident scale.** AAR (immediate, local, closed), RLS (one-page shareable lesson), FLA (signed delegation, no-punitive-action, learning product without recommendations), Learning Review (FLA plus focus groups, academic SMEs, Safety Action Plan with named owners and deadlines). The ladder is a triage scheme: a sev-3 caught-in-staging dev-team retro is an AAR; a sev-1 customer-impact incident across multiple services is an FLA; a security breach with regulatory and board dimensions is a Learning Review (Source: LFUO Part 2, "The tools").

2. **A discipline against the single-root-cause narrative in distributed systems.** "FLAs must avoid causal statements" and "Cause isn't something investigators 'find' or 'discover'; cause is always something we create by recreating the event showing how certain anomalies led to the accident" (Source: LFUO Part 5, "Recommendations vs Lessons Learned"; LFUO Appendix B, "A Deeper Dive into Causal Factors"). The replacement vocabulary is *networked causality* — the combined conditions that collectively influenced the outcome. For distributed systems where multiple improbable events combined to produce the outage, single-cause narratives systematically mislead post-mortem readers and shape the wrong corrective work (Source: LFUO Glossary, "Networked causality"; LFUO Part 7, "The Lessons Learned Analysis").

3. **A design choice about whether the post-mortem produces recommendations.** Recommendations are not an automatic output. If the Delegating Official wants vetted recommendations, the engagement must be designed as a Learning Review with focus groups and a Safety Action Plan with named owners and deadlines. Recommendations on a single data point, without vetting through communities of practice, are framed as a decision-making error that may "add complexity to the workplace, which has the paradoxical effect of increasing risk" (Source: LFUO Part 5, "Recommendations vs Lessons Learned"; LFUO Part 11, "Recommendations"). For software-business work, this maps onto the decision of whether the incident write-up produces a public learning artefact or a binding action plan committing engineering capacity.

Where LFUO is silent for software-business: it has nothing to say about non-incident strategic positioning, build-vs-buy economics, hiring structure, sprint cadence, board memos outside incident contexts, or fundraising. Route those through Jones, Letaw, the OpenStax management / accounting / finance / business-law titles, and the methodology sources.

## Key Concepts for Software-Business

1. **The four-tool ladder for post-incident learning, scaled to incident severity and learning ambition.** Pick by *learning opportunity*, not by outcome severity alone. A near-miss can deserve an FLA; a high-severity outage with little learning yield can deserve only an AAR. The Forest Service's two-inch tree-cutting case (firefighter knocked unconscious; same-day release; two inches of tree-fall variance separates the actual outcome from a fatality) is the canonical illustration that severity-of-outcome is a poor selection criterion (Source: LFUO Part 3, "Starting the process"; LFUO Part 2, "The tools").

2. **The signed delegation as the engineering equivalent of a blameless commitment.** The FLA's signed delegation-of-authority "assuring participants that there shall be no punitive actions" is the procedural commitment that makes honest post-incident accounts possible. The list of administrative actions to exclude is explicit: disciplinary actions, forced retraining, removal from incident, reduction in planned overtime. For software work, the parallel commitment is: no performance-management action against the engineer who pushed the bad deploy; no exclusion from the on-call rotation; no compensation impact (Source: LFUO Part 3, "Administrative assurance of no punitive actions"; LFUO Part 2, "The tools").

3. **The administrative firewall is non-negotiable.** "It is critical to maintain a solid firewall between the FLA and any potential administrative actions that may be taken against the employee. Information is the lifeblood of safety." Without this commitment in writing, the engineering team's account of what actually happened will not surface — engineers will either go silent or curate, and the post-mortem will read as the official cover story rather than as the actual condition-reconstruction (Source: LFUO Part 3, "Administrative assurance of no punitive actions").

4. **Networked causality replaces root-cause analysis in complex systems.** "The combined total of those conditions that collectively influenced the event or outcome" is the working unit (Source: LFUO Glossary, "Networked causality"). For distributed systems with multiple services, multiple deploys per day, multiple human operators, and emergent behaviour at scale, single-cause narratives are the analytic equivalent of fitting a straight line to a non-linear surface. The Lessons Learned Analysis reveals that "multiple improbable events were necessary for the accident to occur" — the same is true of distributed-system incidents (Source: LFUO Part 7, "The Lessons Learned Analysis"; LFUO Appendix B, "A Deeper Dive into Causal Factors").

5. **Cause-as-construction rather than cause-as-discovery.** "Cause isn't something investigators 'find' or 'discover'; cause is always something we create by recreating the event showing how certain anomalies led to the accident" (Source: LFUO Appendix B, "A Deeper Dive into Causal Factors"). For software post-mortems, this reframes the deliverable from *we identified the root cause* (a discovery claim with epistemic baggage the system can't carry) to *we reconstructed the conditions that made the deploy seem reasonable to the engineer at the time* (a sensemaking claim the analysis can actually deliver).

6. **Counterfactual prohibition in the analysis.** "FLA teams must guard against making counterfactual arguments such as: 'If this person had done X, then the outcome would have been Y and the accident would not have occurred.' The FLA is only useful when it learns why people did what they actually did" (Source: LFUO Part 9, "Avoid Counterfactual Arguments"). For software incident reviews, the parallel pattern is *"if the on-call had read the runbook more carefully, they would have caught the misconfiguration"* — a counterfactual that explains nothing about why the runbook didn't get read, why the misconfiguration looked like the right setting at the time, or why the deploy gate didn't catch it. Counterfactuals are forbidden as analytic moves (Source: LFUO Handout C, "FLA 'Watch outs'").

7. **The Five Hows as a decision-reconstruction tool.** For each key action or decision in the incident, the team dialogues through the Five Hows to reach the participants' pre-event situational awareness. The instruction is explicit: "You may find some of these 'hows' do not apply and some will be redundant. Use those that are helpful and don't waste time trying to cook-book the process." The intent is to "explore how and why the decisions and actions that seemed wrong in hindsight were actually valid or at least legitimate in context at the time" (Source: LFUO Part 7, "The Five Hows – A Sensemaking Tool").

8. **Recognition-Primed Decision Making is the default operating pattern, not deliberation.** Defined in the Glossary, citing Gary Klein: people "use experience to generate a plausible option as the first one they consider" (Source: LFUO Glossary, "Recognition primed decision making"). For software incidents, this means the on-call engineer did not deliberate between multiple options at 3am — they generated an experience-based first option and acted. The analyst's job is to understand *why* that first option seemed right, not to enumerate the options the engineer did not consider.

9. **The ETTO principle frames the engineer's tradeoff.** Hollnagel's Efficiency-Thoroughness Trade-Off: "If demands to productivity or performance are high, thoroughness is reduced until the productivity goals are met. If demands to safety are high, efficiency is reduced until the safety goals are met" (Source: LFUO Glossary, "Efficiency-Thoroughness Trade-Off"). For software work, the parallel is the deploy-velocity / quality-gate tradeoff: under sprint pressure, engineers reduce verification depth; under reliability pressure, engineers reduce deploy frequency. Every incident is a position on this tradeoff that the post-mortem needs to surface (Source: LFUO Part 3, "Administrative assurance of no punitive actions"; LFUO Handout A, "Understanding the Work Under a Just Culture").

10. **The Gap — work-as-imagined versus work-as-done.** "The difference between what administrators think is going on and what is really going on in the field" (Source: LFUO Glossary, "GAP"). For software-business work, the parallel gap is between the architecture diagram the CTO presents to the board and the actual call graph, between the runbook the SRE team owns and the runbook the on-call uses, between the SLO commitment and the actual operating point. The Display Misalignments step of the FLA explicitly names this contrast as the diagnostic surface (Source: LFUO Part 9, "Display Misalignments Between Different Aspects or Roles within the Organization"; LFUO Part 1, "Systems Thinking").

11. **Storytelling as the post-mortem's primary learning artefact.** "Cognitive scientists have shown that humans make sense of, and remember, 'facts' by attaching them to narratives (i.e., stories) that give them context and a sensory or emotional association. If given a listing of findings with no story as context, our minds are prone to give these findings relevance by either creating a story, or by associating them with a story that is already within our memory. In other words, the lack of the story behind the 'facts' will actually lead to a distortion of the facts within our minds" (Source: LFUO Part 8, "Why FLA Storytelling?"). For software post-mortems, this maps onto the choice between a structured-template post-mortem (timeline + cause + action items) and a narrative post-mortem (the engineer's decision-by-decision reconstruction). The narrative form is the one that produces vicarious learning across the engineering org.

12. **The recommendation-vetting cost is the difference between an FLA and a Learning Review.** "If the Delegating Official insists on having recommendations, the Learning Review process must be initiated so the recommendations can be vetted with the communities of practice and a Safety Action Plan can be developed that outlines who is responsible to carry them out and by what date" (Source: LFUO Part 5, "Recommendations vs Lessons Learned"). For software-business work, this is the choice between a public incident write-up that shares the lessons (FLA-shape) and a binding action plan that commits engineering capacity to specific remediation (Learning Review shape). Recommendations on a single data point that bypass community-of-practice vetting are likely to fail in implementation and may "add complexity to the workplace, which has the paradoxical effect of increasing risk" (Source: LFUO Part 5, "Recommendations vs Lessons Learned").

13. **Safety as "the reasonableness of risk" — not absolute, contextual, and personal.** "It is a feeling. It is not an absolute. It is personal and contextual and will vary between people even within identical situations." Two experienced, competent professionals can disagree on whether a risk is reasonable, and both can be right (Source: LFUO Handout A, "Understanding the Work Under a Just Culture"; LFUO Part 6, "Principles and Agreements for All Involved in the Facilitation"). For software-business work, this frames the reliability-investment question: there is no objective "how reliable is reliable enough?" — only a contextual judgement of reasonableness that varies across business lines, regulatory contexts, and customer commitments.

14. **The reckless-and-willful threshold as the termination criterion.** The FLA is the right tool below the threshold of "conduct that is intentional, unjustifiable, and occurred with the foreknowledge that the conduct was likely to result in serious harm, death, or injury to a human" (Source: LFUO Appendix C, "Is an FLA the Right Tool?"). For software-business work, the parallel threshold is malicious action (intentional sabotage, intentional data exfiltration, intentional security policy violation). Below this threshold, the post-mortem is a learning process; at or above this threshold, the case becomes an administrative or legal matter and the post-mortem terminates (Source: LFUO Part 9, "Serious Crimes or Reckless and Willful Disregard for Human Safety").

## Questions to Ask During Software-Business Incident Response

### Phase 5a: Choosing the post-mortem scale

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Am I selecting the post-mortem scale by incident severity or by learning opportunity? | If the incident had been an inch different — different deploy timing, different traffic pattern, different customer affected — would I choose a different tool? If yes, why am I choosing the current tool based on outcome severity alone? |
| Is this an AAR, RLS, FLA, or Learning Review? | What is the audience for the learning — the dev team alone (AAR), anyone in the engineering org who might benefit (RLS), the broader business (FLA), or the C-suite with a binding action plan (Learning Review)? |
| Do I want recommendations? | If yes, I am committing to a Learning Review with community-of-practice vetting, focus groups, and a Safety Action Plan with named owners and deadlines. I cannot add binding recommendations to an FLA after the fact. |
| What does it cost me NOT to do this analysis? | Opportunity-cost framing: what learning is closed off if this incident gets a five-line internal Slack write-up instead of a structured review? |
| How close is the analyst to the incident? | Locally-involved analysts bring tacit knowledge but carry hindsight bias and political stake. Outside analysts bring distance but lack context. Mix accordingly. |

### Phase 5b: Setting the conditions for honest engineering accounts

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Has the administrative firewall been committed in writing? | The delegation must explicitly exclude disciplinary action, forced retraining, removal from on-call, performance-management impact, and compensation impact. Without this, the engineer's account will be the official cover story, not the actual reconstruction. |
| Is the engineering team's relationship to the on-call rotation reset? | The engineer who pushed the bad deploy stays on the team and stays in the rotation. The post-mortem is about conditions, not character. |
| Does the team understand this is a learning process, not an investigation? | The vocabulary matters. *"We are investigating the incident"* and *"We are running a post-mortem"* land differently. Use sensemaking vocabulary. |
| Have the participants been protected from re-traumatising recall? | The deflection-question protocol from Part 6 applies: be prepared with questions that walk the engineer mentally away from vivid memories (e.g., the moment they realised the deploy had broken production) toward something they know well (the work they did the day before, their normal routine). |

### Phase 5c: Reconstructing the engineer's sensemaking

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| What beliefs, perceptions, expectations, and paradigms did the engineer hold before the deploy? | The four-category decomposition from the Lessons Learned Analysis. For each: what did the engineer hold to be true that hindsight reveals was incomplete? |
| What were the key decisions or actions in the incident timeline? | Identify the ones that, in hindsight, look like mistakes. The Five Hows is applied to these. |
| How did the decision look at the time? | Recognition-primed: the engineer generated a first plausible option from experience. Why was that option plausible? What past deploys supported it? What signals on the dashboard were available at the time? |
| What were the conditions that shaped the decision? | Technical (call-graph fragility, deploy-tooling state), social (team dynamics, on-call handover), cultural (deploy-velocity norms, who reviewed the PR), political (the feature shipping for a customer commitment). Conditions are the unit of analysis, not characters. |
| What ETTO tradeoff was the engineer making? | Was the decision driven by sprint pressure, on-call fatigue, code-review time pressure, or some other efficiency-thoroughness factor? |
| Were multiple improbable events required for this outcome? | If yes, the incident is not single-cause, and the analysis must reflect that. |

### Phase 5d: Avoiding the post-mortem anti-patterns

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Am I writing a counterfactual? | *"If the on-call had read the runbook more carefully..."* is forbidden. Reframe: why didn't the runbook get read? Why did the misconfiguration look right at the time? |
| Am I labelling a finding "the root cause"? | Cause is constructed by the investigator, not discovered. Replace with: the *conditions* that shaped the engineer's decision; the *combined* factors that made this outcome possible. |
| Am I anchoring on a single root cause? | Multiple improbable events are the norm for distributed-system incidents. If the analysis points to a single cause, look for the chance combinations the analysis is hiding. |
| Am I treating post-event coherence as pre-event clarity? | Hindsight bias: the post-event story feels coherent because memory rewrites it that way. The engineer at the time did not have the post-event story. |
| Am I committing the fundamental attribution error? | Am I blaming the engineer when the deploy tooling, the runbook, the on-call rotation, and the review process shaped the conditions? |
| Am I defaulting to "we just need more training"? | Sometimes that's the answer; often the issue is work-system design: the runbook doesn't match the system; the alerts don't fire on the right thresholds; the deploy gate doesn't catch the misconfiguration. |

### Phase 5e: Deciding on action

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Am I producing a public learning artefact (FLA shape) or a binding action plan (Learning Review shape)? | These are different deliverables with different vetting requirements. Recommendations require the Learning Review's community-of-practice and academic-SME vetting; lessons learned do not. |
| Has each proposed recommendation been vetted by the relevant community of practice? | For software-business, the CoP is the engineering org plus adjacent functions (SRE, platform, security). Recommendations imposed without CoP vetting are likely to fail in implementation. |
| Will this recommendation, when implemented, add complexity that increases risk? | A frequent paradox: a recommendation that adds a verification step, a review gate, or a runbook entry may itself become a new failure surface. Has this been weighed? |
| Does the recommendation have a named owner and a deadline? | If not, it does not belong on the Safety Action Plan. Diffuse accountability ("the platform team will improve the alerting") produces no movement. |
| Does the recommendation address the system, not the individual? | "Safety is in the entire system." Recommendations targeting sharp-end behaviour without addressing the upstream conditions are likely to fail. |
| Has the recommendation been checked against work-as-done, not just work-as-imagined? | Talk to the on-call engineers who handle similar incidents. What looks reasonable in the recommendation may look impossible in production. |

### Phase 5f: Deciding when to terminate

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Is there evidence of intentional malicious action? | The threshold is high: conduct that is intentional, unjustifiable, and occurred with foreknowledge that serious harm was likely. Below this, the post-mortem continues. |
| Is litigation against an engineer or the company likely? | If yes, a confidential administrative or legal investigation is the right tool, not a post-mortem. |
| If terminating, what is the procedure? | Lead writes a memo to the responsible executive recommending administrative or legal investigation, without disclosing post-mortem details. Material evidence goes to the executive; team notes are sealed; team members do not discuss the case except with external authorities outside organisational control. |

## Trigger extensions surfaced by this source

The two seed triggers above are the cleanest fires. LFUO surfaces three additional trigger candidates that the operator may want to fold back into the task spec when revising §2a, since the seed table did not anticipate them:

- **Operator describes a near-miss that did not escalate to a customer-visible incident.** LFUO's two-inch case argues explicitly that near-misses are legitimate FLA scope. Software near-misses (a deploy caught by the canary, a runbook step that almost shipped wrong, an alert that almost didn't fire) carry the same learning potential as actual outages. The seed table currently fires only on actual incidents.
- **Operator names a "we need to write a post-mortem" question without naming the format.** The seed table assumes the operator already knows whether they want an incident-response post-mortem, a security event review, or something else. LFUO's tool-ladder is exactly the triage question that should fire when the operator is unsure which format applies.
- **Operator names a "did this incident require a Safety Action Plan?" decision after the fact.** Distinct from format selection: the question is whether the engineering capacity for remediation has been committed in writing with named owners. LFUO Part 11's Safety Action Plan discipline maps onto this question and is not currently in the seed table.

## What to Look For

### The single-cause narrative reflex

- **Signal.** The first draft of the post-mortem has a section titled "Root Cause" with one or two sentences identifying a discrete fault.
- **Diagnosis.** This is the discovery-vocabulary inheritance from the SAI / RCA paradigm LFUO names as the wrong tool for complex systems (LFUO Part 2, "The tools"; LFUO Appendix B, "A Deeper Dive into Causal Factors"). The single-cause narrative is invariably constructed by selecting one element from a network of contributing conditions and giving it the label *root* — a discovery claim the analysis cannot support.
- **Follow-up.** Rewrite the "Root Cause" section as "Conditions that combined to produce the outcome." List the conditions (technical, social, cultural, political). Make the combination explicit: *"These four conditions had to co-occur for the outage to happen; here is the sequence."*

### The counterfactual creeping into the action items

- **Signal.** Action items phrased as *"If the on-call had checked X, this would not have happened, so we will require checking X going forward."*
- **Diagnosis.** Counterfactual smuggling: the analyst constructed a hypothetical alternative path (the on-call did check X) and used the constructed alternative to justify the action item. LFUO prohibits this (LFUO Part 9, "Avoid Counterfactual Arguments"). The action item is built on a story the analysis cannot tell.
- **Follow-up.** Replace with: *"The condition was Y — the runbook did not have step Z visible at the moment the on-call needed it. We will update the runbook." Why didn't the on-call see step Z?* Drive the analysis to the upstream condition, not to the hypothetical alternative path.

### The recommendation that increases complexity

- **Signal.** The recommendation adds a verification step, a review gate, a new alert, a new runbook entry, or a new approval requirement.
- **Diagnosis.** "In many circumstances, recommendations actually interfere with learning. Often recommendations, when implemented, add complexity to the workplace, which has the paradoxical effect of increasing risk" (LFUO Part 5, "Recommendations vs Lessons Learned"). A new gate is a new failure surface. A new alert is more noise. A new runbook entry is more text the on-call will skim.
- **Follow-up.** For each proposed recommendation, ask: *what new failure mode does this introduce?* If the answer is "none we can see," look harder. If the new failure mode is comparable in cost to the one being addressed, the net effect may be zero or negative.

### The "we need more training" reflex

- **Signal.** Multiple action items in the form *"train the team on X"* or *"send the on-call to a refresher on Y."*
- **Diagnosis.** Training as the default response is the wrong answer most of the time. "When 'we just need more training' comes up… Try to dig deeper. Sometimes, that is the answer, but sometimes it's not" (LFUO Part 6, "Suggestions to FLA Facilitator for the Group Dialogue Session"). Often the issue is work-system design: the system makes the right action hard to execute correctly.
- **Follow-up.** For each training proposal, ask: *if the engineer knew the content of this training, would they have made a different decision at the time?* If no — if the conditions would still have produced the same recognition-primed first option — then training is the wrong intervention. Redesign the work surface (the alert, the runbook, the gate, the dashboard) instead.

### Hindsight contamination in the interview transcripts

- **Signal.** The engineer's account uses certainty language: *"I should have noticed the metric anomaly; I should have rolled back immediately; I should have escalated to the senior engineer."*
- **Diagnosis.** Sensemaking did not stop after the incident. The post-event coherent story has overwritten the pre-event reasoning the analysis is supposed to reconstruct (LFUO Handout C, "FLA 'Watch outs'"). The engineer's certainty is evidence of the contamination, not of the original decision.
- **Follow-up.** Interview as soon as possible after the incident. Keep the engineer focused on *what was visible to them at the time*, not *what they wished they had done differently*. When certainty language appears in the transcript, flag it as hindsight signal and probe for the pre-event reasoning.

## When to Use This Reference

Use LFUO when:

- The incident or near-miss has enough learning potential to justify a structured review beyond a five-line Slack write-up — and the engineering org needs a coherent design discipline for that review.
- The post-mortem audience extends beyond the immediate dev team (an FLA-shape learning artefact for the broader engineering org or business; a Learning-Review-shape Safety Action Plan for the C-suite).
- The incident involves distributed systems, multiple services, multiple human operators, or emergent behaviour at scale — i.e., conditions where single-cause narratives systematically mislead.
- The organisation is moving away from blame-culture incident reviews and needs the procedural commitments (signed delegation, administrative firewall, deflection-question protocol, no-counterfactual rule) that make honest accounts possible.

Use the NHS Just Culture distillation instead when the question is about the *individual* accountability decision tree (was this human error, at-risk behaviour, or reckless behaviour?) rather than the *organisational* learning machinery; the NHS guide carries the individual-decision-aid tier LFUO does not.

Use the TC 25-20 AAR distillation instead when the question is about the *immediate, local, work-unit* learning rhythm rather than the larger-scale FLA / Learning Review tier; the Army AAR carries the recurring-cadence facilitation patterns LFUO references but does not develop at depth.

For non-incident software-business questions (pricing, build-vs-buy, hiring, sprint cadence, board memos, fundraising), route to Jones, Letaw, and the OpenStax management / accounting / finance / business-law / business-ethics distillations. LFUO does not apply.

## Worked Example

A SaaS company runs a major service outage: a configuration change pushed to a feature flag system caused a cascading failure across three downstream services for 47 minutes, affecting a tier-one enterprise customer mid-quarter. The CTO is deciding what scale of post-mortem to run.

A naive post-mortem treats this as a sev-1 incident requiring a sev-1 post-mortem template, identifies the root cause as "feature-flag misconfiguration by Engineer A," lists action items including "additional training for Engineer A" and "mandatory two-reviewer approval for all flag changes going forward," and circulates the document to the customer and the board.

**Selecting the tool.** Applying the four-tool ladder, the CTO first asks whether the right instrument is an AAR, RLS, FLA, or Learning Review. "An FLA is a scalable process used to make sense of an unintended outcome and develop lessons learned for the broader organization without recommendations." The board is asking for "what are we doing to prevent this from happening again?" — which requires a Safety Action Plan, meaning a Learning Review, not an FLA. The CTO commits to a Learning Review. (Source: US Forest Service, *LFUO 2024*, Part 2, "The tools")

**Setting conditions for honest accounts.** The Delegating Official (the CTO) writes a signed delegation explicitly excluding any administrative action against Engineer A — "any type of disciplinary actions and other adverse actions such as forced retraining, removal from an incident or reduction in planned overtime." Without this commitment in writing, Engineer A's account will be the official cover story, not the actual reconstruction. The guide is direct: "It is critical to maintain a solid firewall between the FLA and any potential administrative actions." (Source: Part 3, "Administrative assurance of no punitive actions")

**Reconstructing sensemaking.** The Five Hows applied to the flag-change decision reveals that Engineer A read the UI label, recognised it as the same pattern used on six lower-traffic flags before, and acted on the recognition-primed first option. Recognition-primed decision making: people "use experience to generate a plausible option as the first one they consider." The decision was reasonable given the conditions Engineer A understood at the time. (Source: Glossary, "Recognition primed decision making")

**Networked causality.** The Lessons Learned Analysis identifies four conditions that combined to produce the outcome: (1) the flag-management UI had two settings called "rollout percentage" meaning different things on different pages, and Engineer A read one as the other; (2) the canary deploy gate did not test downstream service interactions because those services were owned by a different team; (3) the runbook for rolling back flag changes had three steps; in fact, the production system required five; (4) Engineer A's on-call shift was day six of a seven-day rotation, with the previous five days having been heavy. "The combined total of those conditions that collectively influenced the event or outcome" is the working unit of analysis. (Source: Glossary, "Networked causality") Cause is not discovered; it is constructed: "Cause isn't something investigators 'find' or 'discover'; cause is always something we create by recreating the event showing how certain anomalies led to the accident." (Source: Appendix B, "A Deeper Dive into Causal Factors")

**The Safety Action Plan.** Vetted through the platform-engineering and SRE communities of practice: (1) fix the UI label inconsistency (named owner: platform-tooling team lead; deadline: 30 days); (2) extend the canary gate to cover downstream-service interactions (named owner: SRE team lead; deadline: 60 days); (3) update the rollback runbook to match production behaviour (named owner: platform-engineering team lead; deadline: 14 days); (4) move on-call rotation from seven to five days and hire two additional engineers (named owner: CTO; deadline: hire by 90 days). "If the Delegating Official insists on having recommendations, the Learning Review process must be initiated so the recommendations can be vetted with the communities of practice and a Safety Action Plan can be developed that outlines who is responsible to carry them out and by what date." (Source: Part 5, "Recommendations vs Lessons Learned")

**Learning product.** The customer-facing post-mortem is a narrative reconstruction — the engineer's decision-by-decision experience — rather than a timeline-plus-action-items template. "Cognitive scientists have shown that humans make sense of, and remember, 'facts' by attaching them to narratives that give them context and a sensory or emotional association." (Source: Part 8, "Why FLA Storytelling?") The naive version would have shipped Engineer A onto a training course, added a mandatory review gate (a new failure surface), and presented a single-cause narrative to the board. The LFUO-applied version names the upstream conditions, commits the engineering capacity in writing, and produces a learning artefact the engineering org can actually use.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Selecting the post-mortem scale by incident severity alone.** A near-miss can deserve an FLA; an outage with little learning yield can deserve only an AAR. Severity-of-outcome is a poor selection criterion.
- **Adding binding recommendations to an FLA-shape write-up.** Recommendations require the Learning Review's CoP and academic-SME vetting; appending them to an FLA after the fact bypasses the vetting and produces unworkable action items.
- **Writing root-cause narratives for distributed-system incidents.** Multiple improbable events are the norm, not the exception; single-cause narratives systematically mislead the post-mortem reader.
- **Using counterfactual reasoning in the analysis.** *"If the on-call had done X..."* is forbidden. Drive to the upstream condition that produced the actual behaviour, not to the hypothetical alternative path.
- **Treating post-event coherent stories as pre-event clarity.** Memory rewrites the pre-event reasoning after the incident; the engineer's certainty at week three is evidence of contamination, not of original deliberation.
- **Defaulting to "more training" as the action item.** Often the issue is work-system design (UI inconsistencies, missing canary gates, outdated runbooks, unsustainable on-call rotations), and training is the wrong intervention.
- **Adding new review gates, alerts, or runbook entries as the default remediation.** New gates are new failure surfaces. New alerts are more noise. New runbook entries are more text the on-call will skim. The recommendation that increases complexity often increases risk.
- **Allowing performance-management consequences for the engineer who pushed the bad deploy.** The administrative firewall is non-negotiable; without it, engineering accounts will be cover stories rather than reconstructions.
- **Producing the post-mortem in a structured-template form when the audience needs the narrative form.** The vicarious-learning effect requires the engineer's decision-by-decision reconstruction, not a timeline-plus-action-items template.
- **Diffusing recommendation accountability across "the platform team" or "the engineering org."** Each Safety Action Plan item needs a named owner and a deadline; diffuse accountability produces no movement.
- **Adjudicating between conflicting engineering accounts of what happened.** Diversity of narrative is resilience; the analysis preserves the difference and surfaces the conditions that produced different perspectives, rather than picking which account is right.
- **Skipping the procedural commitment when the engineer involved is a senior with political stake.** The firewall applies uniformly; senior engineers default to curating their accounts under the same pressures.
- **Treating the post-mortem as an investigation rather than a learning process.** Investigation vocabulary triggers defensive accounting; sensemaking vocabulary invites honest reconstruction.
