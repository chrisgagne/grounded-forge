## Software-Business Relevance

The Open Practice Library is a *moderate-fire* source for software-business work: it does not theorise the technical-commercial intersection, but it is the corpus's densest catalogue of *named, time-boxed, multi-participant practices* that a founder, CTO, engineering manager, or product leader can pick up and run when the intersection demands a session, not a memo. The 266 practices sort by Mobius phase — Foundation (111: facilitation primitives, culture, psychological safety, technical-substrate engineering, meta-practices), Discovery (86: opportunity framing, problem-space exploration), Options (32: ideation, prioritisation, experimentation), Delivery (37: cadence, reliability/safety, feedback/learning) — and that sort is the routing surface for software-business work.

Three load-bearing observations shape the projection. First, **the engineering substrate is in Foundation, not Delivery** (Source: OPL deep reference, Part II): Continuous Integration, Continuous Delivery, GitOps, Everything-as-Code, Containers, Defence in Depth, Test Automation, and Pair Programming are tagged `methods` and `culture`, not `delivery`. The library's curatorial position is that these are habits of the team that hold across the loop, not activities of a sprint — which is the same claim Letaw and Jones make from different angles about the technical-substrate-as-precondition for any software-business decision. Second, **psychological safety and engineering quality are mutually constitutive** (Source: OPL deep reference, Part II, Sub-cluster 2): the *Blameless Postmortem* practice names "Psychological safety of all participants that promotes speaking up openly" as one of its two foundations (Source: Practice "Blameless Postmortem", "How to do it?"), and *Celebrating Failure*, *Check-ins*, *Social Contract*, and *Measuring Psychological Safety* all sit in Foundation. The library's argument: a software business with brittle safety culture cannot run a blameless postmortem, and a software business without blameless postmortems cannot learn from incidents at a rate that preserves customer trust. Third, **AI integration has been folded into the library as Foundation, not as a new phase** (Source: OPL deep reference, Part II, Sub-cluster 4): *Evals* and *Human in the loop* were added in the 2026 cohort and tagged as meta-practices that hold across the loop, not as new Delivery practices. The contrarian framing — *"Traditional tests give you certainty. Evals give you confidence"* (Source: Practice "Evals", "What is it?") and the IBM-1979 line *"A computer can never be held accountable, therefore a computer must never make a management decision"* (Source: Practice "Human in the loop", "Why do it?") — names what an AI-integrated software business owes its customers and its board.

This distillation routes from each software-business phase to the OPL practices the library itself offers. The catalogue becomes a *situation router* for the technical-commercial intersection: the practitioner names a phase, and the distillation names the practices to reach for.

## Key Concepts for Software-Business

1. <!-- concept: mobius-loop --> **The Mobius phase as software-business routing layer.** Foundation = pre-conditions that hold across any software-business decision (culture, safety, engineering substrate, AI substrate); Discovery = strategic positioning and opportunity framing; Options = experimentation, ideation, prioritisation among courses of action; Delivery = cadence, reliability, learning. The `mobiusTag` is the library's first-order signal of *when* in a software-business question a practice applies (Source: OPL deep reference, Part I, Practice count by Mobius phase).

2. <!-- concept: engineering-substrate-as-foundation --> **Engineering substrate as Foundation, not Delivery.** *Continuous Integration*, *Continuous Delivery*, *Continuous Deployment*, *GitOps* (*"If it's not in Git, it's not real"*; Source: Practice "GitOps", subtitle), *Everything-as-Code*, *Containers*, *Defence in Depth*, *Test Automation*, *Test Driven Development*, *Pair Programming*, *Mob Programming*. The technical substrate is the precondition for a software business that can ship reliably under commercial pressure. The library's curatorial claim — these are Foundation, not Delivery — is itself a software-business argument: you do not "add CI/CD when the business gets bigger"; you build the business on it.

3. <!-- concept: learning-climate --> **Psychological safety as commercial infrastructure.** *Check-ins*, *Social Contract*, *Elephant in the Room* (*Preparation & Framing → Identifying the Elephant(s) → Prioritizing & Selecting → Facilitated Discussion → Action Planning & Follow-Up*; Source: Practice "An Elephant in the Room", "How to do it?"), *Celebrating Failure*, *Measuring Psychological Safety*, *Mood Marbles*, *Niko Niko Calendar*, *Team Sentiment*. The library's position: a software business without psychological safety cannot learn from incidents, cannot run a blameless postmortem, cannot surface the failure modes that erode customer trust. Safety practices are *named, repeatable rituals*, not exhortation (Source: OPL deep reference, Part II, Sub-cluster 2).

4. <!-- concept: impact-mapping --> **Impact Mapping as the antidote to the feature shopping list.** Gojko Adzic's *Impact Mapping* (Source: Practice "Impact Mapping", "What is it?") reorients planning away from outputs ("a shopping list of features") toward outcomes (behavioural change of named stakeholders that produces the business goal). The contrarian, stated in the practice body: *"Most planning activities revolve around juggling a 'shopping list of features,' as Gojko calls them. Even though the features are delivered, often the business objective is not achieved"* (Source: Practice "Impact Mapping", "Why do it?"). The map's four columns — Goal / Actors / Impacts / Deliverables — make the chain from feature back to business goal auditable, which is the artefact a board can interrogate.

5. <!-- concept: objectives-and-key-results --> **OKRs as outcome-versus-output discipline.** *Objectives & Key Results* (Source: Practice "Objectives & Key Results (OKRs)", "What is it?") couples a qualitative Objective with measurable Key Results. Nine Good Objectives criteria: outcome focussed, inspiring, ambitious but achievable, qualitative, time bound, clear and memorable, limit to 5, owned by team, support company mission (Source: Practice "Objectives & Key Results (OKRs)", "How to do it?"). The Key Results guidance is contrarian: *"Quantitative measurement of value - Measure desired outcomes and impact, i.e. results, not tasks completed"* (Source: Practice "Objectives & Key Results (OKRs)", "How to do it?"). The library's position against output-measurement is the position a software business needs against vanity metrics in board reports.

6. <!-- concept: north-star-framework --> **North Star Framework as the single load-bearing metric.** *North Star Framework* (Source: Practice "North Star Framework", "What is it?") asks for *one* metric that captures the core value the product delivers to customers. The seven-point checklist for what a North Star Metric is (expresses value, represents vision and strategy, leading indicator, actionable, understandable, measurable, not a vanity metric) is the software-business discipline for choosing the single number a board can ask after every month (Source: Practice "North Star Framework", "Why do it?").

7. <!-- concept: design-of-experiments --> **Design of Experiments as hypothesis-before-decision.** *Design of Experiments* (Source: Practice "Design of Experiments", "What is it?") reframes "decisions" as hypotheses to be tested. Minimum experiment fields: Hypothesis / Current Condition / Target Condition / Obstacles / Pass criteria / Measures / Learning (Source: Practice "Design of Experiments", "How to do it?"). The contrarian: *"Successful experiments are not experiments that have proven our assumption as correct. Successful experiments are those that provide valid and reliable data which shows a statistically significant conclusion"* (Source: Practice "Design of Experiments", "How to do it?"). For software-business work — pricing experiments, build-vs-buy probes, partner-roadmap bets, AI-integration trials — this reframes the question from *"what should we decide?"* to *"what experiment would tell us?"*.

8. <!-- concept: disagree-and-commit --> **Disagree and Commit as the commitment move that doesn't fake consensus.** *Disagree and Commit* (Source: Practice "Disagree and Commit", "What is it?") enables a team to commit to execution without coercing agreement. The contrarian: *"There is a common misconception that collaboration eventually requires consensus; it does not. In fact consensus can come at a very high cost to the team dynamic"* (Source: Practice "Disagree and Commit", "Why do it?"). Two pre-conditions: meaningful choice for every participant, an agreed review point. For software-business work this is the move that lets a CTO and a Head of Sales commit to a roadmap they don't fully agree on without faking alignment.

9. <!-- concept: blameless-postmortem --> **Blameless Postmortem as the incident learning artefact.** *Blameless Postmortem* (Source: Practice "Blameless Postmortem", "What is it?") frames the post-incident review as system investigation rather than individual blame: *"Our job is not to point fingers at an unlucky engineer that applied a wrong configuration file, our job is to figure out why he picked the wrong one and what we personally and as an organization can do to prevent it in the future"* (Source: Practice "Blameless Postmortem", "What is it?"). Two foundations: availability of information; psychological safety. *Wheel-of-Misfortune* (role-play on past postmortems) and *Fire Drills* (people-side chaos exercises) are the follow-on training practices (Source: OPL deep reference, Part V, Sub-cluster 2).

10. <!-- concept: reliability-practices --> **Reliability practices as commercial infrastructure.** *Chaos Engineering* (introducing failures on purpose on production systems to test resilience; Source: Practice "Chaos Engineering", "What is it?"), *Canary Release* (small-user-subset exposure with rollback; Source: Practice "Canary Release", "What is it?"), *Blue Green Deployments*, *Dark Launches*, *Feature Toggles*. The library's reliability cluster is the engineering-practice complement to the financial-decision frame a software business needs when pricing reliability investment against commercial milestones.

11. <!-- concept: ai-evals --> **Evals as the AI-product reliability practice.** *Evals* (Source: Practice "Evals", "What is it?") names the discipline of measuring AI/LLM output quality: *"Evals are systematic methods for measuring the quality of AI and LLM outputs. They serve the same purpose as Test Automation — giving you confidence that your system behaves correctly — but they are fundamentally different. Traditional tests give you certainty. Evals give you confidence"* (Source: Practice "Evals", "What is it?"). Cites Karpathy's "march of nines" for confidence thresholds. For an AI-integrated software business, evals are the reliability artefact a board can ask about — the AI equivalent of unit-test coverage as a quality indicator that doesn't claim certainty.

12. <!-- concept: human-in-the-loop --> **Human in the loop as AI governance.** *Human in the loop* (Source: Practice "Human in the loop", "What is it?") distinguishes three oversight models (HITL / HOTL / HIC) and carries the IBM-1979 line: *"A computer can never be held accountable, therefore a computer must never make a management decision"* (Source: Practice "Human in the loop", "Why do it?"). For software-business decisions about where to put AI in the customer-facing or internal-management decision stack, this is the named position the library takes against silent AI delegation.

13. <!-- concept: architectural-decision-records --> **Architectural Decision Records as the technical-decision audit trail.** *Architectural Decision Records (ADR)* (Source: Practice "Architectural Decision Records (ADR)", "What is it?") — *"Have a quick reference to understand what has been done in the past; allow us to share our thinking and methods with our stakeholders; maintain open and transparent communication"* (Source: Practice "Architectural Decision Records (ADR)", "Why do it?"). The decision is not finished when the meeting ends; it is finished when the rationale is in version control. For software-business work where technical decisions have board-reporting consequences, the ADR is the artefact that survives the meeting and travels to the board paper.

14. <!-- concept: theory-of-constraints --> **Theory of Constraints applied to the engineering organisation.** *The Theory of Constraints* (Source: Practice "The Theory of Constraints", "What is it?") — optimise the constraint, not the non-constraints. The software-business application: most engineering-capacity decisions fail because they invest in non-constraints (more developers when integration is the bottleneck, more features when QA capacity caps throughput, more discovery when delivery is starved).

15. <!-- concept: principles-over-practices --> **Practices over principles, but principles over practices.** *Establish Shared Principles* (Source: Practice "Establish Shared Principles", "What is it?") — the library's most self-aware contrarian: *"Purpose, Values, and Principles are enduring but practices evolve and change over time. Blindly following practices are not encouraged"* and *"Copying just the practices of successful organizations will not get us the same result if we do not also adopt the values and principles that originated these practices"* (Source: Practice "Establish Shared Principles", "Why do it?"). For software-business adoption of agile, DevOps, or AI-native practices, the warning is structural: cargo-culting the practices without the principles reproduces the visible shape but not the value.

## Questions to Ask During Software-Business Work

### Phase 1: Strategic positioning

| Need | Question | OPL practice |
|---|---|---|
| Frame the product strategy by behavioural impact, not feature output | What behavioural change in which named stakeholders produces the business goal? What deliverables produce that change? | *Impact Mapping* (Adzic) |
| Choose the single load-bearing metric | What is the one metric that captures the core value the product delivers to customers? Does it satisfy the seven-point North Star checklist? | *North Star Framework* (Ellis) |
| Set outcome-focussed objectives | What are the qualitative Objectives, and what measurable Key Results would prove them? Are the Key Results outcomes or output counts? | *Objectives & Key Results (OKRs)* |
| Frame the business model on one page | What is the problem, target customer, UVP, solution, channels, revenue streams, cost structure, key metrics, unfair advantage? | *Lean Canvas* (Maurya) |
| Frame the customer's job, not the customer's features | What job is the customer hiring this product to do? | *Jobs to be Done* (Ulwick) |
| Test the strategic frame against failure | If this strategy fails in 12 months, what are the most likely reasons? What does success look like, and what assumptions must hold? | *Backcasting / Pre-mortem* (Klein/Kahneman); *Start At The End* |
| Classify the decision's domain | Is this decision in a Clear, Complicated, Complex, or Chaotic domain? Which response pattern applies? | *Cynefin Framework* (Snowden) |

### Phase 2: Product and engineering economics

| Need | Question | OPL practice |
|---|---|---|
| Reframe the build-vs-revenue tension as constraint optimisation | What is the binding constraint on throughput right now? Are we investing in the constraint, or in non-constraints? | *The Theory of Constraints* |
| Make the engineering substrate visible as Foundation | What CI/CD, GitOps, Everything-as-Code, and Container practices are in place? Is the engineering substrate Foundation or aspiration? | *Continuous Integration*; *Continuous Delivery*; *Continuous Deployment*; *GitOps*; *Everything-as-Code*; *Containers* |
| Measure team flow against utilisation | Where are work-items waiting? Where are they blocked? Are we limiting WIP? | *Kanban* ("Visualize workflow, Limit WIP, Manage flow, Make Process Policies Explicit"; *"Stop starting, start finishing!"*) |
| Run a hypothesis-driven engineering bet | What is the Hypothesis / Current Condition / Target Condition / Obstacles / Pass criteria / Measures / Learning for this bet? | *Design of Experiments* |
| Frame a capacity decision as quantitative experiment, not headcount add | What experiment would let us test this capacity assumption before we hire? | *Design of Experiments*; *Experiment Canvas* |
| Audit the architectural decision and its rationale | Have we written this decision as an ADR with context, decision, and consequences in version control? | *Architectural Decision Records (ADR)* |

### Phase 3: Team and capability building

| Need | Question | OPL practice |
|---|---|---|
| Establish team-level psychological safety as Foundation | Have we set up Check-ins as a routine? Do we have a Social Contract? Is the Elephant in the Room being named or absorbed? | *Check-ins*; *Social Contract*; *An Elephant in the Room* |
| Surface and measure psychological safety | Are we measuring team sentiment? Do we celebrate failure, or only success? | *Measuring Psychological Safety*; *Celebrating Failure*; *Mood Marbles*; *Niko Niko Calendar*; *Team Sentiment* |
| Distribute tacit knowledge through paired practice | Are we using Pair / Mob / Ping-Pong programming to spread tacit knowledge across the team? | *Pair Programming*; *Mob Programming*; *Ping-Pong Programming*; *Mentorship*; *Knowledge Sharing* |
| Structure 1-2-1 coaching conversations | Are we using a structured coaching frame (Goal / Reality / Options / Will)? | *GROW Model for 1-2-1 Coaching* (Whitmore) |
| Make the organisation's why explicit | Do team members know why the organisation exists? Why does this team exist? | *Start With Why* (Sinek); *Vision Statement*; *Why do we exist* |
| Establish shared principles before importing practices | Have we articulated the principles, or are we copying the visible practices of successful organisations? | *Establish Shared Principles* |

### Phase 4: Operations and process

| Need | Question | OPL practice |
|---|---|---|
| Run a cadenced delivery rhythm | Are we using the Scrum cadence (Sprint Planning / Daily Standup / Sprint Review / Sprint Retrospective)? Or a Kanban flow rhythm? | *Scrum*; *Iteration (Sprint) Planning*; *Daily Standup*; *Showcase*; *Retrospectives*; *Kanban* |
| Make decision rights explicit | For each activity, who is Responsible / Accountable / Consulted / Informed? Are decision rights documented, or implicit? | *Stakeholder RACI Map* |
| Surface dissent without faking consensus | Are we using cards or structured questions to make disagreement productive? Are we explicitly committing without requiring consensus? | *Dissent Cards* (Marquet); *Disagree and Commit* |
| Equalise voice in cross-function decisions | Have we used silent generation → small-group convergence → large-group integration to avoid HiPPO dominance? | *1-2-4-All*; *Silent Brainstorming*; *10-for-10* |
| Run retrospectives as the learning artefact | Did the last cycle produce the predicted outcome? What worked? What didn't? What is the action item? | *Retrospectives*; *4Ls Retrospective*; *Rose, Thorn, Bud*; *Plus, Minus, Interesting* |
| Run cross-function ceremonies as interactive demos, not status reads | Is the showcase interactive, with stakeholders touching the product? | *Showcase* / *Sprint Review* |

### Phase 5: Risk, reliability, compliance

| Need | Question | OPL practice |
|---|---|---|
| Run the post-incident review as system investigation | Are we investigating why the engineer picked the wrong configuration file, rather than blaming the engineer? Are both foundations (information availability, psychological safety) in place? | *Blameless Postmortem* |
| Practise incident response before incidents | Are we role-playing past postmortems to build incident-response muscle memory? | *Wheel-of-Misfortune*; *Fire Drills* |
| Test system resilience under controlled failure | Are we introducing failures on purpose to test resilience before the failure tests itself in production? | *Chaos Engineering* |
| Roll out new versions with progressive exposure | Are we using small-subset rollout with monitoring, rather than all-at-once cutover? | *Canary Release*; *Blue Green Deployments*; *Dark Launches*; *Feature Toggles* |
| Treat security as layered substrate, not perimeter | Are security controls layered across the stack, or concentrated at the perimeter? | *Defence in Depth*; *Threat Modeling*; *Security Checklist* (SafeStack) |
| Measure AI output quality with confidence-not-certainty discipline | Are we running evals across the dimensions that matter? Are multiple evals combining to build confidence, rather than relying on a single test? | *Evals* (Karpathy lineage) |
| Keep humans accountable in AI-driven decisions | Where is the human in the loop? Is this HITL, HOTL, or HIC? Where would silent AI delegation create accountability gaps? | *Human in the loop* |
| Monitor risks visibly | Are risks being surfaced and tracked, or absorbed silently? | *Risk Radar*; *Risk Management*; *ROAM Boards* |
| Maintain code-quality discipline as practice | Is code review treated as a quality activity (Inspection / Walkthrough / Short review) or as a gate to pass? | *Code Review* (McConnell, Atwood) |

### Phase 6: Stakeholder communication

| Need | Question | OPL practice |
|---|---|---|
| Open every external session with safety and acknowledgment | Are we starting with a check-in to set tone? Is there a social contract for this engagement? | *Check-ins*; *Social Contract* |
| Run interactive demos for stakeholders, not scripted ones | Are stakeholders touching the product, or watching a script? | *Showcase* / *Sprint Review* |
| Translate strategy to team work the stakeholder can trace | Does the board-paper trace from business goal through behavioural impact to team deliverable? | *Impact Mapping*; *OKRs*; *North Star Framework* |
| Publish the technical decision rationale | Are stakeholders able to read the ADR and follow the reasoning? | *Architectural Decision Records (ADR)* |
| Engage cross-team as relational, not transactional | Is the cross-team agreement framed as a relational contract (social contract → trust foundation → joint vision → guiding principles → terms → governance → consolidation) or as a transactional contract? | *Formal Relational Contract*; *Inter-Team Agreement*; *Collaborative Pathways* |
| Close stakeholder sessions with action commitments | Has each participant committed to a first step (Who / What / When / With)? | *Action steps* |

## What to Look For

**Signal: A team adopts CI/CD or DevOps practices but reliability does not improve.**
- Diagnosis: the team has imported the visible practices without the underlying principles (Source: Practice "Establish Shared Principles", "Why do it?"). The CI pipeline is in place; the small-frequent-change discipline isn't. The post-incident review meeting happens; the blameless framing isn't. The practices are shapes; the values that originated them aren't.
- Follow-up: surface the principles underneath the imported practices. *Establish Shared Principles* before the next round of practice import. Audit the existing practices against their stated `whyDo`: which ones are being executed in shape only?

**Signal: A post-incident review names a specific engineer's mistake as the cause; the recommended action is more training for that engineer.**
- Diagnosis: the review has not run blamelessly (Source: Practice "Blameless Postmortem", "What is it?"). The frame is *who* picked the wrong configuration file, not *why* the configuration was pickable wrongly. Information availability and psychological safety — the practice's two foundations (Source: Practice "Blameless Postmortem", "How to do it?") — are likely absent or compromised.
- Follow-up: restart the review with both foundations in place. If psychological safety is the problem, the *Blameless Postmortem* will not run productively until *Check-ins*, *Social Contract*, and *An Elephant in the Room* have done preparatory work. *Wheel-of-Misfortune* and *Fire Drills* can build the muscle memory the team currently lacks.

**Signal: Roadmap conversations produce long feature lists; the lists ship; the business outcomes don't move.**
- Diagnosis: the team is planning by feature shopping list rather than by impact (Source: Practice "Impact Mapping", "Why do it?"). The chain from business goal back to feature is not being made auditable; the features ship because the list is the target.
- Follow-up: run *Impact Mapping* on the next planning cycle. Goal → Actors → Impacts → Deliverables. Cut deliverables that do not trace to a named behavioural impact on a named actor that produces the goal. Couple with *North Star Framework* for the single load-bearing metric and *OKRs* for the cascade.

**Signal: An AI feature is shipped without a clear quality measurement story; the team relies on "we tested it" without a defined eval regime.**
- Diagnosis: traditional-test thinking is being applied to AI output (Source: Practice "Evals", "What is it?"). *"Traditional tests give you certainty. Evals give you confidence"*; the team has not yet adopted the confidence-not-certainty discipline that AI quality requires.
- Follow-up: name the dimensions on which the AI is being evaluated (accuracy, safety, calibration, latency, cost). Build evals for each. Treat the eval suite as the AI equivalent of CI: the regime that gives the team confidence to ship.

**Signal: A high-stakes decision is being delegated to an AI tool; no human approval gate exists.**
- Diagnosis: silent AI delegation (Source: Practice "Human in the loop", "Why do it?"). The library carries the explicit position via the IBM-1979 line: *"A computer can never be held accountable, therefore a computer must never make a management decision."* When an AI tool makes a customer-facing or board-relevant decision without a human review gate, the accountability surface vanishes.
- Follow-up: classify the decision (HITL / HOTL / HIC). For high-stakes decisions, HITL is the default — a human reviews, approves, or corrects before the action takes effect. If the decision is genuinely low-stakes enough for HOTL or HIC, document the classification so it is auditable.

**Signal: A board paper proposes a single AI-vendor lock-in based on a vendor demo; no experiment design exists.**
- Diagnosis: decision-by-vendor-demo. *Design of Experiments* (Source: Practice "Design of Experiments", "How to do it?") has not been applied; the team has not stated the Hypothesis / Current Condition / Target Condition / Obstacles / Pass criteria / Measures / Learning that would let the demo's claims be tested. The contrarian: *"Successful experiments are not experiments that have proven our assumption as correct."*
- Follow-up: design the experiment before the commitment. State the hypothesis the vendor is being chosen for, the pass criteria, the measures, the learning regime. Run *Backcasting / Pre-mortem* on the lock-in: if this fails in 12 months, what are the reasons?

**Signal: Cross-function meetings produce decisions that collapse during execution; people commit in the room and stall afterwards.**
- Diagnosis: coerced consensus or HiPPO-shaped decisions (Source: Practice "Disagree and Commit", "Why do it?"). The agreement reached in the room is compliance, not commitment. Latent dissent surfaces as execution drag.
- Follow-up: surface the dissent before commitment. *Confidence Voting* (0-5 finger; Source: Practice "Confidence Voting", "How to do it?") makes latent dissent visible. *Disagree and Commit* lets dissenters commit to execution while preserving their disagreement, on two pre-conditions: every participant has meaningful choice; there is an agreed review point. *Dissent Cards* (Marquet) institutionalises productive disagreement.

**Signal: The standup runs long, drifts into status reporting, and produces few action items.**
- Diagnosis: the standup has lost its alignment-and-blocker function (Source: Practice "Daily Standup", "How to do it?"). The library names this anti-pattern explicitly: *"Be careful it doesn't drift into a status update."*
- Follow-up: reset the standup to alignment-and-blockers. The three canonical questions (yesterday / today / blockers) keep it on track. Move status reporting to a different channel (dashboard, async update, weekly review).

## When to Use This Reference

Reach for the Open Practice Library distillation when:

- The software-business question is *how do we run the session?* — facilitation primitives, ceremonies, retros, decision-making meetings, post-incident reviews. OPL has named practices for each.
- The question involves AI productisation, AI integration, or AI governance — *Evals*, *Human in the loop*, *Design of Experiments* are the library's named positions.
- The team needs a named practice they can pick up and run with a timebox, participant list, and recommended sequence. The library is a catalogue of those; other corpus sources are theory.
- The decision involves engineering-substrate questions (CI/CD, GitOps, containers, security) that need to be made visible as Foundation, not as Delivery-phase activities.
- The post-incident review is happening and the team needs the blameless framing as procedure, not aspiration.

Reach for other corpus sources instead when:

- The question is *what theory underlies this decision?* — `openstax-principles-management` for management theory, `jones-evidence-based-sweng` for empirical findings, `letaw-handbook-sweng-methods` for engineering method discipline, `openstax-accounting-vol2` for cost-classification primitives.
- The question is *how is this decision priced?* — OPL does not carry costing frameworks; `openstax-accounting-vol2` (relevant cost, CVP, breakeven) does.
- The question is *what is the empirical evidence?* — OPL is community curation, not empirical research; reach for `jones-evidence-based-sweng` for evidence-based claims about software-engineering productivity, reliability, or estimation.
- The question is high-consequence incident review with legal or regulatory implications — OPL's *Blameless Postmortem* is the session-shape; `nhs-just-culture-guide` carries the formal decision-tree for individual-vs-system culpability; `lfuo-learning-review-guide-2024` carries the high-consequence wildland-fire equivalent.
- The question is the *framework definition* for Scrum or Kanban — reach for `scrum-guide-2020` for the canonical Scrum definition; OPL's *Scrum* practice is the practitioner gloss.

## Worked Example

A founder of a 60-engineer B2B SaaS company has shipped an AI feature that personalises customer onboarding flows. Three weeks in, two enterprise customers report that the personalisation produced regulatory-disclosure-relevant content that should not have been sent. The board wants a response by the next monthly meeting; the engineering team wants to know what to fix; the customer-success team is fielding angry calls.

The founder reaches for the OPL distillation. The situation is multi-phase. Phase 5 (incident response) is the immediate question: she needs to run a post-incident review. Phase 4 (operations) is the medium-term question: she needs to change how AI features ship. Phase 1 (strategic positioning) is the longer-term question: how does the company position AI features going forward, given this incident?

Phase 5 first. She schedules a *Blameless Postmortem* (Source: Practice "Blameless Postmortem", "What is it?") with the engineering team, the head of customer success, and the product manager. Before the meeting she runs *Check-ins* (Source: Practice "Check-ins", "What is it?") and reaffirms the *Social Contract* — the two foundations the *Blameless Postmortem* requires (Source: Practice "Blameless Postmortem", "How to do it?"). In the meeting she frames the question as *why was the configuration that allowed this output pickable, given the system as it was?* — not *who made the mistake?* The team surfaces three contributing factors: the eval suite did not test for regulatory-disclosure patterns; the human-in-the-loop review was disabled for "low-stakes" content where this content turned out to belong; the personalisation feature was shipped without a *Canary Release* rollout. Action items: extend evals to cover regulatory patterns; reinstate HITL for all customer-facing AI content; mandate canary release for AI-feature rollouts.

Phase 4 next. She runs *Impact Mapping* (Source: Practice "Impact Mapping", "What is it?") with the product and engineering leads on how AI features should ship going forward. Goal: trustworthy AI-personalised onboarding that does not produce regulatory liabilities. Actors: enterprise customers, customer-success agents, the engineering team, the compliance officer. Impacts (behavioural changes): customers see consistent disclosure language; agents can audit AI output before customer contact; engineers ship behind eval-and-HITL gates by default; the compliance officer has visibility into AI changes. Deliverables: eval suite extension, HITL reinstatement, canary-release pipeline for AI features, ADR template for AI changes.

Phase 1 third. She writes the board response. *North Star Framework* (Source: Practice "North Star Framework", "What is it?") sharpens the position: the company's North Star Metric is customer trust expressed as net retention, not feature shipping velocity. *OKRs* (Source: Practice "Objectives & Key Results (OKRs)", "What is it?") update the quarterly cascade: Objective — restore customer trust in AI personalisation; Key Results — eval coverage of regulatory patterns at 100%, HITL on all customer-facing AI content, zero regulatory-disclosure-relevant content shipped without human review. The board paper traces from incident → blameless postmortem findings → impact map → OKR cascade → North Star Metric, with an *Architectural Decision Record* (Source: Practice "Architectural Decision Records (ADR)", "What is it?") attached for the technical changes. The narrative meets the board's accountability question (what changed, who owns it, how do we know it worked) and refuses the blame frame the customer-success channel is calling for.

The OPL catalogue functioned as a router across phases: she did not synthesise a methodology from scratch; she picked up the practices the library names for each phase and ran them in sequence.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Copying practices without principles.** *Establish Shared Principles* (Source: Practice "Establish Shared Principles", "Why do it?"): *"Copying just the practices of successful organizations will not get us the same result if we do not also adopt the values and principles that originated these practices."* The cargo-cult failure mode for software businesses adopting agile, DevOps, or AI-native methodologies.
- **HiPPO-shaped technical decisions.** *1-2-4-All* (Source: Practice "1-2-4-All", "Why do it?") is the canonical antidote: silent generation → small-group convergence → large-group integration prevents the Highest Paid Person's Opinion from dominating. For software-business decisions in cross-function rooms (CTO + Head of Sales + CEO + lead engineer), the HiPPO failure mode is particularly costly because the technical and commercial constraints both need voice.
- **Consensus as decision-making requirement.** *Disagree and Commit* (Source: Practice "Disagree and Commit", "Why do it?"): *"consensus can come at a very high cost to the team dynamic."* Forcing consensus on a roadmap decision produces compliance; compliance erodes during execution as latent dissent surfaces.
- **Blaming individuals for system-shaped failures.** *Blameless Postmortem* (Source: Practice "Blameless Postmortem", "What is it?"): the engineer who picked the wrong configuration file is the proximate cause; the system that allowed the wrong configuration file to be picked is the structural cause. Failing to investigate the system reproduces the same incident under a different engineer's hands.
- **Shopping list of features as the strategic frame.** *Impact Mapping* (Source: Practice "Impact Mapping", "Why do it?"): *"Most planning activities revolve around juggling a 'shopping list of features,' as Gojko calls them. Even though the features are delivered, often the business objective is not achieved."* Common in software-business contexts where the board reads feature lists as evidence of progress.
- **Output-measurement when outcome is the question.** *OKRs* (Source: Practice "Objectives & Key Results (OKRs)", "How to do it?"): *"Quantitative measurement of value - Measure desired outcomes and impact, i.e. results, not tasks completed."* The vanity-metric failure mode for AI-feature investment, productivity metrics, and engineering-capacity reporting.
- **Treating an experiment outcome as successful because it confirmed the hypothesis.** *Design of Experiments* (Source: Practice "Design of Experiments", "How to do it?"): *"Successful experiments are not experiments that have proven our assumption as correct."* Common in product-discovery AI work where the team designs the experiment to validate the bet rather than to test it.
- **AI overconfidence in evaluation.** *Evals* (Source: Practice "Evals", "What is it?"): *"Traditional tests give you certainty. Evals give you confidence."* Treating AI output as if traditional unit-test discipline applies produces a false-certainty failure mode that the library names explicitly.
- **Silent AI delegation in high-stakes decisions.** *Human in the loop* (Source: Practice "Human in the loop", "Why do it?"): the IBM-1979 line names what is at stake. Delegating customer-facing or board-relevant decisions to an AI without human review removes the accountability surface.
- **Standup drift to status reporting.** *Daily Standup* (Source: Practice "Daily Standup", "How to do it?"): *"Be careful it doesn't drift into a status update."* The standup is for alignment and blockers; status reporting elsewhere.
- **Treating the engineering substrate as Delivery-phase add-on.** OPL's curatorial decision to put CI/CD, GitOps, Containers, and Defence in Depth in Foundation is itself a software-business position against the "we'll add DevOps when we scale" failure mode (Source: OPL deep reference, Part II, Sub-cluster 3).
- **Scripted showcases that teach stakeholders passivity.** *Showcase* (Source: Practice "Showcase", "Advice"): *"Where possible avoid scripting the showcase — give stakeholders/end users the ability to play around with the new product."* Scripted demos to a board produce theatre, not feedback.
- **Engagement without action commitments.** *Action steps* (Source: Practice "Action steps", "What is it?"): sessions that close with "we'll synthesise and circulate" lose energy. Every session closes with per-participant Who / What / When / With commitments.

## Through the pm-bounded-by-ba-role lens

The OPL catalogue contains practices whose verbs imply authority the PM-bounded-by-BA-role structurally lacks. When a leadership artefact reaches for OPL practices and assigns the PM as the runner, the lens flags the gap between the practice's authority verbs and the PM's actual decision rights.

The reweighting is partial — most OPL practices are coordination-role-appropriate (the PM can run a *Stakeholders Interview*, an *Empathy Mapping*, a *Backlog Refinement*, an *Action steps* close, an *Affinity Mapping* convergence) because the practices are facilitation primitives, not decision-rights claims. The lens fires specifically on a small subset where the artefact's framing of the PM's role exceeds the practice's actual authority surface.

**Practices the lens reads as authority-loaded when assigned to the PM:**

- **Disagree and Commit** (Source: Practice "Disagree and Commit", "What is it?"). The practice has two pre-conditions: every participant has *meaningful choice*, and there is an *agreed review point*. When leadership assigns the PM to run *Disagree and Commit* on a roadmap decision, the lens flags: the PM facilitating the session is one thing; the PM having the authority to grant the participants meaningful choice (or to commit on behalf of the team) is another. If the meaningful-choice precondition is being absorbed by the PM as coordination work (talking to each stakeholder to surface dissent the PM has no authority to resolve), the practice is shape without substance.
- **$100 Prioritisation** and the prioritisation cluster (*MoSCoW*, *RICE*, *Impact-Effort Matrix*; Source: Practice "$100 Prioritisation", "What is it?" and OPL deep reference, Part IV, Sub-cluster 2). The practices produce a prioritisation; the lens reads who has the authority to act on it. If the PM facilitates the session and produces a ranked list, but the actual priorities are set by leadership chain, the practice has produced an artefact that gets overridden upstream — coordination work the artefact didn't budget.
- **Stakeholder RACI Map** (Source: Practice "Stakeholder RACI Map", "What is it?"). Drafting the RACI is coordination work the PM can do; *enforcing* the RACI (when stakeholders default to involving the PM as the Accountable owner against the RACI's "Consulted" assignment) requires authority the role typically lacks. The lens flags artefacts that assign the PM to "own the RACI" as authority-mismatched.
- **Showcase / Sprint Review** (Source: Practice "Showcase", "What is it?"). The practice frames the showcase as interactive, stakeholder-touching the product. When leadership assigns the PM to "run the showcase" and treats it as a status-presentation slot for sales or executive audiences, the practice's design is being subverted to the artefact's framing. The PM coordinates the showcase; the PM does not have authority to refuse a scripted presentation if leadership reframes the event.
- **Architectural Decision Records (ADR)** (Source: Practice "Architectural Decision Records (ADR)", "What is it?"). The ADR is authored by the decision-makers (engineering leads, architects, CTO); the lens flags artefacts that assign the PM to "write the ADR" as a coordination handover dressed as a strategic-authority handover.

**Coordination cost the artefact creates when OPL practices are imported wholesale:**

- *Impact Mapping*, *OKRs*, and *North Star Framework* sessions create downstream coordination work for the PM (writing stories from the impacts, translating Key Results into acceptance criteria, reconciling the cascade with the existing backlog). The artefacts that recommend these practices often do not budget the translation cost.
- *Blameless Postmortem* follow-up actions land in the PM's backlog as stories. The lens reads the volume: a single postmortem can produce 5-15 follow-up action items, each requiring story-writing, refinement, and prioritisation against existing commitments. The artefact that ran the postmortem rarely names this as work the PM will absorb.
- *Establish Shared Principles* sessions produce principle statements that the PM is then expected to use as a gate against stakeholder requests. The gate-keeping requires authority; the artefact often does not provide it.

The recede is also informative: most facilitation primitives (*Check-ins*, *Social Contract*, *Affinity Mapping*, *1-2-4-All*, *Dot Voting*, *Action steps*, *Empathy Mapping*) are coordination-role-appropriate and the lens does not flag them. The PM-bounded-by-BA-role can run these practices without the authority-mismatch problem; the OPL catalogue is, in that sense, friendly to the role's actual decision-rights envelope across the majority of its 266 practices. The lens fires only at the subset where leadership rhetoric about the PM's "product authority" reaches for an OPL practice whose execution requires the authority the title implies but the role does not carry.

Jones argues that most software-engineering "theories" are folklore and that decisions should be evidence-based; OPL's *Design of Experiments* is the practice that operationalises evidence-based-decision discipline. Use Jones for the case that practitioner intuitions are unreliable; use OPL for the structured practice that puts experimentation into the team's hands.

- **Letaw, Handbook of Software Engineering Methods** (`letaw-handbook-sweng-methods-software-business.md`): Letaw provides the academic method-discipline frame (RACI, triple-constraint, microservices boundary case, Tuckman team stages); OPL provides the facilitator-driven practitioner equivalents (*Stakeholder RACI Map*, *The Theory of Constraints*, *Iteration (Sprint) Planning*, *Group Launch Facilitation*). Use Letaw for the textbook treatment; use OPL for the field-ready session shape.

- **The Scrum Guide 2020** (`scrum-guide-2020-software-business.md`): the Scrum Guide is the canonical framework definition; OPL is the practitioner facilitation toolkit. The two diverge on the count of Scrum events (OPL counts five with the Sprint included; the Scrum Guide treats the Sprint as a container with four nested events; Source: OPL deep reference, Part V, Sub-cluster 1). For framework definition use the Scrum Guide; for facilitation depth use OPL.

- **Approach Perfect Field Guide to Scrum Events** (`approach-perfect-field-guide-scrum-events-software-business.md`): the Field Guide is per-event facilitation depth with worked sample agendas; OPL's individual Scrum-event practices are short single-page recipes. - **Open Kanban** (`open-kanban-software-business.md`): OPL's *Kanban* and *Kanban Picture* practices are the facilitation glosses; Open Kanban is the deeper treatment of flow primitives, WIP limits, and cumulative-flow diagnostics. For session-shape use OPL; for the flow-economic argument against utilisation use Open Kanban.

- **NHS Just Culture Guide** (`nhs-just-culture-guide-software-business.md`): OPL's *Blameless Postmortem* is the session structure; the NHS Just Culture Guide is the formal decision-tree for whether an individual or the system carries the corrective-action locus. For high-consequence software-business incidents (regulatory, customer-safety, security-breach) the two pair: NHS for the decision-tree, OPL for the session.

- **TC 25-20 Army AAR** (`tc-25-20-army-aar-software-business.md`): TC 25-20 is the structured AAR protocol from the wildland-fire-adjacent military tradition; OPL's *Retrospectives* cluster (4Ls, Rose Thorn Bud, Plus Minus Interesting, Realtime Retrospective) is the team-facilitation toolkit equivalent. Use OPL for routine retrospectives; reach for TC 25-20 when the event is high-consequence and the protocol needs the more formal AAR shape.

- **LFUO Learning Review Guide 2024** (`lfuo-learning-review-guide-2024-software-business.md`): LFUO is the high-consequence wildland-fire learning protocol; OPL's *Blameless Postmortem* is the lower-stakes general-purpose equivalent. - **OpenStax Principles of Management** (`openstax-principles-management-software-business.md`): OpenStax carries the management theory (organisational structures, motivation theory, Tuckman, Hofstede); OPL carries the practitioner toolkit that operationalises it (*Stakeholder Map*, *Stakeholder RACI Map*, *GROW Coaching*, *Group Launch Facilitation*). - **OpenStax Organizational Behavior** (`openstax-organizational-behavior-software-business.md`): OpenStax names groupthink, escalation of commitment, and conflict-type as phenomena; OPL provides practices that are *designed against* them (*1-2-4-All* against HiPPO; *Backcasting / Pre-mortem* against escalation; *Dissent Cards* against groupthink). Use OpenStax to diagnose the group-dynamics pathology; use OPL to pick the practice that counters it.

- **OpenStax Business Ethics** (`openstax-business-ethics-software-business.md`): OpenStax frames stakeholder theory at the normative level (Donaldson-Preston, Mitchell-Agle-Wood salience, Freeman); OPL provides the operational stakeholder practices (*Stakeholder Map*, *Stakeholder Mapping (Mobius Outcome Delivery)*, *Stakeholders Interview*, *Stakeholder RACI Map*). Use OpenStax to argue who has moral standing; use OPL to bring those stakeholders into the work.

- **OpenStax Accounting Vol 2** (`openstax-accounting-vol2-software-business.md`): OpenStax provides cost-classification, relevant-cost, breakeven, and Theory-of-Constraints primitives; OPL provides the facilitation practices that surround the costed decision (*Design of Experiments* for pricing experiments, *Impact-Effort Prioritisation Matrix* for investment-vs-return sorting, *The Theory of Constraints* practice as the session-applicable gloss of Goldratt). Reach for OpenStax for the costing primitive; reach for OPL for the session that produces the input.

**Named limits of the source.** The OPL is a community-curated catalogue, not a research-findings text: it does not present empirical evidence for the effectiveness of any practice, and the library's cross-practice coverage is dictated by community contribution rather than systematic coverage of any framework. The library does not theorise the software-business decision-making intersection; this distillation adds that routing layer, which is distillation-level synthesis, not the library's own claim. Anti-pattern content in this distillation derives from explicit OPL practice-body caution statements (practice-level "Be careful...," "Advice" sections, and "What can go wrong" framings), not from external critique; the library does not characterise itself as a source of anti-patterns at the catalogue level.
