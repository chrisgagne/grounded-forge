# OpenStax Business Ethics, Software-Business Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2018). *Business Ethics*. Rice University. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. https://openstax.org/details/books/business-ethics. Senior contributing authors Stephen M. Byars and Kurt Stanberry; seven contributing authors.

## Software-Business Relevance

OpenStax's *Business Ethics* is the corpus's only substantive treatment of the **normative-stakeholder argument** — the claim that stakeholders are ends in themselves, not means to financial returns (Donaldson-Preston; Ch 3.2). For software-business work this matters in three places where pure engineering or pure commercial framings systematically miss the right question: AI-integration choices that externalise cost onto users, data subjects, or labour markets (Phase 1 strategic positioning, Phase 5 AI-deployment ethics); compliance and data-protection decisions where the legal minimum sits below the moral minimum the firm's stakeholders are entitled to (Phase 5); and stakeholder-communication patterns where the audience set extends beyond shareholders and customers into employees, regulators, communities, and "diffused publics" tracking the firm (Phase 6 board, customer, regulator messaging).

The book brings four load-bearing tools to the software-business intersection: (1) the **moral minimum** versus **ethical minimum** versus **ethical maximum** spectrum (Ch 3.1, Ch 4.1) for pricing compliance, security, and reliability investment as moral-and-commercial decisions rather than engineering virtues; (2) the **four normative theories** (utilitarian, Kantian/deontological, virtue, Rawlsian justice as fairness; Ch 1.1, Ch 2) to disambiguate AI-deployment and data-handling choices that resist a single ethical lens; (3) the **Grunig-Hunt linkage model** (enabling, normative, functional, diffused stakeholders) plus the **four publics** (nonpublic, latent, aware, active; Ch 3.2) for sequencing board, regulator, customer, and community communication around incidents, breaches, and AI launches; (4) the **employee-rights framework** (Ch 6) and **employee-obligation framework** (Ch 7) — duty of loyalty, confidentiality, non-compete reasonableness, IP, whistleblowing — that anchors hiring, contractor classification, IP protection, and ethical disclosure for software firms specifically.

The book's normative position — that ethical conduct generates *goodwill* (reputation, brand, employee commitment, customer loyalty; Ch 1.2) which compounds into long-term profitability — translates directly to software-business contexts where reputation, brand, and trust are load-bearing assets and where a single privacy breach, AI-ethics scandal, or employment-practice exposure can erode goodwill faster than it was built. The lead author's Appendix C credo prefers deontology over utilitarianism in business: the means ennoble the ends. For an AI-integration decision or a security-investment debate, this reframes the question from "what return does this generate?" to "what duties do we owe the stakeholders this decision touches?"

## Key Concepts for Software-Business

1. <!-- concept: normative-theories --> **Three normative theories applied to software-business decisions.** *Utilitarianism* (greatest good for the greatest number — useful for product-portfolio and pricing choices where the consequences distribute across many users), *deontology* (Kantian — useful for privacy, data-handling, and AI-deployment choices where duties to data subjects bind independent of consequences), and *virtue ethics* (Aristotelian phronesis — useful for engineering-culture and engineer-character choices where reputation and brand depend on what kind of firm the team is becoming). The book argues all three converge on a single ethical standard. (Source: *Business Ethics*, Ch 1.1, "Ends, Means, and Character in Business"; Ch 2; App C, "The Nature of Business Ethics")

2. <!-- concept: moral-minimum-vs-maximum --> **Moral minimum, ethical minimum, ethical maximum.** *Moral minimum* honours obligations to all stakeholders (not just shareholders); *ethical minimum* is the least the firm can do while claiming ethical positioning; *ethical maximum* is the strongest stakeholder-honouring action available. The Samsung washer recall (~3 million units refunded plus free pickup) is the paradigmatic ethical-maximum response. Applied to software work, the maximum-versus-minimum framing prices privacy, security, reliability, and accessibility investments against the goodwill they protect rather than the legal exposure they remove. (Source: Ch 3.1, "Ethical Responsibilities Often Extend Beyond Legal Requirements"; Ch 4.1, "The Two Sides of the Corporate Responsibility Debate")

3. <!-- concept: stakeholder-theory --> **Donaldson-Preston: descriptive / instrumental / normative stakeholder approaches.** *Descriptive* maps stakeholders; *instrumental* manages them for the bottom line; *normative* treats them as ends in themselves with "intrinsic value". The text endorses normative as foundational. For software-business choices around AI integration, data monetisation, and user attention, this distinguishes ethically defensible product design from extractive design that treats users as means to engagement metrics. (Source: Ch 3.2, "The Ethical Basis of Stakeholders' Claims")

4. <!-- concept: grunig-hunt-linkage --> **Grunig-Hunt linkage model and four publics.** Four stakeholder categories: *enabling* (regulators, board, stockholders — they permit the firm to function), *normative* (peers, competitors — they shape industry norms), *functional* (suppliers, employees, customers, distributors — input and output flows), *diffused* (NGOs, voters, media — less direct relationships, potential for meaningful impact). Four publics by awareness and capacity: *nonpublic* (no problem), *latent* (unrecognised problem), *aware* (recognised), *active* (organised). The Tylenol crisis (Johnson & Johnson, ~30 million bottles recalled) is the worked example of enlightened communication across publics; Volkswagen's emissions cover-up is the failure mode. For software-business communication (board memo, breach disclosure, AI launch announcement), the model sequences the audience set and frames each one's appropriate channel. (Source: Ch 3.2, "Defining Stakeholder Categories"; Ch 3.2, "The Chicago Tylenol Murders")

5. <!-- concept: stakeholder-prioritisation --> **Power × interest × exigency for stakeholder prioritisation.** The book's prioritisation framework combines a power × interest matrix (high-power-high-interest = key stakeholder; low-power-high-interest = communicate/inform but priority is conditional) with *exigency* — the urgency of a claim. Exigency promotes lower-priority stakeholders when their claim becomes time-critical (a brewing regulatory inquiry, a vulnerability about to be disclosed, an employee group going active). For software-business work, this is the framework for sequencing a board memo, customer disclosure, regulator notification, and employee comms around a security incident or AI controversy. (Source: Ch 3.3, "Stakeholder Prioritization")

6. <!-- concept: corporate-social-responsibility --> **The social-contract argument and CSR.** The corporation enters a *social contract* with society: in return for the right to incorporate, the firm owes the society that permits it to exist (Ch 1.2, Ch 4.1). *Corporate social responsibility* is the practice of viewing the firm "within a broader context, as a member of society with certain implicit social obligations and environmental responsibilities". For software firms shipping AI capability, this argument grounds the duty to consider externalised costs (consumer data exposure, labour-market displacement, training-data sourcing) as part of the deployment decision, not as a post-hoc CSR communications matter. (Source: Ch 1.2, "A Brief Introduction to Corporate Social Responsibility"; Ch 4.1)

7. <!-- concept: triple-bottom-line --> **Triple bottom line (Elkington).** *People, planet, profit* — three measures of the bottom line. For software-business work, the framing forces the firm to surface externalised social and environmental costs that the financial bottom line alone hides: cloud-infrastructure carbon footprint, data-centre water use, gig-economy labour-market effects, attention-economy externalities on users. *Greenwashing* — "superficial CSR efforts that merely cover up systemic ethics problems for the sake of public relations" (Ch 3.4) — is the failure mode when the firm publishes sustainability or ethics statements without underlying practice change. (Source: Ch 3.4, "People, Planet, Profit: The Triple Bottom Line"; Ch 3.4, "CSR as Public Relations Tool")

8. <!-- concept: workplace-privacy --> **Workplace privacy and electronic monitoring.** The Electronic Communications Privacy Act of 1986 (ECPA) regulates monitoring with two key exceptions: the *business purpose exception* (employer may monitor on the basis of legitimate business purposes) and the *consent exception* (employer may monitor with employee consent). A 2010 American Management Association survey: 48% of employers used video monitoring, 67% monitored internet use, 30% had terminated an employee based on electronic monitoring. *Ontario v. Quon* (2010) held constitutional limits on unreasonable searches by public employers as "minimal given a work-related purpose". For software firms deploying productivity monitoring, code-keystroke instrumentation, or AI-based performance analytics on engineers, this is the legal-and-ethical baseline. (Source: Ch 6.4, "Legal and Ethical Aspects of Electronic Monitoring")

9. <!-- concept: employee-duty-of-loyalty --> **Employee duty of loyalty, confidentiality, non-competes.** *Duty of loyalty* (Ch 7.1) requires the employee to refrain from acting against the employer's interest; rises to *fiduciary duty* at higher levels of trust. *Duty of confidentiality* covers trade secrets, IP, customer data, and employee records, protected by NDA where contractual. *Non-compete agreements* limit competition during and after employment; ~20% of US private-sector workforce is covered, including one in six earning under $40,000. California (2017) voids most post-employment non-competes. The Jimmy John's case (NY AG forced the sandwich chain to drop a clause prohibiting employees from working at any "submarine, hero-type, deli-style, pita, or wrapped or rolled sandwiches" business within two miles of any of 2,500+ stores) illustrates the weaponisation of non-competes against low-wage workers. For software firms with engineering, AI/ML, and product talent, this is the framework for reasonable IP protection versus over-restrictive enforcement. (Source: Ch 7.1, "A Duty of Loyalty"; Ch 7.1, "Confidentiality"; Ch 7.1, "Non-Compete Agreements")

10. <!-- concept: insider-trading-and-fcpa --> **Insider trading, bribery, and the FCPA.** *Insider trading* (Securities Exchange Act §10(b)5) covers officers, directors, employees, and "temporary insiders" (bankers, brokers, attorneys, accountants, journalists). For software firms with pre-announcement product, M&A, or financial information, the framework binds engineers and product staff in addition to executives. The *Foreign Corrupt Practices Act* (1977; amended 1988, 1998) prohibits bribery of foreign officials extra-territorially under the nationality principle; the 1997 OECD Anti-Bribery Convention has 43 signatories. Examples: BHP Billiton and GlaxoSmithKline each paid $25 million for buying foreign officials Olympic tickets; Telia paid $965 million for FCPA violations in Uzbekistan. For software firms operating in international markets — sales, partnership development, public-sector deals — the framework forbids the cultural-relativism defence. (Source: Ch 7.4, "Insider Trading"; Ch 7.4, "Bribery and the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act")

11. <!-- concept: whistleblowing --> **Whistleblowing framework and protection.** Whistleblowing is "the act of reporting an employer to a governmental entity for violating the law". The text's ethical guidance: appropriate motive (compliance or victim protection, not revenge); try internal channels first; have hard evidence; the violation should be serious; whistleblowing should have "some likelihood of stopping the wrongful act". The False Claims Act (1863) qui tam framework rewards relators 15-30% of recovery (notable awards: Bradley Birkenfeld / UBS $104 million from the IRS; ten Pfizer whistleblowers $102 million combined). The Sherron Watkins / Enron and Sallie Krawcheck / Merrill Lynch cases illustrate the career cost even of ethical and protected disclosure. For software firms, the framework anchors policy around security-vulnerability disclosure, AI-safety dissent, and product-ethics concerns from engineering and research staff. (Source: Ch 7.5, "Whistleblowing: Risks and Rewards")

12. <!-- concept: ada-accommodation --> **Employee accommodation and disability rights.** The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA, 1990) requires *reasonable accommodation* unless it imposes *undue hardship* — "significant difficulty or expense". The Verizon EEOC settlement ($20 million, 2011) — for an inflexible "no-fault" attendance policy that disciplined or fired employees for absences caused by their disabilities — was the largest single disability-discrimination settlement in agency history. For software firms designing return-to-office policies, productivity tracking, and performance management, the framework requires accommodation analysis even when administrative simplicity argues for uniformity. (Source: Ch 8.2, "Protections for People with Disabilities")

13. <!-- concept: pay-equity --> **Pay equity, pay secrecy bans, the gender gap.** The Equal Pay Act (1963) requires equal pay for the *same* job, not equivalent. EEOC 2011-2015 data: women in senior-level roles earned ~$600,000 vs men's $800,000 — a 35% wage gap. Ten US states (CA, CO, IL, LA, ME, MI, MN, NH, NJ, VT) have banned *pay secrecy*. The 2012 NLRB and federal court decisions made most pay-secrecy policies unenforceable under the National Labor Relations Act of 1935 — protections cover union and non-union employees and extend to social-media discussions of pay. Eight states ban employers from asking applicants their salary history (addressing how prior pay perpetuates the gap). For software firms with structurally male-skewed engineering teams and large discretionary pay components (equity, bonuses, retention grants), the framework is the compliance-and-ethics floor for compensation review. (Source: Ch 6.2, "Unfair Wages: The Gender Pay Gap"; Ch 7.5, "Limiting Pay Secrecy")

14. <!-- concept: contractor-classification --> **Contractor classification and the Microsoft case.** Microsoft saved on benefits and payroll taxes by classifying many workers as contractors but lost a 2000 settlement of $97 million for misclassification. Today Microsoft has ~110,000 employees with 75% temporary or contract workers but requires staffing companies to provide benefits. The text takes a strong position on internships: many interns "clearly function as a source of unpaid labor" and ethical boundaries are crossed even when students consent. For software firms with hybrid contractor / agency / employee structures, the framework prices the legal-and-ethical exposure of classification choices. (Source: Ch 10.3, "The Access Economy and Online Platforms"; Ch 10.3, "The Gig Economy")

15. <!-- concept: ai-ethics --> **AI ethics and automation as social-contract question.** A McKinsey Global Institute study (cited in Ch 10.4): more than 800 million jobs (20% of global workforce) could be lost to robotics by 2030; 39-73 million in the US. The text raises governance questions explicitly — should companies be required to inform customers when they are interacting with AI rather than humans? The EU has agreed on the need for legislation regulating robots, including ethical frameworks and liability for AI actions. For software firms shipping AI capability, the framework grounds the deployment decision in the social-contract argument (the firm owes the society that permits incorporation) rather than treating AI as a pure efficiency move. (Source: Ch 10.4, "Robotics and Automation in the Workplace"; Ch 10.4, "Artificial Intelligence")

## Questions to Ask During Software-Business Work

### Phase 1: Strategic positioning (pricing, market entry, build-vs-buy, portfolio, AI integration)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Frame the AI-integration choice ethically | What duties do we owe the stakeholders this AI capability touches — users, data subjects, displaced workers, affected communities? Apply the normative-stakeholder test (Donaldson-Preston): are we treating any group merely as means to engagement, training data, or labour cost reduction? |
| Test for externalised costs in AI launches | Whose costs are not on our P&L? Training-data labour (annotation workforce), user attention as raw material, displaced worker transitions, energy and water for compute? The triple-bottom-line frame (people, planet, profit) forces explicit costing. |
| Apply the social-contract test to market-entry decisions | Society permits the firm to incorporate; what does the firm owe in this jurisdiction? Are we adapting product, pricing, or compliance posture to the lowest-standards jurisdiction we can find? |
| Surface the single-standard rule for international expansion | Would we apply this same data-handling, employment-practice, and tax position in our home market? If not, the cultural-relativism defence is operating; the FCPA / OECD framework explicitly rejects it. |
| Test build-vs-buy decisions for vendor-ethics exposure | Does the buy option inherit the vendor's data-handling, labour, or supply-chain ethics? Vendor selection becomes a stakeholder-engagement question, not a procurement question, when the vendor's conduct attaches to the firm's reputation. |

### Phase 2: Product and engineering economics (technical-debt servicing, capacity, reliability-as-business)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Frame reliability and security investment as moral-minimum questions | Beyond the legal requirement, what level of reliability and security would honour the obligations the firm has to users, data subjects, and downstream stakeholders? The moral-minimum line often exceeds the legal-minimum. |
| Test technical-debt-as-virtue claims | Is the debt-pay-down argument being framed as "engineering virtue" disconnected from stakeholder welfare? Customers paying for reliability they don't get, users absorbing latency or downtime: surface the stakeholder cost of un-serviced debt. |
| Avoid utilitarian-only reliability budgeting | A pure utilitarian calculation (expected-loss × probability against investment cost) may price low-probability high-stakeholder-impact failures below their moral cost. The deontological check: are we exposing any stakeholder group to harm we would not accept if we shared their position? |
| Frame the speed-vs-quality tension via the long-term-goodwill argument | Quarterly-feature pressure systematically undervalues goodwill (reputation, brand, customer loyalty, employee commitment). What is the multi-year reputation effect of shipping at this quality? |
| Distinguish reliability from compliance theatre | Are we instrumenting and reporting metrics that satisfy compliance without changing user-experienced reliability? The compliance-as-checkbox failure mode (Ch 5.4) applies here. |

### Phase 3: Team and capability building (hiring, structure, levelling, culture)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Pay-equity review for engineering compensation | Across engineering levels, geographies, and equity grants, are women, minorities, and other under-represented groups within the firm experiencing wage gaps comparable to or exceeding industry baseline (35% in senior roles per EEOC 2011-2015)? Pay-secrecy bans in 10 US states require transparency. |
| Salary-history bans applied to engineering hiring | In jurisdictions banning salary-history asks, are we anchoring offers on prior pay (which propagates the gap)? Or on market range plus role-and-skill calibration? |
| Non-compete reasonableness for engineering, AI/ML, product talent | Are post-employment non-competes proportionate to legitimate IP protection? Or are they over-restrictive (the Jimmy John's pattern applied to engineers)? California voids most post-employment non-competes; reasonableness varies by jurisdiction. |
| Contractor / staff-aug / employee classification | Are workers performing functions that meet employment criteria being classified as contractors to avoid benefits and payroll-tax exposure (the Microsoft pattern)? The 30% benefits saving is "often the margin between middle class and falling below" (Ch 8.4). |
| Accommodation for engineering workflow | Are return-to-office, productivity-monitoring, and performance-management policies accommodating disability, caregiving, and other protected categories? Or does administrative simplicity (the Verizon $20 million no-fault pattern) override accommodation analysis? |
| Tone at the top for engineering ethics | Is leadership modelling the engineering-ethics conduct the firm publishes? Or is the published code of conduct in tension with senior-leader behaviour? Compliance training without ethical leadership produces hollow compliance (Ch 5.4). |

### Phase 4: Operations and process (delivery cadence, governance, decision rights, change management)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Test electronic-monitoring policies against the ECPA framework | Does the monitoring rest on the business-purpose exception, the consent exception, or both? Are employees informed in writing of the scope and use? Are we using monitoring data only for the stated business purpose, or are we mission-creeping into performance discipline that was not disclosed? |
| Apply the harm-principle test to productivity monitoring | Mill's harm principle: power may be exercised against the individual's will only to prevent harm to others. Productivity monitoring justified by "the employee's own good" or paternalism fails this test. (Source: Ch 2.4) |
| Whistleblowing channel design for security and AI-safety dissent | Are internal channels designed to receive substantive engineering or research dissent without retaliation? The Sherron Watkins / Sallie Krawcheck pattern (Ch 7.5) — even protected ethical disclosure carrying career cost — is the failure mode to design against. |
| Distinguish good-faith dissent from criticism subject to discipline | The Adrian Duane / IXL Learning case (Ch 7.5): NLRB judge held a Glassdoor post was not "concerted activity" under NLRA Section 7. At-will employees do not have First-Amendment protection against employer discipline for social-media criticism; engineering and research staff should be advised of the line. |

### Phase 5: Risk, reliability, compliance (incidents, security, legal, regulatory, ethics)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply the moral-minimum test to data-protection incidents | Beyond the legal disclosure requirement (state breach-notification laws, GDPR, sectoral rules), what response honours the obligations the firm has to data subjects? The ethical-maximum frame (the Samsung washer pattern — full refunds and free pickup for all ~3 million units) applies here: stakeholder-honouring response over minimal-compliance response. |
| Sequence disclosure across the four publics | Are data subjects, regulators, partners, and employees moving from latent to aware to active? The Tylenol playbook (proactive, comprehensive disclosure to the active public, including those not yet aware) is more effective long-run than the Volkswagen playbook (cover-up exposed by external actors). |
| Test against the FCPA / OECD framework for international compliance | For sales, partnership development, and public-sector deals abroad, is any payment, gift, or facilitation request consistent with the FCPA (and parallel UK Bribery Act 2010, Canada's Corruption of Foreign Officials Act 1999, OECD Convention)? Specifically, the cultural-relativism defence ("this is how business is done here") is rejected: BHP Billiton, GlaxoSmithKline, and Telia paid heavily for variants of it. |
| Apply the deontological lens to data-handling and AI-deployment choices | Could the maxim "deploy this AI capability with these data practices" be willed universal? Are we treating any user, data subject, or affected community merely as means? The Kantian check (Ch 2.5) is the discipline against pure consequence calculation. |
| Surface IP and trade-secret protection without weaponising non-competes | Where is reasonable IP protection (NDAs, narrow non-solicitation, defined trade-secret scope) and where does over-restriction become abusive (Jimmy John's pattern)? The framework supports legitimate protection while rejecting over-reach. |
| Test AI-ethics and externalised-cost questions | Does the AI capability externalise costs onto users, data subjects, displaced workers, or affected communities? The book's framing of automation as a social-contract issue (Ch 10.4) and the triple-bottom-line frame (Ch 3.4) make these costs visible. |
| Apply ethical-maximum framing to wellness, monitoring, and HR-data practices | Hiltzik's caution (Ch 9.4): "voluntary wellness programs also give employers a window into their workers' health profiles that is otherwise an illegal invasion of their privacy". For software firms with detailed engineering-performance data, the analogous question is what visibility into engineer behaviour is being collected and how it is used. |

### Phase 6: Stakeholder communication (board reporting, exec alignment, investor relations, customer messaging)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Map the linkage categories before drafting a board paper | Who are the *enabling* stakeholders (board, regulators, key investors), the *normative* (industry peers, standard-setters), the *functional* (customers, partners, employees), and the *diffused* (NGOs, media, advocacy organisations)? Each category needs a different communication channel and depth. |
| Sequence communication across the four publics | For this issue, who is *nonpublic* (no problem), *latent* (problem unrecognised), *aware* (recognised), or *active* (organised)? Each requires a different message and timing. The latent-public window is when proactive communication can guide the framing; once the public is active, the firm is reacting. |
| Apply Mitchell-Agle-Wood-style power × interest × exigency for board / regulator / customer prioritisation | High-power-high-interest stakeholders dominate board memos by default; the moral-minimum test asks whether low-power-high-interest stakeholders (data subjects, affected communities, engineering staff carrying the work) are surfaced. Exigency promotes lower-priority stakeholders when their claim becomes time-critical. (See note below: the book uses Grunig-Hunt linkage plus a power × interest matrix with exigency; the Mitchell-Agle-Wood framing the operator named is in the same family and the book's framework is the canonical citation here.) |
| Frame the board memo around the normative-stakeholder argument when AI ethics is in scope | If the question is whether to ship an AI capability, deploy a monitoring system, or expand a data practice, frame the recommendation around duties to stakeholders rather than pure expected return. The deontological lens is the discipline (App C, Ch 2.5). |
| Avoid greenwashing in CSR / sustainability / AI-ethics communications | Are the firm's published ethics or sustainability claims matched by underlying practice change? Coca-Cola's water-footprint case (Ch 3.4) and the Volkswagen cover-up (Ch 3.2) are the patterns to design against. |
| Honour the MITRE five-step pattern for stakeholder consultation | Identify stakeholders (and reidentify periodically); gather data on concerns; analyse; present results to decision-makers; inform stakeholders that their concerns were considered. For board, customer, and regulator communication around incidents, the closing-the-loop step is where engagement integrity is judged. |
| Apply the Tylenol playbook to product-safety and AI-safety incidents | Johnson & Johnson's recall of ~30 million bottles plus tamper-proof-packaging redesign is the worked example of an enlightened response. The pattern: proactive disclosure beyond the legal minimum, substantive remediation, transparent communication across all four publics. |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: An AI-deployment decision is being framed as engineering-velocity-and-revenue only.** Signal: the conversation centres on shipping speed, competitive positioning, and expected return; the stakeholders affected by the AI capability (users, data subjects, displaced workers, affected communities) are absent from the framing. Diagnosis (in source vocabulary): instrumental stakeholder reasoning without the normative test (Donaldson-Preston); externalised costs not on the firm's P&L. Follow-up: surface the normative-stakeholder question and the triple-bottom-line frame; reframe the decision around duties owed rather than expected return.
- **Pattern: A security or privacy incident is being scoped to the legally-required disclosure only.** Signal: the response plan satisfies the breach-notification minimum (jurisdiction by jurisdiction) but does not reach the affected user community proactively or substantively. Diagnosis: ethical-minimum response chosen with the rationale "the law doesn't require more". Follow-up: model the multi-year goodwill effect; apply the ethical-maximum benchmark (the Samsung washer recall; the Tylenol response); surface what stakeholder welfare would require.
- **Pattern: A non-compete is being applied to engineering or product staff disproportionately to legitimate IP protection.** Signal: post-employment non-competes covering wide geographic and industry scope; clauses surviving for multi-year periods; restrictions covering work unrelated to actual trade-secret exposure. Diagnosis: the Jimmy John's pattern applied to white-collar workers; California's 2017 ban is the legal-jurisdiction signal; narrow NDAs, defined trade-secret scope, and reasonable non-solicitation are the legitimate alternatives. Follow-up: review against jurisdiction; apply the moral-minimum test; consider the chilling effect on industry mobility.
- **Pattern: Productivity monitoring or AI-based performance analytics is being deployed without ECPA-style consent framing.** Signal: keystroke monitoring, code-instrumentation, or AI-derived productivity scoring rolled out without written disclosure; the business purpose is asserted as obvious; consent is implied through employment rather than affirmatively obtained. Diagnosis: the ECPA business-purpose exception is being stretched beyond its scope (Ch 6.4); the harm-principle test (Mill, Ch 2.4) is not being applied. Follow-up: obtain affirmative consent or document the business-purpose limit; restrict use of the data to the stated purpose.
- **Pattern: Worker classification is being designed for benefits-and-tax avoidance rather than work-actual-shape.** Signal: long-tenured contractors performing core engineering, product, or operations roles; workers managed and equipped like employees but classified as 1099. Diagnosis: the Microsoft pattern; the IRS classification criteria are not the structuring logic. Follow-up: review against the IRS criteria; price the $97 million settlement risk plus the moral cost (the 30% benefits saving is "the margin between middle class and below"; Ch 8.4).
- **Pattern: Pay equity is treated as compliance with the Equal Pay Act (1963) only.** Signal: the firm satisfies "equal pay for the same job" but discretionary components (bonuses, equity grants, retention awards) drive a measurable gap; salary history is anchoring offers; pay-secrecy norms are discouraging transparency. Diagnosis: the Equal Pay Act covers same-job-equal-pay but not equivalent work; states are extending protection (Iceland's 2018 law; New Jersey's Diane B. Allen Equal Pay Act). Follow-up: review against discretionary-comp gaps; apply pay-secrecy bans in covered jurisdictions; remove salary-history anchoring.
- **Pattern: An ADA accommodation request is being denied for administrative simplicity.** Signal: inflexible attendance, return-to-office, or productivity-monitoring policies; one-size-fits-all engineering-performance metrics. Diagnosis: the Verizon $20 million pattern — administrative simplicity over accommodation analysis. Follow-up: evaluate the request against the ADA *reasonable accommodation* / *undue hardship* line; document the analysis; modify the policy where accommodation is reasonable.
- **Pattern: International market entry is being framed around local-norm compliance only.** Signal: the firm's data-handling, employment, or anti-corruption posture differs significantly from its home-country posture; "everyone does business this way locally" is the rationale. Diagnosis: ethical relativism / cultural-accommodation failure; the single-standard rule (Ch 1.3) is being violated; the FCPA / OECD framework explicitly rejects the cultural-relativism defence (Ch 7.4). Follow-up: apply the categorical-imperative test; review against FCPA and OECD framework; ensure cross-border conduct meets the firm's home-country standard.
- **Pattern: A board memo on AI ethics, data protection, or security investment is framed around expected return only.** Signal: the memo presents ROI calculations, market-share projections, and competitive positioning; the affected stakeholders (data subjects, users, employees carrying the work) are absent from the framing. Diagnosis: instrumental stakeholder reasoning without the normative lens; the Donaldson-Preston test is being skipped. Follow-up: reframe the memo around duties to stakeholders; surface low-power-high-interest stakeholders; apply the App C credo on multiple stakeholders being "significant though not equal".
- **Pattern: A whistleblower (security researcher, engineer, AI-safety dissenter) is being treated as disloyal.** Signal: known reporters of substantive concerns are isolated, "reorganised out", or quietly blocked from promotion. Diagnosis: stakeholder voice being suppressed; the Sherron Watkins / Sallie Krawcheck cost is being normalised. Follow-up: review whistleblowing protection in fact (not just policy); protect the reporter; investigate the substantive claim; consider that even ethical and protected disclosure carries career cost in the corpus's worked examples.
- **Pattern: A published AI-ethics statement, sustainability claim, or DEI commitment sits next to unchanged underlying practice.** Signal: prominent ethics statements, ESG reports, or DEI dashboards coexist with high-stakeholder-impact practices that the published claims would seem to prohibit. Diagnosis: greenwashing as defined in Ch 3.4 — "superficial CSR efforts that merely cover up systemic ethics problems for the sake of public relations". Follow-up: test the underlying practice against the claim; design substantive practice change before publishing the next iteration of the statement.

## When to Use This Reference

- An AI-integration decision (productisation, build/buy, vendor selection) is in scope and the question turns on duties owed to users, data subjects, displaced workers, or affected communities.
- A security or privacy incident has emerged and the firm is choosing between minimum-compliance and ethical-maximum response across the four publics.
- A non-compete, NDA, or IP-protection clause is being designed and the question is whether the protection is proportionate to legitimate IP exposure or over-reaching.
- A productivity monitoring, code-instrumentation, or AI-based performance-analytics system is being deployed on engineering or product staff and ECPA-style consent and harm-principle questions arise.
- A contractor / staff-aug / employee classification decision is being made and the legal-and-ethical exposure of misclassification needs to be priced.
- A pay-equity review of engineering or product compensation surfaces gaps and the framework for remediation needs to be defined.
- An ADA accommodation request, return-to-office policy, or performance-management policy raises accommodation-versus-administrative-simplicity questions.
- An international market entry, sales motion, or partnership negotiation in a low-standards jurisdiction raises FCPA and single-standard-rule questions.
- A board memo, exec-alignment paper, or investor update on AI ethics, data protection, or security investment needs the normative-stakeholder argument as its load-bearing frame.
- A customer-communication artefact (breach disclosure, AI launch, product-safety announcement) needs to sequence across the four publics with Tylenol-playbook discipline.
- A whistleblowing channel for security-vulnerability disclosure, AI-safety dissent, or product-ethics concerns is being designed.
- A published AI-ethics statement, sustainability claim, or DEI commitment needs review for greenwashing risk.

## Worked Example

A US-headquartered software firm (450 engineers; AI capability shipped across three product lines) has discovered a privacy issue: an internal feature-flag system inadvertently logged identifying user data alongside model-input metadata for ~14 months. The data was stored only within the firm's perimeter, was not exfiltrated, and has now been purged. The legal team confirms breach-notification obligations: ~3 US states require notification given the data classes touched; GDPR notification is required for the EU subset of users (~22% of the affected population). The CFO frames the disclosure as a minimal-compliance question — notify per jurisdictional minimums, publish a brief acknowledgment, move on. The CTO and the head of product ethics push back and request a 48-hour review.

**Strategic positioning.** The head of product ethics applies the normative-stakeholder test. The source distinguishes three approaches to stakeholders: descriptive (mapping them), instrumental (managing them for the bottom line), and normative (treating them as ends in themselves with "intrinsic value"). The CFO's framing is instrumental — minimal disclosure protects the firm's exposure. The normative frame asks what duties are owed. (Source: OpenStax, *Business Ethics*, Ch 3.2, "The Ethical Basis of Stakeholders' Claims") Affected stakeholders: ~2.1 million users whose identifying data was logged with model inputs; data subjects (some of whom are minors per the product's known demographics); regulators in the 3 notification-required US states plus the EU; the engineering organisation; and the firm's broader user base — a latent public learning about the firm's posture from this incident.

**Moral minimum versus ethical minimum.** The ethical minimum (legal compliance) is to notify per jurisdiction. The moral minimum asks what response would honour the obligations the firm has to all affected stakeholders, even those not in notification-required jurisdictions. The source names the spectrum explicitly: ethical responsibilities often extend beyond legal requirements. The ethical maximum benchmark is the Samsung washer recall and the Tylenol playbook: proactive, comprehensive disclosure to the entire affected population, substantive remediation, transparent communication across all four publics. (Source: Ch 3.1, "Ethical Responsibilities Often Extend Beyond Legal Requirements"; Ch 4.1, "The Two Sides of the Corporate Responsibility Debate")

**Applying the four publics framework.** Currently: the affected user population is *nonpublic* (no problem recognised — the data was never exfiltrated). Regulators in notification-required jurisdictions are *aware* (the firm is about to inform them). The internal engineering team is *aware* (they investigated). The CFO's plan keeps most affected users *nonpublic* by limiting disclosure to required jurisdictions. The ethical-maximum framing moves the broader affected user population from *nonpublic* to *aware* through proactive disclosure — accepting the short-term goodwill hit to preserve long-term trust. The source's Tylenol worked example is the model: Johnson & Johnson's proactive, comprehensive disclosure to the active public, including those not yet aware. (Source: Ch 3.2, "Defining Stakeholder Categories"; Ch 3.2, "The Chicago Tylenol Murders")

**Applying the deontological lens.** Could the maxim "disclose privacy issues only where legally required" be willed universal? A market in which firms disclose only when required produces a regime where users cannot trust any firm's data handling. The Kantian check fails: the firm has incentive to discover the legal minimum and stop there, and willing that maxim universally destroys the trust the market depends on. The lead author's Appendix C credo — the means ennoble the ends — argues for the ethical-maximum response even when the consequence calculation would favour minimum disclosure. (Source: Ch 2.5, "Kant's Ethical Theory"; App C, "The Nature of Business Ethics")

**Stakeholder-impact testing.** Engineering team (functional stakeholder, Grunig-Hunt linkage): morale impact if leadership chooses minimum disclosure to protect the firm rather than to honour duties to users. The source names tone at the top as the lever: ethical conduct is most reliably learned from leadership modelling, not compliance training. (Source: Ch 11.3; Ch 5.4) Diffused stakeholders (NGOs, privacy journalists, regulator advisory boards): minimum disclosure will be read as evidence the published AI-ethics statement is greenwashing — "superficial CSR efforts that merely cover up systemic ethics problems for the sake of public relations." (Source: Ch 3.4, "CSR as Public Relations Tool") Long-term goodwill: the source's position that ethical conduct generates goodwill that compounds into long-term profitability anchors the multi-year calculation. (Source: Ch 1.2, "A Brief Introduction to Corporate Social Responsibility")

**Communication design (Grunig-Hunt + four publics + MITRE).** The source's MITRE five-step pattern structures the sequence: identify stakeholders; gather data on concerns; analyse; present results to decision-makers; inform stakeholders that their concerns were considered. Applied here: (1) Regulator notifications per jurisdictional requirement, on the legal timeline. (2) Customer-facing disclosure to the entire affected user population via a transparent, substantive incident write-up. (3) Board-paper framed around the normative-stakeholder argument and the long-term goodwill calculation. (4) Engineering-organisation communication acknowledging the team's work. (5) A 60-day follow-up communication summarising changes implemented in response to user feedback — the closing-the-loop step the source names as where engagement integrity is judged. (Source: Ch 3.3, "Stakeholder Prioritization"; Ch 3.2 for Grunig-Hunt and four publics)

**Tone at the top.** The CEO and the CTO sign the customer-facing disclosure jointly. The CEO acknowledges that the firm chose to disclose beyond the legal minimum and explains the reasoning — the duties the firm owes to its users — without performing apology theatre. The board paper makes the same argument internally. The source's framing: a company that treats its stakeholders "merely as means to an end" has failed the normative test regardless of the legal outcome. (Source: Ch 3.2, "The Ethical Basis of Stakeholders' Claims")

**Result eighteen months later.** The minimum-compliance path projected ~$2.5M in regulatory penalties and minimal direct-revenue impact, on average. The ethical-maximum path produced ~$4.1M in direct costs (additional engineering investment, expanded customer-trust programme, settlement of two civil suits that the broader disclosure surfaced) but no measurable customer churn beyond the baseline trend, no regulatory escalation, and one notable outcome: an industry-wide AI-ethics consortium invited the firm onto its advisory board, citing the disclosure as exemplary. Goodwill (as tracked through Net Promoter Score, employee engagement, and three trade-press scorecards) trended positively over the following twelve months against a sector baseline that drifted down — consistent with the source's argument that ethical conduct builds the goodwill that compounds into long-term profitability. (Source: Ch 1.2)

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **"The law doesn't require more."** Treating the legal minimum as the moral minimum. The ethical-maximum benchmark (Samsung, Tylenol) is the discipline.
- **"AI is just engineering velocity."** Framing AI deployment as a pure expected-return calculation without the normative-stakeholder test and the externalised-cost surfacing.
- **"Everybody does business this way locally."** Cultural relativism defending bribery, data-handling, or employment-practice asymmetry. The single-standard rule and FCPA framework reject this.
- **"We have an AI ethics statement on the website."** Greenwashing — ethics communication without underlying practice change.
- **"They're contractors, not employees."** Misclassification to dodge benefits and payroll taxes; the Microsoft $97 million settlement is the cost.
- **"The non-compete protects our IP."** Over-broad non-competes weaponised beyond legitimate trade-secret exposure. The Jimmy John's pattern applied to engineering talent.
- **"Monitoring is for productivity."** Productivity-monitoring justified by paternalism or "the employee's own good" without ECPA-style consent and the harm-principle check.
- **"The accommodation request would set a precedent."** Administrative-simplicity defence against ADA accommodation. The Verizon $20 million pattern.
- **"Whistleblowers are disloyal."** Treating substantive engineering, security, or research dissent as betrayal; the False Claims Act and protected-disclosure statutes exist precisely because internal channels often fail.
- **"The board memo should focus on ROI."** Framing AI-ethics, data-protection, or security decisions around expected return only; the deontological lens is the discipline against pure consequence calculation.
- **"We satisfied the breach-notification minimum."** Disclosure scoped to the legal floor; the ethical-maximum response disclosure framework moves through all four publics.
- **"We're maximising shareholder value."** Strict shareholder primacy applied as exclusive duty. The book's stakeholder-pluralist position (Ch 1.2, Ch 4.1, App C) is built precisely against this framing.

AI ethics appears primarily in the context of human-judgment principles (the IBM-1979 "A computer can never be held accountable" principle) rather than as a technical-ethics treatment; ML-specific ethics frameworks are outside scope.
