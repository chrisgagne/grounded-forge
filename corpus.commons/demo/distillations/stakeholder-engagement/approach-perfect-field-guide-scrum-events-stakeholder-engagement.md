**Source:** Gagné, C. (2020). *The Approach Perfect Field Guide to Scrum Events*. April 3, 2020 edition. Approach Perfect, Limited. Public licence: CC BY-ND 4.0; relicensed CC BY 4.0 by the author for this corpus. https://approachperfect.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/04/The-Approach-Perfect-Field-Guide-to-Scrum-Events-2020-04-03.pdf.

## Stakeholder-Engagement Relevance

Stakeholder engagement is woven through every Scrum event, and the Field Guide names exactly *which* stakeholders engage *when*, *how*, and *under what behavioural constraints*. The "Scrum Events at a Glance" table provides one canonical column on Participants per event; each event section then expands the participant set into three tiers — **Required**, **Strongly Suggested**, and **Invited** — with explicit rules about what invitees can and cannot do during the event.

Three stakeholder-engagement threads run through the guide.

**First, stakeholder participation is structured event-by-event.** The Sprint sees stakeholders and SMEs "participate daily" (Scrum Events at a Glance); the team "should work together daily with business people" (Agile principle #4). Sprint Planning invites stakeholders and SMEs at the team's discretion. The Daily Scrum allows stakeholders to attend as observers under a strict non-interference rule. The Sprint Review is the high-stakes stakeholder event — key stakeholders and end users are *strongly suggested*, invited by the Product Owner, with a working-session (not presentation) format and an explicit trust-building goal. The Sprint Retrospective closes ranks — the Scrum Team only, with non-Team attendance forbidden without explicit invitation from the entire team. Backlog Refinement invites SMEs and stakeholders to support items where their domain knowledge applies.

**Second, the Field Guide encodes behavioural constraints on stakeholder participation.** The recurring rule across multiple events: "Participants who are not *Scrum Team* members must understand that this is not their event and they may not interfere with the team's process, only support the team." The Sprint Review is *not a status meeting* — it's an opportunity for stakeholders to *collaborate* with the team to improve the Product Backlog with shared context. The Sprint Retrospective uses an explicit confidentiality rule (Vegas Rule or Chatham House Rule) to create the safe container for raising difficult issues. The Sprint Review trust-building goal is named co-equally with feedback elicitation — engagement isn't just about extracting input, it's about building the relationship that enables future engagement.

**Third, the Field Guide distributes stakeholder-engagement roles across the Scrum Team.** The Product Owner is the named owner of stakeholder invitation lists (especially for the Sprint Review's "key stakeholders and end users"), the named representative of stakeholders in the Product Backlog, the named decision-maker on Product Backlog ordering after team input. The Development Team owns the Sprint Review demo and the highs/lows/blockers narrative. The Scrum Master owns the time-keeping and the "this is not their event" boundary-setting. The author's worked agendas show stakeholder voice integrated at named time-slots: 5 minutes for stakeholders to share market/context changes at the Sprint Review; the 30-minute demo segment for honest and critical feedback; the parking-lot mechanism that lets stakeholders raise questions or concerns at the Daily Scrum.

This distillation gathers these threads into a working pattern: who's invited to which Scrum event, what behavioural constraints apply, how the Product Owner curates stakeholder participation, and how the Field Guide's worked agendas turn stakeholder voice into Product Backlog adjustments. It complements the Scrum Guide's stakeholder-engagement distillation (which carries the canonical rules) by adding the practitioner's facilitation depth (sample agendas, time-allocations, named techniques, tips for keeping the engagement productive).

## Key Concepts for Stakeholder Engagement

1. **Three-tier participant model across every event.** Each event section uses Required / Strongly Suggested / Invited tiers explicitly. Required tier carries the accountability; Strongly Suggested tier carries the value-add; Invited tier carries the support function. Reading the participant section of an event is the first step in deciding who to bring. (Source: Gagné, *Field Guide to Scrum Events* 2020, each event's "Participants" section)

2. **"Daily collaboration with business people."** From The Sprint: "the *Scrum Team* should work together daily with business people, who are often invited to participate in *Sprint Planning*, *Daily Scrum*, and *Sprint Reviews*." Stakeholder engagement is not a one-event activity — it's a daily collaboration with named touch-points across the cadence. (Agile principle #4.) ("The Sprint", "Participants")

3. **The non-interference rule for non-Team participants.** Repeated across events: "Participants who are not *Scrum Team* members must understand that this is not their event and they may not interfere with the team's process, only support the team." A behavioural constraint that protects the Scrum Team's self-management while keeping stakeholder voice available. ("Sprint Planning", "Participants"; "Daily Scrum", "Tips"; recurring elsewhere)

4. **The Sprint Review is *not* a status meeting.** "This is *not* a status meeting. Rather, it is an opportunity for stakeholders to collaborate with the team and improve the *Product Backlog* with shared context and learnings." The reframing is load-bearing: stakeholders are co-collaborators on the Product Backlog, not recipients of a report. ("Sprint Review", "Tips")

5. **The Sprint Review goal is co-equally trust-building.** Two named goals: "Elicit feedback on the *Increment* from stakeholders—ideally even end users—and collaboratively improve the *Product Backlog* together"; "Foster a culture of transparency and trust between the *Scrum Team* and stakeholders." Trust-building is named alongside Product Backlog improvement. ("Sprint Review", "Goals")

6. **End users alongside stakeholders.** Sprint Review participants: "**Strongly Suggested:** Key stakeholders and end users invited by the *Product Owner*." End users are named explicitly, not collapsed into "stakeholders." The Product Owner curates the invitation list. ("Sprint Review", "Participants")

7. **The 1-hour Sprint Review agenda allocates stakeholder voice deliberately.** 5 min PO welcome and Sprint Goal status; 5 min Development Team highs/lows/blockers; 30 min demo of Done items soliciting "honest and critical feedback"; 5 min PO on Product Backlog state and forecasts; **5 min stakeholders share new knowledge about market or context changes**; 5 min next-release planning; 5 min collaboratively deciding what to do next. Stakeholder voice has a named slot and a named segment-length. ("Sprint Review", "Sample 1-Hour Sprint Review Agenda")

8. **Don't demonstrate work that isn't Done — it erodes trust.** "Don't demonstrate work that isn't '*Done*.' It can significantly erode trust between the *Scrum Team*, stakeholders, and end users if the demonstrated work doesn't get shipped as expected. It also allows the *Scrum Team* to persist the illusion that incomplete work is still valuable. Remember: work in process is a liability, not an asset." A trust-protection rule that operationalises the Sprint Review's transparency goal. ("Sprint Review", "Tips")

9. **The Product Owner curates stakeholder invitation lists.** Sprint Review: "Key stakeholders and end users invited by the *Product Owner*." Backlog Refinement: "Strongly Suggested: The entire *Scrum Team* and any useful subject matter experts or stakeholders" — the team identifies; the PO usually invites. The PO is the named stakeholder interface for invitation curation. ("Sprint Review", "Participants"; "Backlog Refinement", "Participants")

10. **The Daily Scrum allows observers under non-interference.** The Daily Scrum is for the Development Team, but stakeholders may attend as observers; SMEs may attend; the non-interference rule applies. The summary table phrases it: "Stakeholders may attend as observers." ("Daily Scrum", "Participants"; Scrum Events at a Glance)

11. **The Parking Lot lets stakeholders raise concerns after the Daily Scrum.** "Anyone, including those outside the team, may raise 'Parking Lot' topics." One named scenario: "A stakeholder has questions or concerns they'd like to bring to the *Scrum Team*." The mechanism allows stakeholder voice without corrupting the Daily Scrum's 15-minute timebox. ("Daily Scrum", "Tips")

12. **Sprint Planning invites SMEs at the team's discretion.** "Invited: Stakeholders and other subject matter experts as selected by the team to support the *Scrum Team* as they see fit." The behavioural constraint applies. ("Sprint Planning", "Participants")

13. **The Sprint Retrospective closes ranks.** "The entire *Scrum Team*. The *Product Owner* and *Scrum Master* should participate as peers to the *Development Team*. No other may attend unless specifically invited to do so by the entire *Scrum Team*." A boundary that protects the team's psychological safety for difficult conversations. The summary table phrases it: "Others must not attend unless freely invited by all on the *Scrum Team*." ("Sprint Retrospective", "Participants"; Scrum Events at a Glance)

14. **The Vegas Rule or Chatham House Rule for retrospective confidentiality.** Explicit choice between two named rules: the Vegas Rule (unanimous explicit consent before sharing anything) or the Chatham House Rule (information may be shared, identities may not). The rule creates the safe container that lets the team raise difficult issues. ("Sprint Retrospective", "Tips")

15. **Backlog Refinement invites SMEs and stakeholders for domain knowledge.** "Strongly Suggested: The entire *Scrum Team* and any useful subject matter experts or stakeholders. Even if a developer does not have enough context to immediately contribute to improving the *Backlog Items*, they can increase their understanding of the domain and identify novel approaches, edge cases, or risks that others on the team may have missed." Engagement supports both refinement and team learning. ("Backlog Refinement", "Participants")

16. **The Product Owner represents stakeholders in Product Backlog ordering.** Backlog Refinement: "Allow the *Scrum Team* to propose changes to the order based on a thoughtful consideration of its value, estimate, and dependencies (the *Product Owner* makes the final decision." The PO is the single point of stakeholder representation for ordering decisions. ("Backlog Refinement", "Sample 1½-hour Backlog Refinement Agenda")

17. **Capture outcomes, not implementations, in stakeholder-facing items.** The coffee-temperature worked example: a good Backlog Item is "As a coffee drinker, I can have coffee that's just the right temperature so that I can delight in its ideal taste," not "Integrate the Acme steam-heating element into the brew assembly." Outcome-language is stakeholder-language; implementation-language is developer-language. (Agile principles #1 and #11.) ("Sprint Planning", "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda")

18. **Pre-circulate Backlog Refinement items to invited participants.** "The *Product Owner* should send out a list of the *Backlog Items* to be discussed before the *Backlog Refinement* event. This allows the *Scrum Team* to read ahead and familiarize themselves with their description, order, and value. It also helps communicate the full scope of the *Backlog Refinement* event to encourage brevity." Pre-circulation respects invited participants' time and accelerates the event. ("Backlog Refinement", "Tips")

19. **Sprint Review forecasts give stakeholders calibrated futures.** Within the 1-hour agenda: "The *Product Owner* discusses the current state of the *Product Backlog*. They can also offer forecasts, such as the likelihood of completing a given scope by a given date, the expected scope for a given date, or the expected date for a given scope." Three named forecast formats give stakeholders the information they need without making firm commitments. ("Sprint Review", "Sample 1-Hour Sprint Review Agenda")

20. **The Retrospective Prime Directive frames how the team handles its own engagement.** "Regardless of what we discover, we understand and truly believe that everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time, their skills and abilities, the resources available, and the situation at hand." A pre-commitment about how the team will *engage with each other* in the retrospective — the same posture that NHS *A Just Culture Guide* and the US Forest Service Learning Review extend to incident-scale engagement. ("Sprint Retrospective", "Sample 1½-Hour Sprint Retrospective Agenda")

21. **"Connect and celebrate" as named informal engagement.** After the Retrospective: "*This is not an official Scrum event*, but it's very helpful to spend quality, unstructured time hanging out with colleagues." Informal time is named as a Sprint-cadence component. ("The Sprint", "Sample Sprint Agenda (Two-Week Sprint)")

22. **Distributed-team engagement gets extra time at the Daily Scrum bridge.** "Distributed teams should consider scheduling more than 15 minutes for this event. The *Daily Scrum itself* is still time-boxed for 15 minutes, but it can be very useful to keep the video conferencing bridge reserved for 'Parking Lot' topics. Distributed *Scrum Team* members are less likely to speak to one another informally throughout the rest of the day." Recognises that distributed engagement needs structural support. ("Daily Scrum", "Tips")

## Questions to Ask During Stakeholder Engagement

### Phase 1: Identifying (Who are the stakeholders?)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Use the per-event participant tier model | For this Scrum event, who's Required, who's Strongly Suggested, who's Invited? The Field Guide names tiers per event — start there before improvising. |
| Identify the Product Owner's invitation list | For the Sprint Review, has the Product Owner curated the "key stakeholders and end users" invitation list, or is it being run as an open invitation? End users are named explicitly — are they on the list? |
| Name SMEs by domain need | For Sprint Planning or Backlog Refinement, which SMEs would unblock specific Backlog Items? "Other subject matter experts as selected by the team to support the *Scrum Team* as they see fit." |
| Test for stakeholder-engagement gaps in the daily cadence | Is the Scrum Team "work[ing] together daily with business people, who are often invited to participate in *Sprint Planning*, *Daily Scrum*, and *Sprint Reviews*"? If business contact is concentrated at one event per Sprint, the engagement is too sparse. (Agile principle #4) |
| Identify distributed-engagement structural gaps | If the team is distributed, is there a longer bridge for Parking-Lot topics after the Daily Scrum? Distributed members lose the informal interaction; structure replaces it. |
| Surface voiceless stakeholders | Are end users represented? Are upstream / downstream system users represented? The Product Owner "represents the needs of many stakeholders in the Product Backlog" — but who's not at the table whose interests should be? |

### Phase 2: Bounding (Setting the engagement frame)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply the non-interference rule | Have non-Team participants understood that "this is not their event and they may not interfere with the team's process, only support the team"? If they're not clear on the boundary, the engagement will derail. |
| Pick the retrospective confidentiality rule | Is the Vegas Rule (unanimous explicit consent before sharing anything) or the Chatham House Rule (information may be shared, identities may not) the right safe-container choice for the difficult issues this team needs to raise? |
| Set the Sprint Review framing as collaboration, not status | Is the Sprint Review being run as a working session for collaboratively improving the Product Backlog, or as a status presentation? Reframe before running. |
| Honour the Retrospective Prime Directive | Has the team read or recalled the Directive — "everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time…"? A retro without this framing tilts toward blame and degrades engagement quality. |
| Set time-boxes on stakeholder voice | The Sprint Review agenda allocates 5 minutes for stakeholders to share market/context changes. Stakeholder voice is welcomed *and* bounded — it doesn't consume the demo or the what-next decision time. |
| Don't show undone work | Is everything being demonstrated actually Done? Trust erosion from demonstrating work-in-process compounds across Sprints. |

### Phase 3: Engaging (Running the events with stakeholders)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Run the Sprint Review as a 1-hour working session | 5 min PO welcome + Sprint Goal status; 5 min Dev Team highs/lows/blockers; 30 min Done demo with honest and critical feedback; 5 min PO on Product Backlog state with forecasts; 5 min stakeholder market/context updates; 5 min next-release planning; 5 min collaborative what-next. Allocate the segments; don't drift. |
| Solicit honest and critical feedback at the demo | Is the team's framing inviting real challenge, or are stakeholders being asked to validate? "Honest and critical" is the standard. |
| Use the Parking Lot for stakeholder concerns at the Daily Scrum | If a stakeholder has questions or concerns at the Daily Scrum, defer them to a Parking Lot after the 15-minute event — don't let them corrupt the timebox; don't lose them either. |
| Pre-circulate Backlog Refinement items | Has the Product Owner sent the item list ahead of time so invited participants can read in? Pre-circulation respects time, encourages brevity, allows early exit. |
| Capture Backlog Items as customer-perspective outcomes | Are stakeholder-facing Backlog Items expressed as outcomes ("As a coffee drinker, I can have coffee that's just the right temperature so that I can delight in its ideal taste") not implementations ("Integrate the Acme steam-heating element into the brew assembly")? |
| Offer calibrated forecasts to stakeholders | At Sprint Review: offer forecasts in one of the three named formats — likelihood of given scope by given date; expected scope for given date; expected date for given scope. Don't make firm commitments. |
| Watch for status-meeting drift mid-event | If the Sprint Review is degrading into a one-sided demo or status report, name it and reset the frame — "this is not a status meeting, it's an opportunity for stakeholders to collaborate." |

### Phase 4: Listening (Surfacing stakeholder voice)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Solicit market and context updates | At the Sprint Review, what has changed in the market or potential uses for the product that the Scrum Team should know about? Stakeholders are the sensors. |
| Use the 5-Whys for stakeholder-raised concerns | If a stakeholder concern surfaces (at a Sprint Review or via Parking Lot from a Daily Scrum), apply "ask why five times" to reach the underlying issue rather than the surface symptom. "If you're rarely surprised by your root causes, you may not be digging deep enough." |
| Estimate the conversation, not just the number | At Backlog Refinement: a 1-vs-5 estimation spread is information. Don't average it away or defer to seniority — explore the spread with stakeholders/SMEs present. |
| Invite domain SMEs to Backlog Refinement | "Even if a developer does not have enough context to immediately contribute to improving the *Backlog Items*, they can increase their understanding of the domain and identify novel approaches, edge cases, or risks that others on the team may have missed." SMEs surface what the team can't. |
| Use the appreciations round at the Retrospective | Open or close with each team member sharing specific appreciation for another. The author's example: "Alan, I really appreciate how you helped me figure my way out of the memory leak issue when you reviewed my pull request." Engagement-quality is repaired in moments like this. |
| Set the stage with feeling check-ins | At the Retrospective: invite each team member to share "one-word or a brief phrase that describes how they're feeling." The check-in lets the team hear each other before getting into the substance. |

### Phase 5: Updating (Translating engagement into commitment)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Update the Product Backlog at the Sprint Review | "An updated *Product Backlog* that reflects stakeholder feedback and new knowledge about the product's context, such as the competitive landscape and the company's resources." The artefact is the ratification of stakeholder input. |
| Apply the Product Owner's final-ordering decision rule | At Backlog Refinement: the team proposes ordering changes based on value, estimate, and dependencies; the Product Owner makes the final decision. Team voice informs; PO decides. |
| Capture retrospective improvements as decisions or actions | Decisions = changes to Definition of Done, Definition of Ready, or Working Agreement. Actions = new Backlog Items. Both are forms of commitment, registered in different artefacts. |
| Communicate the Sprint Goal to stakeholders | The Sprint Goal "communicates why the Sprint is valuable to stakeholders." Has the PO carried it forward in a form stakeholders can hold? |
| Update the Working Agreement for behavioural patterns | If the team agrees on a new ground-rule (e.g. "imposes a penalty for being late"), is it captured in the Working Agreement? An informal agreement that doesn't change the artefact dissolves. |
| Don't carry over undone work as if Done | Items failing Definition of Done "cannot be released or even presented at the Sprint Review. Instead, [they return] to the Product Backlog for future consideration." Stakeholders need to see the boundary respected. |

### Phase 6: Sustaining (Maintaining the relationship across cycles)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Hold the trust-building goal alongside feedback | Is the team only extracting input from stakeholders, or is it also actively fostering "a culture of transparency and trust between the *Scrum Team* and stakeholders"? Trust is built over many Sprints, not one. |
| Bring snacks (literally) | "Bring snacks! If you're working remotely, have a treat together anyway." Informal hospitality is a small intervention with disproportionate effect on engagement. |
| Schedule connect-and-celebrate after retros | The unstructured time after the Retrospective is named — "very helpful to spend quality, unstructured time hanging out with colleagues." Engagement endures because the relationships endure. |
| Recover the Daily Scrum on the last day of the Sprint | "Some teams will cancel the *Daily Scrum* on the last day of the sprint, but we recommend against that as the team could use this to coordinate their final effort against the *Sprint Goal*." Engagement doesn't take a half-day off. |
| Watch for one-sided demo drift | "Less mature teams try to complete this in 30 minutes, but we've found that this becomes a rapid-fire, one-sided demo, not a true inspection and adaptation opportunity." Engagement requires time enough for two-way conversation. |
| Maintain the Vegas Rule / Chatham House Rule across retros | Confidentiality choices persist beyond a single retrospective — once chosen, they're a feature of the team's engagement contract with itself. Revisit but don't quietly drop. |
| Watch for "we skipped the retro" drift | If retrospectives are being skipped, shortened below 1½ hours, or perfunctory, the team is losing its primary engagement-with-self mechanism. Surface and recover. |
| Engage stakeholders daily, not just at Sprint Review | Agile principle #4 framing: "the *Scrum Team* should work together daily with business people." If stakeholder contact is concentrated at one event per Sprint, deepen the daily cadence. |

## What to Look For

- **Sprint Review running as a status presentation.** If stakeholders are sitting and watching rather than collaborating on the Product Backlog, the format has drifted; the Sprint Review goal is two-sided working session, not one-sided report.
- **Non-team attendees directing team process.** The non-interference rule applies across events; observers who start giving instructions or reassigning work are violating the boundary the guide names explicitly.
- **Work-in-progress presented as Done.** Trust erosion follows quickly and compounds across Sprints when stakeholders see what they thought was Done fail to ship. Check Definition of Done compliance before the Sprint Review demo.
- **Invitation lists that skip end users.** The guide names "key stakeholders and end users" as the Sprint Review's strongly-suggested attendees; engagement that includes only internal stakeholders misses the guide's explicit intent.
- **Retrospective without a confidentiality rule.** The Vegas Rule or Chatham House Rule requires an explicit choice; an implicit assumption is not the same thing and doesn't provide the safe container.
- **Backlog Items written in implementation language.** "Integrate the Acme steam-heating element" is developer-language; stakeholder-facing items should express customer outcomes.
- **Firm commitments replacing calibrated forecasts.** The guide names three forecast formats for the Sprint Review (probability, scope, date) precisely because firm commitments produce the wrong pressure. Watch for language like "we will definitely deliver X by Y."
- **Daily Scrum consumed by stakeholder conversation.** The Parking Lot mechanism exists so stakeholder concerns don't colonise the 15-minute timebox; if the Daily Scrum extends past 15 minutes on stakeholder questions, the mechanism hasn't been activated.
- **One-sided demos at the Sprint Review.** The 30-minute demo segment is for honest and critical feedback, not applause; a review that ends without critical stakeholder input has not achieved its purpose.

## When to Use This Reference

- A *Sprint Review* needs design — who's invited, what segments structure the hour, how to keep it a working session rather than a presentation, how to elicit honest feedback.
- A *Product Backlog ordering* engagement — team proposes, PO decides; how the conversation runs at Backlog Refinement.
- A *Backlog Item drafting* question with stakeholder voice — outcomes vs implementations, Acceptance Criteria as bulleted testable conditions.
- A *Daily Scrum stakeholder-presence* question — observers under non-interference, with the Parking Lot for substantive concerns.
- A *Sprint Retrospective confidentiality* design — Vegas Rule or Chatham House Rule, explicitly chosen.
- A *Sprint Retrospective opening / closing engagement* — set-the-stage feeling check-ins, Norm Kerth's Prime Directive, appreciations rounds.
- A *trust-building* question at the Scrum-Team↔stakeholder boundary — the Sprint Review's co-equal goal alongside feedback.
- A *forecasting communication* — three named forecast formats the PO can offer stakeholders without firm commitments.
- A *distributed-team engagement* design — bridge structure, Parking-Lot availability, named informal time.
- A *pre-circulation* design for Backlog Refinement — what the PO sends, when, and how it lets the event end early.
- An *informal engagement* design — connect-and-celebrate after retros, snacks, time hanging out with colleagues.
- A *non-interference rule* enforcement — naming the boundary when non-Team participants drift into directing the team's process.

## Worked Example

A five-person Scrum team building a patient-scheduling feature for a regional clinic has just started its third Sprint. The product owner (PO) is frustrated: the last two Sprint Reviews felt like one-sided demos, stakeholders asked no substantive questions, and backlog ordering hasn't changed. The Scrum Master decides to redesign the next Sprint Review using the Field Guide's 1-hour agenda.

**Invitation design.** The PO reviews the guide's "key stakeholders and end users" standard (Sprint Review, "Participants"). She identifies three stakeholder groups: two clinic managers (scheduling decisions), one front-desk supervisor (implementation impact), and two patients recruited via the clinic's patient-advisory panel (end users, often absent from reviews). She sends personal invitations with a one-line statement of the Sprint Goal and what feedback she needs.

**Agenda framing.** The Scrum Master sets the framing at the opening: "This is not a status meeting — it is a working session. We want your honest and critical feedback on what we built, and we want to hear what has changed in your world since last Sprint" (Sprint Review, "Tips"). The non-interference boundary is stated for the clinic managers who have previously redirected the development team mid-demo.

**Segment execution.** The team runs the 1-hour agenda: 5 min PO welcome and Sprint Goal status; 5 min team highs/lows/blockers (including a frank account of one item that missed Done and is returning to the backlog); 30 min demo with the two patients walking through the scheduling flow on a real device; 5 min PO on backlog state and three-format forecasts (likelihood of given scope by date; expected scope for date; expected date for scope); 5 min stakeholders share new context (one clinic manager flags an insurance-code change that affects 20% of appointments — information the team didn't have); 5 min next-release planning; 5 min collaborative what-next.

**Trust-protective move.** The item that missed Done is disclosed in the highlights/lowlights segment, not hidden. The PO explains it returns to the backlog unestimated and without partial credit (Sprint Planning, "Sample 3-Hour Sprint Planning Agenda"). The clinic managers nod; this is the first time they have seen this happen and the transparency registers.

**Outcome.** The backlog is updated with three changes driven by the insurance-code disclosure, the patient feedback on the scheduling flow, and a reprioritisation the PO makes after the stakeholder-context segment. The Sprint Review ends with a concrete updated backlog and two clinic managers who participated rather than observed.

The three-tier participant model (Sprint Review, "Participants"), the 1-hour Sprint Review agenda (Sprint Review, "Sample 1-Hour Sprint Review Agenda"), the non-interference rule, the trust-building goal, the Done-only demonstration rule, and the three forecast formats are all `[V]`-marked passages in the deep ref. No verbatim source blockquotes appear in this distillation.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Sprint Review as a one-sided status presentation.** The guide is explicit: "This is *not* a status meeting. Rather, it is an opportunity for stakeholders to collaborate with the team and improve the *Product Backlog* with shared context and learnings."
- **Demonstrating work that isn't Done.** Trust erosion compounds across Sprints; work-in-process is a liability, not an asset.
- **Inviting only internal stakeholders to the Sprint Review.** End users are named explicitly in the guide; their absence removes the most direct source of feedback.
- **Non-team participants directing the team's process.** The non-interference rule is stated across multiple events; observers who redirect work violate the guide's explicit boundary.
- **Daily Scrum extending on stakeholder questions.** The Parking Lot exists for this purpose; using it protects the 15-minute timebox without losing the stakeholder concern.
- **Retrospective without a named confidentiality rule.** An implicit assumption is not a safe container; Vegas Rule or Chatham House Rule requires an explicit choice.
- **Backlog Items in implementation language.** Stakeholder-facing items must express customer outcomes (the coffee-temperature example contrasts both).
- **Firm commitments replacing calibrated forecasts.** Three named forecast formats let the PO give stakeholders what they need without unsustainable promises.
- **Skipping or shortening the retrospective.** The guide is explicit: less than 1½ hours is not enough to surface meaningful improvement even for a one-week Sprint.
- **Accepting estimation consensus without exploring a spread.** A 1-vs-5 point spread is information; averaging it or deferring to seniority loses the knowledge embedded in the disagreement.

## Integration with Other References

- **Schwaber & Sutherland, *The Scrum Guide* 2020** (canonical framework definition): The Field Guide *illustrates* the textbook Scrum the Guide *defines*. The Scrum Guide's stakeholder-engagement distillation carries the canonical rules — "the Product Owner is one person, not a committee," "those wanting to change the Product Backlog can do so by trying to convince the Product Owner," "the Sprint Review is a working session and the Scrum Team should avoid limiting it to a presentation." The Field Guide adds the facilitation depth: who curates the invitation list (PO), how the Sprint Review's 1 hour is segmented, what behavioural constraints apply to non-Team attendees, how the Vegas Rule and Chatham House Rule create safe containers for the Sprint Retrospective. Reach for the Scrum Guide for the rules; reach for the Field Guide for the practitioner's facilitation.
- **Letaw, *Handbook of Software Engineering Methods*** (Ch 4 on requirements; Ch 5 on user-story drafting; Ch 1.2.3 on the Sprint Review with stakeholders): Letaw covers four elicitation methods (interview, focus group, lab study, exploratory research), the five pitfalls of developer-led elicitation, paper prototyping with think-aloud, and the user-story format. The Field Guide names the user-story format as well (the coffee-temperature example) and the Acceptance Criteria as bulleted testable conditions. The two pair tightly at the requirements-elicitation level: Letaw on the methods of eliciting from stakeholders; the Field Guide on the Scrum-event containers that host the elicitation conversations.
- **Jones, *Evidence-based Software Engineering*** (Ch 1 on Agile's evidence base, Ch 5 on team communication overhead): Jones reads Agile critically — stakeholder claims about Agile's effects are folklore-laden post-1980. The Field Guide is unapologetically Agile-positive and operational. Use Jones when the question is "what evidence supports Scrum's stakeholder-engagement model?", and the Field Guide when the team has already chosen Scrum and needs to run the engagement well.
- **NHS, *A Just Culture Guide*** and **US Forest Service, *Learning Review Guide* (2024)**: both are post-incident-learning resources with a blameless-by-default stakeholder-engagement posture. The Retrospective Prime Directive ("everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time…") is the same stance at Sprint cadence. The Vegas Rule / Chatham House Rule for retrospectives is structurally similar to the protections NHS and FS build into incident reviews. Use the Field Guide for the per-Sprint cadence; use NHS / FS for incident-scale stakeholder engagement.
- **OpenStax, *Business Ethics*** (Ch 3 on stakeholder theory; Mitchell-Agle-Wood salience model): provides the theory of *who counts as a stakeholder* and *how to prioritise competing claims*. The Field Guide's Product Owner role — "represents the needs of many stakeholders in the Product Backlog" — is one operational instantiation of stakeholder salience. Use Business Ethics for the theory; use the Field Guide for the per-event practical engagement.
