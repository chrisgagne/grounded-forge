**Source:** US Forest Service, Risk Management Council & Office of Safety & Occupational Health (2024). *Learning From Unintended Outcomes and Learning Review Implementation Guide*. Revised April 2024. 98 pages. **Licence:** Public domain (17 USC §105 — US federal-employee work). **Distribution note:** No copyright obligation propagates from this source; derivative works are unrestricted under US copyright law.

## Stakeholder-Engagement Relevance

The LFUO guide is, at its heart, a stakeholder-engagement manual. The FLA and Learning Review processes are structured ways to engage participants, supervisors, Delegating Officials, peers, unions, regulators, academic SMEs, and communities of practice in dialogue about an unintended outcome. The engagement is high-stakes (people died, equipment was lost, careers are at risk), emotionally charged (grief, fear, blame, vindication), and asymmetric in power (Delegating Officials have authority, sharp-end participants have information). The guide's argument is that you cannot extract learning from these stakeholders without first creating the conditions — trust, fairness, dignity, dialogue, control over the setting — under which they will give honest accounts.

The distillation projects four contributions:

1. **A stakeholder-identification scheme rooted in roles, not in titles.** The FLA team's role inventory (Process Coach, Team Leader, Lead Facilitator, Storyteller, SMEs/Peers, Technical Specialists, Union Representative, Documentation Specialist, etc.) plus the participant inventory (those directly involved, supervisors, project leaders, Delegating Officials, cooperators, permittees, outfitters, interagency partners) is a stakeholder map specific to incident learning.

2. **A facilitation discipline for high-stakes group dialogue.** The Ten Principles and Agreements, the deflection-question protocol, the "you are not your point of view" opening, the "exploit points of departure" move, the "listen for the silent voice" instruction — these are the craft elements of the engagement.

3. **A treatment of restorative justice within stakeholder engagement.** The guide explicitly frames restorative justice as a fourth component of Just Culture and as an engagement modality after a serious accident: "After a serious accident, employees are often emotionally harmed, and relationships can be strained or broken. With its focus on transparency and rejection of blame, the FLA process seeks to restore victims to wholeness" (Part 1, "Just Culture").

4. **A treatment of community-of-practice engagement for vetting recommendations.** The Learning Review's focus groups (line officers, engine crewmembers, hotshots, helitack, wildland fire modules, sawyers, trail crew, timber markers, etc.) are the vetting mechanism for recommendations.

Where the guide is silent (e.g., on broader community engagement outside the agency, on normative ethical frames for stakeholder claims, on negotiation theory), the silence is acknowledged.

## Key Concepts for Stakeholder-Engagement

1. <!-- concept: just-culture --> **Just Culture as the precondition for honest stakeholder accounts.** Just Culture is the intersection of trust, fairness, and dignity, in which "everyone can speak." Without it, sharp-end stakeholders will not give accurate accounts of what happened — they will either go silent or curate. The Thirtymile-Cramer-Esperanza arc is the canonical worked example: criminal prosecution of firefighters after the first two events led to non-cooperation in the third. Stakeholder engagement in the wake of accidents is impossible without explicit administrative-firewall commitments. (Source: Part 1, "Just Culture"; Appendix A, "A Deeper Dive Into Just Culture")

2. <!-- concept: accountability --> **Accountability redefined as giving accounts, not receiving punishments.** "An account is something you tell and learn from. Accountability is taking that story and looking towards the future..." The Glossary makes the etymology explicit. In stakeholder engagement, this redefinition shifts the engagement from interrogation to invitation. The accompanying term *Accountabilism* names the failure mode: claiming to want accounts but punishing via judgmental language or training-as-punishment. (Source: Glossary, "Accountability"; Glossary, "Accountabilism")

3. <!-- concept: administrative-firewall --> **The administrative firewall is non-negotiable.** "It is critical to maintain a solid firewall between the FLA and any potential administrative actions that may be taken against the employee. Information is the lifeblood of safety. We must cherish it and protect it." The list of administrative actions to exclude is explicit: disciplinary actions, forced retraining, removal from incident, reduction in planned overtime. Without this commitment in writing in the delegation of authority, the engagement will not produce honest accounts. (Source: Part 3, "Administrative assurance of no punitive actions")

4. <!-- concept: stakeholder-inventory --> **The participant role-and-stakeholder inventory.** Distinct stakeholder categories with distinct engagement requirements: (a) those directly involved; (b) supervisors; (c) project planners, project leaders, Delegating Officials; (d) cooperators, outfitters, permittees, interagency partners; (e) family members of those killed or seriously injured (Family Liaison role); (f) union representatives; (g) regulators (OSHA, OIG — separated from FLA process, evidentiary material shared but team deliberations confidential); (h) the community of practice (focus groups for Learning Review). (Source: Part 5, "Potential FLA team members and roles"; Part 6, "Participants")

5. <!-- concept: trust-bridge-liaisons --> **Liaisons as trust-bridge stakeholders.** "Provide employees with a trusted liaison who can explain the FLA process and who will be available to support employees until the FLA is completed." Multiple liaison roles are named: Family Liaisons (for fatalities), Hospital Liaisons, CISM Coordinators, CAP Coordinators, Employee Assistance Program liaisons, agency representatives, IMT liaisons, regional/state office liaisons, cooperator liaisons. (Source: Part 5, "Priority Delegating Official Actions"; Appendix E)

6. <!-- concept: restorative-justice --> **Restorative justice as an engagement modality.** "After a serious accident, employees are often emotionally harmed, and relationships can be strained or broken... It allows all parties to discuss how they have been affected and collaboratively decide how they want to proceed." The Restorative Justice Checklist (Appendix F) is the operational tool. The framing draws on Dekker & Breakey 2016 *Safety Science* [BT]. (Source: Part 1, "Just Culture"; Appendix F; Glossary, "Restorative justice")

7. <!-- concept: setting-the-stage --> **Setting the stage for engagement.** "Throughout the FLA process, team members should be prepared to explain the history of FLA and its major underpinnings, such as the degree of confidentiality, Just Culture and The Gap." Without explicit framing, participants often worry that "this time the 'investigators' are out to get them." (Source: Part 6, "Setting the stage")

8. <!-- concept: participant-control --> **Giving participants control over the setting.** "The team should attempt to provide as comfortable environment as possible. Perhaps the best way to accomplish this is to give as much control as possible to the participants." Practical options: engine bay, saw shop, tac shed, picnic tables; the field where the incident occurred is "almost always" the best location. (Source: Part 6, "Site visits, Interviews and Group Dialogue sessions")

9. <!-- concept: two-interviewer-team --> **The two-interviewer team for key participants.** "For key accident participants or witnesses, the team leader should consider using a team of two interviewers. One interviewer will take notes, while the other engages the participant." The caveat: "because using two interviewers can sometimes be intimidating to the interviewee, this approach should be used with appropriate caution and understanding." (Source: Part 6, "Interviews")

10. <!-- concept: deflection-questions --> **Deflection questions to handle emotional recall.** A specific protocol: if a participant takes the interview into a difficult point of recall, the interviewer is prepared with deflection questions to mentally walk the person away from vivid memories. Worked example: "take me back to that morning when you guys were at the engine bay, what time did your duty day start?" The deflection question uses something that went right or that the person knows well. (Source: Part 6, "Interviews")

11. <!-- concept: ten-principles-and-agreements --> **The Ten Principles and Agreements for the group dialogue.** A formal opening agreement covering: no administrative actions; respectful discussion; learning matters more than past blame; willingness to be vulnerable; the FLA team is not here to fix a problem; we all make mistakes; almost all human actions are intuitive responses based on past experience; accidents are usually combinations of normal performance variability and unlikely event sequences; safety is never absolute; safety = the reasonableness of risk. (Source: Part 6, "Principles and Agreements for All Involved in the Facilitation")

12. <!-- concept: facilitator-moves --> **Facilitator moves for productive dialogue.** Named explicitly: "Location, location, location" (the field is almost always best); "You are not your point of view" (open with a story where a smart person was convinced and wrong); "Invite uncertainty" (dare people to suspend certainty and listen for what they did not know); "Exploit points of departure" (moments of inconsistency that reveal different perspectives); "Encourage sensemaking, not adjudication"; "Listen for the silent voice" (work quiet participants into the dialogue). (Source: Part 6, "Suggestions to FLA Facilitator for the Group Dialogue Session")

13. <!-- concept: points-of-departure --> **Points of departure as the engagement target, not consensus.** "Points of departure refers to the moments of inconsistency that occur in our accounts of the event that reflect the different perspectives that members hold. The idea is not to validate one story over the other but to reveal when these discrepancies occur so that all involved have a chance to see from another's perspective." (Source: Part 6, "Suggestions to FLA Facilitator"; Part 7, "The Lessons Learned Analysis")

14. <!-- concept: narrative-diversity --> **Multiple stories as resilience, not weakness.** Dekker, Cilliers, Hofmeyr 2011 [BT]: "respect otherness, difference in accounts about what happened as a value in itself. Diversity of narrative can be seen as an enormous source of resilience in complex system not as weakness." When stakeholder accounts disagree, the engagement should preserve the disagreement in the learning product. (Source: Part 8, "Different Perspectives")

15. <!-- concept: readback-validation --> **The Readback validates the story with participants.** "All of the key individuals involved with the accident should have an opportunity to hear the finalized FLA learning product's story read out loud by the FLA Team. They should be requested to correct or clarify important details and ensure that their lessons learned are captured correctly. Remember this is their story, not yours." (Source: Part 8, "Validating Stories through the Readback process"; Part 9, "Readbacks")

16. <!-- concept: concurrent-investigations --> **Concurrent investigations are insulated, not blocked.** OSHA and OIG investigations, prescribed-fire reviews, claims investigations, and law-enforcement investigations proceed alongside the FLA but are kept procedurally separate. Material evidence is shared; interview transcripts and team deliberations are confidential. The "Morning Briefing" pattern — leaders of each investigation team present themselves jointly to interviewees — is the named introduction modality. (Source: Part 5, "Cooperation with Other Investigations")

17. <!-- concept: community-of-practice-vetting --> **Community-of-practice focus groups for vetting.** The Learning Review's recommendations are vetted through focus groups drawn from communities of practice: "line officers, engine crewmembers, hotshots, helitack crewmembers, wildland fire module crewmembers, sawyers, trail crew members, timber markers." The purpose is to "concentrate on understanding the applicability of conditions to the actual (real) world of how work is accomplished." (Source: Part 10, "An overview of the process")

18. <!-- concept: learning-review-board --> **The Learning Review Board as final stakeholder ratification.** Chaired by the Delegating Official or designee, the LRB receives the complex narrative, the network of influences map, and the proposed recommendations from the focus groups, and approves or modifies them. The board encompasses "a broad range of experience and diverse backgrounds." Approved recommendations become a Safety Action Plan. (Source: Part 11, "Learning Review Board")

19. <!-- concept: learning-products --> **Two learning products for two stakeholder audiences.** The Organizational Learning Product targets policy-makers and senior leaders; it focuses on the gap between work-as-imagined and work-as-performed. The Field Learning Product targets district and field-office employees; it can take any format (multimedia, podcast, story map, scenario-based exercise, narrative) and is designed for dialogue, simulation, or personal reflection. (Source: Part 11, "Learning Review Products")

20. <!-- concept: storytelling --> **Storytelling as the primary stakeholder-engagement artefact.** "Storytelling is widely recognized by educators as the most effective tool for experiential teaching and leading cultural change within an organization." The story is a factual reconstruction told with sensory detail, character development, and the participants' internal voices. Multiple perspectives are preserved. (Source: Part 8, "Why FLA Storytelling?"; Glossary, "Storytelling")

21. <!-- concept: peer-participation --> **Brief peer participation is non-negotiable.** Appendix E flags this as CRITICAL: "Ensure that peers are part of the team. The importance of having people on the team that are 'peers' to the people involved in the unintended outcome cannot be overstated. Don't try to save money or minimize the footprint of the team by cutting these positions." (Source: Appendix E, "Lessons and Advice for fla teams")

## Questions to Ask During Stakeholder Engagement

### Phase 1: Mapping the stakeholder set

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Who is directly involved in the event? | The sharp-end stakeholders — the people whose decisions and perceptions are the subject of the engagement. They are the priority for interviewing and for the group dialogue. |
| Who are the supervisors and project leaders? | These stakeholders' perspectives on risk should be interviewed, but presence in the group dialogue is a tradeoff. Strong supervisors may suppress the voices of those with different perspectives. |
| Who is the Delegating Official and what is their commitment? | The Delegating Official's signed assurance of no punitive action is the precondition for honest engagement. Without this in writing, the engagement cannot proceed. |
| Who are the affected non-employee stakeholders? | Families of those killed or injured; cooperators, outfitters, permittees; interagency partners; the public (via the learning product). Each requires a distinct engagement modality. |
| What concurrent investigations are running? | OSHA, OIG, claims, prescribed-fire review, law enforcement — each must be insulated from the FLA but supported with evidentiary material. The team leader is the single point of contact. |
| Who is the community of practice for vetting? | For a Learning Review, focus groups drawn from the relevant CoP are the vetting mechanism. |
| Whose voice will be hardest to hear? | The silent voice — quiet participants, junior crewmembers, contractors without permanent status, family members — needs deliberate engagement design. |

### Phase 2: Establishing the conditions for honest accounts

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Is the administrative firewall in writing? | The delegation of authority must explicitly commit to no disciplinary action, no forced retraining, no removal from incident, no reduction in planned overtime. |
| Have trusted liaisons been assigned? | Multiple liaison roles: Family, Hospital, CISM, CAP, EAP, agency representative, IMT, regional/state office, cooperator. Each bridges the agency to a stakeholder group. |
| Does each participant understand the FLA process and its degree of confidentiality? | Without explicit framing — Just Culture, the Gap, the no-punitive-action assurance — participants will default to investigation-style defensiveness. |
| Is there pre-trauma support in place? | CISM Teams, EAP, mental health professional contacts — both for participants and for FLA team members themselves. |
| Has the Delegating Official committed to disseminating the lessons publicly? | Appendix C asks this as a yes-or-no precondition; if no, the FLA process should be dropped. |

### Phase 3: Conducting the interviews

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Who is interviewing whom, and where? | Team of two for key participants (one engages, one notes), with awareness that two can be intimidating. Comfortable, participant-chosen settings — not the available conference room. |
| Has rapport been built before the substantive questions? | The deflection-question protocol depends on knowing what the participant is comfortable with. The opening of the interview is for rapport, not for substantive questions. |
| Is the participant being walked away from re-traumatising recall? | Deflection questions ready: "take me back to that morning when you guys were at the engine bay, what time did your duty day start?" |
| Are quotes being captured? | The FLA facilitator should remind interviewers to collect quotes for use in the learning product. |
| Is the interviewer aware of their biases? | Hindsight bias, fundamental attribution error, anchoring on first impressions — all need explicit team conversation (Handout C is the watch-out list). |
| Is the team debriefing daily? | The lead facilitator schedules end-of-day sharing — interview results, adjustments, team well-being. |

### Phase 4: Designing the group dialogue

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Have the Ten Principles and Agreements been read aloud and agreed? | The opening agreement is the procedural commitment for the engagement. |
| Has the location been chosen to give participants comfort and control? | Field > engine bay > saw shop > picnic tables > available conference room. The setting is the stage. |
| Are supervisors and Delegating Officials in the room or out? | As a general rule, supervisors should not be present unless they were directly involved in the event. |
| Are silent voices designed into the agenda? | The facilitator's job is to attend to quiet participants and work them in. |
| Are points of departure being surfaced rather than adjudicated? | Inconsistencies between accounts are the engagement target. The facilitator's job is to make the inconsistencies visible, not to decide which account is right. |
| Are sand-table / Google Earth / ArcMap aids set up? | Reconstruction aids help participants share what they perceived; the act of placing a marker on a map often surfaces a disagreement about position or timing. |

### Phase 5: Reconstructing the participants' sensemaking

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Have the team's beliefs, perceptions, expectations, and paradigms before the event been surfaced? | The four-category decomposition from the Lessons Learned Analysis is the question scaffold. |
| Has the Five Hows been applied to each key decision or action? | Use the hows that are helpful; skip the redundant ones. The intent is to reach the participants' pre-event situational awareness. |
| Is the tension between team-member perspectives being preserved? | Consensus is not the goal. Tension is valuable. |
| Are conditions being identified rather than causes attributed? | Environmental, social, cultural, political conditions — the unit of analysis is conditions, not characters. |

### Phase 6: Capturing and validating the story

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Does the story respect multiple perspectives? | Different accounts of the event should be preserved in the learning product, with non-judgmental language. Diversity of narrative is resilience. |
| Has the story been read aloud to those directly involved first? | The readback is in two phases — those directly involved first, then other participants, supervisors, and administrators. Hard copies are collected afterward. |
| Have the participants had a chance to correct or clarify? | "Remember this is their story, not yours." Even controversial comments belong in the learning product if captured appropriately in context. |
| Have potentially identifying details been removed? | The story tells us who these people are without using actual identifying information; sensory and contextual detail substitutes for personal data. |

### Phase 7: Vetting recommendations (Learning Review only)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Have focus groups been convened from the relevant communities of practice? | Multiple focus groups may be required — line officers, sharp-end crew members, technical specialists, supervisors. Each provides a different perspective on the applicability of conditions to real-world work. |
| Have academic SMEs been engaged? | Where appropriate — to provide an outside perspective on organisational operations and to ensure recommendations are consistent with current research. |
| Has the Learning Review Board reviewed the products? | Chaired by the Delegating Official; broad range of experience and diverse backgrounds; final ratification of recommendations. |
| Has a Safety Action Plan been written with owners and deadlines? | Each recommendation needs a named owner and a deadline. The Delegating Official is responsible for follow-up. |

### Phase 8: Restoring relationships (restorative justice)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Who has been harmed, and what do they need? | The Restorative Justice Checklist (Appendix F) is the operational tool. |
| Whose obligation is it to meet those needs? | The organisation's obligation, not the individual's. Restorative justice is about repairing trust and relationships, not about punishing offenders. |
| Have all affected parties had a chance to discuss how they have been affected? | Restorative justice "allows all parties to discuss how they have been affected and collaboratively decide how they want to proceed." |
| Is there ongoing support for the FLA team itself? | FLA team members may also need mental health support after engaging with traumatic material. |

### Phase 9: Engaging the broader organisation

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| What learning products are right for which audiences? | Organizational Learning Product (for policy-makers and senior leaders) and Field Learning Product (for district and field-office employees). |
| What format reaches the audience best? | Written reports, multimedia, podcasts, story maps, scenario-based exercises, tabletop exercises. |
| Is the story inspiring rich dialogue rather than offering closure? | Field Learning Products should ask the reader/viewer to reflect, not deliver conclusions. |
| Have lessons been emphasised emotively, experientially, or reflectively? | Dialogue, simulation, personal reflection — each is appropriate in different contexts. |

## What to Look For

**Signal:** Participants in the group dialogue give short, careful, non-committal answers.
**Diagnosis:** The administrative firewall commitment has not been made in writing, or participants are not aware of it. The engagement atmosphere is investigation-flavoured, not learning-flavoured.
**Follow-up:** Pause the group dialogue. Return to the Ten Principles and Agreements and, if needed, to the Delegating Official for written confirmation of the no-punitive-action commitment.

**Signal:** One stakeholder group (e.g., supervisors) is physically present in the group dialogue and junior participants are visibly silent.
**Diagnosis:** Power-asymmetric seating or invitation design has suppressed the voices the engagement most needs.
**Follow-up:** Restructure the dialogue — separate workshops for different groups if needed. Supervisors' perspectives on risk are captured through interviews, not through presence in the group dialogue.

**Signal:** The FLA team's draft learning product reads like an accident report, naming individuals and attributing blame.
**Diagnosis:** The condition-not-character framing has not been applied to the draft narrative.
**Follow-up:** Rewrite from conditions outward. Strip identifying information; replace individual-attribution language with situational and systemic language.

**Signal:** The Learning Review's recommendations are rejected by the communities of practice they were meant to help.
**Diagnosis:** Focus groups from the relevant CoP were either not convened or were convened too late in the process to shape the recommendations.
**Follow-up:** Return the recommendations to CoP focus groups before finalisation. The point is not validation but applicability — do these recommendations match the actual world of how work is accomplished?

## When to Use This Reference

- An unintended outcome has occurred and the organisation needs a learning process that will produce honest accounts without triggering self-protective silence.
- The engagement will involve participants at different levels of the power hierarchy and the risk of suppression is real.
- Restorative justice is needed — relationships and trust have been damaged and the next engagement event is not possible without repairing them first.
- Community-of-practice vetting is needed — recommendations that come from a small investigation team without CoP endorsement are likely to fail in implementation.
- The learning product needs to reach two distinct audiences (policy-level and field-level) with different registers and formats.
- The engagement involves concurrent investigations (OSHA, OIG) and the procedural separation between the FLA and those investigations needs explicit design.

## Worked Example

A regional wilderness management agency experiences a near-miss: a chainsaw crew working a post-storm debris clear encounters an unmarked buried utility line. No one is injured, but the incident triggers an FLA because the crew was six inches from a fatality.

The Delegating Official (a district ranger) signs the administrative firewall commitment — no disciplinary action, no forced retraining, no removal — before anyone is interviewed. A Process Coach and Lead Facilitator are brought in from outside the district. Two trusted liaisons are identified: one from the crew's union, one from the district's safety office who knows the crew personally.

The Lead Facilitator conducts separate semi-structured interviews (team of two) with each crew member, the crew leader, and the project planner who authorised the debris clearance. The interview setting for the crew is the crew's own equipment bay — participant-chosen, on their ground. For the crew leader, it is a picnic table outside the ranger station.

One interview surfaces a point of departure: the crew's understanding of the pre-work hazard briefing differs materially from the project planner's account of what was communicated. The group dialogue is designed to surface this without adjudicating it: "Exploit points of departure" is the named move.

The Ten Principles and Agreements are read aloud at the start of the group dialogue. The Delegating Official is not present; the district safety officer is present as a learning participant, not as an evaluator. The crew leader is seated beside the crew, not across from them.

The resulting story preserves both accounts of the hazard briefing as separate, valid perspectives — not adjudicated, but placed beside each other so the organisational learning can address both the briefing design (work-as-imagined) and the briefing reception (work-as-performed).

The scenario is operator-authored. ## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Engaging stakeholders without first committing to the administrative firewall.** Without the no-punitive-action assurance in writing, honest accounts will not surface.
- **Treating accountability as receiving punishment** rather than as giving an account. The criminal-justice frame for accountability undermines the stakeholder engagement before it starts.
- **Using investigation vocabulary** ("investigators," "the investigation") when the engagement is meant to be a learning process. Participants will default to defensiveness.
- **Placing supervisors in the group dialogue with sharp-end participants** without thinking through power dynamics. Strong supervisors suppress voices with different perspectives.
- **Skipping the deflection-question protocol** during emotionally heavy interviews, leaving participants stuck in re-traumatising recall.
- **Working toward consensus** in the group dialogue rather than surfacing points of departure. Different perspectives are the value the engagement is supposed to produce.
- **Adjudicating which account is right** when multiple accounts disagree. Diversity of narrative is resilience; preservation, not adjudication, is the engagement output.
- **Cutting peer participation from the team to save money or footprint.** Appendix E flags this as CRITICAL.
- **Allowing concurrent investigations to contaminate the FLA process.** OSHA, OIG, law enforcement proceed alongside but separated; team leader is the single point of contact.
- **Producing recommendations without community-of-practice vetting.** The Learning Review's focus groups exist specifically to prevent recommendations that are unworkable at the sharp end.
- **Engaging the broader organisation through a single learning product** when senior leaders and field-office employees need different formats and registers.
- **Skipping restorative justice** after a serious accident; relationships and trust do not heal on their own.
- **Cutting trusted liaisons from the post-incident response.** Liaisons are the trust bridge; without them, sharp-end stakeholders default to anxiety and silence.
- **Allowing the FLA team to operate without daily debriefs.** Team well-being, calibration of interviewing technique, and bias-spotting all depend on this rhythm.
