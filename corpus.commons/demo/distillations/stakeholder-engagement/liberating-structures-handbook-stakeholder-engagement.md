## Stakeholder-Engagement Relevance

The handbook is, at its core, a stakeholder-engagement source. The full title is *Engaging Everyone with Liberating Structures* — the word *everyone* is load-bearing, not rhetorical. The handbook's introduction frames LS as "frameworks that make it possible for people and organizations to create, to do new things, to be innovative." The half-life problem the handbook is responding to is squarely about engagement durability: "The half-life of the energy and commitment to new ways of being after these events can be short when participants return to their organizations and fall back into default ways of meeting. Frequently, the changes are not sustained. So, how can we extend that half-life? How can we make the enlivening experience that characterizes these energetic events available every day? How can we put the power to host and facilitate high engagement in the hands of everyone in the organization?"

The handbook's most direct stakeholder-engagement framing is captured in two paired principles. *Nothing About Me Without Me* is the standing principle: "When searching in your organization or community for behavior and solutions that are unusually powerful and effective (positively deviant), it helps to broaden your conversations to include the people most involved. This is especially powerful when you can bring together people that don't normally talk with one another." *Engage the Unusual Suspects* is the inviting principle: "Who's not here that could contribute to our conversation?" The handbook calls this "one of the most important questions you can ask."

The ownership-versus-buy-in distinction is also an engagement distinction. Buy-in is the failure mode of a *consultative* style — appearing participatory while in fact asking people to implement someone else's decision. The handbook's claim is that aiming for buy-in produces "lukewarm, pallid implementation and mediocre results"; that for "intractable socio-technical behavioral problems in systems the notion of buy-in is just not useful — people in the system need to own the new behaviors." Every named LS interaction design is positioned as an instrument for ownership rather than buy-in.

A second engagement-discipline thread is *space and aesthetics as engagement infrastructure*. The handbook's first three named sections after the introduction (Choosing Space, Aesthetics Are Important, Working with the Space You're Given) treat tables, chairs, lighting, walls, and elbow-room as structural variables, not logistics. A standard conference-room round table is too large for "juicy conversation"; fluorescent light makes people "wilt"; rows of fixed chairs can be bent by inviting people to stand and turn.

A third thread is the handbook's contrarianism about *engagement forums*. Multiple structures replace conventional default formats: Celebrity Interviews replace panels; Gallery Walks replace report-outs (which are flagged "BORING!!!" because they "drain the life out of whatever conversation that took place in the small group"); Taking Questions in batches of 3–4 replaces serial Q-A-Q-A, which "reinforces the 'central tendency' versus the distributed intelligence of the group"; Take 20 Seconds replaces the facilitator's 6-second-average wait.

A fourth thread is the *pattern-language* framing of engagement: any LS structure is composed from four primitives — "rhythm/timing of each round of interaction, the boundaries of group size and inclusion, physical containers like space and room set-up, and conceptual containers created by the way a question is phrased." Designing engagement is choosing within these primitives, and the catalogue is a vocabulary of named combinations rather than a recipe book.

## Key Concepts for Stakeholder Engagement

1. **Engagement begins upstream of the room — at the invitation list.** "Who's not here that could contribute to our conversation? This may be one of the most important questions you can ask. Widen the network. Get more voices into the conversation." Companion principle: "Thinking about the Unusual Suspects is an important part of creating an invitation list for any gathering you design." (Source: *Engaging Everyone with LS Handbook*, "Engage the Unusual Suspects"; "Nothing About Me Without Me")

2. **"Nothing About Me Without Me" is a substantive engagement principle, not a slogan.** Two worked examples: a nurse saying "we would wear gloves if we had the supplies, but they never bring enough" prompts the question "Who could we talk with in supplies that might be able to help us fix this?" The principle re-frames complaints-about-others as invitations to engage. ("Nothing About Me Without Me")

3. **Buy-in is the diagnostic word for an engagement failure.** "Aiming for buy-in creates lukewarm, pallid implementation and mediocre results… Anytime you or someone around you thinks or talks about buy-in, beware! It is a danger signal telling you that your development and implementation process is missing the essential ingredient of involving all who should be involved." ("Ownership versus Buy-in")

4. **Mind the "window-dressing" trap on invitations.** "One of the challenges in doing this is figuring out how to craft an invitation that feels authentic and welcoming. Many people have been to too many meetings where they were invited as window dressing without the opportunity to contribute, so they may be suspicious." Inviting people who have been historically tokenised requires explicit re-establishment of trust about how their participation will matter. ("Engage the Unusual Suspects")

5. **Space and aesthetics are engagement infrastructure.** "Many meetings and events work well with NO TABLES in the room." Small "4-tops" beat the round tables conference centres usually provide ("too large for juicy conversation because people end up too far apart and have to yell across to be heard in a full room"). Natural light, colour, flowers, long walls for graphic recording — treated as structural rather than decorative. ("Choosing Space"; "Aesthetics Are Important"; "Working with the Space You're Given")

6. **The storyboard makes the participant experience visible before the event.** "Try using a storyboard instead of an agenda… Unlike an agenda, it also includes and illustrates something about what the participants will experience." Diagnostic: "if your storyboard has a whole lot of podium sessions in a row, it's not going to be very engaging!" The storyboard is a pre-engagement design tool that an agenda is not. ("Storyboard Design and Planning")

7. **Get everyone speaking early — the air-time arithmetic.** Impromptu Speed Networking: "everyone gets to speak early in the meeting. The distance between not having said anything and making a first comment is vast. But the distance between having said something and saying another thing is much smaller." In a 30-minute session, everyone will have had 12-15 minutes of air time — no matter how many participants. ("Impromptu Speed Networking")

8. **Knee-to-knee proximity beats around-a-table.** Peter Block's [BT] framing: "the power of small groups sitting knee-to-knee — without the mediating effect of a table. Whenever possible invite participants to move closer together to create more intimacy." ("Knee-to-Knee Conversation")

9. **Avoid one-voice-for-more-than-five-minutes.** Celebrity Interviews replace conventional panels in part to "Avoid draining/deadening the energy in the meeting, which happens when people have to listen to any one voice for more than 5 minutes — especially if they are being given large amounts of data particularly text on slides." ("Celebrity Interviews")

10. **Batch questions; refuse Q-A-Q-A.** "If you invite participants to ask questions or share comments after Celebrity Interviews or other exercises, have participants speak in batches (3-4 at a time) rather than 1 by 1." The serial pattern "reinforces the 'central tendency' versus the distributed intelligence of the group." ("Taking Questions")

11. **Wait at least 20 seconds after asking a question.** "20 seconds of silence in a group can feel like a very long time. On average, facilitators begin speaking after six seconds." During the pause "people in the group are often formulating their answers and working up the courage to respond. If you start talking too soon, you truncate that process and communicate, unwittingly, that you're the one with the answers." Tactic: "look down at your shoes" to break eye contact. ("Take 20 Seconds")

12. **Report-outs drain energy; bring the experience into the next conversation instead.** "'Report Outs' from breakout sessions are BORING!!!… most of the time, we don't really need to hear serial summaries of all the discussions. And, it typically drains the life out of whatever conversation that took place in the small group. It is more effective to ask participants to bring the experience they had in previous conversations into a new conversation. The important stuff will turn up again." Gallery Walk is named as a poster-based alternative. ("A Note about Report Outs"; "Gallery Walk")

13. **Wise Crowds: structural humility of the listening role.** Three-phase structure: the client turns their chair around so their back is to the consulting team, and listens silently while the team converses for at least 15 minutes. "The Consulting Team's job is not to decide 'this is what The Client ought to do.'" The client returns with "Here's what I heard that intrigued, surprised, or resonated with me." ("Wise Crowds")

14. **Troika Consulting: peer engagement at three.** Three-person 10-minute rounds; each gets a turn as focal person. Partners first ask questions to hone and improve ideas, then partners engage with each other, and finally with the participant. Three rounds per group; closing pattern-noticing conversation. ("Troika Consulting")

15. **Appreciative Interviews: focus shapes engagement.** "What we focus on tends to get bigger. So focusing on what works rather than on what doesn't work creates the context for more progress." Story-based interviews about "a time when…" something worked. Lineage: Appreciative Inquiry [BT] (appreciativeinquiry.case.edu). ("Appreciative Interviews")

16. **World Café: large-group engagement in small-table rounds.** Four chairs per table; three or more 20-minute rounds; "each member of the group moves to a different new table. They may or may not choose to leave one person as the 'table host' for the next round, who welcomes the next group and briefly fills them in." Lineage: theworldcafe.com [BT]. ("World Café")

17. **Conversation Café: six agreements as engagement scaffolding.** Open-mindedness, Acceptance, Curiosity, Discovery, Sincerity, Brevity. Three structured rounds plus open dialogue plus final round, mediated by a talking object. Lineage: conversationcafe.org [BT]. ("Conversation Café Dialogue")

18. **Fishbowl: making lived experience audible to the larger group.** Inner circle of 5–6 with direct experience talk to each other ("NOT the audience"); outer circle asks questions; then dialogue between the circles. Centers the experience that conventional forum design tends to translate, dilute, or speak for. ("Fishbowl")

19. **Just In Time Topics: agenda formation as engagement.** Self-organising agenda from Open Space lineage [BT] (openspaceworld.org). Operating laws: "Whoever comes are the right people" and "The law of two feet. If you're not contributing or getting value, use your feet to go somewhere else." ("Just In Time Topics")

20. **Network Weaving: mapping engagement infrastructure across organisations.** Participants draw maps of network connections; analytic moves include closing triangles, integrating clusters, and expanding the periphery — explicitly asking which "types of individuals, organizations or perspectives are missing or underrepresented (gender, race/ethnicity, class, neighborhoods, etc)". ("Network Weaving")

21. **Tingsha bells, talking objects, music: the small-craft of engagement.** Yelling and microphone-tapping are explicitly rejected as recall mechanisms; bells (Tingsha or "Kid's xylophones also work") preserve mood. Talking objects signal the speaking role. Music and poetry "create the conditions for creativity and out-of-the-box thinking." ("Tingsha Bells"; "Talking Objects"; "Music & Poetry")

22. **Discovery & Action Dialogues: stakeholder engagement that ends in named next steps.** Six-question protocol ending in "What has to happen next to make that happen? Who will do what when next?" The last two questions are the structural commitment that distinguishes a DAD from a conversation. Lineage: Plexus Institute / Positive Deviance [BT]. ("Discovery & Action Dialogues")

## Questions to Ask During Stakeholder Engagement

### Phase 1: Mapping (Identifying who has standing)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Apply the "Unusual Suspects" question | Who's not here that could contribute to our conversation? Whose voice would change what we think about the situation? Whose absence will hurt the implementation? "This may be one of the most important questions you can ask." |
| Apply "Nothing About Me Without Me" as a design test | Is anyone being talked about who could be in the conversation? When a complaint about another group surfaces, re-frame: "Who could we talk with in [the absent group] that might be able to help us fix this?" |
| Map the network around the project | Run Network Weaving: have participants draw maps of who matters around this project, goal, or initiative. Then ask: "Who are network hubs? Who are isolates? What types of individuals, organizations or perspectives are missing or underrepresented?" |
| Identify the people who have historically been tokenised | "Many people have been to too many meetings where they were invited as window dressing without the opportunity to contribute, so they may be suspicious." For invitees in this category, what does authentic re-establishment of trust look like? |
| Surface the "consulting team" implicit in any decision | When someone is making a decision, who would naturally be on the consulting team? Could Wise Crowds make those voices structurally audible? |

### Phase 2: Framing (Bounding the engagement before convening)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Test for "buy-in" framing in the engagement plan | Is the engagement plan trying to produce buy-in? If yes, that's a "danger signal." Re-frame: how do we get the affected people into the development, not into the implementation? |
| Test for "expert-built scenarios for community buy-in" pattern | Is a strategic scenario, plan, or design being built by experts with the intent to bring it to the wider community? Re-frame as participatory development. |
| Storyboard the engagement before designing the agenda | Have we drawn the storyboard of what participants will experience? If the storyboard has "a whole lot of podium sessions in a row, it's not going to be very engaging!" |
| Choose the room before designing the agenda | Will the room support the engagement we're planning? "No tables" or "4-tops"; natural light; long walls for graphic recording. If the room is fixed, how do we bend it (knee-to-knee groups; stand-and-turn-around moves)? |
| Choose the rhythm before choosing the structures | What's the time-shape — a 30-minute Impromptu Speed Networking, a 10-minute-per-focal-person Troika round, three 20-minute World Café rounds? Rhythm is one of the four pattern-language primitives the handbook names. |
| Compose the question deliberately | The question is one of the four primitives ("conceptual containers created by the way a question is phrased"). Is the question wicked (paradox/tension), appreciative ("a time when…"), or operational ("Who will do what when next?")? |

### Phase 3: Convening (How to engage in the moment)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Open with a structure that gets everyone speaking early | Knee-to-Knee Conversation set up before arrival; Impromptu Speed Networking with three rounds; 1-2-4-Whole Group from individual reflection scaling up. Everyone has spoken in the first 15-30 minutes. |
| Use One Minute of Silence before any substantive question | "Everyone benefits from having a moment to think about something before jumping into a discussion." |
| Replace panels with Celebrity Interviews | "Avoid draining/deadening the energy in the meeting, which happens when people have to listen to any one voice for more than 5 minutes." |
| Take questions in batches of 3–4 | Refuse the Q-A-Q-A pattern. Collect questions on post-its or cards; let the responder combine or select. |
| Honour the 20-second wait | After asking a question, wait at least 20 seconds. Look down at your shoes if necessary. |
| Run Wise Crowds when one person needs help on a complex challenge | 5–6 person groups; client describes ~10 min; client turns back 15+ min while team converses; client rejoins with "Here's what I heard that intrigued, surprised, or resonated with me." |
| Run Troika Consulting when multiple people have parallel challenges | Groups of three; 10-minute rounds; each gets a turn as focal person, honed by two peers. |
| Use Fishbowl when lived experience needs to be centred | Inner circle of 5–6 with direct experience talk to each other, not the audience. |
| Use Discovery & Action Dialogues to surface in-system solutions and produce named next steps | Six-question protocol ending in "What has to happen next? Who will do what when next?" |

### Phase 4: Capturing (Recording what was said and decided)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Refuse default report-outs from breakouts | Use Gallery Walk (posters arrayed for walk-through) or bring the experience into the next conversation. |
| If output is genuinely needed, engage participants in the format decision | "Engage participants in a conversation about who actually needs this material and what might be the best and most useful way for them to get it." |
| Capture in modes that preserve voice | Graphic recording on long walls; post-it harvests; index cards collected from 25-to-10. The artefact preserves the participant voice rather than translating it into facilitator paraphrase. |
| End with Now-What in the AAR debrief | "WHAT? SO WHAT? NOW WHAT?" — in order. The Now What question lands the engagement by surfacing action and "Who else should be here?" — looping back to Phase 1. |
| Use DAD's final two questions to commit | "What has to happen next to make that happen? Who will do what when next?" Named-owner-and-next-step is the structural move that converts engagement into action without coercion. |

### Phase 5: Sustaining (Maintaining engagement over time)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Watch for the half-life problem | "The half-life of the energy and commitment to new ways of being after these events can be short when participants return to their organizations and fall back into default ways of meeting." What structural changes did the engagement produce, beyond the motivational ones? |
| Apply 15% Solutions across implementers | Each implementer has only their 15%. What does each person's 15% look like? Aggregate across the implementing community without waiting for the 85% (the broader context) to change first. |
| Run Appreciative Interviews on the engagement's after-life | Pairs interview each other about "a time when…" the engagement actually produced change. Surface the working patterns before debugging the broken ones. |
| Audit the Levels of Accountability | Where is the group sitting — "Take Action and Learn / Make it happen" at the top, or "Wait 'n Hope," "Blame self," "Blame others," "Denial" near the bottom? |
| Use Network Weaving's clusters-and-periphery questions periodically | "Are there as many nodes in the periphery as in the core? If not, how might you expand the network's periphery? What types of individuals, organizations or perspectives are missing or underrepresented?" |

### Phase 6: Closing (Adjourning well)

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Run a What/So What/Now What as the closing | Three sequential questions, in order. Refuse the temptation to jump to Now-What before the What and the So-What have been answered. |
| Use Take 10 (Minutes) as a closing reflection | "How has your thinking changed about [blank] since we started this morning? What have you heard that's really got your juices flowing?" |
| Use a Final Round in the Conversation-Café idiom | "Each person says briefly what challenged, touched or inspired them." Talking-object-mediated; one voice at a time. |
| Wave hands instead of clapping for in-meeting acknowledgement | "Some groups find that having a norm of waving hands in the air is nicer than clapping. It makes the point without breaking the mood." |

## What to Look For

**Signal:** The engagement ends with energy in the room but no change follows.
**Diagnosis:** The half-life problem. The engagement produced motivational flow but no structural change; when participants return to default ways of meeting, the energy dissipates.
**Follow-up:** Identify what structural change the engagement was supposed to produce. Apply 15% Solutions: what is each person's 15% that does not require the other 85% to change first? Lock named next steps using DAD's final two questions.

**Signal:** Senior leaders consistently do most of the talking in multi-stakeholder sessions.
**Diagnosis:** The default format (panel, open Q&A, round-table discussion) has no structural protection against the HiPPO failure mode.
**Follow-up:** Replace the panel with Celebrity Interviews (coached toward generative questions). Open with Knee-to-Knee Conversation before anyone speaks to the large group. Take questions in batches of 3–4, not serially.

**Signal:** Breakout groups have rich conversations; the plenary report-outs are flat and draining.
**Diagnosis:** Report-outs are translating the small-group conversation into summary, which strips the texture that made the conversation valuable.
**Follow-up:** Stop the report-outs. Ask participants to bring the experience of the small-group conversation into the next large-group conversation. Or use a Gallery Walk — posters arrayed for participants to walk through and annotate.

**Signal:** The invitation list for a planning session is the same as last time.
**Diagnosis:** The "Unusual Suspects" question was not asked.
**Follow-up:** Run Network Weaving to map the project's network. Ask explicitly: who is not here that could contribute? Which types of individuals, organisations, or perspectives are missing or underrepresented?

**Signal:** Implementation of an agreement is weak despite strong in-room commitment.
**Diagnosis:** The engagement produced buy-in, not ownership. People agreed to implement someone else's decision rather than developing the decision themselves.
**Follow-up:** Re-examine the development process. Were the people who are now implementing involved in developing? The buy-in / ownership distinction predicts this failure; the structural fix is earlier and broader involvement.

## When to Use This Reference

- An *engagement is being designed* — workshop, retreat, town hall, change-management session, retrospective — and the convener is reaching for a default panel-and-breakout structure.
- A *consultative style* is producing weak implementation and the team cannot figure out why — diagnose the buy-in trap.
- An *audience* contains people who have been historically tokenised and the invitation needs to be re-built for authentic participation.
- A *strategic scenario or plan* is being built by experts and is at risk of being ignored by the community it is meant to serve.
- A *single person has a complex challenge* and would benefit from being structurally separated from the option-generating conversation (Wise Crowds).
- A *cohort of people each have parallel challenges* and would benefit from peer-honed next-steps (Troika).
- A *post-event conversation* needs to land in named-owner-and-next-step territory rather than dissolving — DAD's last two questions or AAR's Now What.
- A *facilitator pattern* of dominating the airtime is suppressing group voice — Take 20 Seconds, batched questions, knee-to-knee small groups.
- A *meeting space* is structurally hostile to engagement — large round tables, fluorescent light, fixed rows of chairs.
- An *organisation has had energetic engagement events* whose effect did not survive the return to default ways of meeting.
- A *network or coalition* needs to be widened, clustered, or weaved across.
- A *culturally-sensitive* facilitation question — particularly around talking-stick borrowing — needs explicit handling.

## Worked Example

A housing authority is designing a community engagement process for a proposed mixed-income development in a neighbourhood with a long history of urban-renewal displacement. Residents have attended many "consultation" meetings over the years that produced no visible change. Trust is low.

The facilitation team begins with the "Unusual Suspects" question: who is not on the standard consultation list? They add recent arrivals (not on voter rolls), long-term renters (who attend few public meetings), and youth (whose futures the development will most shape). Each group gets a targeted outreach call, not a mass mailer.

The design team produces a storyboard rather than an agenda. The first 20 minutes are Impromptu Speed Networking: everyone has a partner-conversation about what they love about the neighbourhood and what they want to protect. Everyone speaks before any authority figure speaks. The senior planner is placed in a Celebrity Interview format — not at a podium, but on a chair beside an interviewer who has been coached toward generative questions ("What surprised you when you talked with residents last year?" rather than "What does the plan say?").

Breakout groups run Troika Consulting on the question "What should the development protect that's not currently on anyone's list?" Report-outs are replaced with a Gallery Walk: each table's output is posted on the wall and participants walk through and add sticky-note reactions.

The session closes with a Discovery & Action Dialogue on one identified issue — the displacement risk for current renters. The final two questions land: "What has to happen next?" produces three named commitments with owners and dates.

Post-session: the facilitation team uses Network Weaving to map which participants now know each other, and which isolates (single-household participants with no other network connections) need follow-up.

The scenario is operator-authored. The "Unusual Suspects" framing, the buy-in/ownership distinction, the storyboard discipline, Impromptu Speed Networking air-time arithmetic, Celebrity Interviews, Troika Consulting, Gallery Walk, Discovery & Action Dialogues, and Network Weaving are all `[V]`-marked passages in the deep reference. No verbatim source blockquotes appear in this distillation.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Selecting participants by who is easy to invite** — the "Unusual Suspects" question and the "Nothing About Me Without Me" principle are the antidote.
- **Designing an engagement to produce buy-in rather than ownership** — buy-in is the diagnostic word for an engagement failure.
- **Running a panel when the goal is distributed intelligence** — Celebrity Interviews with generative questions replace panels; one voice for more than five minutes drains the room.
- **Serial Q-A-Q-A** — batches of 3–4 questions preserve the group's distributed intelligence rather than reinforcing the "central tendency."
- **Asking a question and filling the silence in under six seconds** — the 20-second wait discipline is what allows participation to form.
- **Running breakout report-outs** — they drain the energy the breakout produced; Gallery Walk or bringing-the-experience-forward are the alternatives.
- **Treating the room setup as logistics rather than design** — the round table that is too large for "juicy conversation" is an engagement-quality variable.
- **Inviting historically-tokenised groups without addressing the trust deficit** — "many people have been to too many meetings where they were invited as window dressing."
- **Designing an engagement that stops at motivation without landing structural change** — the half-life problem names this; 15% Solutions and DAD's final two questions are the structural moves.
- **Running a talking-stick exercise without naming its lineage** — the handbook cites Dr. Locust (1998) and recommends either naming the borrowing or choosing a non-religious object.
- **Treating the engagement event as the outcome** — the Network Weaving questions and the Levels of Accountability audit are the sustained-engagement tools, not the event.
