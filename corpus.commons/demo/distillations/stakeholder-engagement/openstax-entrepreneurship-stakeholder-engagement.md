# OpenStax Entrepreneurship, Stakeholder-Engagement Distillation

**Source:** OpenStax (2020). *Entrepreneurship*. Rice University. Senior contributing authors Michael Laverty and Chris Littel; 19 contributing authors. CC BY-NC-SA 4.0.

## Stakeholder-Engagement Relevance

The OpenStax *Entrepreneurship* text gives stakeholder engagement a load-bearing role from chapter three onward. The text formally distinguishes shareholders from stakeholders and treats the older shareholder-primacy doctrine as superseded by the 2019 Business Roundtable position that customers, employees, suppliers, communities, and the environment are all parties to whom the entrepreneur owes consideration (Ch 3.1, "Stakeholders"). The text returns to stakeholder framing across the venture life cycle: pitching to multiple audiences with different asks (Ch 7.3), founders' agreements among the startup team (Ch 15.1), customer-empathy mapping (Ch 11.2), advisor and network building (Ch 12), and post-harvest mentoring/championing (Ch 15.4).

For stakeholder engagement practitioners, the text is especially useful on the differential-audience pitch matrix (Ch 7.3, Table 7.3), the customer-discovery and value-proposition tools (Ch 11.2), the social entrepreneurship and B-corp canvases (Ch 3.2, Ch 11.2), and the founders'-agreement template (Ch 15.1). It is less developed on conflict resolution among stakeholders with directly competing interests; that work is implied in the founders'-agreement and buyback-clause discussion but not given its own framework treatment.

## Key Concepts for Stakeholder Engagement

1. <!-- concept: shareholder-vs-stakeholder --> **Shareholder vs. stakeholder distinction.** The text contrasts shareholders, "a small group who are the owners (or stockholders)", with stakeholders, "a large group that includes all those people and organizations with a vested interest in the business" [V] (Source: OpenStax, *Entrepreneurship*, Ch 3.1, "Stakeholders").

2. <!-- concept: business-roundtable --> **Business Roundtable stakeholder commitment.** The 2019 statement commits to delivering value to customers, investing in employees, dealing fairly with suppliers, supporting communities, and generating long-term shareholder value. (Source: Ch 3.1)

3. <!-- concept: customer-empathy-map --> **Customer empathy map.** Portrays a target customer's pains, gains, and jobs-to-be-done as a design-ideation tool. (Source: Ch 11.2)

4. <!-- concept: value-proposition-canvas --> **Value proposition canvas.** Pulls customer segment and value proposition out of the Business Model Canvas for in-depth fit analysis. (Source: Ch 11.2)

5. <!-- concept: social-entrepreneurship --> **Social entrepreneurship.** Social entrepreneurship "describes ventures launched by entrepreneurs who are first and foremost advocates or champions for a social cause" [V] (Source: Ch 3.2, "Social Entrepreneurship") — building economically viable organisations to advance the cause.

6. <!-- concept: b-corporation --> **B-corporation certification.** A formal certification by B Lab that audits compliance with social-and-environmental performance, public-transparency, and accountability standards. (Source: Ch 3.2)

7. <!-- concept: four-lenses-framework --> **Four Lenses Strategic Framework.** Used for social-enterprise development, assessing stakeholder engagement, resource mobilisation, knowledge development, and culture management. (Source: Ch 2.4)

8. <!-- concept: pitching --> **Audience-tailored pitch matrix.** Different audiences (friends and family, elevator-pitch competitions, investors, employees, trade groups, grant-making agencies) require different pitch lengths, content, and asks. (Source: Ch 7.3, Table 7.3)

9. <!-- concept: founders-agreement --> **Founders' agreement.** Pre-launch document covering compensation, vesting, contributions, performance evaluation, dispute resolution, and a buyback clause. (Source: Ch 15.1)

10. <!-- concept: code-of-conduct --> **Code of conduct.** Formal statement aligning organisational behaviour with values; can be principle-based or rule-based. (Source: Ch 15.1)

11. <!-- concept: networking-groups --> **Networking groups: open, closed, hybrid.** Differ on membership barriers and signal commitment level. (Source: Ch 12.1)

12. <!-- concept: entrepreneur-advisors --> **Five advisor types.** Accountant, attorney, banker, insurance agent, industry expert make up the entrepreneurial dream team. (Source: Ch 12.2)

13. <!-- concept: weak-ties --> **Strong vs. weak ties.** Weak ties bridge across functional areas and create opportunities; strong ties carry deep support but offer fewer new opportunities. (Source: Ch 6.1, citing Granovetter)

14. <!-- concept: resource-dependence --> **Resource dependence theory.** Mergers, vertical integration, joint ventures, and joint political activity manage external dependencies among same-industry stakeholders. (Source: Ch 14.3, citing Pfeffer and Salancik)

15. <!-- concept: post-harvest-roles --> **Three post-harvest stakeholder roles.** Mentor (informal), consultant (formal), champion (network-builder). (Source: Ch 15.4)

16. <!-- concept: social-capital --> **Social capital.** Social capital is "the goodwill we create by giving back to individuals, organizations, and communities" [V] (Source: Ch 15.4).

17. <!-- concept: brand-advocacy --> **Customer service and brand advocacy.** Loyal customers passing on the word; the chapter treats brand advocates as a stakeholder category to recruit and reward. (Source: Ch 8.4)

18. <!-- concept: corporate-social-responsibility --> **CSR and sustainability.** CSR is "the practice by which a business views itself within a broader context: as a member of society with certain implicit social obligations and environmental considerations and issues" [V] (Source: Ch 3.2, "Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)"). Sustainability is defined as "the ability to be maintained at a certain rate or level" [V] (Ch 3.2, "Sustainability"), extending beyond environmental concerns to natural resources, human resources, and supply chains.

19. <!-- concept: benefit-corporation --> **Stakeholder mapping for a benefit corporation.** Benefit corporations direct decisions toward maximisation of benefits for all stakeholders, not solely shareholders. (Source: Ch 13.2)

20. <!-- concept: ethics-survey --> **Personnel ethics survey findings.** 60 per cent of reported workplace misconduct involved someone with managerial authority; strong-ethics-culture firms see 60 per cent of misconduct as one-time. (Source: Ch 15.2, citing Ethics Resource Center)

## Questions to Ask During Stakeholder Engagement

### Phase 1: Stakeholder identification

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Map the stakeholder universe | Who beyond the shareholders has a vested interest in this venture? Customers, employees, suppliers, community, environment, regulators? (Ch 3.1) |
| Distinguish primary from secondary | Whose support is required for the venture to exist, and whose support shapes its long-term sustainability? (Ch 3.1) |
| Identify the social mission stakeholders | If this is a social enterprise, who are the beneficiaries and how are they distinct from the customers? (Ch 3.2, Ch 11.2 social BMC) |
| Identify the regulatory stakeholders | Which government agencies, certifications, and licensing bodies have authority over this venture? (Ch 14.1) |
| Identify the network stakeholders | Who in our local community, trade association, or alumni network is positioned to advance or block our progress? (Ch 12.1) |

### Phase 2: Customer engagement

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Understand customer pains and gains | What is the customer trying to accomplish, and what frustrates them about current solutions? (Ch 11.2, customer empathy map) |
| Validate value proposition | Does our value proposition relieve a real pain or create a real gain? (Ch 11.2) |
| Segment the customer base | Which segment is the early-adopter target, and which is the mainstream goal? (Ch 8.2, Ch 4.2, Rogers's diffusion) |
| Build customer-discovery interviews | What questions reveal whether the customer's stated preferences match observed behaviour? (Ch 8.2, Ch 11.1) |
| Plan customer-feedback loops | How will we collect, route, and act on customer feedback as the venture grows? (Ch 8.6, Ch 10.1) |

### Phase 3: Founders, employees, and culture

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Align founders pre-launch | Have we agreed on vision, compensation, vesting, dispute resolution, and exit terms? (Ch 15.1) |
| Plan for member exit | What is our buyback clause, and how do we value contributions if a founder leaves before harvest? (Ch 15.1) |
| Codify culture | Does our code of conduct match the culture we actually want, and is it principle-based or rule-based for our context? (Ch 15.1, Ch 3.3) |
| Engage employees on values | Do millennial and Gen-Z hires see their values in ours? (60 per cent will accept 15 per cent less pay for value alignment, Ch 3.3) |
| Build accountability | Do we have whistleblower channels, ethics training, and consequence systems? (Ch 3.3, Ch 15.2) |

### Phase 4: Investors and capital partners

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Match the pitch to the audience | Friends and family, angels, VCs, trade groups, and grant agencies need different pitch lengths and asks. Which audience is this? (Ch 7.3) |
| Disclose the right amount | What information do investors at this round actually need, and what is held back? (Ch 7.4 NDAs, Ch 9.1) |
| Set investor expectations | Have we made clear whether we are building a lifestyle venture or pursuing harvest? (Ch 15.1) |
| Choose investor fit | Does this angel investor have industry knowledge and network that complement what we lack? (Ch 15.3) |
| Plan governance | If we take VC money, how will board composition and reporting requirements change our stakeholder dynamics? (Ch 9.1, Ch 13.2) |

### Phase 5: Suppliers, partners, and the network

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Choose the advisor team | Which of the five advisor types (accountant, attorney, banker, insurance agent, industry expert) do we need first, and at what fee structure? (Ch 12.2) |
| Build weak-tie bridges | Where are the cross-functional weak ties that could open doors we cannot open ourselves? (Ch 6.1, citing Granovetter) |
| Manage resource dependencies | Where are we dependent on a single supplier, customer, or partner, and what RDT-style mitigation (joint venture, vertical integration, supplier diversification) reduces that risk? (Ch 14.3) |
| Engage trade and industry associations | Which associations carry credibility with our customers and regulators, and what do they require for membership? (Ch 12.1) |
| Engage the local community | Are we taking a chamber-of-commerce role, hosting community events, or sponsoring local causes? (Ch 12.1, Ch 3.2) |

### Phase 6: Community, regulators, and the public

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Demonstrate CSR commitment | Are we tracking and disclosing the social and environmental impact of our operations? (Ch 3.2) |
| Comply with the regulatory layer | Which licences, permits, and registrations does our jurisdiction and industry require? (Ch 14.1, Ch 13) |
| Manage public perception during a mistake | Do we have a "Tylenol-style" admit-and-fix protocol, or a "GM ignition switch-style" deny-and-delay pattern? (Ch 3.3) |
| Plan for media and PR | How will we route press requests, sponsor community events, and earn goodwill before we need it? (Ch 8.1) |
| Engage with marginalised groups our venture affects | If our customer or workforce includes underrepresented groups, what specific support, mentorship, and inclusion practices do we offer? (Ch 10.3) |

### Phase 7: Post-harvest stakeholder roles

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Choose post-harvest role | After exit, am I best suited as serial entrepreneur, mentor, consultant, or champion? (Ch 15.4) |
| Avoid mentor conflicts | Have I clarified confidentiality, conflicts of interest, and compensation with the venture I am mentoring? (Ch 15.4) |
| Build social capital | What contributions am I making to the entrepreneurial ecosystem that earned my own success? (Ch 15.4) |

## What to Look For

- **Pattern: founder treats customers as a market to extract from rather than a stakeholder to serve.** Signal: marketing copy emphasises features over customer outcomes; customer-feedback channels are absent or unused. Diagnosis: shareholder-primacy frame applied to customer relations (Ch 3.1). Follow-up: build a customer empathy map; route product decisions through customer-discovery interviews.

- **Pattern: founders treat their team like outsiders rather than co-owners.** Signal: no founders' agreement, ad hoc compensation, no buyback clause, dispute resolution by gut feel. Diagnosis: pre-launch stakeholder gap (Ch 15.1). Follow-up: pause growth, formalise the founders' agreement before adding employees or taking outside capital.

- **Pattern: founders' values and the code of conduct mismatch.** Signal: published code says "respect" while founder behaviour models intimidation; employees notice. Diagnosis: weak ethics culture, predicted to produce repeat misconduct (Ch 15.2). Follow-up: hold a values-alignment session; revise the code to match what is actually rewarded; institute consequence systems.

- **Pattern: pitch is one-size-fits-all across audiences.** Signal: same deck used for F&F, angel investor, and trade-association talk. Diagnosis: missing audience-tailored stakeholder framing (Ch 7.3). Follow-up: prepare three pitch variants — emotional/relational for F&F, market-and-margin for investors, ecosystem-impact for trade groups.

- **Pattern: angel investor brings money but no domain knowledge or network.** Signal: founder closes a cheque from anyone willing. Diagnosis: missed investor-fit assessment (Ch 15.3). Follow-up: prefer angels with industry knowledge over higher-cheque alternatives without; structure the relationship to include advisory time.

- **Pattern: dependence on a single customer, supplier, or distribution channel.** Signal: more than 30 per cent of revenue or supply concentrated in one party. Diagnosis: resource-dependence vulnerability (Ch 14.3). Follow-up: identify diversification options, joint-venture options, or vertical-integration plays per RDT.

- **Pattern: community and regulatory engagement happens only after a problem surfaces.** Signal: no chamber participation, no licence-renewal calendar, no proactive PR. Diagnosis: reactive stakeholder posture. Follow-up: build a stakeholder-engagement calendar; identify three community organisations to support proactively.

- **Pattern: social-mission claims without verification.** Signal: founder says "we give back" but cannot quantify how, or to whom. Diagnosis: greenwashing risk (Ch 3.2). Follow-up: pursue B-corp certification or document specific, measurable social-impact commitments; align CSR claims with verifiable practice.

- **Pattern: ignoring the marginalised groups the venture's industry historically excludes.** Signal: leadership team and customer-segment definition both default to majority demographics. Diagnosis: missed stakeholder-equity work (Ch 10.3). Follow-up: review who is missing; build inclusive recruitment, mentorship, and customer-engagement practices.

- **Pattern: handling a mistake by deflecting blame.** Signal: PR statements emphasise "isolated incident" or "unfortunate outcome" rather than admitting fault and committing to fix. Diagnosis: GM-ignition-switch pattern (Ch 3.3, "If You Make a Mistake"). Follow-up: adopt the J&J Tylenol response template — admit, communicate, take immediate action, root-cause analyse, prevent recurrence.

## When to Use This Reference

- The user is structuring a social enterprise, B-corp, or hybrid venture and needs to map stakeholder commitments.
- The user is preparing pitches for multiple audiences and needs an audience-matrix approach.
- The user is forming a founding team and drafting the founders' agreement.
- The user is building or revising a code of conduct.
- The user is engaging investors, suppliers, customers, or community partners and wants to clarify the differential expectations of each.
- The user is post-harvest and considering mentor/consultant/champion roles.
- The user is responding to a stakeholder crisis (PR incident, customer revolt, regulator action) and needs a structured response framework.

The framework is less suited to pure M&A negotiations between equally-sophisticated parties, where game-theoretic and negotiation-specific frameworks dominate; for those, supplement with negotiation-specific references not covered in detail by this text.

## Worked Example

A founder is preparing to launch a community-based food cooperative that buys produce directly from local farmers and sells through a subscription model to neighbourhood households. The cooperative is structured as a benefit corporation. The founder has secured F&F seed funding, identified ten farmer suppliers, and pre-sold 200 household subscriptions. She is planning a community kickoff event in three weeks and is unsure how to balance the often-competing interests of her stakeholders.

Using the framework:

1. *Map the stakeholder universe (Ch 3.1).* Customers (subscribing households), suppliers (the ten farmers), employees (one part-time delivery coordinator), community (the neighbourhood), regulators (state agriculture department, local health department), the founder herself, and the F&F seed investors. As a benefit corporation, beneficiaries (lower-income households the venture commits to serving via subsidised subscriptions) are a distinct stakeholder category (Ch 13.2, Ch 11.2 social BMC).

2. *Tailor the pitch by audience for the kickoff event (Ch 7.3, Table 7.3).* The community event needs three messages: customer message ("fresh, local, predictable price"); supplier message ("guaranteed weekly volume, predictable pay schedule"); community message ("twelve subscriptions per quarter underwritten for low-income households").

3. *Use the customer empathy map for subscribers (Ch 11.2).* Pains: time pressure for grocery shopping, distrust of supermarket produce origin. Gains: connection to local farmers, predictability, family meal planning. Jobs-to-be-done: feed family well within budget without weekly grocery decisions.

4. *Apply the Four Lenses Strategic Framework (Ch 2.4).* Stakeholder engagement: monthly farmer roundtable, quarterly subscriber survey. Resource mobilisation: bulk-order funding from F&F investors. Knowledge development: monthly seasonal-eating newsletter. Culture management: every employee and farmer signs the cooperative's values statement.

5. *Document the founders' agreement and code of conduct (Ch 15.1).* Even with a single founder, the agreement covers what happens if the founder is incapacitated, how supplier farmers are paid if the cooperative dissolves, and what the buyback path looks like for F&F investors at three-year and five-year marks.

6. *Plan resource-dependence mitigation (Ch 14.3).* Ten farmer suppliers spread harvest risk; no single farmer should exceed 20 per cent of weekly volume. Backup suppliers identified for each crop category.

7. *Build the regulatory engagement calendar (Ch 14.1).* Annual food-handling licence renewal, quarterly state agriculture compliance check, weekly health-department-required temperature logs.

8. *Decide the post-launch CSR-disclosure cadence (Ch 3.2).* Quarterly impact report covering subsidised-subscription count, total dollars to local farmers, and waste reduction.

The framework's contribution is to surface that the cooperative has eight distinct stakeholder categories with overlapping but distinct interests, and that the kickoff event must communicate to all of them — not just to subscribers — without conflating them. It also surfaces the founders'-agreement gap and the resource-dependence risk before they become crises. ## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Confusing customers with stakeholders.** Treating only the paying customer as having a stake in the venture, ignoring suppliers, employees, community, and environment (Ch 3.1).

- **Same-pitch-for-everyone fundraising.** Using the same deck and ask with F&F as with VCs, leading to either insulted F&F or unconvinced VCs (Ch 7.3).

- **Founders'-agreement-after-the-fact.** Drafting the agreement only when a dispute arises, by which point negotiating-while-conflicted produces worse terms (Ch 15.1).

- **Generic code of conduct.** Adopting a template code that does not match the actual culture or values, producing predictable hypocrisy (Ch 15.1, Ch 3.3).

- **Single-supplier or single-customer dependence.** Letting resource-dependence accumulate without RDT-style mitigation, producing existential vulnerability when the dependent party defects (Ch 14.3).

- **CSR theatre.** Marketing claims about social impact without verification, producing eventual reputation damage when claims are tested (Ch 3.2).

- **Crisis-response by deflection.** GM-ignition-switch-style pattern of denying problems until forced to admit them, producing far worse outcomes than the J&J Tylenol pattern of admit-and-fix (Ch 3.3).

- **Treating mentors as free advice.** Failing to clarify confidentiality, conflicts of interest, and compensation, leading to soured relationships and IP risk (Ch 15.4).
