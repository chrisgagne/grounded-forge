## Stakeholder Engagement Relevance

The SSDL *Systems Thinking Foundations* briefs are fundamentally about *seeing the whole system* — and the whole system always includes the network of stakeholders whose decisions, beliefs, and accumulated history shape its behaviour. The five briefs make four load-bearing contributions to stakeholder engagement work.

First, *Brief 1.02* names "multiple stakeholders with different goals whose decisions and actions impact each other" as one of the five characteristics of complex problems. Stakeholder conflict in complex domains is not a process failure to be facilitated away; it is a structural feature of the problem space that needs to be surfaced and worked with. Stakeholders "experiencing systems differently, therefore have different incentives and motivations" — engagement design needs to assume conflicting goals as the baseline, not as the exception.

Second, *Brief 1.03* on Mental Models is the most directly applicable: the blindfolded-elephant parable is a stakeholder-engagement parable. Each stakeholder holds a partial, valid mental model of the whole; engagement that does not surface and integrate these partial models leaves the practitioner working from one stakeholder's view as if it were the whole. The brief's named tool — Group Model Building (GMB, citing Hovmand 2014 [BT]) — is a stakeholder-engagement method: bringing people together to develop a model of the system, "to create shared insights and common language, and to build consensus for implementing change."

Third, *Brief 1.06* on Accumulations supplies a sharp tool against a recurring stakeholder-engagement failure mode: treating every stakeholder interaction as a fresh-slate encounter when the stakeholder actually arrives carrying years of accumulated experience. "Meeting that [stakeholder] as an accumulation of their history, not just the short interaction of that moment" reframes engagement from event-management to history-aware relationship work.

Fourth, *Brief 1.07* on System Archetypes is stakeholder-engagement-shaped at two levels. The archetypes themselves describe patterns of stakeholder dynamics — *Success to the successful* names the structural mechanism of resource-allocation inequity between stakeholder groups; *Shifting the burden* names the dynamic of treating symptoms in one stakeholder group while the fundamental conditions that produced those symptoms remain in another; *Limits to growth* names the dynamic of a stakeholder relationship that scales beyond its capacity. The brief is also explicit about engagement-practice cautions: archetypes used prescriptively in workshops can reinforce expert/novice dynamics and "overshadow the lived experience of the participants" — a direct warning to the stakeholder-engagement practitioner about their own positioning.

The Brief 1.07 framing — "Many of the structures that cause these patterns can be found all around us — in schools, in businesses, in non-profit organizations, in health systems, and in communities" — is the explicit cross-domain warrant for using these concepts in any stakeholder system, not just the education-equity domain the worked examples illustrate.

## Key Concepts for Stakeholder Engagement

1. **Multi-stakeholder, conflicting-goals as a structural feature of complex problems.** "Administrators, teachers, students, parents, and teachers are experiencing systems differently, therefore have different incentives and motivations." Generalised: in any complex stakeholder system, the participants have different goals because they experience the system from different positions. The engagement design move is to *assume* conflicting goals as a baseline; engagement that designs for alignment-by-default produces lukewarm outcomes when alignment is not there. (Source: Brief 1.02, "+(Some) Characteristics of complex problems in education")

2. **The blindfolded-elephant parable as stakeholder-engagement frame.** "Complex systems in which we work are like this elephant. Each of us has our own, valid mental picture of the parts, but our understanding of the whole is limited by our own unique vantage point." Each stakeholder's view is *valid* and *partial*; engagement work is the integration of partial views, not the imposition of one stakeholder's view on the others. (Source: Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT")

3. **Mental models shape engagement language.** "Our mental models influence how we define a problem, the language we use to describe it, and the actions we take to solve it." When two stakeholders describe the same situation in different language, that is not (usually) a translation problem — it is a mental-model surfacing opportunity. The engagement move is to slow down and surface the model, not to pick the "right" language. (Source: Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT")

4. **No stakeholder has the complete picture, and the practitioner does not either.** "None of us have complete knowledge of systems. Our understanding is informed by our lived experiences, values, assumptions, power, and biases." For the practitioner, this is a humility check: the facilitator's mental model is also partial, also shaped by their position. (Source: Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT")

5. **Mental models are sticky, especially among the unaffected.** "Mental models are resistant to change, especially among individuals who aren't personally experiencing any negative system effects." For stakeholder engagement, this is a direct claim about *who* is most resistant to revising the frame: stakeholders insulated from the system's downsides. Engagement that treats all stakeholders as equally open to model revision will systematically under-shift the views of those whose views are most consequential and least pressure-tested. (Source: Brief 1.03, "+CONSIDERATIONS")

6. **Group Model Building (GMB) as the named participatory method.** "Group model building (GMB) is a process for bringing people together to develop a model of the system, to create shared insights and common language, and to build consensus for implementing change. While such processes may incite difficult conversations and disagreement, they can help us to build a more complete picture of systems and, ultimately, create shared mental models for change. To build equitable and sustainable solutions to complex problems, we must uncover the whole 'elephant,' which requires amplifying the perspectives and voices of groups with differing vantage points." GMB is named, but the briefs do not develop the facilitation craft in detail; for that, pair with the Liberating Structures Handbook and Barbrook-Johnson & Penn distillations. (Source: Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT", citing Hovmand 2014 [BT])

7. **Difficult conversations are inherent to the work.** "Such processes may incite difficult conversations and disagreement" — a feature, not a bug. Engagement design that protects participants from difficult conversations protects them from the very work the engagement is supposed to produce. (Source: Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT")

8. **Voice amplification is structurally required.** "To build equitable and sustainable solutions to complex problems, we must uncover the whole 'elephant,' which requires amplifying the perspectives and voices of groups with differing vantage points." The engagement-design implication: participation alone is not enough — the structural design of the engagement must amplify under-heard voices, not just admit them. (Source: Brief 1.03, "+ CONCEPT")

9. **Stakeholders arrive as accumulations of their history, not as blank slates.** "When meeting a new [stakeholder], it can be easy to assume that [they] are coming to you with a blank slate — an empty tub. But that [stakeholder] might have had years or generations of distrust that has built over time, and you are meeting that [stakeholder] as an accumulation of their history, not just the short interaction of that moment." The engagement-design implication: design the engagement to surface and account for accumulated history, not to start from zero each time. Single-event engagements are flow-only; relationships are stocks. (Source: Brief 1.06, "+ WHY DOES THIS ACCUMULATION CONCEPT MATTER?")

10. **Trust is a stock, not a flow.** Generalising from the brief's framing (the bathtub metaphor, citing Meadows 2008 [BT]): stakeholders' trust in a relationship, in an organisation, or in a process is an accumulated state. Trust-building events (workshops, briefings, community sessions) are *flows*; what matters for engagement quality is the net accumulation over time. Single events that do not address the prior stock will under-fire. (Source: Brief 1.06, "+ WHAT DO WE MEAN BY ACCUMULATION?" — extending the bathtub metaphor to relationship stocks)

11. **Feedback thinking changes the diagnostic for "why is the stakeholder behaving like this?".** "Feedback thinking recognizes relationships and interdependencies between the parts... we look to feedback structure to understand why problems persist despite our best efforts — why we might push on one part of the system and see a response in another, in sometimes unexpected ways." A stakeholder's resistance, withdrawal, or unexpected response is rarely an individual choice in isolation; it is usually the visible end of a feedback loop the practitioner has not yet mapped. (Source: Brief 1.05, "+Linear vs. Feedback Perspective")

12. **CLDs as engagement structures, not as expert outputs.** "A CLD is a hypothesis — there is not one single 'right' answer to the variables that are included or the relationships that exist between them. The model we produce is informed by each of our individual mental models, which come from our unique vantage point and lived experiences." Used in engagement, the CLD is a structured surface on which stakeholders can express, negotiate, and refine shared understanding; it is not the practitioner's analysis to be defended. (Source: Brief 1.05, "+ CONSIDERATIONS")

13. **System archetypes as patterns of stakeholder dynamics.** Four of Brief 1.07's five archetypes are stakeholder-engagement-shaped at their core (all five from Kim 1993 [BT]):

    - **Success to the successful** describes the structural mechanism of resource-allocation inequity between two stakeholder groups: when engaging with two groups in resource competition, the archetype names what the dynamic structurally is — not a failure of individual choice but a feedback structure that reproduces itself.

    - **Shifting the burden** describes the dynamic of addressing one stakeholder group's symptoms while leaving the conditions that produced those symptoms (in the same or another stakeholder group) untouched. Engagement that focuses on the symptomatic group without naming the underlying conditions repeats this trap.

    - **Drifting goals** describes a stakeholder system that, faced with chronic gap between aspiration and reality, quietly lowers aspiration to close the gap. When stakeholders consistently lower their commitments rather than facing the corrective action needed, the engagement has slid from goal-defence to goal-erosion.

    - **Limits to growth** describes the dynamic of a stakeholder relationship that scales beyond its capacity — engagement-as-growth running into a balancing loop driven by relational, attentional, or institutional capacity constraints.

    The fifth archetype, *Fixes that fail*, applies particularly to engagement strategies themselves: a quick communication fix that addresses the surface symptom but produces a side effect (engagement fatigue, message-saturation, perceived insincerity) that worsens the underlying relationship over time. (Source: Brief 1.07, "+ APPLYING SYSTEM ARCHETYPES IN K-12 EDUCATION")

14. **The Tragedy of the Commons is implied but not named in Brief 1.07's selected set.** Brief 1.07 selects five archetypes from "a broad list of eight to twelve core archetypes." The Tragedy of the Commons — the canonical stakeholder-engagement archetype where individually rational action by multiple stakeholders depletes a shared resource — is in the broader canonical set but is not one of Brief 1.07's five. For stakeholder engagement work involving shared-resource situations, the Tragedy archetype is the right starting hypothesis; the SSDL deep reference does not name it, but the structural pattern is consistent with the brief's claim that "There is no definitive list of system archetypes." (Source: Brief 1.07, "+ CONCEPT")

15. **The archetype-as-label engagement failure mode.** "Jumping in to use archetypes when facilitating a group model building workshop or community session has the potential to reinforce a dynamic of expert vs. novice. The modeler draws on their modeling 'expertise' to diagnose an archetype in a way that overshadows the lived experience of the participants, who actually understand their situation more deeply. These individuals or groups may not want or need intellectualized external labels to be placed on their model." A direct warning to the practitioner about positioning. (Source: Brief 1.07, "+ CONSIDERATIONS")

16. **System-dynamics tooling can create barriers to entry.** "Jargon and academic language can create a barrier to entry and an expert/novice dynamic that fails to recognize the value of people's lived experiences and understanding of problems." For engagement, the language of the engagement — including the language of any systems-thinking apparatus — is itself an inclusion/exclusion variable. (Source: Brief 1.06, "+ CONSIDERATIONS WHEN APPLYING THE CONCEPT OF ACCUMULATIONS")

17. **Acknowledge when system-dynamics apparatus is not what stakeholders need.** "Sometimes what's actually needed is a simple calculation... a survey... or even just a meeting between stakeholders. Sometimes the path forward is obvious, or a decision has to be made immediately." The practitioner's commitment to systems-thinking should not override the stakeholders' actual situation. (Source: Brief 1.02, "+CONSIDERATIONS")

## Example: applying systems-thinking concepts to stakeholder engagement

Consider a software product team running a quarterly community advisory board (CAB) with external integrators who depend on the team's APIs. The team has historically experienced the CAB as adversarial — integrators repeatedly raise the same complaints, the team responds with road-map commitments, and the next CAB surfaces the same complaints anyway.

A conventional engagement frame names the cause-effect link: *X (insufficient communication) causes Y (repeated complaints)*; therefore *publish more frequent road-map updates*. The intervention runs for two quarters. Initial response is positive. By Quarter 3, complaints are louder than before, and three of the most engaged integrators have stopped attending.

A systems-thinking diagnostic surfaces a different shape.

*Surface the mental models* (Brief 1.03). The product team's mental model: the CAB is for road-map alignment; integrators raise complaints because they do not know what is planned. The integrators' mental model (surfaced by asking them, not assuming it): the CAB is the only forum where their voice reaches the product team; they raise the same complaints repeatedly because they have no evidence the complaints have been heard. Both mental models are valid; both are partial; neither matches the other.

*Account for accumulation* (Brief 1.06). Each integrator arrives at the CAB carrying years of accumulated experience — past commitments missed, escalations that produced no response, peer-network stories about other integrators' frustrations. The CAB is not the start of the conversation; it is a snapshot of an accumulated relationship stock. The team has been treating each CAB as a fresh-slate event when integrators experience it as the latest entry in a long ledger.

*Apply the feedback diagnostic* (Brief 1.05). The "road-map update" intervention added a small balancing loop on the surface complaint (more information → temporarily less complaint-pressure). But the underlying feedback loop is between *integrator perception of being-heard* (a stock) and *integrator participation* (a flow); road-map updates do not change the *being-heard* stock, because the updates are one-directional information transfer. The reinforcing loop *less-heard → less-participation → less-input-to-team → less-responsiveness-to-integrator-priorities → less-heard* continues despite the road-map intervention.

*Name the archetype as hypothesis* (Brief 1.07). The pattern matches *Shifting the Burden* — the road-map update is the symptomatic fix; the fundamental solution would be redesigning the engagement so that integrator input visibly shapes product decisions. The symptomatic fix is easier (the team controls the road-map updates entirely) and faster (one quarter to launch), but every quarter of road-map-update-only engagement erodes the conditions for the fundamental solution (because integrators interpret the unchanged decision pattern as evidence that input does not matter).

*Design the structural intervention.* Rather than another communication-pattern change, redesign the engagement so that integrator input is visibly tied to product decisions: published criteria for what integrator-raised concerns will get road-map slots; per-concern ownership inside the team; quarterly reporting back on the disposition of each prior-quarter concern; integrator co-authorship on the road-map document for the integration surface. The intervention adds a new feedback loop — integrator input → visible product decision → integrator perception of being-heard → integrator engagement.

A second worked example — engaging a coalition of regulatory, customer, and supplier stakeholders in a sustainability initiative. The conventional engagement frame builds the coalition around a shared sustainability goal and runs joint working sessions. After six months, the regulator's representative is largely silent; the customer's representative is increasingly vocal; the supplier's representative has stopped attending.

The systems-thinking diagnostic surfaces *Success to the successful*: the customer has the most resources to commit to working-session preparation, the strongest mandate from their leadership, and the most direct influence on the initiative's framing. Their input therefore disproportionately shapes the agenda; the agenda reinforces customer-priority framings; the regulator and supplier — operating with thinner resources, weaker internal mandate, or constraints the customer does not share — find their concerns crowded out; their declining participation further concentrates customer influence. The structural intervention is not "encourage the regulator and supplier to participate more" but to redesign the agenda-setting and resource-allocation patterns so that under-resourced stakeholders are not structurally disadvantaged in shaping the work.

Both examples illustrate the same point: engagement problems in complex stakeholder systems usually have a structural shape that linear, communication-pattern-level intervention cannot reach.

The scenarios are operator-authored. The multi-stakeholder-conflicting-goals framing (Brief 1.02), the mental-model surfacing approach (Brief 1.03), the accumulation-as-stock framing (Brief 1.06), the feedback-thinking diagnostic (Brief 1.05), the Shifting the Burden and Success to the Successful archetypes (Brief 1.07), and the archetype-as-hypothesis (not archetype-as-label) discipline (Brief 1.07, "+ CONSIDERATIONS") are all `[V]`-marked passages in the deep reference. No verbatim source blockquotes appear in this distillation.

## Questions to Ask During Stakeholder Engagement

### Phase 1: Mapping the stakeholder system

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify who experiences this system | Who are the stakeholders? Who has decisions or actions that impact others in this system? Are the stakeholders' goals likely to conflict (the multi-stakeholder characteristic from Brief 1.02 predicts they will)? |
| Surface the mental models in the room | What does each stakeholder believe is producing the pattern under discussion? Are they describing the same situation in different language? — that is often a mental-model boundary, not a translation problem. |
| Test for absent stakeholders | Who is not in the room whose mental model would shape the picture differently? Brief 1.03's whole-elephant claim requires "amplifying the perspectives and voices of groups with differing vantage points." |
| Audit the practitioner's own mental model | What do I, as the practitioner, take for granted? Whose lived experience is closest to mine? Whose is furthest? My partial view is also partial. |

### Phase 2: Diagnostic — is the engagement issue event-shaped or structure-shaped?

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Replace X-causes-Y framing of the engagement issue | What loops produce the stakeholder behaviour I am seeing? Where does the effect feed back to the cause? Is there a reinforcing loop driving the pattern (e.g., participation-and-being-heard), or a balancing one constraining it (e.g., capacity vs. demand)? |
| Look for structural drivers of conflicting goals | Are stakeholders' conflicting goals individual differences, or are they produced by their structural positions in the system? Brief 1.02's framing is structural: "experiencing systems differently, therefore have different incentives and motivations." |
| Test for stickiness asymmetries | Who in the stakeholder system is least exposed to the consequences of the current frame? Brief 1.03 predicts those stakeholders are the most resistant to model change. Engagement that does not account for this asymmetry will systematically under-shift the views that matter most. |

### Phase 3: Accounting for accumulated history

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify relational stocks | What is the accumulated history this stakeholder brings to this engagement? Trust, distrust, prior unmet commitments, peer-network stories from other interactions — these are stocks. |
| Distinguish event from relationship | Am I designing a single engagement event, or am I building a relationship over time? Single events affect flows; relationships are stocks; mismatching event-design to stock-level need produces under-fire engagements. |
| Audit the "blank slate" assumption | Am I treating new stakeholders, or new interactions with familiar stakeholders, as if no prior accumulation exists? If yes, what is the prior history I am missing? |
| Plan for stock-building over event-management | What accumulation does this engagement need to build? What is the rate of inflow (commitments kept, listening events, evidence of input shaping decisions)? What is the rate of outflow (unmet commitments, message saturation, perceived insincerity)? |

### Phase 4: Surfacing mental models in the engagement

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Choose a participatory format that surfaces models | Am I asking stakeholders to defend conclusions or to surface mental models? The first hides models; the second exposes them. (Pair with Liberating Structures — Wise Crowds, Troika Consulting, Discovery & Action Dialogues — for facilitation primitives.) |
| Use systems-thinking apparatus in a stakeholder-appropriate way | Am I introducing CLDs, stock-and-flow diagrams, or archetypes in a form stakeholders can engage with? Brief 1.06 warns that "jargon and academic language can create a barrier to entry and an expert/novice dynamic." |
| Treat the diagram as the engagement, not as the deliverable | A CLD produced by the practitioner and shown to stakeholders is a one-way information transfer. A CLD built by stakeholders together, with the practitioner as facilitator, is the engagement work itself. |
| Acknowledge incomplete-and-partial as the baseline | Am I positioning anyone (myself, a stakeholder, a working group) as having the complete picture? Brief 1.03's load-bearing claim: no one does. |

### Phase 5: Working with conflicting goals as a feature

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Surface goal conflict rather than smoothing it | Do stakeholders feel safe naming what they actually want, including where their wants conflict with others'? Hidden goal conflict shapes engagement outcomes more than declared goal conflict does. |
| Host difficult conversations productively | Brief 1.03: GMB processes "may incite difficult conversations and disagreement" — a feature. Design the engagement to make those conversations safe and structured, not to prevent them. |
| Identify the structural mechanism of the conflict | Are the conflicting goals individual choices, or are they produced by stakeholders' structural positions (resources, mandates, accountabilities)? If structural, address the structure, not the individuals. |
| Test for power asymmetries in the engagement design | Which stakeholders have more resources to commit to engagement-preparation? Which can speak in the language the practitioner uses? Which are most exposed to the consequences of the engagement's outcome? |

### Phase 6: Pattern-matching against engagement-trap archetypes

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Test against *Success to the successful* | Are some stakeholder groups receiving disproportionate engagement attention, agenda influence, or follow-through capacity, while others fall progressively further behind in influence? The structural intervention is to redesign the engagement allocation, not to invite the disadvantaged stakeholders to "engage more." |
| Test against *Shifting the burden* | Is the engagement addressing one stakeholder group's symptoms (complaints, withdrawal, escalation) while leaving the conditions in another stakeholder group untouched? The symptomatic fix erodes the conditions for the fundamental solution. |
| Test against *Drifting goals* | Are stakeholders' commitments quietly lowering over time as gaps between aspirations and reality grow uncomfortable? The drift is hard to spot because the metric improves (the gap shrinks) even as substance does not. |
| Test against *Limits to growth* | Is the engagement function scaling its reach beyond its capacity? Are stakeholders experiencing the engagement as thinner and less-responsive as it scales? The constraint is usually capacity (attention, follow-through, relationship-stewardship), not communication-volume. |
| Test against *Fixes that fail* (applied to engagement strategy) | Is the proposed engagement intervention a short-term communication fix that will produce engagement-fatigue or perceived-insincerity side effects worse than the original problem? |
| Test against *Tragedy of the Commons* (not in Brief 1.07's five but in the broader canonical set) | Are multiple stakeholders making individually rational choices that deplete a shared resource (attention, trust, organisational capacity, regulatory goodwill)? If yes, the structural intervention is shared-resource governance, not individual stakeholder communication. |
| Use archetypes as hypothesis, not label | If I name an archetype, am I closing the inquiry or opening it? In a workshop with stakeholders, naming an archetype risks the expert/novice dynamic the brief warns against. The pattern is for the practitioner's diagnostic, not for the stakeholders' labelling. |

### Phase 7: Engagement-design discipline against systems-thinking failure modes

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Avoid event-only engagement when relationships are stocks | If the goal is to build a stakeholder relationship, am I designing a series of disconnected events, or am I designing a sustained engagement with accumulation in mind? |
| Avoid jargon-as-barrier | Is the language of the engagement accessible to all stakeholders, or am I using systems-thinking vocabulary in a room where it creates an expert/novice dynamic? |
| Avoid practitioner-as-analyst overshadowing stakeholder-as-knower | Am I, as the practitioner, diagnosing the situation in front of stakeholders, or am I hosting the stakeholders' diagnosis? Brief 1.07's *Considerations* on prescriptive-archetype use is directly relevant. |
| Avoid the illusion-of-accuracy trap | If the engagement produces a stock-and-flow diagram or a CLD that "looks formal and mathematical," am I treating it as proof rather than as a structured conversation? |
| Avoid systems-thinking apparatus when a meeting will do | Brief 1.02's hike-and-broken-ankle test: sometimes what is needed is a meeting between stakeholders, not a system dynamics model. |
| Audit for absent voices | Who would have spoken if they had been in the room? Who has been routinely absent from prior engagement, and why? |

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- **Treating stakeholder conflict as a process failure rather than as a structural feature of complex problems.** Multi-stakeholder, conflicting-goals is one of the five characteristics of complex problems; engagement that designs for alignment-by-default produces lukewarm outcomes when alignment is not there.
- **Acting on one stakeholder's mental model as if it were the whole.** Each stakeholder's view is valid and partial; the engagement is the integration of partial views, not the imposition of one view on others.
- **Treating stakeholders as blank-slate arrivals.** Each stakeholder arrives carrying accumulated history; engagement designed for the event ignores the stock.
- **Treating trust as a flow rather than a stock.** Single trust-building events have flow effects; what matters for engagement quality is the accumulated relationship over time.
- **Smoothing over difficult conversations.** GMB and stakeholder engagement processes "may incite difficult conversations and disagreement" — that is the work, not a process failure.
- **Failing to amplify under-heard voices structurally.** Participation alone is insufficient; the structural design of the engagement must surface voices that are systematically quieter.
- **Treating mental-model differences as translation problems.** Different language usually marks a mental-model boundary, not a vocabulary issue.
- **Acting as if the practitioner is the integrator of stakeholder views without acknowledging the practitioner's own partial view.** The practitioner's mental model is also partial.
- **Letting the most-insulated-from-consequences stakeholder dominate model revision.** Brief 1.03 names this asymmetry: those least exposed to the system's downsides are the most resistant to revising the frame.
- **Communication-pattern interventions to structural engagement problems.** When stakeholders' relational stocks are the issue, more newsletters, briefings, or road-map updates miss the variable that matters.
- **Labelling stakeholder dynamics with system archetypes in front of stakeholders.** The pattern is for the practitioner's diagnostic; using it as a label in a workshop reinforces the expert/novice dynamic.
- **Letting *Success to the Successful* concentrate engagement influence.** Without explicit redesign, engagement allocation tends to concentrate on the stakeholders most able to participate.
- **Letting *Shifting the Burden* defer fundamental engagement redesign.** Communication-volume increases as a recurring response when relationship-shape change is what is needed.
- **Letting *Drifting Goals* erode stakeholder commitments quietly.** Aspirations quietly lowered close the gap on the metric but not on the substance.
- **Scaling an engagement function past its relationship-stewardship capacity.** *Limits to Growth* — without capacity investment, engagement reach hits a ceiling and stakeholders experience the function as thinner over time.
- **Treating shared-resource depletion as individual-stakeholder behaviour.** *Tragedy of the Commons* — the structural intervention is shared-resource governance, not individual stakeholder communication.
- **Producing systems-thinking artefacts when stakeholders asked for a conversation.** Brief 1.02's hike-and-broken-ankle test applies: sometimes a meeting is what is needed.
