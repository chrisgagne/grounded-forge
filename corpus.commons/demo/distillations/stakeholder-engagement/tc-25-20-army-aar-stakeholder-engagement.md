# US Army TC 25-20, Stakeholder-Engagement Distillation

**Source:** Headquarters, Department of the Army (1993). *A Leader's Guide to After-Action Reviews*. Training Circular 25-20, 30 September 1993. **Licence:** Public domain (17 USC §105 — US Government work product). Open scope; no copyleft propagation.

## Stakeholder-Engagement Relevance

TC 25-20 is the canonical primary source for the after-action review (AAR) methodology, and the AAR is fundamentally a structured, multi-perspective stakeholder-engagement protocol disguised as a training-feedback tool. The whole point of the AAR is to surface, aggregate, and integrate perspectives held by stakeholders who saw different parts of the same action — the unit leader, the soldiers on the ground, the supporting OCs, the OPFOR, the higher echelons — and produce shared learning. The TC's load-bearing stakeholder-engagement contributions are (a) the AAR-vs.-critique distinction, which is a method choice between transmissive engagement (one perspective imposed) and aggregative engagement (many perspectives surfaced and integrated); (b) the horseshoe seating discipline that suppresses rank-driven voice silencing; (c) the open-ended questions, not yes/no rule that invites narrative rather than self-defence; (d) the OPFOR perspective requirement that includes the adversary's view as part of the engagement; (e) the climate-and-spirit framing in the Preface and Chapter 4 that names atmosphere as load-bearing; (f) the experience-over-rank OC-selection rule and the related two-echelons-above evaluator rule; and (g) the who-attends discipline that differentiates squad/platoon (everyone) from company-and-above (key players only).

The TC is unusual in this corpus in that it does not derive its method from a theoretical literature — it is doctrine, not research. Its claims are operational rather than empirical, but the operational claims are sharp and durable: spirit and climate are load-bearing; rank suppression of voice is a design problem with a seating solution; the adversary's perspective is part of the truth, not noise; the leader's job is to guide, not to tell. These are stakeholder-engagement design principles, expressed in the vocabulary of small-unit military training.

## Key Concepts for Stakeholder-Engagement

1. <!-- concept: aar-vs-critique --> **Aggregative engagement, not transmissive engagement.** The TC's load-bearing contrast is between the AAR (multi-perspective discovery) and the critique (one perspective imposed). The AAR is positioned as epistemically superior because "[a] critique only gives one viewpoint and frequently provides little opportunity for discussion of events by participants. Soldier observations and comments may not be encouraged. The climate of the critique, focusing only on what is wrong, prevents candid discussion of training events and stifles learning and team building" (Ch 1, "Definition and Purpose"). Engagement design decision: when stakeholders saw different parts of the same action, surface their perspectives in dialogue; do not collapse them into one observer's report.

2. <!-- concept: aar-climate --> **Climate is load-bearing.** The Preface frames the climate of the AAR explicitly: "Key is the spirit in which AARs are given. The environment and climate surrounding an AAR must be one in which the soldiers and leaders openly and honestly discuss what actually transpired in sufficient detail and clarity that not only will everyone understand what did and did not occur and why, but most importantly will have a strong desire to seek the opportunity to practice the task again" (Preface). "Soldier participation is directly related to the atmosphere created during the introduction" (Ch 4, "Introduction and Rules"). Spirit and climate are operational concerns, not soft skills.

3. <!-- concept: discovery-learning --> **Discovery, not transmission, is the mechanism.** "An after-action review (AAR) is a professional discussion of an event, focused on performance standards, that enables soldiers to discover for themselves what happened, why it happened, and how to sustain strengths and improve on weaknesses" (Ch 1, "Definition and Purpose"). The leader's role is to facilitate discovery, not to deliver conclusions. Stakeholders who discover what happened internalise the finding more strongly than stakeholders who are told.

4. <!-- concept: open-ended-questions --> **No yes/no questions.** "He should not ask yes or no questions, but encourage participation and guide discussion by using open-ended and leading questions. An open-ended question has no specific answer and allows the person answering to reply based on what was significant to him. Open-ended questions are also much less likely to put him on the defensive" (Ch 4, "Summary of Recent Events"). The TC's worked contrast — "SGT Johnson, what happened when your Bradley crested the hill?" versus "SGT Johnson, why didn't you engage the enemy tanks to your front?" — is concrete: the first invites narrative; the second invites self-defence.

5. <!-- concept: horseshoe-seating --> **Horseshoe seating with rank suppression.** "Seating should be in a horseshoe shape when possible. … The leader of the unit undergoing the AAR should be seated in the front and center of the horseshoe. Higher echelon leaders and commanders should be seated to the flanks and rear of the horseshoe. … This allows soldiers taking part in the AAR to feel that their comments are valued as much as those of senior leaders. It is important that the atmosphere of the AAR be one of open discussion, not one stifled by rank consciousness" (App A, "Organization of the AAR Site"). The physical layout is a stakeholder-voice design.

6. <!-- concept: opfor-perspective --> **OPFOR perspective belongs in the engagement.** "The leader may also receive input from OPFOR leaders, players, and OCs. The enemy's perspective is often useful in identifying why a unit was or was not successful. During formal AARs, the OPFOR commander briefs his plan and intent to set the stage for a discussion of what happened and why" (Ch 3, "Collect Observations from Other Observers and Controllers"). The adversary's perspective is part of the truth-set the engagement is trying to surface; including it is a design choice, not an afterthought.

7. <!-- concept: aar-attendance --> **Attendance discipline matches scale.** At squad and platoon levels, everyone should attend and participate. At company or higher, "it may not be practical to have everyone attend because of continuing operations or training. In this case, friendly and OPFOR commanders, unit leaders, and other key players (fire support team (FIST) chief, radio telephone operator (RTO), and so on) may be the only participants" (Ch 2, "Determining Attendance"). The attendance rule scales — full participation at small scale, key-player participation at large scale — and explicitly names the roles whose voices must be present at large scale.

8. <!-- concept: ungraded-learning --> **The AAR does not grade.** "An AAR is not a critique. No one, regardless of rank, position, or strength of personality, has all of the information or answers. After-action reviews maximize training benefits by allowing soldiers, regardless of rank, to learn from each other. An AAR does not grade success or failure. There are always weaknesses to improve and strengths to sustain" (Ch 4, "Introduction and Rules"). Engagement designed to grade produces self-defence; engagement designed to learn produces candour.

9. <!-- concept: leader-facilitation --> **The leader's facilitation role.** "Enter the discussion only when necessary; Reinforce the fact that it is permissible to disagree; Focus on learning and encourage people to give honest opinions; Use open-ended and leading questions to guide the discussion of soldier, leader, and unit performance" (Ch 4, "Introduction and Rules"). The leader is a facilitator, not a presenter — but the facilitator must ensure "specific issues are revealed, both positive and negative in nature. Skillful guidance of the discussion will ensure the AAR does not gloss over mistakes or unit weaknesses" (Ch 4, "Summary of Recent Events").

10. <!-- concept: experience-over-rank --> **Experience-over-rank OC selection.** "If trainers must choose between experience and understanding of current TTPs or rank, they should go with experience" (Ch 2, "Selecting and Training Observers and Controllers"). Convening rule: the person who facilitates the engagement should have the experience to recognise what matters, not just the rank to convene.

11. <!-- concept: evaluator-independence --> **Two-echelons-above evaluator independence.** External evaluations are conducted "two levels above the unit being evaluated" (Ch 2, "The After-Action Review Plan"). Engagement-design principle for independent evaluators: the immediate next echelon is too close to evaluate without conflict of interest; two echelons up has enough distance to assess honestly and enough proximity to understand the conditions.

12. <!-- concept: site-comfort --> **Site comfort is operational.** "The trainer should make soldiers attending the AAR as comfortable as possible (by removing helmets and so on), providing shelter from the elements (sun, cold, rain, snow), having refreshments (coffee, water), and creating an environment where participants can focus on the AAR without distractions. Participants should not face into the sun, and key leaders should have seats up front. Vehicle parking and equipment security areas should be far enough away from the AAR site to prevent distractions" (Ch 2, "Selecting Potential AAR Sites"). Physical conditions are part of the engagement design, not separate from it.

13. <!-- concept: separate-leader-aar --> **Soldier/leader skills in a separate AAR.** "Often it is best to discuss leader skills in a separate meeting or AAR specifically for that purpose. This allows for a candid discussion of leadership issues without wasting unit AAR time best spent on reviewing the entire training exercise. … The AAR leader for follow-on meetings should be a member of the unit so participants can candidly address key training issues without fear of airing dirty laundry in front of outsiders" (Ch 4, "Soldier/Leader Skills"). Engagement-design principle: certain conversations need different rooms with different attendees. Leader-skills discussion in front of subordinates and outsiders suppresses candour.

14. <!-- concept: closing-and-leaving --> **The closing-and-leaving convention.** "During the summary, the AAR leader reviews and summarizes key points identified during the discussion. He should end the AAR on a positive note, linking conclusions to future training. He should then leave the immediate area to allow unit leaders and soldiers time to discuss the training in private" (Ch 4, "Closing Comments"). Engagement-design principle: the convener's exit is itself a design move; private time after the structured engagement is part of the protocol, not after it.

15. <!-- concept: aar-plan --> **The AAR plan as upfront convening agreement.** "The AAR plan specifies who must attend each AAR" (Ch 2, "Determining Attendance"). The TC's worked example shows the plan covers: observer, element, priority tasks, who attends, when held, location, special requirements (Ch 2, Figure 2-1). Engagement-design principle: the attendance, agenda, location, timing, and special needs are all decided in advance, written down, and shared — not improvised in the moment.

16. <!-- concept: guided-open-questions --> **Open-ended questions but specific issues surfaced.** The leader uses open-ended questions but must ensure that specific issues, positive and negative, are revealed. The facilitator's craft is to keep the questions open while still steering toward the substance. "Skillful guidance of the discussion will ensure the AAR does not gloss over mistakes or unit weaknesses" (Ch 4, "Summary of Recent Events").

17. <!-- concept: three-discussion-techniques --> **Three discussion-organisation techniques.** Chronological order (narrative recall, effects across units), battlefield operating systems (systemic patterns across phases), key events/themes/issues (tight focus when time is limited). The leader can switch between techniques within a single AAR. Engagement-design principle: the organising structure of the conversation is a deliberate choice that matches what the engagement needs to produce.

18. <!-- concept: mandatory-deep-topics --> **Fratricide as a mandatory deep-discussion topic.** "All incidents or near incidents of fratricide … will be discussed in detail" (Ch 4, "Fratricide"). Engagement-design principle: certain severity levels override normal time-budget discipline. Identify the equivalents in your context — events whose discussion takes priority regardless of how long it takes.

## Questions to Ask During Stakeholder-Engagement

### Phase 1: Mapping

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Identify who saw which part of the action | Who has direct evidence about what happened? The TC's discipline is to gather observations from "subordinate units, combat support (CS) and combat service support (CSS) units, or adjacent units" — plus the OPFOR. In our context, who are the equivalents? Subordinate teams, supporting functions, adjacent teams, customers, suppliers, regulators, competitors. |
| Identify the adversary perspective | The TC includes the OPFOR commander's plan and intent as part of the AAR. In our context, who is the OPFOR equivalent? The party whose plan and intent shaped the action's outcome from the other side — the competitor in a product launch, the counterparty in a negotiation, the customer in a service failure, the regulator in a compliance event. |
| Identify the invisible-voice stakeholders | Who is affected by the action but not normally heard? The TC's everyone-attends rule at squad and platoon level is a low-rank-inclusion principle. In our context, who is the equivalent of the junior soldier whose perspective could change the picture if surfaced? |
| Identify roles that must be present even at key-player-only attendance | The TC names FIST chief, RTO, friendly and OPFOR commanders, unit leaders as required at company-and-above scale. In our context, what are the roles whose absence would compromise the engagement's truth-finding capacity? |
| Identify the two-echelons-above independent evaluator | If the engagement needs independent assessment, the immediate next level is too close. Who is two echelons above the unit being engaged-with? |

### Phase 2: Framing the question

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Is this an AAR-style engagement (aggregative) or a critique-style engagement (transmissive)? | The choice shapes everything downstream. If the engagement is meant to deliver a verdict, it is a critique and will produce self-defence. If the engagement is meant to surface what happened and why, it is an AAR. |
| What is the standard against which performance is being evaluated? | The TC's frame is performance against the Army standard for the tasks being trained. In our context, what is the published criterion? Without it, the engagement will drift toward opinion. |
| Is the engagement designed to grade or to learn? | "An AAR does not grade success or failure" (Ch 4). If we are about to assign performance ratings, blame, or a pass/fail outcome, we should expect candour to drop accordingly. Separate the two purposes if both are required. |
| Have we identified our equivalent of fratricide? | What are the events whose discussion overrides normal time budget regardless of how much else needs covering? Identify them in advance. |
| Have we identified the leader-skills equivalent that needs a separate room? | What conversations would suppress candour if held in the wide engagement and need a smaller, unit-internal room with no outsiders present? |

### Phase 3: Convening

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Have we picked the facilitator by experience, not by rank? | If the facilitator has rank but not experience-in-the-role, will they recognise what matters? The TC's rule: experience beats rank when in tension. |
| Have we written down the convening agreement? | The TC's AAR plan (Figure 2-1) specifies observer, element, priority tasks, who attends, when held, location, special requirements. Have we written down our equivalent before the engagement? |
| Have we set the climate explicitly? | The TC's three opening rules — dynamic, candid, professional discussion focused on standards; not a critique; does not grade — are stated at the start of every AAR, not assumed. What are the corresponding rules we need to state out loud at the start? |
| Have we designed the seating? | Horseshoe with the engaged-unit leader front-and-centre and higher echelons to the flanks and rear is a deliberate rank-suppression move. In our context, what is the equivalent arrangement? Who needs to be physically positioned where to keep junior voices from being pre-empted? |
| Have we made the site comfortable? | Helmets off, shelter from the elements, refreshments, no facing into the sun, distractions parked away. Our equivalents: physical comfort, privacy, refreshments, freedom from interruptions. The TC treats these as operational, not as nice-to-haves. |
| Have we picked the right scale and duration? | Platoon (intimate, everyone, 30–45 min) versus battalion-and-above (key players, 2 hours) is a scale choice. Do we have the right combination of attendance, duration, and depth? |

### Phase 4: Surfacing conflict

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Are we asking open-ended questions? | If we are asking "did you...?" or "why didn't you...?", we are inviting self-defence. The TC's Bradley/hill versus engage/tanks-to-front contrast is the test. Reframe to "what happened when...?" |
| Are we entering the discussion only when necessary? | The TC's facilitator rule: enter the discussion only when necessary; reinforce that disagreement is permissible. Are we over-facilitating, or letting the participants build the picture? |
| Are we ensuring specific issues, positive and negative, are revealed? | Open-ended questions can produce a soft, surface-level discussion that glosses over what mattered. The TC's reminder: skilful guidance ensures the AAR does not gloss over mistakes or weaknesses. Are we steering toward substance while keeping the questions open? |
| Have we organised the discussion the right way? | Chronological (narrative recall), systemic (BOS-equivalent for cross-phase patterns), key-events (tight focus when time is limited). Mid-discussion, can we switch organising techniques if a systemic issue surfaces? |
| Are we suppressing rank-driven voice silencing? | If junior voices are not speaking, is the seating wrong, the question wrong, the climate wrong, or the leader's behaviour wrong? The TC's rule that "no one, regardless of rank, position, or strength of personality, has all of the information or answers" should be re-stated when this happens. |
| Have we included the OPFOR perspective? | If we have not heard from the adversary equivalent — the competitor, the customer, the counterparty — we have not heard half the story. |

### Phase 5: Reaching agreement

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Have we agreed on what good looks like? | The TC's frame is performance against the standard. By the end of the AAR, "soldiers must clearly understand what was good, bad, or average about their performance" (Ch 5, "Immediate Retraining"). Has the engagement produced shared understanding of the standard and where performance fell relative to it? |
| Have we agreed on what changes? | The TC's discipline: AARs may surface SOP problems; if so, "unit leaders must revise the SOP and make sure units implement the changes during any future training" (Ch 5, "Revised Standing Operating Procedures"). What changes — to procedure, to assignment, to training, to relationship — has this engagement surfaced and locked? |
| Have we agreed on next training? | The TC's real-benefits-come-from-follow-up principle: an engagement whose output never reaches a next-action commitment has not produced its benefit. What is the next action, who owns it, when does it happen? |
| Have we acknowledged what is to be sustained as well as what is to be improved? | "There are always weaknesses to improve and strengths to sustain" (Ch 4). An engagement that produces only a deficit list creates resentment. What strengths is the engagement explicitly recognising? |
| Have we ended on a positive note linked to future engagement? | The TC's closing-comments rule: review key points, end positively, link to future. Are we sending participants out with a forward-looking framing? |

### Phase 6: Ratifying

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Have we left the participants space to discuss in private? | The TC's convention: the AAR leader leaves the immediate area after closing comments to allow unit leaders and soldiers time to discuss in private. Our equivalent: have we created post-engagement private time for the team? |
| Have we communicated to non-attending stakeholders? | The TC's attendance discipline scales: at large scale, not everyone is in the room. What has been agreed, and who outside the room needs to know it? In what form, and with what framing? |
| Have we locked the next training event with the right T-P-U-equivalent assessment? | Trained / Practiced / Untrained is a three-bucket assessment that drives next-training design. Our equivalent: what is the categorical assessment that drives the next iteration? |
| Have we revised the AAR plan (the convening agreement) for the next round? | If the engagement surfaced a problem with the convening itself — wrong attendance, wrong duration, wrong format — has the next convening agreement been updated? |

### Phase 7: Post-engagement

| Need | Question |
|---|---|
| Has the next-action commitment happened? | The benefit lives in the follow-up. If the next action committed during the engagement has not occurred on the timeline committed, the engagement has not produced its benefit. |
| Has the SOP-equivalent been revised? | If the engagement surfaced a procedural problem, has the procedure been changed and used in the next iteration? An unrevised procedure after a review that surfaced a problem is a silent regression. |
| Has the relationship strengthened or weakened? | The TC's claim is that the AAR promotes unit bonding and esprit. The stakeholder-engagement equivalent: has this engagement strengthened the relationship between the parties, or weakened it? If weakened, where did the climate or process fail? |
| Are we ready for the next engagement at higher quality? | The TC's apprenticeship discipline: inexperienced AAR leaders observe properly conducted AARs before leading. Are we coaching the next generation of facilitators by example? |
| Have we updated our standing list of fratricide-equivalent topics? | What near-miss events emerged that should be added to our list of severity-overrides-time-budget topics for future engagements? |

## What to Look For

**Open-ended question drift toward yes/no form.**
**Signal:** The facilitator asks "Did the team communicate well?" or "Was the handoff clear?" rather than "What happened when the handoff came?"
**Diagnosis:** The question form has shifted from invitation to interrogation. Yes/no questions invite a two-word answer and then silence; they also shift focus toward self-justification rather than narrative reconstruction. The TC's contrast between "SGT Johnson, what happened when your Bradley crested the hill?" and "SGT Johnson, why didn't you engage the enemy tanks to your front?" captures the difference exactly.
**Follow-up:** Reframe the question before the participant answers if possible. If the yes/no answer has already landed, follow it immediately with an open continuation: "And what led up to that?" or "Walk us through what you were seeing at that point." Resist the urge to paraphrase the yes/no answer as a finding.

**Rank-driven voice concentration.**
**Signal:** Only the most senior people in the room are speaking; junior voices wait for a pause that never comes or give short, safe answers that mirror what the senior person already said.
**Diagnosis:** The seating arrangement, the question direction, or the leader's eye contact is routing all engagement through the top of the hierarchy. The TC's load-bearing claim is that no one, regardless of rank, position, or strength of personality, has all of the information or answers. If only one stratum is speaking, the engagement is functioning as a critique, not an AAR.
**Follow-up:** Re-state the no-grade, everyone-has-a-piece-of-the-picture framing explicitly. If seating is the cause, adjust it. Direct open-ended questions by name to people who have not yet spoken: "What did you see from your position when...?" After a junior voice speaks, do not immediately redirect to a senior for comment — let the junior's contribution sit for a moment.

**Engagement closing without a next-action commitment.**
**Signal:** The review produces a clear account of what happened and why but ends with a positive summary and no named owner, next step, or timeline.
**Diagnosis:** The TC is explicit that "the real benefits of AARs come from taking the results and applying them to future training." An engagement that does not produce a committed next action has completed the ceremony but not the cycle. The follow-up step is not optional.
**Follow-up:** Before closing, ask directly: "What changes? Who owns it? By when?" If the answer is "we'll figure that out," treat that as the engagement not yet complete. The TC's closing-comments convention is to review key points, link to future training, and then leave — the sequence requires the future-training link to be named before the leader exits.

**Same issues surfacing in successive reviews without SOP revision.**
**Signal:** The retrospective or after-action surfaces a problem — a coordination gap, a handoff failure, a communication breakdown — that also appeared in the last review. The team acknowledges it, agrees it needs to change, and moves on.
**Diagnosis:** An unrevised procedure after a review that surfaced a problem is a silent regression. The TC names this directly: "unit leaders must revise the SOP and make sure units implement the changes during any future training." If the SOP was not revised after the last review, the problem was identified but not fixed, and naming it again in this review will produce the same outcome.
**Follow-up:** Ask whether the procedure was revised after the last review. If not, find out why — was the revision not assigned, not resourced, or not followed through? The fix is not to discuss the problem more thoroughly; it is to assign a named owner to the revision with a deadline before the next review cycle begins.

**OPFOR-perspective absence.**
**Signal:** The review covers what the team did and why, but no one in the room represents or articulates the perspective of the party on the other side — the customer, the counterparty, the regulator, the competing team.
**Diagnosis:** The TC's discipline is that the OPFOR perspective is part of the truth-set. An account of a negotiation that does not include what the counterparty was seeing is incomplete in the same way an account of a contact without the OPFOR brief is incomplete. The friendly-force perspective alone will systematically miss explanations for why the engagement went the way it did.
**Follow-up:** Before the review closes, ask explicitly: "What was the other party seeing and trying to achieve?" If no one in the room can answer this, identify who could — a customer-facing team member, someone who spoke to the counterparty, a regulator liaison — and either include them in the next review or brief their perspective in before the next one ends.

## When to Use This Reference

Use TC 25-20 when the core task is designing or facilitating a structured, multi-perspective review of a shared event — a project retrospective, an incident debrief, a post-negotiation review, a sprint review, or any equivalent where several stakeholders saw different parts of the same action and the goal is integrated learning rather than a verdict.

The TC is the right first reference when any of these conditions hold: the group includes people at very different levels of seniority and you need junior voices to speak freely; the event involves an adversary or counterparty whose perspective is part of the explanation for what happened; the engagement has historically produced self-defence rather than discovery; or the previous review produced clear findings that were never acted on.

Reach for this reference before a review when you are designing the convening agreement (attendance, seating, duration, facilitator selection, opening framing). Reach for it during a review when the discussion has stalled, collapsed into the senior voice, or drifted into grading. Reach for it after a review when assessing whether the follow-up commitments have been kept.

This reference is less useful when the task is exploratory planning (no past event to review), when the relationship between parties is not yet established enough to support candid disclosure, or when the engagement is genuinely evaluative and a graded verdict is the correct output. In those cases, the TC's no-grade framing would work against the engagement's purpose.

## Worked Example

A software product team has just completed a two-month feature build that shipped two weeks late and with three critical bugs found in production the first week. The delivery manager convenes a retrospective and, by default, chairs it herself because she owns the project. She asks the product owner and the three senior engineers to walk through what happened. The junior engineers and QA are in the room but do not speak. The session produces a list of twelve things that went wrong and ends with an agreement to "do better next time." No changes are assigned. The same pattern appears in the retrospective three months later.

A practitioner applying TC 25-20 would redesign the convening before the next event. The first change is facilitator selection: the delivery manager owns the project, which puts her in the same position as the commander evaluating her own command — the TC's advice is that it is hard to be objective about your own performance. A more experienced facilitator from outside the immediate team, or at minimum the delivery manager stepping back from facilitation to participation, would improve independence. The second change is the convening agreement: write down before the retrospective who attends, what the priority topics are, and what the format will be. The fact that the junior engineers and QA were silent in the previous session is a signal that the seating and question-direction were routing conversation through the senior engineers. A horseshoe arrangement with the team lead front-and-centre and the delivery manager to the flank, combined with named open-ended questions directed at junior voices first, addresses this.

The opening of the retrospective names the no-grade framing explicitly: this is not a performance review; there are always things to sustain and things to improve; no one person has the full picture. The facilitator asks open-ended questions — "What were you seeing when the integration tests started failing?" rather than "Why weren't the integration tests catching these bugs?" — and does not redirect to a senior after each junior answer. When the OPFOR perspective is missing, the facilitator asks directly: "What was the customer support team hearing from users in the first week? Can someone speak to that, or should we bring them in?" Before closing, the session produces three named changes — one to the staging environment policy, one to the handoff protocol between engineering and QA, one to the release-day monitoring checklist — each with an owner and a date. The facilitator summarises, ends on a forward note, and leaves the room so the team can debrief privately. At the next retrospective, the first question is whether those three changes were made and used.

## Anti-patterns This Reference Helps Avoid

- Convening an engagement designed to grade or assign verdicts, then being surprised when stakeholders go into self-defence.
- Asking yes/no questions and getting yes/no answers when narrative-eliciting questions would have surfaced the underlying story.
- Letting the highest-ranked person in the room set the framing before junior voices have spoken.
- Seating senior leaders front and centre, then expecting junior voices to speak freely.
- Including only the friendly perspective and treating the OPFOR-equivalent perspective as noise.
- Treating spirit and climate as soft skills to handle if time permits, rather than as operational concerns to design in.
- Convening one large engagement to handle event review, leader-skills discussion, and verdict assignment in sequence — and producing inferior output on all three.
- Choosing the facilitator by rank when experience-in-the-role would produce better facilitation.
- Picking an immediate-next-echelon evaluator when independence requires two echelons of distance.
- Designing an engagement without a written convening agreement (attendance, agenda, location, timing, special needs).
- Closing the engagement without a next-action commitment and a timeline.
- Closing the engagement without leaving the participants space to process in private.
- Repeating the engagement without revising the convening agreement when the previous round surfaced a problem with it.
- Sending the convening commitment up to formal AAR scale when an informal AAR (pinecones-and-a-squad-leader equivalent — fifteen minutes, on the spot, with whatever's at hand) would have produced more honest discussion.
- Recording the engagement with elaborate gadgetry when a notebook would produce more useful notes.
- Reporting statistics that grade rather than statistics that identify trends and reinforce teaching points.

**Borrowed-through gaps.** The TC's References page names two field manuals that this corpus does not hold as primary sources: FM 25-100, *Training the Force* (November 1988), cited as the parent doctrine for training planning and the requirement to schedule retraining as a normal part of the planning process [BT] (Ch 5, "Immediate Retraining"; References); and FM 25-101, *Battle Focused Training* (September 1990), cited as the doctrine this TC supplements and as the source of additional AAR detail in its Appendix G [BT] (Preface; App A, closing). Three further structures are cited as operational sources rather than bibliographic references: ARTEP mission training plans (AMTPs) and soldier's manuals, cited as the source of training-and-evaluation outlines (TEOs) providing tasks, conditions, and standards [BT] (Ch 2, "Reviewing the Training and Evaluation Plan"); the Combined Arms Training Strategy (CATS), cited as the source from which critical gate tasks are derived [BT] (Ch 5, "Immediate Retraining"); and the combat training centers (CTCs), cited in Appendix A as the source of professional AAR practice distilled into general technique [BT] (App A, opening). None of these five sources is held in this corpus; claims originating in them should be verified against the primary doctrine before being presented as independently confirmed.

**Named limits of the source.** TC 25-20 is military doctrine written in 1993, not empirical research. It asserts operational claims — that horseshoe seating suppresses rank-driven voice silencing, that open-ended questions produce better recall than yes/no questions, that climate is load-bearing — on the basis of CTC practitioner experience, not controlled studies. The TC contains no bibliography of research literature, no statistical evidence, and no comparative studies. Its claims are operationally sharp and durable, but they are the distilled experience of professional AAR practitioners, not findings that have been subjected to external scrutiny. Applying this reference to settings outside small-unit military training (corporate retrospectives, medical debriefs, policy reviews) requires the practitioner to judge which principles transfer and which are artefacts of the military context. The TC also predates virtual and hybrid engagement entirely; it has no guidance on facilitating distributed or asynchronous reviews.

**Evidence-marker continuity.** The tc-25-20-army-aar deep reference carries 82 [V] markers, 5 [BT] markers, 2 [AP] markers, and 0 [AR] markers. The high [V] count and zero [AR] count reflect the TC's character as a doctrine document: nearly every claim is a direct statement of procedure or standard, with no authorial reasoning chains working through competing arguments. The two [AP] markers flag the facilitator's craft principles stated as direct guidance rather than demonstrated outcomes. The 5 [BT] markers correspond exactly to the five borrowed-through items listed above. The absence of [AR] markers is informative: this source does not argue for its positions; it states them as doctrine. Practitioners drawing on the TC should supply their own reasoning for why a principle applies in their context, since the TC itself does not.
