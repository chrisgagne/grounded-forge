Why Org Topologies?

The Hidden Complexities of Org Change

When new ways are layered onto existing elements without addressing deeper
organizational patterns, the result is often increased complexity rather than
improved performance. Also, people learn new terminology and attend training
sessions, but fundamental behaviors remain unchanged. This creates
frustration at all levels as people experience the burden of change without
seeing meaningful benefits.
Additionally, many change initiatives focus too narrowly on individual teams
while missing the bigger picture. A single high-‚Äãperforming team can't deliver its
full potential if surrounding structures and processes remain unchanged. True
organizational impact requires alignment across all levels‚Äî‚Äãfrom the
organization's strategic decisions to HR and Finance policies, from group
structures to how teams collaborate daily.
Real change happens when everyone in the organization understands their role
in the transformation and sees how improved organizational design can make
their work more effective and meaningful at the global organizational level.

The Horizontal Axis

The horizontal dimension represents the scope of skills mandate
that is given to an organizational unit. This corresponds to the
level of autonomy a unit has in delivering items of work. The more
rightward an organizational unit is on the map, the more that unit
can quickly deliver without dependencies, and so the better the
flow of value delivery.
To make a connection to the popular software development
approach Scrum, in that model, the horizontal axis is the
increasing completeness of the Definition of Done.
In economic terms, this axis represents the transaction costs
(a.k.a. overhead costs) to deliver value (decreasing from left to
right!), and with that, how much waste from concept to cash.
The lightbulbüí°in the top-‚Äãright corner symbolizes the real
problem to solve. The more capabilities the units have, the closer
they are to the real problem, and the easier it is for them to
create a skillful solution. Note that to start solving the real
problem, progress along only the horizontal axis is not enough.

Incomplete versus Complete Archetypes

In certain organizational designs, individuals are viewed primarily as ‚Äúresource units‚Äù assigned to specific
tasks. These archetypes create partial solutions and rely on ‚Äúthinkers and planners‚Äù in other archetypes
that coordinate and prepare work to ensure full utilization of the ‚Äúresources‚Äù.
This perspective‚Äî‚Äãoften implicit‚Äî‚Äãcan be made explicit and visual with Org Topologies mapping.
These archetypes are incomplete because they require other archetypes to deliver customer value
collectively. Incompleteness arises whenever an organizational unit holds only part of the skills or does
not have the mandate to apply all the skills needed to serve customers. By contrast, complete archetypes
can independently deliver customer value without depending on other archetypes. Members of these
archetypes typically focus on ‚Äúvalue‚Äù and emphasize crucial concepts like ‚Äúoutcome‚Äù and ‚Äúflow.‚Äù
One consequence of an org design with complete archetypes is that they can become independently
operating silos. Whether that‚Äôs a virtue or vice depends.

Directing Archetypes

This quadrant represents directing archetypes that oversee the

Strategic Org Design

Something important not every leader knows: (1) different org
designs can have different goals (adaptiveness, flow, ‚Ä¶), (2)
organizational goal can help or hinder a business objective
(market share, profit, ‚Ä¶), (3) leaders can and should
deliberately design an organization to be fit for purpose: that
is, the org design goal (e.g. ‚Äúadaptive‚Äù) should align with and
influence the business objective (e.g. ‚Äúprofit‚Äù).
This is strategic org design in which every organizational
element‚Äî‚Äãfrom structures to career paths to policies to
processes‚Äî‚Äãis intentionally decided and aligned so that each
part reinforces the others to be fit for purpose (‚Äúthe org goal

This approach contrasts sharply with adopting a pre-‚Äãexisting
framework that promises to ‚Äúsolve your problems.‚Äù And it
contrasts with the idea of senior management only defining
business objectives; in strategic org design, leaders
understand and design the details of their internal
organization to be fit for purpose. They align the external
(business objective) and internal (org design).

There are three common Org Topologies to know, starting

In this topology, the value-‚Äãcreation work takes place among
the Doing Archetypes in the lower left quadrant. However,
none of these can independently produce end-‚Äãto-‚Äãend
customer value. They depend on each other due to narrow

understanding of customer value, as their work mandate
focuses solely on specific tasks or capabilities.
The Directing Archetypes in the upper left quadrant treat
the Doing Archetypes as ‚Äúresources,‚Äù matching skill demand
with skill supply. The Delivery Archetypes do not participate
in high-‚Äãlevel discovery due to ‚Äòefficiency‚Äô justifications.
Consequently, analysis in the form of detailed specifications

traditional project management with significant upfront
planning and estimation, and frequent monitoring and
replanning in cases of high variability.
Learning in this topology is largely confined to improving
existing skills rather than discovering new ones. The
Resource Topology is fit for organizations that rent available
resources for their existing defined skills, reaching their
business objectives (e.g. making profit) by keeping 100% of
