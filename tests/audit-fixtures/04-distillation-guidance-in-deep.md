## Part VII: Motivation (Ch 7)

### Expectancy theory

Vroom's expectancy theory proposes that motivation is a multiplicative function of three components: expectancy (effort → performance), instrumentality (performance → outcome), and valence (value of the outcome) [AP] (Ch 7.4, "Expectancy Theory"). Because the product is multiplicative, a zero on any component produces zero motivation regardless of the other two [AR] (Ch 7.4).

The text presents three managerial implications: ensure employees believe effort leads to performance (address training and resource gaps), ensure they believe performance leads to rewards (clarify the reward mechanism), and ensure the rewards on offer are ones employees actually value (individualise reward selection) [AR] (Ch 7.4, "Implications for Management").

When diagnosing low motivation using this framework, ask:
- Has the employee received sufficient training and resources to believe their effort will translate to performance?
- Is the link between performance and reward clear, transparent, and consistent?
- Have you confirmed the offered rewards match what this employee actually values, rather than assuming standard incentives apply?

Porter and Lawler extended the model to show that satisfaction follows from perceived equity of reward, not directly from reward receipt [AP] (Ch 7.4, "Porter-Lawler Model").
