## Part XIV: Conflict and negotiation (Ch 14)

### Five Thomas conflict-resolution modes

The text presents Thomas's two-dimensional model of conflict-resolution behaviour, scoring each mode on assertiveness (concern for one's own outcomes) and cooperativeness (concern for the other party's outcomes) [AP] (Ch 14.2, "Conflict Resolution Modes"). Five modes are catalogued at the four corners and centre of the resulting grid:

- **Competing** (high assertiveness, low cooperativeness): pursue one's own concerns at the other party's expense.
- **Accommodating** (low assertiveness, high cooperativeness): yield to the other party's concerns.
- **Avoiding** (low both): withdraw from the conflict.
- **Collaborating** (high both): work jointly toward an outcome that satisfies both parties.
- **Compromising** (moderate both): find an expedient middle ground.

The text takes a measured position: no mode is universally best; the appropriate mode depends on the stakes, the relationship, the time available, and the relative power of the parties [AR] (Ch 14.2).

### Skilled vs average negotiator performance

A study of skilled and average negotiators reports that skilled negotiators consider 5.1 options per issue versus 2.6 for average negotiators, and spend 39% of planning time on areas of common ground versus 11% for average negotiators (Ch 14.4, Table 14.4) [AP].
